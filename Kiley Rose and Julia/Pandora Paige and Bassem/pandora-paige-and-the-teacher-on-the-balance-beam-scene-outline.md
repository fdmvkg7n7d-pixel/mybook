# Pandora Paige and the Teacher on the Balance Beam — Scene Outline

## SCENE INFORMATION

- **Characters:** Pandora Paige Key (8), Gymnastics Teacher
- **Onstage animal:** None (Burden does not arrive until age 14)
- **Locations:** Gymnastics building — French Woods (Hancock, New York)
- **When:** Session 2, Brandi Jessica and Pandora Paige's first summer at French Woods (July 2053). Several days into the session, after a Gymnastics class has ended.
- **Status:** New scene outline
- **Tone:** Quiet, vulnerable, intimate — the beam as a place for truth

## SCENE PURPOSE

This scene captures the moment Pandora Paige sits on the balance beam with her Gymnastics teacher and tells her what Brandi Jessica went through — the enucleation, the chemotherapy, the radiotherapy — and what it was like to be the twin who watched all of it happen and couldn't stop it.

It establishes:
- That Pandora carries the weight of what she witnessed during Brandi's treatments — not as a burden she resents, but as an experience she has never put into words for anyone at camp.
- That the balance beam — Pandora's strongest apparatus, the place where she is most in control — becomes the place where she lets go of control and tells the truth. The irony is intentional. The beam holds her up while she says the things she has been holding.
- That the teacher, who has noticed Pandora's fierce drive and her frustration with the uneven bars, asks the right question at the right time — and listens to the answer.
- That sitting on the beam changes the conversation. Up high, four feet off the ground, the gym looks different. The truth sounds different.
- That Pandora does not share this for sympathy. She shares it because the teacher asked, and Pandora has never been able to lie when someone asks her directly.

## BEATS

### Beat 1: After Class — Pandora Stays Late

- Gymnastics class ends. The other campers file out — Wren, the other girls, heading to their next activity or to the cafeteria.
- Pandora stays. She has been working on the uneven bars all class, and the glide kip is still inconsistent — she catches it sometimes and misses it other times. She is frustrated.
- The teacher is resetting the equipment: adjusting the springboard, folding the mats, bringing the bars back to standard height.
- Teacher: "You're staying again?"
- Pandora, chalking her hands: "I need to get this kip."
- Teacher: "You'll get it. You get everything eventually."
- Pandora: "Eventually isn't good enough. I want to get it now."
- The teacher pauses. She has heard this tone before — not from a child who wants to be the best, but from a child who is used to fighting for things that cannot be fought for.
- Pandora tries the kip once more. Misses it. Drops to the mat. Gets up immediately and chalks her hands again.
- The teacher watches her. She does not correct the technique — the form is right. The problem is something else.

### Beat 2: The Teacher Invites Pandora to the Beam

- The teacher finishes resetting the bars. She walks to the balance beam and sits on it, legs dangling, hands flat on the leather. She is a compact, muscular woman, and she sits on the beam the way she sits on a bench — easy, comfortable, like it is the most natural place in the world.
- Teacher: "Pandora. Come here."
- Pandora looks up from the chalk. She walks over, still holding the chalk block.
- Teacher: "Sit."
- Pandora mounts the beam and sits beside the teacher. Their shoulders are a few inches apart. The beam is four inches wide, four feet off the ground. Their feet dangle side by side.
- It is quiet in the gym. The other campers are gone. The fans hum overhead. The sunlight comes through the high windows in long slants of gold.
- Pandora: "Why are we sitting on the beam?"
- Teacher: "Because the spring floor is for coaching. The beam is for talking."
- Pandora looks at her. The teacher is not looking at her. She is looking out across the gym, her feet swinging slightly, easy and unhurried.
- Teacher: "On the first day, you said you weren't scared of heights or of the apparatus. You said you were scared of lots of things — just not this. I've been wondering what you meant."
- Pandora stops swinging her feet. She holds the chalk block in both hands.
- Pandora: "You really want to know?"
- Teacher: "I wouldn't have asked if I didn't."

### Beat 3: Pandora Tells Her About Her Fears

- Pandora sets the chalk block on the mat below. She sits on the beam with her hands flat on the leather on either side of her, the way you sit on a wall.
- Pandora: "Last year, my twin sister Brandi got cancer. Retinoblastoma. It's eye cancer. They found it two days before our seventh birthday."
- The teacher nods slowly. She was briefed about Brandi Jessica before the summer — she knows about the blindness. But she has not heard this from Pandora's perspective.
- Pandora: "First they removed both of her eyes. Bilateral enucleation. February. She was seven. She hasn't been able to see since."
- Her voice is steady. She is not crying. She is sitting on a balance beam four feet off the ground, and she is telling the truth.
- Pandora: "Then she had chemotherapy. She was so sick. She couldn't eat. She couldn't stop throwing up. Her hair fell out in clumps. Mommy and Mama — my moms — they held a bowl for her and they held her hand and they held all of us together, but Brandi was so scared. She was scared of the needles and she was scared of the smell of the hospital and she was scared that it was never going to end."
- A beat.
- Pandora: "Then she had radiotherapy. She was tired all the time. She'd fall asleep in the middle of a sentence. She'd ask me to read to her and then she'd be asleep before I finished the first page. The radiation made her skin red and sore. She had to wear a mask for the treatment — a mask that held her head still — and she was so scared of the mask that Mommy and Mama had to teach us Partner Yoga just to help her calm down enough to lie still."
- A beat.
- Pandora: "I was there for all of it. Every chemo session. Every radiation appointment. Every night in the hospital. I slept in the chair next to her bed. I held the bucket when she threw up. I read to her when she couldn't keep her eyes open. I learned Yoga with her because Mommy and Mama said it would help, and it did help — when all four of us were on the mat together, it was the only time Brandi wasn't afraid."
- She looks down at her hands on the beam.
- Pandora: "But I could see. I could see everything. I saw her lose her hair. I saw the tubes. I saw the way her face changed. I saw the mask they put on her for radiation. I saw her lying in that hospital bed, and I couldn't do anything about it. I couldn't fix it. I couldn't take it away. I just had to watch."
- Her voice is steady. She is not crying. She has told parts of this story before — to friends at school, to the camp administration. But she has never told anyone what it was like to watch.
- Pandora: "That's what I'm scared of. I'm scared of watching something happen to someone I love and not being able to stop it. I'm scared of being helpless. When I'm on the beam or on the silks, I'm not helpless. I can control what my body does. I can commit to the movement and land it. That's why I'm not scared of this."
- She looks at the uneven bars. "That's why the kip frustrates me so much. Because I should be able to control it. And I can't. Not yet."

### Beat 4: The Teacher Responds

- The gym is quiet. The fans hum. The bars sway slightly from the last attempt.
- The teacher lets the silence sit. She does not rush to fill it.
- Then she says, carefully:
  - "Pandora, what you described — watching your sister go through all of that, and being there for every moment of it — that is not helplessness. That is the bravest thing I have ever heard."
- Pandora: "I wasn't brave. I was just there."
- Teacher: "You were there. You stayed. You saw everything and you didn't look away. You held the bucket. You read the stories. You got on the yoga mat with her when she was scared. That is not helplessness. Helplessness is leaving. Helplessness is not showing up. You showed up every single day."
- Pandora is quiet. Her eyes are bright but she does not cry.
- Teacher: "And what you do on the beam and the silks — that's not the opposite of fear. That's what you built on top of fear. You took the thing that scared you most — not being in control — and you found a place where you could be in control. That's not running away from your fear. That's answering it."
- Pandora: "I never thought of it that way."
- Teacher: "That's because you've been too busy doing it to step back and see it."
- They sit on the beam for a moment longer. The sunlight has shifted. The gym is golden and quiet.
- Teacher: "You know what I like about the beam?"
- Pandora: "What?"
- Teacher: "It's the only apparatus in here that holds still. The bars move. The vault gets hit and bounces. The spring floor gives under your feet. But the beam just sits there. It doesn't move unless you move on it. It's the most stable thing in the room, and everyone thinks it's the scariest."
- Pandora: "Because you can fall off."
- Teacher: "Because you can fall off. But that's true of everything. The beam is just honest about it."
- Pandora looks down at the beam under her hands — the leather, the four-inch width, the solid construction beneath the padding.
- Pandora: "It doesn't pretend to be wider than it is."
- Teacher: "No. It doesn't."

### Beat 5: The Kip

- They sit a little longer. Then the teacher slides off the beam and lands on the spring floor.
- Teacher: "Come down. I want to show you something."
- Pandora drops off the beam. The teacher walks to the low bar and adjusts the height — a fraction of an inch lower.
- Teacher: "Try the kip."
- Pandora mounts the bar. She swings into the glide, pulls on the kip — and catches it. Clean, smooth, controlled. She circles the bar and dismounts with a stuck landing.
- She stares at the bar. Then at her hands. Then at the teacher.
- Pandora: "You lowered it."
- Teacher: "A quarter inch. Your glide was slightly too long for the standard height. Your body knew it before your mind did."
- Pandora: "I've been trying to fix my hands."
- Teacher: "Your hands were never the problem. The problem was that you were fighting the bar instead of working with it. Sometimes the thing that feels out of control isn't actually out of control — it just needs a small adjustment you haven't seen yet."
- Pandora looks at the bar for a long time. She is thinking about more than gymnastics.
- Pandora: "Is that how it works? You just keep making small adjustments until it fits?"
- Teacher: "That's exactly how it works. In gymnastics. And in everything else."
- Pandora nods. She chalks her hands and mounts the bar again. She catches the kip a second time. And a third.
- By the fourth try, it feels like it belongs to her.

### Beat 6: Leaving the Gym

- Pandora gathers her things — her water bottle, her towel, her chalk bag. The gym is empty and quiet. The late afternoon light has shifted.
- At the door, she turns back to the teacher.
- Pandora: "Thank you. For listening. And for the bar."
- Teacher: "Pandora — one more thing."
- Pandora: "Yeah?"
- Teacher: "Your sister is lucky to have you. But you're also allowed to have yourself. You don't always have to be the one who watches. Sometimes you get to be the one who flies."
- Pandora stands in the doorway for a moment. Then she smiles — a real, unguarded smile that the teacher has never seen before.
- Pandora: "I'm going to tell Brandi you said that. She's going to love it."
- Teacher: "Tell her whatever you want. Just make sure you hear it too."
- Pandora walks out of the gymnastics building into the afternoon sun. She feels lighter than she has all summer.

## RELATION TO CONTINUITY

- This scene takes place during **Session 2**, several days into the twins' first summer at French Woods (July 2053).
- The Gymnastics teacher is the same teacher from Pandora's first day — the compact, muscular woman who recognized Pandora's commitment.
- This scene follows directly from Pandora's line on the first day: *"I'm scared of lots of things. Just not this."* The teacher remembered and asked.
- Pandora's fears — watching Brandi go through enucleation, chemotherapy, and radiotherapy, seeing everything Brandi couldn't, and being unable to stop it — are consistent with the established medical timeline: Brandi was diagnosed at age seven (December 2051), had bilateral enucleation in February 2052, then underwent chemotherapy, then radiotherapy.
- The Partner Yoga detail — Kiley Rose and Julia teaching both twins Yoga during treatments to help Brandi calm down — is consistent with established canon. All four on the mat together.
- The teacher was briefed about Brandi Jessica before the summer (through the April phone call from Kiley Rose and Julia), so she knows about the blindness. But she has not heard the story from Pandora's perspective.
- The glide kip on the uneven bars was established as Pandora's weakest skill in the first-day scene. The teacher's adjustment — lowering the bar a quarter inch — gives her the physical adjustment she needed, while the conversation gives her the emotional one.
- The teacher's line — *"Sometimes you get to be the one who flies"* — connects directly to Pandora's description of the silks (*"like flying"*) and to Brandi's response that night: *"It sounds like flying. Then I'm flying too. Because you told me."*
- The beam as a place for truth — the teacher says *"the beam is for talking"* — mirrors the beam's quality of honesty. The beam doesn't pretend. Pandora doesn't pretend. The truth belongs up there.
- This scene does NOT change Pandora's role as Brandi's helper. It deepens the understanding of why she is so fierce in that role — she is not just protecting Brandi; she is answering her own fear of helplessness.
