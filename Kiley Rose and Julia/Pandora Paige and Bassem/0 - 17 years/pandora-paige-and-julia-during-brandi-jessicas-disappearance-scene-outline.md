# Pandora Paige and Julia During Brandi Jessica's Disappearance — Scene Outline

## SCENE INFORMATION

- **Characters:** Pandora Paige Key (six years old), Julia (eighteen years old)
- **Offstage / referenced characters:** Brandi Jessica Key (missing)
- **Characters also staying at the townhouse:** Mike Key, Terri Anne Key, Rachel Darcy Key (twenty-five years old), Sabrina Bryna Key (nineteen years old), the Police / Detectives
- **Location:** Kiley Rose and Julia's townhouse, New York City
- **When:** December 27 through December 31, 2051 (The days following Brandi Jessica's kidnapping on December 26)
- **Status:** New scene outline
- **Tone:** Raw, tender, heartbreakingly intimate, fiercely protective, survival-focused, the quiet devastation of the twin left behind

---

## SCENE PURPOSE

This scene outlines **Pandora Paige's emotional devastation and fierce clinginess in the days and weeks following her identical twin sister Brandi Jessica's kidnapping — and Julia's role as her anchor.**

While Kiley Rose is consumed by the police investigation, the coordination with Mike's channels, and the agonizing wait for news, **Julia becomes Pandora Paige's primary emotional lifeline.** The whole family — **Mike, Terri Anne, Rachel Darcy, and Sabrina Bryna — are staying over at the townhouse**, refusing to leave Kiley, Julia, and Pandora alone. But even surrounded by love, the six-year-old is unmoored. Half of her body, half of her language, half of her entire existence has been ripped away in the night. The twin bond that has defined every moment of her six years of life has been severed without warning, and Pandora does not know how to exist in a world where Brandi Jessica is not beside her.

She clings. She clings with a ferocity that is primal and unrelenting — to Julia's neck, to Kiley's lap, to anyone who will hold her. She refuses to be put down. She refuses to sleep alone. She refuses to eat unless someone is holding her. She wets the bed in the night, too terrified and too deep in grief to wake herself, and the shame of it makes her cling even harder. She is not being difficult. She is trying to keep herself from disappearing too.

Julia — eighteen years old, not yet the twins' legal parent, not yet married to Kiley, but already the steadiest presence in that townhouse — does not push her away. She does not tell her to be brave. She does not say *"It's going to be okay"* when she cannot promise that. She holds her. She holds her for hours, for days, letting Pandora's small fists grip her shirt and her tears soak her collar, because Julia understands something the police and the investigators do not: **Pandora is not grieving a sister. She is grieving half of herself.**

But Julia cannot stay forever. On **December 30**, she must report to **Key Porn Studios** for a scheduled film shooting. The separation — even temporary — is devastating for Pandora, who has not been out of Julia's arms since the kidnapping. **Mike Key steps in to hold Pandora while Julia works.** **Terri Anne drives Julia to the studio.** The arrangement is necessary, practical, and emotionally brutal for everyone involved.

## SCENE ARC / BEATS

### Beat 1: The First Morning — December 27
- It is the morning after the kidnapping. The townhouse is quiet in a way that is wrong — no squeaking of small feet on hardwood, no twin laughter from the bedroom. Police have come and gone. Kiley Rose is in the kitchen, her voice hoarse, her eyes hollow. Mike is at the townhouse, in the study, coordinating search efforts on his phone and laptop.
- Pandora Paige wakes up alone in the twins' shared bed. Brandi's side is empty. The sheets are cold. She sits up and immediately begins to cry — not the loud, sharp cry of a child who wants attention, but the deep, guttural sob of a child who has lost something she cannot name. Her hands reach across the mattress, patting the empty space, as if Brandi might still be there.
- Julia hears her from the hallway and is in the room before Pandora can scream. The moment Julia reaches the bed, Pandora launches herself at her — arms around Julia's neck, legs wrapping around her waist, face buried against her throat. She is shaking so hard her teeth chatter.
- Julia holds her. She sits down on the edge of the bed with Pandora wrapped around her like a koala and rocks her gently. She does not say *"It's okay."* She says: *"I've got you. I'm right here."*
- Pandora's grip does not loosen. It tightens. Her small fingers dig into Julia's shoulders, her collar, her hair. She is holding on as if letting go will mean she disappears too.

### Beat 2: Refusing to Be Put Down
- Over the next several days, a pattern establishes itself: **Pandora Paige refuses to be put down.** The moment Julia or Kiley tries to set her on the couch, on a chair, on the floor, Pandora's hands seize up, her breathing turns panicked, and she clings harder.
- It is not a tantrum. It is survival. Every time she is set down, the absence beside her becomes unbearable — the empty chair at the table, the empty side of the bathtub, the empty pillow. Being held means she is not alone in the space Brandi left behind.
- Julia carries her. She carries her to the kitchen, to the bathroom, to the window where they watch the snow fall. She holds Pandora on her hip while she heats soup with one hand. She holds her while she talks to the detectives, Pandora's face pressed into her neck, small fingers gripping her shirt.
- When Julia's arms need rest, Rachel or Sabrina takes a turn holding Pandora — but Pandora always reaches back for Julia. Rachel rocks her in the armchair, murmuring softly, and Pandora tolerates it for ten minutes before her hands start reaching. Sabrina holds her and signs stories into her palm the way she does with the younger cousins, and Pandora watches her hands, briefly distracted, before the grief swells again and she reaches for Julia's neck.
- The police notice. One detective, watching Julia hold a trembling Pandora while answering questions, quietly tells his partner: *"That kid isn't acting. That's real."* Pandora's clinginess — her raw, unfiltered need — is the single most compelling piece of evidence that this family had nothing to do with Brandi's disappearance.
- Julia does not complain. Her arms ache. Her back aches. She has not slept more than two hours at a time since the kidnapping. But she does not put Pandora down. She will not be the one who lets go.

### Beat 3: The Night Terrors and Wetting the Bed
- At night, Pandora sleeps in Kiley and Julia's bed, wedged between them. She cannot sleep in the twins' room. The empty bed is too much.
- She has night terrors. She wakes up screaming Brandi's name, thrashing, her eyes open but seeing nothing. She reaches out into the dark, patting the mattress, the pillows, Julia's arm — looking for her sister.
- **Pandora wets the bed.** In the deep, exhausted sleep that follows her night terrors, her small body simply lets go. She wakes up in the morning in damp sheets, her face flushed with shame, and the humiliation makes her cling even harder to Julia — as if she needs to prove she is not a baby, as if she is afraid Julia will put her down if she is too much trouble.
- **Pandora tries to apologize.** She looks up at Kiley Rose and Julia with wet, guilty eyes, her voice tiny and trembling: *"I'm sorry. I'm sorry I wet the bed. I didn't mean to. I'm sorry."* She says it over and over, as if the apology itself might make her worthy of being held.
- Kiley Rose kneels down in front of her and takes her small hands. Her voice is steady, her eyes locked on Pandora's: *"You don't have to be sorry, baby. Not ever. You are upset. Your body is upset. That's all. You did nothing wrong."* Julia crouches beside her and strokes Pandora's hair back from her forehead: *"Nothing to apologize for. Not ever. Okay?"* Pandora nods, but her chin trembles. She does not believe it yet. But she holds onto the words.
- **Kiley Rose or Julia draws her a warm, soothing bath.** The water is perfect — not too hot, not too cold, with the soft bubble bath the twins love. They lower Pandora into the tub gently, and the warmth seeps into her small, tense body. Kiley or Julia kneels beside the tub, washing her with a soft cloth, her movements slow and careful and full of love. The water, the warmth, the gentle hands — it is the opposite of everything the world has become. For a few minutes, Pandora's trembling stops. She leans her head back against the tub and closes her eyes, and Kiley or Julia holds her hand above the water, not letting go, not even for a second.
- After the bath, wrapped in a warm towel, Pandora climbs back into Julia's arms. The message is clear: *You can fall apart here. I am not going anywhere.*
- After a night terror, Pandora will not go back to sleep unless Julia is holding her hand or has her arm around her. She needs the physical proof of another body beside her — warmth, breath, a heartbeat that is not hers.
- Sometimes, in the dark, Pandora whispers: *"Brandi doesn't like the dark, Julia. She's scared. Who's holding her?"*
- Julia has no answer. She just holds her tighter.

### Beat 4: The Christmas Dress
- On the morning of December 28, Pandora finds Brandi's red Christmas dress — the one they both wore on Christmas Day — draped over a chair where it was left after the party. She picks it up and presses it to her face, breathing in.
- She carries it everywhere. She will not let it out of her sight. She holds it against her chest when Julia carries her, she sleeps with it balled up against her cheek, she wraps her hands in the fabric when she is sitting on Julia's lap.
- It is not a comfort object. It is the last thing Brandi wore in this house. It still smells like her — like little-girl shampoo and the cocoa they had before bed.
- Julia sees her holding it and does not take it away. She does not suggest they wash it. She lets Pandora hold onto whatever remains.

### Beat 5: Eating Only While Being Held — The Applesauce
- Pandora will not eat unless someone is holding her. She refuses to sit at the table — the empty chair across from her is unbearable. She refuses to hold a spoon herself; her hands are too busy gripping Julia or Kiley.
- Julia feeds her. She sits with Pandora on her lap at the kitchen counter, one arm around her, the other bringing a spoon to her mouth. Soup. Pancakes. Whatever she will accept.
- **Pandora will only eat applesauce.** Not because it is her favorite — but because it is the one thing Brandi Jessica cannot stand. Brandi hates applesauce. She makes a face every time it appears on the table. She pushes the bowl away and says *"Ew"* in that dramatic, identical-twin way that always makes Pandora laugh.
- Now Pandora eats it. She eats it on purpose. Every spoonful is a quiet, defiant act of love — eating the thing her sister hates, keeping it alive, keeping Brandi's *"ew"* alive in the space where she should be sitting. She is eating for both of them. She is being disgusted for both of them. The applesauce is not food; it is a prayer.
- Julia understands this without being told. She does not offer anything else. She does not say *"Do you want something different?"* She just brings the applesauce, one spoonful at a time, and lets Pandora eat her sister's hatred like a sacrament.
- Sometimes, mid-bite, Pandora stops and looks at the empty chair and says, very quietly: *"Brandi hates applesauce."* And Julia nods and says: *"I know, baby. She does."* And they sit there, and the silence fills the space where a six-year-old should be making a disgusted face.

### Beat 6: Pandora Asking for Brandi
- Over the following days, Pandora asks the same questions, over and over, in different ways:
  - *"When is Brandi coming home?"*
  - *"Is Brandi cold?"*
  - *"Does Brandi have her red dress?"*
  - *"Is Brandi eating pancakes?"*
  - *"Who is holding Brandi when she's scared?"*
- Each question lands on Julia like a blade. She cannot answer them. Kiley cannot answer them. No one can.
- Julia does not lie to her. She does not say *"Soon"* or *"Tomorrow."* She says: *"We are looking for her. Mom and Mike and the police are looking everywhere. And I am not going to stop holding you until she is home."*
- Pandora nods. She does not cry when Julia says this. She just tightens her grip and buries her face again. The answer is not enough, but the arms around her are.

### Beat 7: The Twin Language Breaks
- Pandora, in her grief, occasionally slips into the twin language — the private, wordless language she and Brandi share. She babbles sounds that have no meaning to anyone but her sister, her small hands making gestures that were only ever meant for one person.
- She catches herself. She stops. The silence that follows is the loudest sound in the townhouse.
- Julia witnesses this and her heart shatters, but she does not react with pity. She simply reaches over and takes Pandora's hand, letting her know she is there, even if she cannot speak that language.
- Sometimes, in the quiet, Pandora whispers the twin words into Julia's ear, as if Julia might understand them, as if she might be able to send the message to wherever Brandi is. Julia listens to every sound. She memorizes them. She does not know what they mean, but she holds them as if they are sacred.

### Beat 8: Julia's Private Collapse
- Julia does not break in front of Pandora. She holds it together with every ounce of the discipline and composure that define her. But late at night, when Pandora is finally asleep in her arms, Julia sits in the dark and cries.
- She cries silently, her jaw clenched, her body rigid, because if she shakes she will wake Pandora. She cries for Brandi Jessica, who is out there somewhere in the hands of a monster. She cries for Kiley, who is being torn apart by guilt and terror. And she cries for Pandora, this small, fierce girl who has lost the other half of her soul and still reaches for Julia's neck every morning like she is the only thing keeping her on the earth.
- She does not tell Kiley about these moments. They are hers. She carries them the way she carries Pandora — with everything she has.
- When Kiley finds her once, sitting on the bathroom floor at 3 a.m., Julia wipes her face and says simply: *"I'm fine. Go back to Pandora."* But Kiley does not go back. She sits down next to her on the tile floor and holds her hand. They do not speak. They do not need to.
- **Mike and Terri Anne hold Julia too.** One evening, when Pandora is finally asleep and Rachel is watching her, Julia steps into the hallway and leans against the wall, and something in her finally cracks. She does not slide down — she does not let herself fall — but her shoulders shake, and her hand comes up to cover her mouth, and she makes a sound that is barely human. Mike is there before she can stop herself. He wraps his arms around her from the side, his big hand on the back of her head, pulling her against his chest. Terri Anne comes from the other direction, her arms around Julia's waist, her forehead pressed against Julia's shoulder. They hold her together — Mike and Terri Anne, the two people in this house who understand what it costs to be the strong one. Julia does not fight it. She lets them hold her. For two minutes, she is not the anchor. She is the one who needs holding. Then she wipes her face, straightens up, and goes back to Pandora.

### Beat 9: December 30 — Julia Leaves for Key Porn Studios
- On the morning of **December 30**, Julia has to leave. She is due at **Key Porn Studios** for a scheduled film shooting. It is not optional — it is work, it is contracted, and in the middle of the crisis she still has to earn. The reality of her life does not pause because a child has been kidnapped.
- **Terri Anne arrives at the townhouse to drive Julia to the studio.** She stands in the hallway, coat on, keys in hand, waiting. She can hear Pandora crying upstairs before Julia even comes down.
- Julia tells Pandora she has to go. The reaction is immediate and catastrophic. Pandora's hands seize Julia's shirt and will not let go. She is not crying — she is screaming, a raw, primal scream that comes from the same place as the night terrors. Her legs wrap around Julia's waist, her face presses into Julia's neck, and she will not unclench.
- Julia holds her and whispers into her hair. She does not rush her. She does not pry her fingers loose. She stands in the bedroom and rocks her until the screaming turns to hitching, broken sobs.
- **Mike Key steps in.** He is already in the house, coordinating search efforts on his phone and laptop. The whole family is staying over — Mike, Terri Anne, Rachel, Sabrina — but Mike is the one Pandora will accept after Julia. He is big and steady and he smells like Kiley's house, and that is enough.
- Julia gently peels Pandora's fingers from her shirt, one by one, and transfers her into Mike's arms. The moment Julia lets go, Pandora reaches back for her, her small hands grasping at air. Mike holds her firmly against his chest — his big, steady hand cradling the back of her head, her face pressed against his shoulder.
- Mike says nothing about her tears. He does not shush her. He holds her the way Julia holds her — still, patient, unyielding. He walks her to the window and stands there, rocking her gently, letting her look out at the street.
- Julia stands in the doorway, watching, her own eyes wet. She almost doesn't leave. Terri Anne touches her arm gently: *"She's in good hands. Let's go."*
- Julia kisses the top of Pandora's head and whispers: *"I'll be back. I promise."* She walks out of the bedroom and down the stairs. She can hear Pandora crying behind her, and every step feels like a betrayal.

### Beat 10: At Key Porn Studios — Julia and Greenlee
- **Terri Anne drives Julia to Key Porn Studios in silence.** The city moves past the windows — snow, taxis, people bundled in coats — and Julia sits in the passenger seat, her hands empty, her arms aching from the absence of Pandora's weight.
- Terri Anne does not try to make conversation. She drives steadily, one hand on the wheel, occasionally glancing at Julia. She understands what this costs.
- At the studio, Julia works. She does her job with the same composure she brings to everything — disciplined, professional, controlled. But her hands are slightly shaking, and between takes, she steps away and checks her phone for messages from Mike or Kiley. No news. No update on Brandi.
- **Greenlee is at the studio.** Julia and Greenlee must work together on a daily webcam — a contractual obligation that does not pause for anyone's grief. Greenlee's hatred of Kiley Rose is deep-rooted and personal. The studio does not understand the context; they only see two performers who have to work.
- **Greenlee knows who has Brandi Jessica.** She knows. She has known since the kidnapping. Because **Andrew is Greenlee's cousin.** The kidnapper is her blood. Her own cousin took a six-year-old from the Christmas party, and Greenlee knows exactly who he is and what he's done. And she says nothing. She sits across from Julia on that webcam, day after day, watching Julia's hands shake, watching her check her phone for news, watching her compartmentalize grief and perform — and Greenlee holds the single most devastating piece of information in the entire crisis inside her mouth like a loaded weapon. She does not tell Julia. She does not tell anyone. The knowledge gives her power, and she keeps it.
- Julia does not know that Greenlee knows. She sees Greenlee's hostility, her hard face, her cold silences, and she attributes it to the same old resentment — the bad blood between Greenlee and the Key family, whatever history fuels it. She has no idea that the woman sitting three feet away from her could end the search. Could bring Brandi Jessica home. Could stop a six-year-old from being drugged and dressed and passed between men in a hotel room.
- Julia does her best to compartmentalize. She separates the webcam from Pandora, from Brandi, from the townhouse. She performs because she must. But the collision of worlds is suffocating: she is sitting in a studio with a woman who hates her family, pretending everything is normal, while her girlfriend's six-year-old daughter is missing and her other six-year-old is at home crying in Mike's arms.
- And Greenlee watches. She watches Julia's pain the way you watch something burn — not with empathy, not with guilt, but with the cold satisfaction of a woman who knows something the person across from her does not. She holds the answer to the townhouse's agony, and she chooses silence.
- Then, mid-webcam, **Greenlee starts laughing.** Not a small laugh, not a smirk — a full, loud, open-mouthed laugh that comes from somewhere deep and cruel. It fills the studio space. The crew pauses, confused. Julia freezes.
- Julia stares at her, stunned. She does not understand what is funny. Nothing is funny. A six-year-old is missing. Another six-year-old is at home drowning in grief. The townhouse is a crime scene. And Greenlee is laughing.
- Julia asks: *"What is so funny?"*
- Greenlee does not answer. She just keeps laughing, wiping her eyes like it's the funniest thing she's ever seen. And the horror of it — the absolute, bottomless horror — is that Greenlee is laughing because she knows. She knows where Brandi Jessica is. She knows who has her. She knows that the answer to Julia's agony is sitting right here in this room, three feet away, and Julia will never guess. The laugh is not humor. It is power. It is Greenlee holding a child's life in her mouth and finding it hilarious that no one can make her speak.
- **Then Greenlee drops the Easter egg.** After the laugh fades, she leans back in her chair, feigning casualness, and says — as if it's the most throwaway observation in the world:

> *"You know, I've always loved hotels on the outskirts of the city. So quiet. So private. You can just... disappear out there."*

- She says it lightly, almost dreamily, her eyes drifting to the ceiling. The crew doesn't notice. It's just small talk. It's nothing.
- But it's everything. Julia hears it and files it away without understanding why it lands wrong — a small, uncomfortable prickle at the back of her neck, the sense that something was just said that she should catch but can't. She is too exhausted, too grieving, too buried in the crisis to process it. She dismisses it. Greenlee's weird. Greenlee's cruel. Greenlee says things like that.
- Greenlee watches Julia dismiss it, and the corner of her mouth twitches. That's the game. Hide the truth in plain sight. Drop it like a pebble in a pond and watch the ripples die. Julia will remember this moment later — when the truth comes out, when Andrew's name surfaces, when the hotel location is revealed — and the horror of what Greenlee said will come crashing back. *She told me. She sat right there and told me, and I didn't hear it.*
- Julia does not know any of this. She only sees Greenlee laughing at her pain, making strange comments about hotels, and she has no idea why. She has no idea that Greenlee's cousin is the man who took Brandi. The not-knowing is its own kind of torture.
- She thinks about Pandora, reaching for her as she walked away. She thinks about Brandi Jessica, somewhere out there, and no one reaching for her at all. The guilt of leaving — even temporarily, even necessarily — sits in Julia's chest like a stone.
- When the shooting wraps, Julia is out of the studio before anyone can say goodbye. Terri Anne is already waiting in the car.

### Beat 11: The Foyer — Julia, Terri Anne, and Greenlee
- Julia makes it as far as the foyer of Key Porn Studios before she stops. She cannot go outside. She cannot get in the car. Her body simply refuses to move another step. She stands in the foyer, her back against the wall, her hands pressed flat against the cold tile, and everything she has been holding in all day — the webcam, Greenlee's laugh, the strange comment about hotels, Pandora's screaming, the empty bed, the missing child — comes crashing through her composure.
- **Julia breaks down in Terri Anne's arms.** Terri Anne, who has been waiting in the car, sees her through the glass doors and comes inside. Julia collapses into her — not gracefully, not with composure, but fully, like a building coming down. Her hands fist into Terri Anne's coat and she sobs — ugly, heaving sobs that echo in the empty foyer. She says, between breaths:

> *"She's six years old, Terri. She's six. And she's out there somewhere with that man, and I can't — I can't do anything. I'm standing in a studio doing a webcam while a six-year-old is —"*

- She cannot finish the sentence. She will not say what Brandi Jessica might be enduring. The weight of it — the helplessness, the guilt of leaving Pandora, the impossibility of being in two places at once — is crushing her.
- Terri Anne holds her. She holds her the way she held her own children when they were small and the world was too big. She wraps both arms around Julia and presses her cheek against the top of her head and says, her voice breaking:

> *"I know, baby. I know. We are going to find her. Mike is going to find her. That little girl is coming home."*

- Julia shakes her head against Terri Anne's shoulder. *"What if she doesn't? What if she doesn't come home? What do I tell Pandora? What do I —"*
- Terri Anne pulls back and takes Julia's face in her hands, firm and steady, the way only a mother can be: *"You do not go there. You hear me? We do not go there. We fight. We hold Pandora. We wait. And we bring her home."*
- Julia nods, her jaw clenched, her eyes red. She wipes her face with her sleeve. She breathes. She pulls herself together, piece by piece, the way she always does. Terri Anne hands her a tissue and Julia blows her nose and they stand there together in the foyer, two women holding each other up in the cold light of a studio that has nothing to do with the real world.
- **Greenlee walks past them.** She is leaving the studio, her bag over her shoulder, her heels clicking on the tile. She slows as she passes the foyer. She sees Julia — red-eyed, shaking, collapsed against Terri Anne. She hears fragments: *"She's six years old... I can't do anything..."*
- **Greenlee smiles.** Not a laugh this time. Something quieter, something worse — a slow, private smile that spreads across her face like a secret. She does not stop. She does not speak. She just smiles, adjusts her bag, and walks out the door into the winter night. The smile says everything: *I know where she is. I know who has her. And you are standing here crying, and you will never know.*
- Julia does not see the smile. Terri Anne does not see the smile. The foyer is empty except for the two of them, and Greenlee is already gone, her heels fading on the sidewalk, carrying the answer to the townhouse's agony into the dark.

- **Greenlee is going to the hotel.** She walks out into the winter night, gets into her car, and drives — out of the city, past the limits, toward the secluded luxury hotel where her cousin Andrew is holding Brandi Jessica. She is not going there to help. She is not going there to intervene. She is going there because the kidnapping is hers too — she is part of it, complicit in it, connected to it by blood and by choice. She left a six-year-old in the hands of her cousin, walked into a studio, sat across from that child's family, laughed, smiled, and dropped Easter eggs. And now she is driving to the hotel to meet them.
- The implication is unspeakable: while Julia stands in the foyer of Key Porn Studios, sobbing about a six-year-old she cannot save, the woman who just smiled at her pain is driving directly to that child. Greenlee will walk into that hotel room, see Brandi Jessica in the black dress and nylons, see the empty juice glass on the nightstand, see whatever Andrew has done — and she will do nothing to stop it. She will sit down in that room, in that luxury suite, with her cousin and a drugged, captive six-year-old, and she will be exactly where she has chosen to be: on the inside of the nightmare, looking out at the people searching for answers they will never find.
- Julia and Terri Anne get into the car and drive back to the townhouse. In the opposite direction, on the same dark road, Greenlee drives toward the hotel. Two cars, two women, one city — and a six-year-old sitting in the space between them, waiting for someone to come who never will.

### Beat 12: Coming Home — December 30
- When Julia walks through the townhouse door, she finds Mike in the living room, sitting in the armchair by the window with Pandora asleep on his chest. His shirt is damp with her tears. His hand is still cradling the back of her head. He has been holding her for hours.
- Mike looks up at Julia. His eyes are red. He has been on the phone all day, coordinating search efforts with one hand while holding a trembling six-year-old with the other. He does not say anything. He just carefully, gently, shifts Pandora toward Julia.
- Pandora's eyes open the moment she feels Julia's arms. She does not cry this time. She just wraps herself around Julia's neck and holds on, her small fists gripping Julia's collar, her face buried against Julia's throat. Her breathing is ragged, her body still trembling from a day of grief.
- Julia holds her and says: *"I'm back. I told you I'd come back."*
- Pandora does not answer. She just grips tighter. And Julia understands — the grip is the answer. *You came back. You came back. You came back.*
- Mike stands up, his arms finally free. He looks at Julia, then at Pandora, then back at Julia. He says, quietly: *"She asked for you every hour."* Julia nods. She knows.
- Terri Anne, still in the hallway, watches this and says nothing. She takes Mike's coat, hands him a glass of water, and sits down on the couch. The townhouse is quiet again — the kind of quiet that holds its breath.

### Beat 13: Closing Image — December 31
- It is New Year's Eve. The townhouse is quiet. There are no celebrations. No champagne. No countdown. The family is all here — **Mike, Terri Anne, Rachel Darcy, and Sabrina Bryna are staying over at the townhouse**, refusing to leave Kiley, Julia, and Pandora alone in this house.
- Kiley is in the study with Mike, their voices low and steady as they coordinate the search. Rachel sits beside Kiley, holding her hand. Sabrina is in the kitchen, keeping watch over the police radios, signing updates to anyone who needs them.
- Julia sits in the armchair with Pandora Paige in her lap. Pandora is holding the red dress and staring at nothing. Her grip on Julia's shirt has loosened slightly — not because she trusts the world, but because Julia came back, and that is enough for now.
- Julia looks out the window at the falling snow and makes a silent promise: *I will hold this child for as long as it takes. I will not let go. And when Brandi comes home, I will hold her too.*
- The scene holds on the two of them — an eighteen-year-old woman and a six-year-old girl, wrapped together in the quiet devastation of a house that is missing one small, irreplaceable person — as the new year arrives without fanfare, without joy, and without Brandi Jessica.
