# New Year's Day — Kiley Rose and Julia's Bedroom — Scene Outline

## SCENE INFORMATION

- **Characters:** Kiley Rose Key (twenty-one years old), Julia (eighteen years old), Terri Anne Key
- **Offstage / referenced characters:** Pandora Paige Key (six years old, downstairs with Mike and the aunts), Brandi Jessica Key (missing), Mike Key, Rachel Darcy Key, Sabrina Bryna Key, Andrea Jennifer Key (seventeen), Ashley Erin Key (fifteen), the teachers and administrators at Convent of the Sacred Heart, Pandora's classmates
- **Location:** Kiley Rose and Julia's bedroom, the townhouse, New York City
- **When:** January 1, 2052 — New Year's Day, 2:00 p.m.
- **Status:** Revised scene outline
- **Tone:** Three women in a bedroom, trying to solve a problem that has no solution — whether and how Pandora Paige goes back to Convent of the Sacred Heart without her identical twin

---

## SCENE PURPOSE

This scene outlines **the conversation between Terri Anne, Kiley Rose, and Julia about whether and how Pandora Paige should return to Convent of the Sacred Heart without Brandi Jessica.**

It is two o'clock in the afternoon on New Year's Day. They are in Kiley Rose and Julia's bedroom. The door is closed. Pandora is downstairs with Mike and Rachel and Sabrina and Andrea Jennifer and Ashley Erin — the first time she has been out of Julia's arms since December 26. And for the first time in six days, the three women who have to make this decision are in a room together without the child it affects most.

The townhouse is open concept. Downstairs is one continuous space — the living area flows into the dining area flows into the kitchen, and the staircase rises through the center of it all, open-riser, no walls, visible from every angle. Pandora sits on Mike's lap on the couch and stares at the staircase because the staircase is where Julia will appear when she comes down. She can see it. She is always looking at it.

The winter term at Sacred Heart begins in days. Pandora's name is on the class list. So is Brandi Jessica's. The story is already in the newspaper — Mike Key's granddaughter, kidnapped — which means the school already knows. The parents already know. The girls already know. There is no controlling this information. There is no quiet way to prepare anyone. Pandora will walk into Sacred Heart and every person in that building will already know why she is alone.

And three women must decide what to do with a six-year-old who has been wetting the bed, eating only applesauce, and clinging to Julia's shirt so hard her fingernails leave marks — whether to send her back into a world that already knows her sister is gone.

---

## SCENE ARC / BEATS

### Beat 1: 2:00 p.m. — The Room
- It is two o'clock in the afternoon on January 1, 2052. The bedroom door is closed. Kiley Rose is sitting on the edge of the bed. Julia is standing at the window. Terri Anne is in the chair by the other window — she has been sitting there since Mike took Pandora downstairs at noon, two hours ago.

- The room is quiet. Not peaceful — held. The way a room holds its breath when something needs to be said and no one has said it yet.

- Terri Anne is the one who says it. She is always the one who says the things that need to be said in this family.

> *"We need to talk about Pandora and school. The term starts Monday."*

- The silence that follows is not surprise. It is the silence of two women who have been avoiding the question and have just had it placed in front of them like a glass of water they are not sure they can swallow.

### Beat 2: Kiley Rose's Response — Keep Her Home
- Kiley Rose's answer is immediate:

> *"She's not going."*

- She says it the way she says everything about Pandora now — fast, protective, a door slamming shut. She has spent the last six days watching her surviving daughter come apart. She has seen the wet sheets. She has seen the applesauce. She has seen the hands that will not let go of Julia's shirt. She has heard the question — *"When is Brandi coming home?"* — asked seventeen different ways, and she has answered every one of them with *"We're looking, baby. We're looking,"* and the answer has never been enough, and Kiley Rose cannot imagine sending this child into a school building where she will be separated from Julia, separated from home, separated from the only things that are keeping her tethered to the world.

- *"She can't go back there,"* Kiley says. *"Not without Brandi. Not to that school. Every person in that building knows them. Every teacher. Every girl in her class. She walks into that building and every single person is going to look at her and see Brandi. They're going to ask her where her sister is. They're going to ask her questions she doesn't have answers to, and she is going to fall apart, and I am not going to let that happen."*

- Her voice is shaking. Not from anger — from fear. The fear that if Pandora leaves this house, something else will take her. The fear that the world outside this townhouse is the world that took Brandi Jessica, and it is not safe, and Pandora is not safe in it, and the only thing Kiley Rose can control is whether her daughter walks out the front door.

### Beat 3: Terri Anne's Response — She Has to Go Back
- Terri Anne does not argue. She does not contradict. She listens. She lets Kiley finish. And then she says, gently, the way you say things to someone you love that they do not want to hear:

> *"Kiley. She has to go back."*

- Kiley's head comes up. Her eyes are hard. Terri Anne does not flinch.

- *"I know you want to keep her here. I know you want to wrap her in this house and never let her out. I would want the same thing. But keeping her home is not protecting her. It's freezing her. She is six years old, and she has spent the last six days learning that the world is a place where her sister disappears and she cannot leave Julia's arms. If she stays home, she learns that the world is also a place she cannot re-enter. She learns that she is broken and the world is not safe and she has to hide. And that — that is not what Brandi would want for her. And it is not what she needs."*

- Terri Anne speaks from experience. She has raised children through crisis. She knows what happens when you let grief become the architecture of a child's life — the walls close in, the doors lock, and the child stops growing because the world has been reduced to the size of the room where they feel safe. Safety is not the same as healing. Safety is the precondition. Healing requires movement.

- *"She needs routine,"* Terri Anne says. *"She needs structure. She needs to get up in the morning and go to a place that is not this house, where people care about her, where she has a desk and a teacher and a schedule. She needs to be Pandora at school, not just Pandora the twin who was left behind."*

### Beat 4: The Empty Chair
- Julia has not spoken. She has been standing at the window, listening, her arms crossed over her chest. But now she speaks, and what she says is not about whether Pandora should go back. It is about what will happen when she does.

> *"The chair."*

- One word. Kiley and Terri Anne look at her.

> *"Brandi's chair. Her desk. Her hook in the coatroom. Her place in line. Pandora has walked into that school beside Brandi every single day since kindergarten. Every morning, they walk in together. Every morning, they hang their coats side by side. Every morning, they sit down at their desks and they are two. And now she's going to walk in and there's going to be an empty chair where Brandi sits, and she is going to have to walk past it. Every day. Every morning. Every class."*

- Julia's voice does not crack. But it is close. She has been the one holding Pandora. She knows the geography of this child's grief better than anyone — the way she reaches for a hand that is not there in the night, the way she turns to say something to an empty space beside her, the way she sleeps on the left side of the bed because the right side is Brandi's. Julia knows that the empty chair at Sacred Heart will not be an absence. It will be a presence. A wound that opens every morning at 8:15 and does not close until 3:00.

- *"They already know,"* Julia continues. *"It's in the paper. Mike Key's granddaughter. Every parent at Sacred Heart has read it. Every girl in that class has heard her parents talk about it. They know Brandi is gone. And Pandora is going to walk into that building carrying Brandi's face on her own, and every person who looks at her is going to see the missing one. They're going to look at her and think 'that's the twin' and their faces are going to do the thing that faces do when people are sad and scared and they don't know what to say, and Pandora is going to see that on every face, every hallway, every classroom, and I don't—"*

- She stops. She presses her palm flat against the window glass.

> *"I don't know how to prepare her for that."*

### Beat 5: What the School Needs — The Practical Reality
- Terri Anne brings them back to the practical. Someone has to. She has already spoken to Sacred Heart — quietly, carefully, without giving details that the school does not need and the family cannot afford to have circulating.

- *"I called Sister Margaret,"* Terri Anne says. *"The school already knows. It's in the papers — Mike's name, the kidnapping, all of it. Sister Margaret called me before I called her. She's been waiting to hear from us. She knows Brandi will not be returning for the winter term. She knows Pandora will be coming back alone. And she knows to call Kiley and Julia — they're listed as Pandora's legal guardians. The school will deal with them directly."*

- She lists what the school needs:
  - **The teacher needs to speak to the class before Pandora arrives** — not to tell them, because they already know, but to set the rule: no questions. The girls have heard the news from their parents. The teacher needs to make it clear that Pandora is not there to answer them.
  - **The empty desk needs to be handled.** Terri Anne asks whether Brandi's desk should be removed before Pandora returns, so the absence is not as stark, or whether removing it would be erasing Brandi, which is its own cruelty.
  - **The coatroom hook** — Brandi's name is above her hook, next to Pandora's. Does it stay or go?
  - **The attendance** — when the teacher calls the roll, she will call Brandi Jessica's name out of habit. Every teacher at Sacred Heart knows both twins by name. The sound of Brandi's name in that classroom could break Pandora before the day begins.
  - **The other parents** — it's already out. The newspaper story made sure of that. The family needs to decide how much *more* information to give the school, knowing that anything they share beyond what's already public will be repeated.

- Kiley listens to the list and her face changes. The anger goes out of it. What replaces it is worse: the dawning recognition of how many ways this can hurt Pandora, how many landmines are waiting in a building she thought was safe, how a school — a place of structure and routine and familiarity — can become a field of small daily devastations.

### Beat 6: Kiley Breaks — The Stairs
- Something in the list undoes Kiley. Not the desk. Not the attendance. It is the image — the simple, unavoidable image — of Pandora walking into Sacred Heart alone.

> *"She's walked in by herself before."*

- Kiley's voice is small. Not the fierce, protective voice from before. A smaller, cracked voice.

> *"When Brandi was sick. Last year — the flu — Brandi was home for three days, and Pandora went by herself. She was fine. She walked in, she hung up her coat, she sat down. She was fine."*

- She says *"she was fine"* twice, and the repetition is not reassurance — it is grief. Because it was different. It was illness. Illness has an end. Illness means your sister is at home on the couch watching cartoons and drinking juice and she'll be back in two days. Illness is not this. Illness is not a door that closed and never opened again.

> *"But she hated it. She walked in by herself but she hated it. Every morning, she'd look at me at the door and say 'Can I hold your hand instead?' And I'd say 'Brandi's going to be back on Thursday,' and she'd say 'I know,' and she'd walk in. But she always counted the days. She always counted the days until Brandi came back."*

- Kiley stops. Her hand comes up to cover her mouth.

> *"Every morning since kindergarten, when they walk in together, they hold hands on the steps. They have this thing where they squeeze each other's hand three times before they go through the door — one, two, three — and Brandi always goes first and Pandora always follows. And when Brandi was sick, Pandora would still squeeze three times. She'd squeeze her own hand. Her left hand squeezing her right hand, one, two, three, and then she'd go through the door by herself. And I used to think that was — I used to think it was the sweetest thing I'd ever seen."*

- Her voice breaks.

> *"She's going to squeeze her own hand again. And this time, there's no Thursday. There's no 'Brandi's coming back.' She's going to stand on those steps and squeeze her own hand and walk through that door alone, and I can't—"*

- Kiley stops. Her shoulders shake. She does not make a sound.

- Terri Anne moves first. She reaches from the chair and takes Kiley's hand. Not to comfort. To anchor. The way you hold someone who is about to go under.

- Julia moves second. She comes from the window and sits on the bed behind Kiley. She does not hug her — she puts both hands on Kiley's shoulders, her thumbs pressing into the tight muscles of Kiley's neck, and she holds her there, standing, not letting her fall.

- They hold her like that — Terri Anne's hand, Julia's hands — until Kiley's breathing evens out. She does not wipe her eyes. She sits with the tears on her face and the image of those stairs in her mind, and she does not pull away from their hands.

### Beat 7: Julia's Concern — Separation
- When Kiley can speak again, Julia says what she has been holding:

> *"There's something else. If she goes back, I can't be there."*

- This is the truth that has been sitting under the whole conversation. Julia is Pandora's anchor. Julia is the one Pandora reaches for in the night, the one whose shirt she grips, the one she will not be put down for. Julia is not Pandora's legal parent. Julia is not a student at Sacred Heart. Julia cannot sit in the classroom with Pandora. Julia cannot walk the halls with her. Julia cannot hold her hand through the school day. If Pandora goes back to Sacred Heart, she goes back without Julia.

- *"She has never been away from me for a full school day since this happened,"* Julia says. *"Not once. The longest she's been apart from me is the day I went to the studio — five hours — and she screamed the whole time. Mike held her. Mike is wonderful with her. But she screamed. And school is seven hours. Seven hours in a building where she has never been without Brandi. Seven hours without me. Without Kiley. Without anyone she can reach for when it gets bad."*

- Julia's voice is steady but her hands have not left Kiley's shoulders, and her thumbs are still pressing into the tight muscles, and that tells Terri Anne everything: Julia is terrified. Not of the school. Not of the questions. Of what will happen to Pandora when the person she has been using as a life raft is not in the building.

- *"What if she panics?"* Julia asks. *"What if she starts crying and she can't stop? What if she wets herself? She's been wetting the bed, Terri. Every night. She doesn't have control of her body right now. What if she's in class and she can't hold it? What if the other girls see? What if she's humiliated on top of everything else?"*

- The questions are not theoretical. They are the questions of someone who has been washing the sheets, holding the shaking body, murmuring *"You don't have to be sorry"* at three in the morning. Julia knows what Pandora's grief looks like from the inside, and she is looking at a school building and seeing a seven-hour stretch in which every one of Pandora's vulnerabilities could be exposed in front of twenty classmates.

### Beat 8: Terri Anne's Bridge — The Plan
- Terri Anne does not dismiss Julia's fears. She does not say, *"She'll be fine."* She does not minimize. She listens, and then she builds a bridge — piece by piece, the way Terri Anne has always built bridges in this family.

> *"What if I take her?"*

- Kiley and Julia look at her.

> *"What if I take her to school on the first day? I walk her in. I walk her to her classroom. I stay until she's settled. And if she needs me — if she panics, if she starts to cry, if she can't stay — the school calls me and I come get her. No questions. No pressure. She tries. And if she can't do it, she comes home, and we try again the next day. But she tries."*

- Terri Anne's plan is not rigid. It is a bridge with handrails. Pandora does not have to cross the whole bridge on the first day. She has to step onto it. Terri Anne will hold her hand while she does.

- *"And I'll talk to Sister Margaret about the attendance,"* Terri Anne continues. *"Brandi's name should be removed from the roll before the first day. The teacher should be briefed. The girls should be told — gently, age-appropriately — that Brandi is not coming back this term and that they should not ask Pandora about it. And the desk—"*

- She pauses. The desk.

> *"I think we leave the desk. For now. Removing it is... it's like saying she's not coming back. And we're not saying that. We're not saying that to Pandora, and we're not saying that to the school. The empty desk is hard. But removing it is harder."*

- Kiley does not agree or disagree. She sits with the image of the empty desk — Brandi's desk, the desk next to Pandora's, the desk where Brandi sat every day drawing horses and whispering to Pandora across the gap — and she feels the weight of both choices. Leave it: the wound is visible, every day, every class. Remove it: the wound is covered, but the covering is a kind of giving up. Neither choice is right. Neither choice is merciful.

### Beat 9: Kiley's Condition — The Uniform
- Kiley is quiet for a long time. Then she speaks, and her voice is not the fierce voice from before and not the cracked voice from the stairs. It is the voice of a mother who has made a decision she does not want to make.

> *"If she goes back, she wears her own clothes. Not the uniform."*

- Terri Anne and Julia look at her.

> *"The uniform is — they wear the same uniform. Identical. Every morning, they put on the same dress, the same tights, the same shoes. They stand next to each other in the same outfit and they look like one person split in two. And if Pandora puts on that uniform and walks into that building without Brandi beside her wearing the same one, she is going to look in the mirror and see half of herself missing. The uniform is not just clothes. It's proof that they're a pair. And I am not sending her to school dressed like half of a pair. She wears her own clothes. She wears something that is just hers."*

- Terri Anne nods slowly. She understands. The uniform is a symbol. It says: *I am one of two. I have a twin. We match.* Sending Pandora to school in the uniform without Brandi is sending her into the building branded by her own absence. Her own clothes — a dress that is not the Sacred Heart plaid, a sweater that no one else is wearing — make her Pandora. Just Pandora. Not one of a pair missing its other half.

- Julia nods too. She has seen what the red dress did to Pandora — the way she carried Brandi's Christmas dress like a relic. Clothes are not neutral in this child's grief. The uniform would be a daily reminder that she and Brandi used to match, and now she is alone in the matching, and the matching is a lie.

### Beat 10: What They Will Tell Pandora
- The final question: what do they say to her? How do they tell a six-year-old that she has to go back to the place where she and her sister were always together, and she has to go alone?

- Terri Anne: *"We tell her the truth. We tell her that school is starting and she is going back, and that it will be hard, and that it's okay if it's hard. We tell her that Brandi's desk will be there, and that she can look at it or not look at it, and either one is okay. We tell her that the other girls might ask questions and she can answer them or not answer them, and either one is okay. We tell her that if she needs to come home, she tells the teacher, and one of us will come get her. No questions. No consequences. She tries, and if she can't, she comes home."*

- Julia: *"I'll tell her the night before. I'll hold her when I tell her. I'll let her cry. I'll let her say she doesn't want to go. And then I'll tell her that I love her and that Brandi loves her and that going back to school doesn't mean leaving Brandi behind. It means being brave the way Brandi would want her to be brave."* Julia says this, and there is no hesitation in her voice, because Julia is not a legal guardian on paper but the school knows her name and the school will call her and the school treats her as one. The school has never questioned it. The school has never needed an explanation. At Sacred Heart, Julia is Pandora's parent. That is simply how it is.

- Kiley does not speak for a moment. Then:

> *"I'll tell her that Brandi went to school every day with her best friend beside her. And that now it's Pandora's turn to be brave for both of them."*

- The words land heavily. *For both of them.* It is a burden. It is also a gift — the gift of purpose in a situation that has none. Pandora has been helpless for six days: helpless to find her sister, helpless to stop the grief, helpless to do anything but cling. Sending her to school gives her something to do. Something that is hers. Something that is not just surviving.

### Beat 11: Closing Image — Downstairs
- The conversation is over. The decision has been made. The room is quiet again, but it is a different quiet — not the held-breath quiet from before, but the quiet that comes after something hard has been decided and there is nothing left to say and the only thing to do is live with it.

- Julia stands from the bed. She looks at the door. She looks at Terri Anne.

> *"She's going to ask where I went."*

- Terri Anne nods. *"She's been looking at the staircase all day."*

- Because the townhouse is open concept. The staircase is visible from everywhere downstairs. Pandora has been sitting on Mike's lap for two hours, staring at the open staircase, waiting for Julia to come down. And Julia has been up here, talking about sending her away for seven hours a day, and the cruelty of that is not lost on any of them — they are planning to send Pandora somewhere Julia cannot follow, and Pandora cannot even endure Julia being one floor away.

- Julia goes to the door. She opens it. She looks down the staircase — and she can see all the way down, the open-riser stairs, the living room below, the couch where Mike is sitting, and the small rigid figure on his lap with her eyes fixed on the stairs, and the moment Pandora sees Julia, her whole body shifts toward the staircase, and her hand reaches out, and Mike holds her, and Julia starts down.

- Terri Anne stays in the room. She sits back in the chair by the window. She looks out at the snow falling on the city. She thinks about the stairs at Sacred Heart — the steps the twins climb every morning, the hand-squeeze, one-two-three, Brandi first, Pandora follows. She thinks about what it will look like when Pandora climbs those steps alone. She thinks about the empty desk and the coatroom hook and the attendance roll and the small, merciless questions of six-year-olds. She thinks about all of it, and then she puts her hands over her face, and she sits there in the chair by the window, and she does not make a sound.

- Downstairs, in the open concept living area, Julia reaches the bottom of the staircase and Pandora is already reaching for her, and Mike lets go, and Pandora's arms go around Julia's neck, and Julia holds her, and she is warm and she is here and she is not at Sacred Heart yet, and Monday is coming, and there is nothing any of them can do to stop it.

### Beat 12: The Phone
- Then Mike's phone rings.

- In the open concept townhouse, sound carries. The ringtone fills the space — the staircase, the living area, the kitchen, all of it. Julia is standing at the bottom of the stairs with Pandora in her arms, and she hears it, and her body goes still, because the phone has been ringing for six days and every ring could be news and every ring could be nothing and you have to answer every ring because the one you don't answer could be the one.

- Mike answers. He is still on the couch, still holding the phone, and Rachel is beside him and Sabrina is at the table and Andrea Jennifer is on the floor and Ashley Erin is on the carpet, and they all hear Mike say, *"Yes,"* and then nothing, and then his face changes.

- His face does something that nobody in this room has seen it do. It goes tight. It goes sharp. His jaw locks and his eyes widen and he stands up from the couch — stands fast, the way you stand when you cannot stay sitting — and he says, *"Send it,"* and then he is looking at his phone screen and whatever he is seeing is making his hand shake.

- Julia sees his hand shake from across the room. Pandora, against Julia's chest, feels Julia's body go rigid.

- Mike looks up. He looks at Julia. He looks at the staircase — at Terri Anne, who has come to the top of the stairs and is standing there, looking down into the open living area, because she heard the phone ring and she heard Mike stand up fast and she heard the way the room went quiet the way a room goes quiet when the air pressure drops.

> *"The police called. They have a video."*

- Julia's arms tighten around Pandora. Terri Anne comes down the stairs.

> *"It's five seconds,"* Mike says.

### Beat 13: The Five Seconds
- They gather in the living area. Rachel takes Pandora from Julia — Pandora's hands scrabble, her fingers reach for Julia's shirt, and Julia says, *"Just for a minute, baby. Just for a minute. I'll be right here,"* and Rachel holds her on the couch, and Pandora's eyes are on Julia, and Julia is standing with Kiley and Terri Anne around Mike's phone.

- The screen is small. Grainy. Black and white. Surveillance footage. A corridor. A door.

- Mike presses play.

- **One:** Brandi Jessica enters the frame from the left. She is walking. She is not running. She is not being dragged. She is walking on her own. A small figure in a dark dress. Her legs moving. Her arms at her sides.

- **Two:** A woman enters the frame behind Brandi. She is walking close — not touching, but near, the way you walk near a child you are escorting. She is tall. Dark hair. Her face is angled away from the camera — deliberately away, turned toward Brandi, as if she knows where the camera is. She moves with the casual authority of someone who belongs in this corridor, someone who has walked it before.

- **Three:** Brandi looks up. Her face turns toward the camera. The footage is grainy and the resolution is low and the angle is bad but it is her face. It is Brandi Jessica's face. The same face as Pandora's. The same face that Kiley has been seeing in her mind's eye every second of every day for six days, except now it is on a screen and it is real and it is moving and she is alive. And behind her, the woman reaches out and puts her hand on Brandi's shoulder. The woman's face stays turned away.

- **Four:** A man enters the frame from the right. He is tall. His face is also hidden — turned away from the camera, angled toward Brandi, the same deliberate angle as the woman. His hand lands on Brandi's other shoulder, and now there are two hands on Brandi Jessica. The woman on one side, the man on the other. They are steering her. Gently, firmly, the way you steer a child who has stopped walking and needs to keep walking. Both of them faceless. Both of them known to each other. Both of them walking Brandi Jessica toward a door that is offscreen like this is normal, like this is routine, like they have done this before.

- And on the man's hand — on the third finger of the hand that is gripping Brandi's shoulder — there is a ring. Dark. Wide. A thick band with a stone that catches the corridor light and flashes once, briefly, before the hand moves and the angle changes. It is a small detail. Five seconds of footage and the ring is visible for less than one. But it is there.

- **Five:** Brandi Jessica walks out of the frame to the right, between the two of them. The woman's hand stays on one shoulder. The man's hand stays on the other. They walk her through a door that is offscreen. Then they are gone. Then the frame is empty. Then the video ends.

- Five seconds. One. Two. Three. Four. Five. Gone.

### Beat 14: After the Video
- The phone screen goes dark. And nobody breathes.

- Then Kiley makes a sound. Not grief. Something else. The sound of a body that has just received proof that the thing it has been terrified is dead is alive, and the proof is five seconds long, and five seconds is not enough.

> *"Play it again."*

- Her voice is cracked. It is barely a voice. Mike presses play. The five seconds play again. Brandi enters the frame. The woman behind her. Brandi's face. The two hands. The door. Gone.

> *"Again."*

- Mike replays it.

> *"Again."*

- He will replay it as many times as she needs. He will replay it until the phone dies and then he will charge it and he will replay it again, because the video is five seconds of Brandi Jessica and five seconds is all they have and Kiley needs to see it.

- Julia watches it each time. She watches Brandi's walk — not running, not dragged, walking on her own two feet. She clings to every detail: her legs work. Nobody has hurt her legs. She looked up. She turned toward the camera. She is alive. She is alive.

- But Julia is also watching the woman. Every time the video plays, Julia watches the woman. The dark hair. The height. The way she walks behind Brandi with the casual authority of someone who belongs. The way her face stays turned away from the camera — deliberately, as if she knows where it is. The woman is someone. The woman is not a stranger. The woman is part of this. And Julia stares at the grainy shape of her and burns with the not-knowing.

- Terri Anne watches both of them — the man and the woman. Two people. Two hands on Brandi Jessica's shoulders. Both faces hidden. Both turned away. Her jaw clenches. Her eyes go hard. She does not say what she is thinking: *They know where the camera is. They are not afraid. They have done this before.* But her hand goes to Kiley's back and presses flat between her shoulder blades, and Kiley does not pull away, and the press says: *I saw them. I will remember everything about them.*

- And then Mike does something. He has been watching Kiley replay the video, watching Julia watch the woman, watching Terri Anne watch both of them. And he looks across the room at Rachel, who is sitting on the couch with Pandora on her lap, and Pandora is not looking at the phone — she is looking at Julia, because she is always looking at Julia, because Julia is where she looks when the world is too much. And Mike thinks: *She should see it. She should see her sister.*

- He walks across the room. He crouches down in front of Pandora. Rachel shifts, tightening her hold, but Mike holds out his hand — not for Pandora, for the phone. He has the phone. And he holds it at Pandora's eye level, and he says, very gently:

> *"Pandora. I want to show you something."*

- Pandora looks at him. She has not spoken since Julia handed her to Mike at noon. Her face is blank in the way a six-year-old's face goes blank when they are holding too much to express any of it. She does not reach for the phone. She does not pull away. She looks at Mike, and Mike looks at her, and then he presses play.

- The five seconds play on the small screen, right in front of Pandora's face.

- One: Brandi enters the frame. Walking. Dark dress. Small figure.

- Pandora's eyes lock onto the screen. Her body goes still. Not rigid-still — the stillness of a body that has stopped breathing.

- Two: The woman enters the frame behind Brandi. Dark hair. Face turned away.
