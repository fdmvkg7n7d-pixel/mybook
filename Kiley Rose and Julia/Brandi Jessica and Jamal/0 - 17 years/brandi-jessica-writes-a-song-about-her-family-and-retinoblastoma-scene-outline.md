# Brandi Jessica Writes a Song About Her Family and Retinoblastoma — Scene Outline

## SCENE INFORMATION

- **Characters:** Brandi Jessica Key (8), Songwriting Teacher, Pandora Paige Key (8), other songwriting students
- **Onstage animal:** None (Burden does not arrive until age 14)
- **Locations:** Music building / songwriting studio — French Woods (Hancock, New York)
- **When:** Session 3, Brandi Jessica and Pandora Paige's first summer at French Woods (July 2053). Several days into the new session, during a Songwriting class period.
- **Status:** New scene outline
- **Tone:** Raw, surprising, artistic — an eight-year-old finding language for something she has never put into words before

## SCENE PURPOSE

This scene captures the moment Brandi Jessica — who has expressed herself through movement (Ballet, Pointe, Yoga) and through counting and mapping — discovers that songwriting gives her a different kind of language. She writes a song about her family, her Retinoblastoma, and what it means to survive something you can no longer see.

It establishes:
- That Brandi added Songwriting as a new class for Session 3 — one of her changed majors — and it becomes the place where she processes what Ballet and Pointe and Yoga hold in the body but cannot say.
- That Brandi's relationship to words is different from her relationship to movement. In Ballet, she counts. In Pointe, she feels. In Yoga, she breathes. In Songwriting, she says.
- That the Charlie Brown musical is part of the Session 3 theater season at French Woods, and Brandi is involved — but her own songwriting is something different. The musical is performance; the songwriting is confession.
- That an eight-year-old can write about cancer with the same precision she brings to the barre — no self-pity, no dramatization, just the truth of what happened.
- That the Songwriting teacher recognizes that Brandi is not writing a clever song for a child. She is writing a real song, and it deserves to be treated like one.

## BEATS

### Beat 1: Brandi Jessica's First Songwriting Class

- It is Session 3. Brandi Jessica has changed some of her majors and minors. Songwriting is new — one of the classes she chose after Visiting Day, after the beam, after deciding she wanted to try different things.
- The songwriting studio is in the music building — a small room with a keyboard, a whiteboard, a circle of chairs, and a rack of guitars and ukuleles. The walls are covered with lyric sheets, chord charts, and hand-drawn musical notation.
- Brandi walks in with her cane. She finds a chair by touching the back of it, the way she finds the barre. She sits down. She cannot see the whiteboard or the lyric sheets on the walls.
- The Songwriting teacher is a young man with curly hair and paint on his jeans — a working songwriter who spends his summers at French Woods because he believes children can write real music.
- He goes around the circle. Each student says their name and why they're here. Most kids say things like "I like music" or "I want to write a pop song" or "My mom made me take this class."
- Brandi: "I'm Brandi Jessica. I'm blind. I want to write a song about my family. And about cancer. Because I had it. And I don't know how to talk about it but maybe I can sing about it."
- The room goes quiet. The other kids look at each other. The teacher looks at Brandi — really looks at her, the way the Gymnastics teacher looked at Pandora when she said she was scared of lots of things.
- Teacher: "Okay. Then that's what we'll do."

### Beat 2: The Assignment

- The teacher gives the class their first assignment: "Write a song about something that changed you. Not something that happened to you — something that changed you. There's a difference. Something that happened to you is a story. Something that changed you is a song."
- He writes the prompt on the whiteboard. Brandi cannot see it. She does not need to. She already knows what changed her.
- Teacher: "You can write alone or with a partner. You can use the keyboard or the guitar or just your voice. You can write lyrics first or melody first. There's no wrong way. The only rule is that it has to be true."
- The other kids start brainstorming — some excited, some nervous, some staring at blank paper. Brandi sits very still. She is not brainstorming. She is listening.
- A girl next to her whispers: "What are you going to write about?"
- Brandi: "I already said. My family. And cancer."
- The girl: "Isn't that sad?"
- Brandi: "It's not sad. It's true. Sad and true aren't the same thing."
- The teacher overhears this. He doesn't say anything. He just nods once.

### Beat 3: Brandi Starts Writing

- Brandi does not use the keyboard. She does not use the guitar. She sits in her chair with a Braille slate and stylus and she starts writing lyrics — pressing the stylus into the paper, dot by dot, the way she reads. Slow. Precise. Each word a decision.
- She does not write about the diagnosis first. She does not write about the hospital first. She writes about before.
- She writes:
  - *Before I lost my eyes I saw my mama's face / I saw the way she looked at me from across the kitchen space / I saw my Julia's hands before they held the bowl / I saw my sister's shadow running with me to the goal*
- Then she stops. She runs her fingers over the Braille dots she just made. She reads them back to herself. Then she presses more dots.
  - *I saw the orange crackers on the counter by the sink / I saw the way my mama smiled before I learned to think / About the things I'd lose and the things I'd keep / Before I learned to count my steps before I learned to sleep*
- She is not crying. She is working. The same precision she brings to the barre — first position, tendu, dégagé — she brings to this. Each word is a step. Each line is a combination.

### Beat 4: The Enucleation and Cancer Verses

- The next part is harder. She has to write about what happened after.
- She presses the stylus into the paper. Slowly.
  - *Two days before my birthday the doctor said a word / I didn't understand it but I heard the way it blurred / My mama's voice went quiet and my Julia held her hand / And my sister sat beside me like she'd always always planned*
- She pauses. She reads it back. Then she writes the enucleation — she writes it first because it happened first:
  - *In February they took my eyes and gave them to the dark / I woke up and the world was gone except for the spark / Of my sister's voice beside me saying I am right right here / And my mama's hand on mine and my Julia's in my ear*
- Then chemotherapy:
  - *Chemo made me sick and I couldn't keep it down / My hair fell out in clumps and I couldn't hear a sound / That wasn't beeping or wasn't counting or wasn't someone saying it'll be okay / And I didn't know if okay was a place I'd ever stay*
- Then radiotherapy:
  - *They put a mask on me and it held my head so still / I couldn't move I couldn't breathe I couldn't even will / My body to stop shaking so my mommies taught me how / To breathe in and breathe out and find the stillness now / On the yoga mat together all four of us in line / The only time the fear would let me close my eyes that time*
- She presses the last line of that verse and her hand shakes slightly. She stops. She breathes — the same breath she learned on the yoga mat during radiotherapy, the same breath she uses when she holds warrior two. In through the nose. Out through the mouth. The breath that got her through the mask.

### Beat 5: The Last Verse

- She writes the last part — the now. She does not write around it. She writes through it.
  - *I can't see the mirror but I feel the barre / I can't see the music but I know the way it are / I can't see the orange crackers but I taste them in my mind / I can't see my family but they're the only things I find / When I reach out in the dark they're always always there / Without any single doubt they're always everywhere*
- The last two lines — *"without any single doubt"* — she writes them and then she presses the stylus down hard on the period. Not a question mark. A period. It is a fact.

### Beat 6: The Melody

- Brandi has lyrics now. But she does not have a melody. She does not play an instrument. She has her voice.
- She sits in the songwriting studio after class — the other kids have gone to their next activities. She asks the teacher if she can stay.
- Teacher: "Do you need help with something?"
- Brandi: "I have words. But I don't have the music. I can hear music in my head but I can't write it down. Can you help me find it?"
- The teacher sits at the keyboard. "Sing me what you hear."
- Brandi sings. Her voice is small and clear and not trained — she is not a singer the way she is a dancer. But she knows what she wants the melody to do. She hums the first line, and the teacher plays it back. She hums the second line, and he adjusts. They work together — her voice, his hands on the keys — until the melody matches the words.
- The verse about before is gentle, almost like a lullaby. The enucleation verse is quiet, almost whispered. The chemo verse shifts — the rhythm gets tighter, more urgent, like the beeping of the hospital monitors she remembers. The radiotherapy and yoga verse slows down, breathes, opens up — the same way Partner Yoga opens up. Then the last lines rise — *"without any single doubt"* — not loud, but certain. The way Brandi is certain.
- When they finish, the teacher plays it through once, all the way. Brandi sits with her hands on the Braille slate and listens to her own song.
- Teacher: "Brandi, how old are you?"
- Brandi: "Eight."
- Teacher: "That song wasn't written by an eight-year-old."
- Brandi: "Yes it was. I'm eight and I wrote it. That's what eight-year-olds can do when they have something to say."
- The teacher is quiet for a moment. Then he nods.
- Teacher: "You're right. I'm sorry. I meant that it's very good. And very true."
- Brandi: "Thank you. Can I come back tomorrow and work on it more?"
- Teacher: "You can come back whenever you want. This room is yours."

### Beat 7: Brandi Tells Pandora

- That evening, after dinner, the twins sit on Brandi's bunk in Birch Cabin. The other campers are writing letters or brushing their teeth. The cabin is noisy and warm.
- Brandi: "Pandora. I wrote a song today."
- Pandora: "In Songwriting class?"
- Brandi: "Yes. It's about us. About our family. About the cancer. About the mask and the chemo and the yoga and everything."
- Pandora is quiet. She does not say "that's sad" or "are you okay." She knows Brandi the way Brandi knows her.
- Pandora: "Can you sing it to me?"
- Brandi: "It's not finished yet."
- Pandora: "I don't care. Sing what you have."
- Brandi sings. In the dark of the cabin, with the sounds of other kids around them, she sings her song. Her voice is small and clear and it carries the same precision as her hands on the barre. Pandora sits very still. She does not interrupt. She does not cry. She listens the way Brandi listens — with her whole body.
- When Brandi finishes, Pandora is quiet for a long time.
- Pandora: "You wrote that about me too. About all four of us."
- Brandi: "It's about all of us. Everything is about all of us."
- Pandora: "Sing the last part again."
- Brandi sings: *"I can't see my family but they're the only things I find / When I reach out in the dark they're always always there / Without any single doubt they're always everywhere."*
- Pandora: "That's us."
- Brandi: "That's us."
- They sit on the bunk the way they sit on the beam — shoulders touching, breathing together. The cabin is noisy around them. They are quiet.

### Beat 8: Pointe Class — The Song in Her Body

- The next day, Brandi goes to Pointe class. She has been doing Pointe since Session 2. She laces her shoes by feel, rises onto the boxes, and does her combinations at the barre.
- But today, something is different. Today, the song she wrote is in her body. The melody runs under her movements the way counting used to. She doesn't count out loud today. She hums — barely audible, just under her breath — the melody of her song.
- The Pointe teacher notices. Brandi's timing is different. Not worse — more musical. More fluid. The song has given her a new way to feel the rhythm of the combinations.
- Teacher: "Brandi, what were you humming?"
- Brandi: "A song I wrote."
- Teacher: "It suits you. Whatever it was, keep it."
- Brandi nods. She rises onto pointe again, and this time she does not count at all. The music is inside her now.

## RELATION TO CONTINUITY

- This scene takes place during **Session 3**, after the twins have changed their majors/minors (July 2053).
- **Songwriting** is one of Brandi's new classes for Session 3. This is consistent with the twins asking to change their majors at the end of Session 2 Visiting Day.
- The **Charlie Brown musical** ("You're a Good Man, Charlie Brown") is part of the Session 3 theater season at French Woods. Brandi may be involved in the production in some capacity, but her songwriting in this scene is separate — her own work, not the musical.
- Brandi writes on a **Braille slate and stylus**, consistent with her being blind and using Braille throughout her camp experience.
- The song lyrics reference established canon: the orange crackers on the kitchen counter (from the beam scene), two days before her birthday (December 23 diagnosis), enucleation in February (eyes removed first), chemo, hair falling out, the radiation mask, Partner Yoga during radiotherapy ("all four of us on the mat"), Pandora's voice beside her, and the family phrase "without any single doubt."
- The song connects backward to established scenes: the kitchen counter memory (beam scene), Partner Yoga during treatments (Yoga scenes), Sloan/Riley confrontation ("I held warrior two" — not in the song but the same strength), and forward to the Pointe class where the melody replaces counting.
- Brandi's shift from **counting** to **humming** in Pointe class marks a development in her relationship to movement. Counting is external structure; melody is internal. She has moved from relying on the map to carrying the music inside her.
- The Songwriting teacher's line — *"That song wasn't written by an eight-year-old"* — and Brandi's response — *"That's what eight-year-olds can do when they have something to say"* — echoes the family philosophy: there are no no's, only hows. Don't tell her what she can't do. She'll show you what she can.
- The song is not finished by the end of this scene. Brandi will continue working on it. It may be performed at a camp showcase, at a future Visiting Day, or it may remain private — a song she wrote for herself and her family.

## NOTES FOR FUTURE SCENES

- **The Charlie Brown musical** — Brandi's involvement needs to be developed. What role does she play? Is she in the ensemble? Does she audition? How does a blind eight-year-old participate in a musical production? This could be its own scene.
- **The song's completion** — Brandi continues working on it. A future scene could show her finishing it, or performing it for the first time.
- **Performing the song** — Could Brandi perform this song at the Session 3 or Session 4 showcase? At Visiting Day for Kiley Rose, Julia, Mike, and Terri Anne? This could be devastating in the best way.
- **Pandora's response to the song** — Pandora heard it. She may want to write her own song, or she may want Brandi to teach it to her so they can sing it together.
- **The shift from counting to melody** — this is a major development. Future Ballet and Pointe scenes should reference this. Brandi no longer counts every step. She hums. The music has replaced the map.
- **Songwriting as ongoing practice** — this could become a major part of Brandi's identity, especially as she grows older. Songwriting is where she processes what dance holds and what speech can't reach.
- **The song's structure** — the lyrics in this scene are first-draft. They can be refined in future drafts. Brandi is eight; the lyrics should sound like an extraordinarily gifted eight-year-old, not an adult songwriter. They should be raw, precise, and unadorned.
