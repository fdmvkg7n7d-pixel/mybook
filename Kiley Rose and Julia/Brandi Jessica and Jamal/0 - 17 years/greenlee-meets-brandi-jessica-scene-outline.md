# Greenlee Meets Brandi Jessica — Scene Outline

## SCENE INFORMATION

- **Characters:** Brandi Jessica Key (six years old), Andrew (the kidnapper), Greenlee (Andrew's cousin)
- **Offstage / referenced characters:** Kiley Rose Key, Julia, Mike Key, Pandora Paige Key, the Poker Game Men
- **Location:** The secluded, high-end luxury hotel room on the outskirts of New York City (outside the city limits)
- **When:** December 30, 2051, evening through midnight into January 1, 2052 (Greenlee arrives the same day Julia is at Key Porn Studios; four days after Brandi Jessica's kidnapping on December 26)
- **Status:** New scene outline
- **Tone:** Ice-cold complicity, devastating contrast, the cruelty of familiarity, a child's instinct, the horror of two people who should be family choosing to be monsters

---

## SCENE PURPOSE

This scene outlines **Greenlee's arrival at the hotel room to meet Brandi Jessica** — the moment when Andrew's cousin comes face to face with the kidnapped six-year-old she has been complicit in holding, and then becomes an active participant in her abuse.

Greenlee has just come from Key Porn Studios, where she sat across from Julia on a daily webcam, laughed at her pain, dropped an Easter egg about hotels on the outskirts of the city, and smiled at Julia's breakdown in the foyer. Now she drives to the hotel, walks into the room, and sees Brandi Jessica — a six-year-old in a short black dress and black nylons, with no shoes and no comfort objects, sitting on a bed where she has been drugged, bathed against her will, and passed between men's laps at a poker game.

**Greenlee is Andrew's cousin by blood.** She is connected to this nightmare through Andrew — she is his family, and she chooses his side. But she does not simply sit in that hotel room and look away. She asks about the poker game and makes Brandi Jessica narrate her own abuse. She asks if Brandi Jessica is *"ready for her next step"* and when Andrew says *"Almost,"* she takes the child into the bathroom for thirty minutes and emerges declaring *"She is ready."* She stays to celebrate the new year with Brandi Jessica — *especially* with Brandi Jessica — sitting on the bed next to her while Andrew withholds the ketamine so the girl remains fully conscious. They tell Brandi Jessica they are taking her to the United Arab Emirates to meet *"a man who will love you so much"* — the word *love* weaponized the same way *fun* and *adventure* are weaponized, meaning the opposite of itself in Greenlee's mouth. The scene is about the specific horror of someone who was welcomed into this family's home not merely seeing a child in crisis and choosing complicity, but escalating the crisis herself — and about Brandi Jessica, who has been taught by Kiley Rose and Julia that family is safety, encountering someone from the Christmas party who is the opposite of safe and the opposite of passive.

## SCENE ARC / BEATS

### Beat 1: Greenlee Arrives
- It is evening, December 30. The hotel corridor is quiet — high-end, discreet, the kind of place where no one asks questions. Greenlee walks down the hallway, her heels clicking softly on the carpet, her bag over her shoulder. She has driven here from Key Porn Studios, still wearing the same clothes, still carrying the same smile that spread across her face when she walked past Julia sobbing in Terri Anne's arms.
- She knocks on the door. Andrew opens it. They look at each other — cousins, blood, the same family tree — and Andrew steps aside without a word. There is no surprise. He was expecting her.
- Greenlee walks into the hotel room and takes it in: the suite, the desk with the laptop and offshore routing screens, the polished cart from room service, the empty juice glass on the nightstand. The room smells like coffee and something sweet — the remnants of the pancakes. It looks almost normal. Almost.
- Then she sees **Brandi Jessica.**

### Beat 2: First Sight — Brandi Jessica on the Bed
- Brandi Jessica is sitting on the bed, her knees pulled up to her chest, her bare feet tucked under the hem of the short black dress. She is holding the edge of a pillow, gripping it with both hands, her knuckles white. Her eyes are red-rimmed and hollow. The nylons on her legs catch the lamplight. She has no shoes. She has no toys. She has nothing from home.
- Greenlee stands in the doorway and looks at her. **She looks exactly like Pandora Paige.** The identical twin — the same face, the same small frame, the same dark hair. Greenlee has seen Pandora Paige at family gatherings, at Christmas, at the Key mansion. She knows this face. And seeing it here, in a hotel room, in a black dress, with hollow eyes — the dissonance is staggering.
- But Greenlee does not stagger. She adjusts her bag, walks further into the room, and sits down in the chair by the window. She does not rush to the child. She does not ask if she is okay. She does not kneel down and say, *"I'm going to get you out of here."* She sits down, crosses her legs, and looks at Andrew.
- Andrew closes the door.

### Beat 3: The Cousins Talk — Brandi Jessica Listens
- Andrew and Greenlee talk. Not about Brandi Jessica — not directly. They talk about the plan. About the offshore accounts. About how much longer Andrew needs before the transfers are complete. About whether the police have gotten closer. Greenlee updates him on what she heard at the studio — Julia's breakdown, the family's desperation, the fact that no one has any leads.
- *"They're falling apart,"* Greenlee says, and there is a note of satisfaction in her voice that she does not bother to hide. *"The younger one — the twin — she won't let go of anyone. She's clinging to everyone. And Julia fell apart in the foyer today. Sobbing. It was—"* She pauses, and the corner of her mouth twitches. *"It was something."*
- Andrew nods, absorbed in his laptop. He does not react to the cruelty. He does not enjoy it the way Greenlee does. It is simply data to him — the family's pain is a metric, not a pleasure. He asks about Mike's movements. Greenlee tells him what she knows.
- Then, without looking up from the screen, Andrew says — the way you describe a product that exceeded expectations:

> *"She's been a very perfect bitch."*

- He says it about Brandi Jessica. About a six-year-old. *Perfect* — because she did what she was told. Because she served the drinks. Because she sat on the laps. Because she drank the juice without fighting. Because she stopped crying. Because she learned. *Bitch* — because that is what she is to him. Not a child. Not a person. Not Brandi Jessica Key. A bitch. A thing that performed exactly as required.

- Greenlee does not correct him. She does not flinch. She does not say, *"She's six, Andrew."* She does not say, *"That's a child."* She takes a sip of the drink the room service brought and says nothing, because the language does not shock her. It is how people in their world talk about people who are not in their world.

- **Brandi Jessica hears it.** She does not know what the word *bitch* means — not really. She has heard it on television, once, when someone was angry. She knows it is a bad word. She knows you are not supposed to say it. But she hears the way Andrew says it — flat, casual, like he is describing the weather — and she understands, even without the vocabulary, that he is talking about her, and that whatever the word means, it means she is not a person to him anymore.

- She is six years old, and a man has just called her a bitch, and the woman in the room did not react, and now they are talking about something else. The word lands on her like a hand — not hitting, but pressing. A weight. A label. Something she will carry without understanding its shape for years.

- **Brandi Jessica sits on the bed and listens.** She does not understand most of it — offshore accounts, transfers, police leads. But she understands the tone. She understands that these two people are talking about her family like it is a game. She understands that the woman who just walked in knows her mommies, knows her grandfather, knows Pandora — and is sitting here, in this room, talking to the man who took her, like they are colleagues.
- She understands, with the terrible clarity of a child who has learned too much in four days, that this woman is not going to help her.

### Beat 4: Greenlee Looks at Brandi Jessica
- After the business conversation, Greenlee turns her attention to Brandi Jessica. She looks at her — really looks at her — for the first time.
- She sees the black dress. The nylons. The bare feet. The red-rimmed eyes. The grip on the pillow. The small, rigid body that has been drugged and bathed and dressed and passed between laps. She sees all of it.
- **And Greenlee feels nothing.** Not a flicker. Not a tremor. Not a moment of *"What am I doing?"* She looks at this six-year-old — the same way she looked at Julia sobbing in the foyer: with the cold, detached interest of someone watching something burn.
- She says, almost conversationally, to Andrew: *"She looks just like Pandora."*
- Andrew does not look up from his laptop. *"She is just like Pandora."*
- Brandi Jessica hears her name — Pandora's name — and her grip on the pillow tightens. Her twin. Her other half. The person she has been whispering about in the dark. And these two people are saying her sister's name like it is an observation, like Pandora is a fact to be noted, not a person to be loved.

### Beat 5: Brandi Jessica Speaks to Greenlee
- Brandi Jessica looks at Greenlee. She is six years old, and she has been drugged and silenced and made invisible for four days. But something in her — the same fierce spark that made her whisper *"They're going to find us"* into the empty room — makes her speak.
- She says, her voice small but steady: *"You were at the party."*
- Greenlee turns and looks at her. For a moment, the room is absolutely still. Brandi Jessica has done what she did with Andrew — named the person in front of her, placed them in the world she knows. *You were at the party. You were in my house. You were with my family.*
- Greenlee does not laugh. Andrew already laughed. Greenlee does something worse. **She smiles.** A slow, private smile — the same smile she gave Julia's breakdown in the foyer. Not a laugh, not cruelty for show, but the quiet, devastating smile of someone who holds all the cards and finds it amusing that a six-year-old is trying to play.
- *"Yes, I was at the party,"* Greenlee says. Her voice is light, almost friendly. *"I was at the party, and now I'm here. Funny how that works."*
- Brandi Jessica stares at her. She does not understand why this woman is smiling. She does not understand why someone who was in her house, who ate her family's food, who stood in the same rooms as her mommies and her grandfather, is sitting in the hotel room where she is being held and smiling at her.
- But she understands this: **the smile is the answer.** The smile says, *I know where you are. I know who has you. I am not going to help you. And there is nothing you can do about it.*
- Brandi Jessica's hands tighten on the pillow. She does not speak again. She has learned, in four days, that speaking to people who smile like that is useless. She saves her words for the dark, when she whispers to the empty room: *"They're going to find us. Mom and Julia are coming."*

### Beat 6: The Poker Game Question
- Greenlee is still looking at Brandi Jessica. The smile from *"I was at the party"* has not fully left her face — it lingers, a shadow at the corner of her mouth. She crosses her legs, leans back in the chair, and asks — casually, like someone asking about a playdate:

> *"So. I hear you had visitors last night. A poker game. Did you have fun?"*

- She says it the way you ask a child about a birthday party. *Did you have fun?* The question is obscene. Andrew glances up from his laptop — a flicker, brief, almost imperceptible — and then looks back down. He does not stop her. He does not redirect. Greenlee is free to ask whatever she wants.

- Brandi Jessica looks at her. She does not want to talk about the poker game. She has not talked about it — not to Andrew, not to herself, not in the dark. The memory sits in her body, not her words: the laps, the hands adjusting her like a cushion, the heat of strangers through suit pants, the tray in her hands, the moving from one to the next.

- But Greenlee asked. And something about the way Greenlee asked — the lightness, the fake warmth, the echo of how adults at the Christmas party used to ask her things — makes Brandi Jessica answer. Not because she trusts Greenlee. Because Greenlee asked the way someone asks when they actually want to know, and no one has asked Brandi Jessica anything in four days.

- She says, quietly: *"I sat on their laps."*

- Greenlee tilts her head. A small, encouraging nod. The way you coax a story out of a child. *"Mm-hmm. And what was that like?"*

- Brandi Jessica's brow furrows. She is trying to find the word for something she does not have language for. She is six. She does not know the word *arousal.* She does not know the word *erection.* She does not know what happened to those men's bodies while she sat on them. She only knows that something was there — something she felt through the nylons, through their suit pants — something that was not right, something that made her stomach turn, something she did not have a name for.

- She says: *"I felt something funny in their laps."*

- The word *funny* hangs in the room. She does not mean *ha-ha.* She means *wrong.* She means *strange.* She means *something that shouldn't be there.* A six-year-old's word for a grown man's response to a child sitting on his lap. *Funny.* The most devastating word in the English language when spoken by a girl who does not understand what she is describing.

- Greenlee hears it. She hears exactly what it means. She is a grown woman. She works in adult entertainment. She knows what *"something funny"* means when a man holds a six-year-old on his lap. She knows what those men felt. She knows what their bodies did.

- **And Greenlee does not flinch.** She does not look away. She does not say, *"What do you mean, funny?"* She does not ask a follow-up question, because she already understands. She understood before she asked. The question was not curiosity — it was sport. She wanted to hear Brandi Jessica say it. She wanted to make a six-year-old describe her own abuse using the inadequate words of childhood, and she wanted to watch.

- Greenlee nods slowly, her expression unreadable. *"Mm,"* she says. Just that. A small, satisfied sound — the sound of someone who got what they came for.

- Andrew does not react. His fingers do not pause on the keyboard. He heard it. He knows what it means. He is the one who put her on those laps. And he does not care.

- Brandi Jessica watches Greenlee's face, looking for something — horror, surprise, sympathy, anything. She finds nothing. Greenlee's face is smooth, pleasant, closed. The smile is gone. What replaced it is worse: nothing. The absence of any reaction at all. A six-year-old told a woman that something *funny* happened to her on strangers' laps, and the woman said *"Mm"* and moved on.

- Brandi Jessica pulls her knees tighter to her chest. She does not say anything else about the poker game. She has learned another lesson in this room: the things that happened to her are not things that upset the people here. They are not shocking. They are not unusual. They are just information — data, like the offshore accounts, like the police leads. A child felt something funny in a man's lap, and it was not even worth a full sentence.

### Beat 7: The Bathroom
- Greenlee asks the question the way you ask about a delivery date, a milestone, a next phase in a project that is running on schedule:

> *"Is she ready for her next step?"*

- **Her next step.** Not *the* next step. *Her* next step. As if Brandi Jessica is on a track. As if there is a progression, a sequence, a ladder of things that will be done to her — and the poker game, the laps, the *"something funny"* was only the current rung, and Greenlee wants to know when they move to the next one.

- Andrew looks up from his laptop for the first time since the poker game question. He looks at Brandi Jessica on the bed — her knees pulled up, her hands gripping the pillow, her hollow eyes watching them — and he considers the question the way he considers everything: as a calculation.

> *"Almost."*

- One word. *Almost.* She is almost ready for whatever comes next. The poker game was not the end. The laps were not the end. The black dress and the nylons and the ketamine juice were not the end. There is a next step, and Brandi Jessica is approaching it, and two people are sitting in this room discussing her readiness for it the way you discuss whether a candidate is prepared for a promotion.

- Greenlee nods slowly. She looks at Brandi Jessica. Then she stands up from the chair, sets her drink on the side table, and walks to the bed. She extends her hand — not roughly, not kindly, just functionally, the way you take a child by the hand to cross a street.

> *"Come with me."*

- Brandi Jessica looks at the hand. She looks at Andrew. Andrew has already gone back to his laptop. He does not look up. He does not intervene. There is no one in this room who will stop this.

- She takes Greenlee's hand.

- **Greenlee walks Brandi Jessica into the bathroom and closes the door.**

- The click of the latch is the loudest sound in the hotel room. Andrew does not look up. His fingers continue on the keyboard. The bathroom door is closed, and behind it is a six-year-old girl and a grown woman who drove here from a studio where she laughed at that child's family's grief, and now she has taken that child into a room with a lock on the door.

- **Thirty minutes pass.**

- Andrew works. The heating hums. The city moves outside the window. In the bathroom, there is no sound — or if there is, it does not reach the main room. No screaming. No crying loud enough to hear. Just thirty minutes of silence behind a closed door, which is worse than any sound could be, because silence means Brandi Jessica learned not to make noise, or Greenlee taught her not to, or what is happening does not require noise.

- The door opens.

- **Greenlee walks out first.** Her expression is the same as when she walked in — composed, unchanged, her blouse still tucked, her hair still in place. She has done something in that bathroom and it has not touched her at all. She is not flushed. She is not agitated. She is not affected. She crosses the room, sits back down in the chair by the window, picks up her drink, and takes a sip, as if she has just returned from washing her hands.

- **Then Brandi Jessica comes out.**

- She moves slowly. Her feet are bare on the carpet. The black dress is still on but it is slightly askew — one strap has slipped off her shoulder and she does not fix it. Her hands hang at her sides. She is not gripping the pillow anymore. She is not gripping anything. Her fingers are open and loose, the way hands get when the body has given up clenching.

- **Her eyes are full of tears.** Not spilling. Not streaming. Full. A six-year-old's eyes holding more water than they should be able to hold, the tears sitting in the rims, not falling. This is not the sobbing that will come later when she hears about the trip and the man — this is the stunned, soundless weeping of a child who has just been through something and has not yet found her voice. The tears are there. They are visible. But they do not fall. She stands in the doorway of the bathroom, barefoot, in the crooked dress, with her eyes full, and she does not make a sound.

- She does not look at Andrew. She does not look at Greenlee. She walks to the bed, climbs onto it, pulls her knees to her chest, and sits. She does not reach for the pillow. She does not reach for anything.

- Greenlee sets her drink down and looks at Andrew. She says it the way you confirm a shipment, a completed inspection, a product that has passed quality control:

> *"She is ready."*

- **She is ready.** Not *almost.* Ready. Whatever *almost* meant before Greenlee took her into that bathroom, Greenlee has corrected it. She has taken a six-year-old who was *almost* ready and she has made her ready. Thirty minutes. A closed door. And now Brandi Jessica sits on the bed with her eyes full of tears that will not fall, and Greenlee sips her drink and confirms the milestone, and Andrew nods and goes back to his laptop, and the next step — whatever it is, whatever was planned for her — is now greenlit.

- Brandi Jessica does not speak. She does not cry out. She sits on the bed and stares at nothing and the tears sit in her eyes like water in a glass that has been filled to the brim but not tipped, and the clock she could not see but could hear ticking — *almost, almost, almost* — has stopped ticking. Because *almost* is over. She is ready. And whatever comes next will come.

### Beat 8: The Little Trip
- Greenlee is still sitting in the chair by the window. Andrew has closed his laptop for the first time since she arrived. The business is done. The milestone has been confirmed. Now they tell Brandi Jessica.

- Andrew says it the way you tell a child about a weekend getaway:

> *"We're going on a little trip, Brandi."*

- A little trip. The words are small and round and almost cheerful — the kind of thing a parent says on a Friday night. *We're going on a little trip.* Brandi Jessica has heard those words before. Kiley says them when they drive to the beach. Julia says them when they visit Mike's house. *A little trip* means packing a bag, means sitting in the back seat, means arriving somewhere that is not home but is still safe because the people driving are the people who love you.

- These are not those people.

- Greenlee leans forward in her chair, her elbows on her knees, and adds — softly, conspiratorially, the way you share exciting news:

> *"Aren't you excited? You and us. A little adventure."*

- *You and us.* Brandi Jessica is included. She is part of the trip. She is not being left behind in this hotel room — she is being taken somewhere else. Somewhere new. Somewhere further from the townhouse, further from Kiley, further from Julia, further from Pandora, further from anyone who is looking for her.

- Then Andrew says where. He says it the way you name a destination on a boarding pass — flat, factual, as if it is no more remarkable than Newark:

> *"The United Arab Emirates."*

- Brandi Jessica does not know what the United Arab Emirates is. She has never heard the words. They are long and foreign and they do not sound like any place she has ever been. She knows New York. She knows the townhouse. She knows Mike's house and the beach and the hospital where the doctor said the word about her eyes. She does not know the United Arab Emirates. The name itself tells her nothing except this: *it is far away.*

- Then Greenlee adds something. She says it the way a mother tells a child about a new babysitter — reassuring, bright, the voice that is supposed to make you feel safe:

> *"And you're going to meet a man there, Brandi. A man who will love you so much."*

- *Love you so much.* The words hang in the room like smoke. Brandi Jessica knows what love means. Love is Kiley holding her when she has a nightmare. Love is Julia braiding her hair before school. Love is Mike lifting her onto his shoulders so she can see over the crowd. Love is Pandora's hand reaching for hers under the table. Love is safe. Love is warm. Love is the thing that keeps you.

- But Greenlee said it. And Greenlee said *"fun"* about the poker game. And Greenlee said *"adventure"* about being taken to a country Brandi Jessica has never heard of. And now Greenlee is saying *"love"* about a man in the United Arab Emirates, and the word does not mean what it means when Kiley says it, and it does not mean what it means when Julia says it, because the mouth that is saying it belongs to a woman who closed a bathroom door behind her thirty minutes ago.

- **Another man.** Not Andrew. Not the poker players. Someone new. Someone waiting. Someone who has been arranged, who has been prepared for, who is the reason for the trip, the reason for the bathroom, the reason Brandi Jessica had to be made *ready.* The next step has a name and a location and a face she has never seen, and whatever that man will do to her is what *"love"* means in Greenlee's mouth.

- Andrew does not elaborate. He does not say the man's name or describe him or explain what he wants. The man is a fact — a destination inside the destination. You go to the UAE. You meet the man. The man loves you. The sentence is that simple, and that simple is that terrifying, because Brandi Jessica understands — even at six, even without the words — that this man's *love* is the same kind of *fun* as the poker game, the same kind of *adventure* as the trip, the same kind of *ready* as the bathroom. It is a word that means the opposite of itself.

- She thinks of the men at the poker game. She felt something *funny* in their laps. She does not want to meet another man. She does not want to be loved by another man. She wants to go home. She wants Kiley. She wants Julia. She wants Pandora. She wants the word *love* to go back to meaning what it meant before this room, before this dress, before this woman and this man and this hotel and this country she cannot pronounce.

- But she does not say any of this. She has learned that wanting things in this room does not make them happen. She pulls her knees tighter and watches Andrew pack, and the word *love* sits inside her like a stone — heavy, foreign, and wrong.

- And then she breaks.

- Not slowly. Not gradually. All at once, the way a dam doesn't crack — it bursts. Everything she has been holding since the poker game, since the bathroom, since *"she is ready,"* since the UAE, since the man who will *love* her so much — it all comes out of her at once, and there is nothing controlled about it, nothing quiet, nothing six-year-old-dignified. She is a child and she is breaking and she cannot stop.

- **Brandi Jessica starts to sob.** Not the silent, held-in tears from the bathroom. Not the two thin lines that will come later on New Year's Eve. This is a full-body, chest-heaving, gasping-for-air sob — the kind that comes from the diaphragm, the kind that makes a child's whole body shake, the kind that cannot be stifled or swallowed or willed away. She opens her mouth and the sound that comes out is not a word. It is not a scream. It is the raw, unprocessed grief and terror of a six-year-old who has been drugged and dressed and passed between laps and taken into a bathroom and told she is *ready* and told she is going to a country she cannot pronounce to meet a man who will *love* her, and she cannot hold any of it anymore.

- She cries the way only a child can cry — with her whole face, with her whole chest, with her whole body. Her nose runs. Her eyes swell. Her hands come up to her face and she presses her palms against her eyes and the sobs come in waves, each one deeper than the last, and she cannot catch her breath between them. She is making sounds that are not words — *"uh-uh-uh-uh"* — the rhythm of a body that is trying to breathe but keeps getting hit by the next wave before the last one is done.

- She cries for Pandora. She cries for the empty side of the bed. She cries for Kiley, who is somewhere looking for her and cannot find her. She cries for Julia, who held her and told her *"I'm right here"* and is not here. She cries for the red dress. She cries for the applesauce she ate without Brandi making the face. She cries for the word *bitch* that Andrew said about her. She cries for the *something funny* she felt in the men's laps. She cries for the bathroom door closing. She cries for the thirty minutes of silence. She cries for the man in the UAE she has never met and already fears. She cries because she is six years old and she is in a hotel room in a black dress with no shoes and no pillow and no one is coming and she is about to be taken further away from home than she has ever been and there is nothing she can do.

- She is not quiet about it. She is not brave about it. She is not composed. She is a child who has run out of the strength that was never meant to hold this much, and she is breaking in full view of the two people who have broken her, and she does not care if they see, because she is six and she is overwhelmed and her body is doing what bodies do when the world is too much.

- **Andrew does not move.** He stands at the desk with his laptop case in his hand, and he watches her cry the way you watch a child have a tantrum in a grocery store — mildly inconvenient, something to wait out. His expression does not change. He does not kneel down. He does not say, *"It's okay."* He waits for it to pass the way you wait for rain to stop.

- **Greenlee does not move either.** She sits in the chair by the window, her legs crossed, her champagne in hand, and she watches Brandi Jessica sob with the same expression she wore when the girl emerged from the bathroom — smooth, pleasant, closed. She does not flinch. She does not soften. She does not look away. She watches the way you watch something that does not affect you, because it does not affect her. A six-year-old is sobbing three feet away and Greenlee feels nothing, and the nothing is the worst thing in the room, worse than the sobs, worse than the word *bitch*, worse than the bathroom, because it means that everything Brandi Jessica is feeling right now — every ounce of terror and grief and desperation — is landing on a surface that absorbs nothing. It is performing for no one. The cruelty is not even enjoying it. The cruelty is indifferent.

- The sobs go on for minutes. Brandi Jessica cannot stop them. She tries — she presses her hands harder against her eyes, she bites her lip, she holds her breath — but the body overrides the will. She is six. Her body does not know how to swallow this. She does not know the techniques that adults learn for containing grief, the jaw-clenching, the breathing-through-the-nose, the counting-to-ten. She only knows that she is falling apart and she cannot catch herself and no one in this room is going to catch her either.

- Eventually — minutes later, after the sobs have become hiccups and the hiccups have become shudders and the shudders have become the stillness that comes after a body has spent everything — she stops. Not because she is calm. Because she is empty. She sits on the bed with her knees pulled up, her face swollen and streaked, her hands lying open in her lap, and she stares at nothing. She has cried herself out. There is nothing left. The tears are gone. The sobs are gone. What remains is the silence of a child who has been emptied by her own grief and is now sitting in the shell of herself, waiting for whatever comes next because she does not have the strength to resist it anymore.

- Greenlee takes a sip of her champagne. Andrew zips his laptop case closed.

- The room is quiet again. The sobbing is over. Brandi Jessica sits on the bed, hollowed out, watching Andrew pack, and the word *love* still sits inside her like a stone, but now the stone is at the bottom of an empty well.

- But she sees Andrew's face when he says the UAE. She sees the way his eyes move to the window, to the dark, to the city that will soon be behind them. She sees Greenlee nod slowly, the way you nod when the itinerary is confirmed, and something in the adult certainty of that nod — the fact that neither of them needs to explain, neither of them hesitates, neither of them treats the destination or the man as anything unusual — tells Brandi Jessica that this is not a drive. This is not a car ride. This is not the beach or Mike's house or anywhere the city limits sign says welcome to.

- She does not know the word *international.* She does not know the word *jurisdiction.* She does not know that the UAE has no extradition treaty that would compel Andrew to return her, that the distance is not measured in miles but in legal systems, in diplomatic cables, in the willingness of a foreign government to hand a child back to a family they have never heard of. She does not know that once she is on that plane, the New York City Police Department becomes powerless, the FBI's jurisdiction ends, Mike Key's money and connections hit a wall of sovereign borders and different laws, and the search that is already failing will fail at the speed of a twelve-hour flight.

This sobbing makes the later, quieter tears on New Year's Eve even more devastating: they are what comes after the body has already been emptied, the grief that outlasts the capacity to express it.
