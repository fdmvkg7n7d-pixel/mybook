# Brandi Jessica Tells Her Ballet and Yoga Teachers About Andrew — Scene Outline

## SCENE INFORMATION

- **Characters:** Brandi Jessica Key (8), Ballet Teacher, Yoga Teacher
- **Onstage animal:** None (Burden does not arrive until age 14)
- **Locations:** Dance pavilion (after Ballet), Yoga studio (after Yoga) — French Woods (Hancock, New York)
- **When:** Session 3, Brandi Jessica and Pandora Paige's first summer at French Woods (July 2053). Seven days into the session, a day when Brandi stays after both Ballet and Yoga to talk to her teachers.
- **Status:** New scene outline
- **Tone:** Quiet, trusting, hard — the thing she doesn't tell campers, she tells the two women who held the space when Sloan and Rilee tried to take it

## SCENE PURPOSE

This scene captures the moment Brandi Jessica — who did not tell Sloan or Rilee or any camper about Andrew, who kept the kidnapping private at camp — chooses to tell her Ballet teacher and her Yoga teacher what happened to her. Not because they don't already know. They do. Kiley Rose and Julia briefed the camp before the summer. The teachers know the facts. What they don't know is what it sounds like when Brandi says it out loud.

It establishes:
- That the teachers already know everything — the diagnosis, the kidnapping, the beating, the chemo, the radiotherapy, the enucleation. They were briefed before the summer. Brandi is not giving them information. She is giving them something harder: the sound of her own voice saying the words.
- That there is a difference between knowing something on paper and hearing it from the person who lived it. The teachers knew the story. Now they know the girl.
- That Brandi tells the teachers not for sympathy, but because they backed her without ever letting on that they knew. They treated her like every other camper. They held the space for her without holding her back. She wants them to know — from her — why she counts every step, why she holds warrior two like her life depends on it, why she will not drop a pose when someone tells her she doesn't belong.
- That telling the teachers is Brandi's choice. No one suggested it. No one asked her to. She decides, on her own, that the women who held the barre and held the mat for her should hear it from her.

## BEATS

### Beat 1: After Ballet — Brandi Stays

- Ballet class ends. The other campers file out of the dance pavilion — their shoes squeaking on the sprung floor, their voices echoing as they head to their next activities.
- Brandi stays. She is at the barre, her hand on the tactile strip, her ballet shoes still on. She is not practicing. She is standing still, which is unusual for Brandi. Brandi is always moving — to the next combination, to the next count, to the next step. Stillness is not her default.
- The Ballet teacher is at the far end of the pavilion, stacking the practice CDs, adjusting the barre heights. She notices Brandi hasn't left.
- Teacher: "Brandi? Everything okay?"
- Brandi: "Can I talk to you?"
- The Ballet teacher walks over. She does not rush. She has learned that when Brandi asks for something, it matters.
- Brandi slides down to the floor. She sits cross-legged on the sprung floor next to the barre, her ballet shoes stretched out in front of her, her hand still resting on the tactile strip. She is eight years old and she is sitting on a dance floor and she is about to say something she has never said out loud to anyone at this camp.
- The Ballet teacher sits down on the floor across from her. She doesn't sit on a chair. She sits on the floor, so they are at the same level. She does this without thinking about it.
- Teacher: "I'm listening."
- Brandi: "You already know. About me. About what happened. My moms told the camp before the summer."
- Teacher: "I know."
- Brandi: "I want to say it anyway. Not because you need to hear it. Because I need to say it. To you."
- The teacher nods. She does not say "you don't have to" or "only if you want to." She just nods. She understands that this is not a choice between saying it and not saying it. This is a choice between saying it now and never saying it at all.

### Beat 2: Brandi Tells the Ballet Teacher About Andrew

- Brandi keeps one hand on the tactile strip. It grounds her the way the barre always grounds her. She is about to go somewhere the barre can't take her.
- Brandi: "On December 23, two days before my seventh birthday, they found the cancer. Retinoblastoma. On December 26, the day after my birthday, a man named Andrew took me from my house. He kidnapped me."
- She says it plainly. No drama. No pause for effect. The way she says everything — precisely, directly, without wasted movement.
- Brandi: "He beat me. And he —"
- She stops. Her hand tightens on the barre. Her jaw sets. The word she needs is there but it is heavy, heavier than any word she has ever said out loud. She has said "cancer." She has said "enucleation." She has said "blind." This word is different. This word carries something the others don't.
- Brandi: "He touched me. He —"
- She can't finish the sentence. Not because she doesn't know the word. Because the word is too big for an eight-year-old mouth. Because saying it out loud makes it real in a way that even living through it didn't.
- Brandi: "He sexually assaulted me."
- She says the whole phrase. Clinical. Precise. The way a doctor would say it. The way a police report would say it. Not because she feels nothing. Because she has learned that the clinical words are the ones that get her through the saying.
- The two words — "beat me" and "sexually assaulted me" — land in the empty pavilion. They are small words for big things. The teacher does not flinch. She does not gasp. She sits very still on the floor and lets the words exist.
- Brandi: "He held me in a room. He beat me. He sexually assaulted me. I was seven. I had just found out I had cancer three days before. I had just turned seven the day before. And he took me and he hurt me and he —"
- Her voice catches. She stops. She breathes. The same breath she uses at the barre. In through the nose. Out through the mouth.
- Brandi: "My moms fought to get me back. My mama — Kiley Rose — she sounded like steel on the phone. My Julia — she held me so tight when I came home that I could feel her heartbeat through her arms. And Pandora — my sister was at home waiting for me and she never forgave herself for not being there when it happened."
- A beat.
- Brandi: "After that, they removed both of my eyes. Bilateral enucleation. February. Then chemotherapy. Then radiotherapy. So first they told me I had cancer, then Andrew took me and beat me and sexually assaulted me, then they took my eyes, then I was sick from the chemo, then I was sick from the radiation. All in the same year. All before I turned eight."
- She sits on the floor of the dance pavilion. Her pointe shoes are stretched out in front of her. Her hand is on the barre. She has just said the thing she has never said out loud at this camp.
- Brandi: "So when Sloan said I shouldn't be in Ballet because I can't see the mirror — she doesn't know that I learned to count steps because I was in a dark room and counting was the only thing I could do. She doesn't know that I hold the barre the way I hold it because I learned that some things will hold you up if you let them. She doesn't know that I do the combinations perfectly because when you can't control anything else, you control the thing in front of you. She doesn't know any of that. And I didn't tell her, because she didn't ask right. But you — you backed me without letting on that you knew. You treated me like every other kid in this class. And I wanted you to hear it from me. Because you earned that."
- The Ballet teacher is quiet for a long time. The pavilion is empty. The light comes through the high windows. The sprung floor is silent beneath them.
- Teacher: "Brandi."
- Brandi: "Yeah?"
- Teacher: "I knew the story. I've known it since before you arrived. But I want you to know something. Hearing you say it — that's different. That's not a file I read. That's you. And I want you to know that this barre is yours. This studio is yours. This class is yours. Not because of what happened to you. Because of who you are in spite of it. And who you are is a girl who counts her steps and trusts the barre and does every combination like it's the most important thing in the world. That's not surviving, Brandi. That's dancing."
- Brandi nods. She doesn't cry. She already cried today — for the cast list, for the director, for the thank-yous. This is different. This is the thing that comes after crying. The quiet.
- Brandi: "I come here and I feel safe. That's why I count the steps to the door. Forty-two, eighteen, seven. Not because I'm afraid of getting lost. Because I want to make sure I never lose the way back."
- Teacher: "You won't. I'll make sure of it."
- Brandi stands up. She finds the barre by touch. She takes off her pointe shoes and puts on her sneakers. She picks up her cane.
- Brandi: "Thank you. For letting me say it."
- Teacher: "Anytime, Brandi. This studio is yours."
- Brandi walks out of the pavilion. She counts her steps.

### Beat 3: After Yoga — Brandi Stays Again

- Later that day, after Yoga class. The other campers roll up their mats and leave. The studio still smells of cedar. The afternoon light is warm on the floor.
- Brandi stays. She is sitting on her mat, cross-legged, her hands on her knees. She is in the same position she holds during meditation — still, centered, breathing.
- The Yoga teacher is putting away the bolsters and blankets. She notices Brandi hasn't moved.
- Teacher: "Brandi? Class is over."
- Brandi: "I know. Can I talk to you?"
- The Yoga teacher sits down on the mat across from Brandi. She does this the way she does everything — quietly, fully present, no hesitation.
- Brandi: "You already know. Like the Ballet teacher. My moms told the camp everything."
- Teacher: "I know."
- Brandi: "I want to say it to you too. I just said it to her and I need to say it to you. Because you held the space for me when Rilee tried to take it and I never told you why I needed the space so badly."
- Teacher: "I'm here."
- Brandi: "On December 23, they found the cancer. Two days before my seventh birthday. On December 26, the day after my birthday, a man named Andrew kidnapped me. He held me in a room. He beat me. He sexually assaulted me. I was seven years old. I had just found out I had cancer. I had just turned seven. And he took me and he hurt me in every way a person can hurt another person."
- She says it the same way she said it to the Ballet teacher — plainly, directly, without wasted movement. The words are the same. The voice is the same. But the mat is not the barre, and this teacher is not that teacher, and the space between them is different.
- Brandi: "When they found me and brought me home, I couldn't stop shaking. What he did to me — the beating, the touching — my body remembered what my mind wanted to forget. My moms couldn't get me to be still. Nothing worked. And then they tried Yoga. Partner Yoga. All four of us on the mat together — me and Pandora and Mommy and Julia. And when all four of us were breathing together, I stopped shaking. For the first time since Andrew took me, I was still."
- The Yoga teacher's eyes are bright. She does not look away.
- Brandi: "Then they removed my eyes. February. Bilateral enucleation. And after that, the chemotherapy started. And I was sick and scared again. And the yoga helped then too. And then the radiotherapy, and the mask they put on my head to hold me still, and I was so scared of the mask that the only thing that could calm me down was the breathing. All four of us on the mat. Breathing together. The only time I wasn't afraid."
- A beat.
- Brandi: "So when Rilee said I shouldn't be in Yoga because I can't see the poses — she doesn't know that Yoga is the reason I can sit still. She doesn't know that the mat is the place where the shaking stops. She doesn't know that warrior two isn't just a pose for me — it's the first thing I ever did that made me feel like my body was my own again after someone took it and hurt it in every way. She doesn't know any of that."
- A beat.
- Brandi: "And I didn't tell her. Because she didn't earn the story. But you — you held the space for me. You let me stay in the pose. You didn't make me explain myself. You just let me be in it. And I wanted you to hear it from me."
- The Yoga teacher reaches across the mat. She does not touch Brandi — she has learned that Brandi initiates physical contact on her own terms. She places her hand flat on the mat between them. An offering. Not an intrusion.
- Teacher: "Brandi. I knew the story. I've known it since April. But hearing you say it — that's not the same as reading it in a file. That's you, sitting on this mat, choosing to tell me. And I want you to know that the practice of Yoga is about many things, but one of the most important is this: the mat is a place where you are held. Not by a person. By the practice itself. The breath holds you. The pose holds you. The stillness holds you. You learned that in the hardest possible way — not in a studio, but at home, with your family around you, when nothing else could make you still. That means you understand something about this practice that takes most people a lifetime to learn. The mat is not a place where you perform. It is a place where you are held."
- Brandi reaches out and puts her hand on top of the teacher's hand on the mat. Brief. Light. Then she pulls back.
- Brandi: "Thank you for holding the space when Rilee tried to take it."
- Teacher: "I will always hold the space for you. That is what this studio is for."
- Brandi: "I know. That's why I count the steps here too."

### Beat 4: Swimming Lessons

- Brandi rolls up her mat and puts on her shoes. She picks up her cane and leaves the Yoga studio.
- She walks the path from the Yoga studio to the swimming pool. She counts her steps. She has done this route a hundred times — the same way she counts the route to the barre, the same way she counted the route to the beam.
- The swimming pool is outdoors, the water bright and still in the late afternoon sun. No classes are in the pool right now — it's the gap between the last activity and dinner, and the pool is open for free swim.
- Brandi puts her cane against the wall. She takes off her shoes and her cover-up. Underneath, she is wearing her swimsuit — she wore it under her dance clothes all day, the way she always does, so she doesn't have to change for water activities.
- She walks to the edge of the pool by feel. She does not hesitate. She dives in.
- The water catches her. The water always catches her. It is the one place where she does not count. The pool has lanes and the walls echo and she can feel where she is by the pressure of the water and the sound bouncing off the tiles. She does not need a cane in the pool. She does not need the tactile strip. She does not need anyone's voice.
- She swims. Freestyle, clean and fast. The water holds her the way the barre holds her, the way the mat holds her, the way the beam holds her. Different kind of holding. Same feeling: she does not have to hold herself up. She just has to trust.
- She swims laps. Back and forth. The water is cool on her skin. She can feel the lane lines under her fingertips when she reaches the wall. She does a flip turn — clean, practiced, precise — and swims back.
- After a while, she stops. She treads water in the deep end. The pool is quiet. The late afternoon light is golden on the water. She cannot see the light but she can feel it on her face, warm through the windows.
- She has said the thing out loud now. Twice. To two different women in two different studios. She has said Andrew's name. She has said he beat her. She has said it plainly, the way she says everything, without wasted movement.
- The water holds her. She floats on her back. The ceiling is above her — she cannot see it but she can hear the echo of the pool, the hum of the filter, the distant sound of campers walking past on their way to dinner.
- She floats. She breathes. The shaking that started when she was seven and never fully stopped — it is still now. The water does that. The mat does that. The barre does that. The beam does that. The song does that.
- She floats for a long time. Then she swims to the edge, pulls herself out of the pool, and sits on the side with her feet in the water.
- She does not tell Pandora what she did. Not because it's a secret. Because this was hers to give. Her story. Her choice. Her teachers.
- Some things you tell your sister. Some things you tell your mothers. Some things you tell the women who held the barre and the mat.
- Brandi told all of them. Each one differently. Each one true.
- She sits by the pool and lets the water hold her feet. Then she gets up, finds her cane, and walks back to the cabin for dinner.
