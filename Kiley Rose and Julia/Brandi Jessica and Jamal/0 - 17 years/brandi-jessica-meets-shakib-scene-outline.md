# Brandi Jessica Meets Shakib — Scene Outline

## SCENE INFORMATION

- **Characters:** Brandi Jessica Key (seven years old), Andrew, Greenlee (Andrew's cousin), Shakib, Shakib's three wives, Shakib's twenty children
- **Offstage / referenced characters:** Kiley Rose Key, Julia, Mike Key, Pandora Paige Key
- **Location:** A private compound in Sharjah, United Arab Emirates
- **When:** January 2052, immediately after Brandi Jessica's arrival in the UAE. She is seven years old — seven days past her seventh birthday, thirteen days since the kidnapping on December 26. She still has her eyes; the bilateral enucleation will not happen until February.
- **Status:** New scene outline
- **Tone:** The arrival at the destination. The man who will "love you so much" has a name, a face, a household. The horror is no longer anticipation — it is presence.

---

## SCENE PURPOSE

This scene outlines **Brandi Jessica's arrival at Shakib's compound in Sharjah** — the moment the man Greenlee promised arrives in full: not as a name on a boarding pass, not as a fear of the unknown, but as a body in a room. A man around forty, six-foot-five, two hundred and fifty pounds, with black hair and hazel eyes and shoulders that fill a doorway. And behind him, a household: three wives, twenty children, a compound that is its own country within a country.

The scene is about the shift from *not-knowing* to *knowing.* In the hotel room, Brandi Jessica feared a man whose name she did not know, in a country she could not pronounce. Now the man has a face. The country has streets. The "love" has a household around it. The horror is no longer abstract. It is real, and it is wearing the shape of a family.

The scene also establishes the architecture of what will follow: Shakib has a family. Wives. Children. The wives take on the role of preparing Brandi Jessica — dressing her in Arabic dance wear — the same way Greenlee prepared her in the hotel bathroom. The pattern repeats: a woman who should be safe, in a domestic role, making a child ready.

## SCENE ARC / BEATS

### Beat 1: The Arrival
- After the flight from New York — twelve hours, an ocean, a continent, a legal jurisdiction — Brandi Jessica arrives in the UAE.
- Andrew and Greenlee are with her. Andrew does not hold her hand. He does not speak to her. He walks beside her the way you walk beside a piece of luggage you are delivering. Greenlee walks on her other side, silent, watchful.
- Brandi Jessica is seven years old. She has not slept. She has barely eaten. She is still wearing the short black dress from the hotel room — the same dress Greenlee put her in, the one with the slipped strap, the nylons on her legs, bare feet in the shoes someone finally put on her at the airport.
- She still has her eyes. They are red-rimmed and hollow, the same eyes from the hotel room. She can see, but what she has seen in the last thirteen days has hollowed out the act of seeing.
- They are met at the airport by a driver. A car. Another journey — not long, but through streets that look nothing like New York. The signs are in Arabic. The buildings are different. The light is different. Nothing is familiar. Nothing is in English. Nothing is home.

### Beat 2: The Compound in Sharjah
- The car arrives at a compound in Sharjah. Walls. A gate. A property that is its own enclosed world.
- Brandi Jessica sees the compound through the car window. She does not know what Sharjah is. She does not know what the UAE is. She does not know what a compound is. She only knows that this is where she is being taken, and that the people who love her have never been here, and that no one is coming.
- The gate opens. The car enters. The gate closes behind them.
- Andrew gets out of the car first. He stands and looks at the compound the way you look at a delivery address you have confirmed. He is calm. He is not nervous. This is not his first time here, or this is a transaction he has completed before.
- Brandi Jessica gets out. Her feet are small on the gravel. She looks up at the compound — the walls, the buildings, the courtyard, the people moving in the distance. She looks up at Andrew. She does not ask where she is. She has learned that the answers are worse than the questions.

### Beat 3: Shakib
- A man comes out of the main building to meet them.
- **Shakib.**
- He is around forty years old. Black hair. Hazel eyes. Large shoulders — six-foot-five, two hundred and fifty pounds. A body that fills the doorway behind him. A man who is used to being the largest thing in a room.
- He walks toward Andrew and Brandi Jessica with the unhurried confidence of someone who owns the ground he is walking on.
- He does not kneel down to Brandi Jessica. He does not crouch. He does not smile warmly or offer a hand. He stands at his full height and looks down at her — way down, seven years old is far below a six-foot-five man — and he takes her in the way Andrew took her in on the first night: as a product being inspected, a delivery being confirmed.
- He speaks to Andrew. The language is not English, or it is a mix — Andrew understands, Brandi Jessica does not. The words are transactional. The tone is business. Brandi Jessica hears the shape of the conversation without the content: this is a handoff, a confirmation, a completion.
- Then Shakib looks at Brandi Jessica again. He says something to her. It may be in Arabic. It may be in broken English. She does not understand the words. But she understands the look. It is the same look Andrew gave her when he called her a *very perfect bitch.* It is the same look Greenlee gave her when she said *she is ready.*
- **This is the man who will love her so much.**

### Beat 4: The Wives and the Children
- Shakib does not take Brandi Jessica inside alone. He calls for his household to come out.
- **Three wives** emerge from the compound. Women in hijab, in long garments, their faces partially or fully covered. They stand in a row — three women, arranged by age or by order, and they look at Brandi Jessica the way three women look at a new arrival in their household.
- **Twenty children** emerge behind them. Children of various ages — some small, some older, some babies carried, some running. Twenty children who live in this compound, who have grown up here, who know this place as home. They look at Brandi Jessica with the curiosity of children seeing a new face.
- Brandi Jessica is seven. She is surrounded by children her own age and younger. She is surrounded by adults who should be safe. She is in a household that looks, from the outside, like a family.
- But she is not joining this family. She is not a new sister. She is not a cousin. She is not a guest. She is the thing that was delivered.
- Shakib introduces her. He says her name — or Andrew says her name — and the wives nod, and the children stare, and Brandi Jessica stands in the courtyard of a compound in Sharjah and understands that she is being shown to the household the way you show a new piece of furniture.

### Beat 5: The Wives Take Brandi Jessica
- After the introductions, Shakib speaks to one or more of his wives. He gestures toward Brandi Jessica. The gesture is clear: *take her.*
- The wives approach. They do not kneel. They do not ask if she is okay. They do not offer comfort. They take her by the hands — gently, functionally, the way you take a child by the hand to bathe her — and they lead her away from the courtyard, into the compound.
- Andrew and Greenlee do not leave. They stay at the compound. They watch as the wives lead Brandi Jessica away. They are here to see what happens next. They are here to see her dance.
- Brandi Jessica looks back once. She sees Andrew and Greenlee standing with Shakib in the courtyard. She sees the children still watching. She sees the gate that closed behind her.
- Then the wives lead her inside, and the door closes, and she is in the compound with the three wives and the twenty children, and Andrew and Greenlee are still here, and no one is coming.

### Beat 6: The Dressing
- The wives take Brandi Jessica into a room inside the compound. A bedroom, or a dressing room, or a room where they keep garments.
- They remove the black dress. The dress from the Christmas party, the dress from the hotel, the dress from the poker game, the dress from New Year's Eve. The dress with the slipped strap. The nylons. They take it all off.
- Brandi Jessica stands in the room, seven years old, in a foreign compound, surrounded by three women who do not speak her language, and they begin to dress her.
- **They dress her in Arabic dance wear.** The garments are unfamiliar — fabric she has never felt, shapes she has never worn, colors that are not the black of the dress, not the red of the dress she wanted to wear to the party. They dress her the way you dress a doll, the way you dress a performer, the way you dress a child for something she does not yet understand.
- The wives do not explain what the dance wear is for. They do not tell her what she will do in it. They simply dress her, adjusting the fabric, fastening the clasps, arranging her hair.
- Brandi Jessica does not resist. She has learned, in thirteen days, that resisting does not change what happens. She stands still while three women dress her in a foreign country in garments she does not understand, and she waits for whatever comes next.

### Beat 7: The Mirror
- When the wives finish, they turn her toward a mirror.
- Brandi Jessica looks at herself. She sees a seven-year-old girl in Arabic dance wear. She does not recognize herself. The face is the same — the hollow eyes, the red rims, the small frame — but the body is dressed as something she is not. The dance wear is for performance, for display, for the gaze of the man who will love her so much.
- She thinks of the black dress. The black dress was bad. The black dress was the poker game, the bathroom, New Year's Eve. But the black dress was still a dress. It was still a child's dress. It was still something a seven-year-old could wear to a Christmas party.
- This is not a child's dress. This is not a dress for a Christmas party. This is a dress for whatever the dance wear is for, and she does not know what that is, and she is seven years old, and she is in a compound in Sharjah, and the man with the black hair and the hazel eyes is waiting.
- She looks at herself in the mirror and she does not see Brandi Jessica. She sees the thing they have dressed her as. She sees the product. She sees the *very perfect bitch* Andrew called her, now wearing the costume for the next performance.

### Beat 7.5: The Offer
- While Brandi Jessica is being positioned in the center of the room, Shakib speaks to Andrew and Greenlee.
- He gestures to two of his children — two of the twenty who are standing in the courtyard or doorway.
- He offers them to Andrew and Greenlee for their stay at the compound.
- The offer is transactional. The same tone as the delivery of Brandi Jessica. The same shape as a business deal being closed. These children are being offered as compensation, as payment, as a gesture of hospitality for the work that Andrew and Greenlee have done.
- The two children do not react. They have been offered before. They know what it means. They step forward when Shakib gestures, the way you step forward when you have been told to step forward.
- Andrew and Greenlee accept. They accept the children the way you accept a gift at the end of a successful transaction.
- The two children come to them. They climb onto Andrew's lap and Greenlee's lap. They sit there the way a child sits on a lap — small, quiet, obedient. They have been taught to sit. They have been taught to be still. They have been taught that this is what children do when they are offered.
- Andrew and Greenlee sit with the children on their laps. They hold them the way the men at the poker game held Brandi Jessica. The same shape. The same posture. The same thing: a child on an adult's lap, being watched by other adults.
- The children do not react. They do not cry. They do not struggle. They sit on the laps of the people who just accepted them as payment, the way you sit when you have been taught to sit.
- The adults do not explain. They do not apologize. They do not acknowledge what is happening. It is simply part of the transaction. Part of the delivery. Part of the arrangement.
- Brandi Jessica sees it happen. She sees two children being offered. She sees them climb onto Andrew's and Greenlee's laps. She sees them sitting there. She sees that this is normal. She sees that this is how things work here. She sees that children are being moved the way packages are moved. She sees that children are being sat on laps the way she was sat on laps. She sees the pattern. She recognizes it. She lived it.

### Beat 8: The Dance
- The wives lead Brandi Jessica into a room — a performance space, or a courtyard, or a room designed for display. The room is large. The floor is tile or marble. The walls are decorated.
- Shakib is there. He is sitting or standing in the room, waiting. He has the posture of a man who is about to watch something. He is expecting the performance.
- Andrew is there. He is standing or sitting, watching. He is expecting the performance. He brought her here for this.
- Greenlee is there. She is standing or sitting, watching. She is expecting the performance. She prepared her for this — in the bathroom on December 30, in the bathroom on New Year's Eve. She made her *ready.* Now she is here to see if the preparation worked.
- The wives position Brandi Jessica in the center of the room. They do not give her instructions. They do not tell her what to do. They step back and wait.
- Brandi Jessica stands in the center of the room, seven years old, in Arabic dance wear, surrounded by adults who are watching her. She does not know what to do. She does not know what dance to perform. She does not know what they expect.
- She stands still. She does not move. She looks at the adults watching her. She looks at Shakib. She looks at Andrew. She looks at Greenlee. She looks at the wives. She looks at the children who have gathered in the doorway or around the edges of the room to watch.
- Then she moves. Not a dance she knows. Not a dance she was taught. Not a dance that has a name or a structure. She moves because she has to move. She moves because she is standing still and the adults are waiting and she does not know what else to do. She moves the way a child moves — awkwardly, uncertainly, without grace or technique. She moves because she is seven and she is terrified and she is in a foreign country and she does not know what is expected of her.
- The adults watch. Shakib watches. Andrew watches. Greenlee watches. The wives watch. The children watch. They watch a seven-year-old girl in Arabic dance wear move awkwardly in the center of a room in a compound in Sharjah. They watch her not know what to do. They watch her move anyway.
- The details of the dance are not specified. The reader fills in the space. What the reader imagines will always be worse than what the page can hold.
- What is specified is this: she is seven years old. She is in a compound in Sharjah. She has been dressed in Arabic dance wear by three women who do not speak her language. She is being watched by a man around forty, by the man who kidnapped her, by the woman who made her *ready,* by three wives, by twenty children, and she is moving because she does not know what else to do.
- This is what the dance wear is for. This is what she was brought here to do. This is what the man who will *love* her so much wanted to see. A seven-year-old girl in Arabic dance wear, moving in the center of a room in a compound in Sharjah, not knowing what is expected of her, moving anyway because she does not know how to stop.

## RELATION TO CONTINUITY

- **Fits the Brandi Jessica Captivity Arc:** This scene is the destination of the "little trip" announced in the Greenlee scene. The hotel room was staging. The UAE was delivery. The compound is where the delivery is completed.
- **Shakib as the Man Who Will "Love Her So Much":** Shakib is the man Greenlee promised. He is the face of the word *love* in Greenlee's mouth — the word that means the opposite of itself. The promise is now a person. The person is a man around forty with a household, and the household is the architecture of what will follow.
- **The Household as Context:** Shakib does not exist in isolation. He has three wives and twenty children. This is not a man living alone — this is a man with a domestic order, a hierarchy, a way of life. The wives are part of that order. The children are part of that order. Brandi Jessica is being inserted into that order as something new, and the wives' role in dressing her is part of integrating her — the way you prepare a new piece before you put it in its place.
- **The Pattern of Women Preparing the Child:** Greenlee prepared Brandi Jessica in the hotel bathroom. Now the wives prepare her in the compound. The pattern repeats: a woman who should be safe, in a domestic role, making a child ready. Greenlee was a cousin from the Christmas party. The wives are strangers in a foreign country. The shape is the same: adult women, a child, a closed door, a preparation for whatever the man requires.
- **Brandi Jessica Still Has Her Eyes:** At this point in January 2052, Brandi Jessica is still sighted. The bilateral enucleation will not happen until February. She can see Shakib's face. She can see the wives. She can see the children. She can see the compound. She can see herself in the mirror. Everything she sees will stay in her visual memory — the same visual memory she will carry into blindness after the surgery.
- **The Thirteen Days:** This is thirteen days since the kidnapping. Thirteen days since the townhouse, since Pandora, since Kiley, since Julia, since home. Thirteen days since she last saw her family. The Christmas party, the poker game, the bathroom, the trip announcement, the sobbing, New Year's Eve, the flight — all of it compressed into thirteen days, and now she is in a compound in Sharjah being dressed by three women for a man she has just met.
- **The Contrast with Home:** Brandi Jessica comes from a family where children are protected, where love is safety, where family is the thing that keeps you. Shakib's household is a family in shape — wives, children, a compound — but it is a family where a seven-year-old girl is delivered and dressed for performance. The word *family* means different things in different mouths, the way *love* means different things in Greenlee's mouth, the way *fun* means different things in Greenlee's mouth.
- **Andrew and Greenlee Stay at the Compound:** Andrew and Greenlee do not leave after delivering Brandi Jessica. They stay at the compound. They are here to see what happens next. They are here to see her dance. The delivery was not the end of their involvement — they are witnesses to what follows. The girl in the compound is still being watched by the people who brought her here.
