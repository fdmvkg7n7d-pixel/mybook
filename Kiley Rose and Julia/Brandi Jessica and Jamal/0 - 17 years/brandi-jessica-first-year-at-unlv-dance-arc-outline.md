# Brandi Jessica’s First Year at UNLV — Dance Arc Outline

## ARC INFORMATION

- **Primary character:** Brandi Jessica Key
- **Supporting characters:** Kiley Rose Key, Julia, Pandora Paige Key, **Kassie Myller** (Brandi’s female first-year roommate and fellow Dance student), Brandi’s first-year ballet professor (name TBD; member of the admissions committee that selected Brandi), UNLV dance faculty, accessibility staff, dance classmates
- **Onstage animal:** Burden, Brandi Jessica’s Labernese guide dog
- **Locations:** Brandi Jessica’s coeducational UNLV dormitory; the UNLV campus; dance studios, classrooms, rehearsal rooms, and performance spaces
- **When:** Brandi Jessica’s first academic year at the University of Nevada, Las Vegas, ~2062–2063
- **Age:** Seventeen at the beginning of the academic year, turning eighteen on Christmas Day
- **Field of study:** Dance
- **First-year housing:** Mixed-gender residence hall and floor; Brandi shares a standard two-person bedroom with **Kassie Myller**, another female first-year student in the UNLV Dance program, while students of different genders share the floor’s common spaces
- **Status:** First-year arc outline
- **Tone:** Independent, demanding, physical, exploratory, artistically awakening

---

## ARC PURPOSE

This arc backtracks to Brandi Jessica’s first year at UNLV, before her relationship with Jamal begins.

Brandi arrives in Las Vegas at just **seventeen years old** to study **dance**. She will turn eighteen on Christmas Day during the academic year. Her first year establishes that she is not attending university merely to prove that a blind student can survive a dance program. She is there to receive serious technical, academic, and creative training.

The year follows her transition from an experienced young dancer into an emerging dance-maker. She begins university intending to deepen her performance training. Through first-year composition work, she discovers that she may also want to create movement for other dancers. This becomes the earliest foundation of her later decision to become a professional choreographer.

Brandi does not know where dance will eventually take her. During this first year, the important choice is the field itself—not a future city, company, or stage.

---

## FIRST-YEAR COURSE FRAMEWORK

Brandi’s first-year dance studies include:

- Ballet technique
- Contemporary / modern technique
- Dance composition and improvisation
- Music and rhythmic analysis for dancers
- Dance history
- Anatomy and kinesiology
- Production and stagecraft fundamentals
- Rehearsal laboratory / ensemble work

Her academic materials are made available through accessible digital text, screen-reader-compatible documents, Braille where useful, tactile diagrams, and verbal description.

## ARC STRUCTURE / BEATS

### Beat 1: Arrival at the UNLV Dormitory

- After the family completes its final vacation legs in Natal and at **Amazonat Jungle Lodge**, **Julia takes Brandi Jessica and Burden to Las Vegas** before the academic year begins.
- Kiley Rose takes Pandora Paige to Port Douglas and does not accompany Brandi to UNLV move-in.
- Brandi is just seventeen. Kiley Rose completes any required parental consent, housing, emergency-contact, and medical authorization paperwork before the travel split or through the university’s secure system. Julia is the adult physically present for move-in.
- The signatures do not give Julia, faculty, or housing staff permission to speak over Brandi. Brandi remains the person who directs her accessibility and daily life.
- Brandi lives in a **coeducational UNLV dormitory for her entire first year**.
- Male and female students live on the same floor and share the lounge, study room, laundry area, and other common spaces.
- Brandi shares a **standard two-person bedroom with Kassie Myller**.
- Kassie is another female first-year student studying **Dance** in the same UNLV program as Brandi.
- Brandi does not request or need a specially accessible bedroom. Blindness does not require wider doorways, adapted furniture, or a physically modified room.
- The standard room has two beds, two desks, and separate storage areas. Brandi and Kassie choose a designated place for Burden’s bed, food, grooming equipment, and working supplies.
- Both students need room for dance bags, rehearsal clothes, shoes, notebooks, and recovery equipment.
- Brandi uses the residence hall’s ordinary bathroom facilities like the other students on her floor.
- Kassie is not assigned as Brandi’s guide, aide, monitor, or caretaker. They enter the room as equal first-year dance students.
- Brandi and Kassie agree on practical shared-room rules:
  - Furniture remains in agreed locations unless they discuss moving it first.
  - Shoes, bags, and laundry do not block the clear walking route.
  - Each student’s desk, wardrobe, and storage remain private.
  - Guests require notice and agreement.
  - Headphones, sleep schedules, alarms, and study time are negotiated like any other roommate arrangement.
  - Burden is not called, fed, petted, or redirected while working.
- Brandi learns the dormitory systematically:
  - Counts steps between her room, elevator, stairs, lounge, laundry area, and building exit.
  - Establishes fixed locations for her room key, phone chargers, medications, dance bag, and Burden’s supplies.
  - Labels her own controls and storage tactually.
  - Maps the lobby, emergency routes, mail area, dining route, and campus pickup point.
- Julia helps unpack only the items Brandi asks her to handle while Kassie organizes her own half of the room.
- Kiley and Pandora call from Port Douglas during move-in. Pandora does not remotely direct the room arrangement; she lets Brandi and Kassie establish their own shared space.
- Julia resists the urge to reorganize everything. The shared room belongs equally to Brandi and Kassie, so the two students choose the system together.
- The penthouse does not belong to this first-year chapter. Kiley Rose and Julia purchase it later, after Brandi has completed her year in campus housing.

### Beat 2: Julia Leaves Las Vegas

- On the morning Julia leaves Las Vegas, Brandi walks her to the dormitory entrance with Burden beside her.
- Julia asks one final time whether Brandi wants her to remain longer.
- Brandi says no.

> “If I wait until I’m not nervous, I’ll never start.”

- Julia checks that Brandi knows the residence hall’s emergency plan, then stops herself from asking ten more questions.
- Kiley and Pandora already said their in-person goodbyes when the family divided after the Amazonat Jungle Lodge leg. They expect a call from Port Douglas after Brandi’s first dance class.
- Julia hugs Brandi, tells her she is ready, and leaves alone.
- The dormitory doors close behind Julia.
- Brandi returns to the shared room with Burden and listens to the unfamiliar residence-hall sounds around her: doors closing, elevator chimes, conversations in the corridor, and music leaking through walls.
- Kassie asks whether Brandi wants to go to the dining hall together. Brandi says yes; Burden guides Brandi while Kassie walks beside them as a peer, not as an escort.
- The resident assistant reviews emergency procedures and asks Brandi what the floor should know about Burden.
- At the first floor meeting, male and female residents introduce themselves by name so Brandi can connect voices with neighbors.
- The resident assistant makes the coeducational arrangement ordinary: everyone knocks before entering another student’s room, respects closed doors, and shares common spaces under the same conduct rules.
- Brandi explains that students must not call, feed, pet, or redirect Burden while he is working.
- Dormitory life immediately requires boundaries: residents of every gender are welcome to speak to Brandi, but Burden is not the floor’s shared pet and the shared bedroom is not open without both roommates’ consent.
- Brandi receives no automatic escort, curfew, or gender-based restriction because she is blind.
- This is the Las Vegas version of the Manhattan Drops: no parent secretly following half a block behind her now.

### Beat 3: Learning the UNLV Campus

- Before classes begin, Brandi works with orientation and mobility staff to learn her main campus routes.
- Burden works in harness while they map:
  - The route from her drop-off point to the dance building.
  - Accessible entrances and elevators.
  - The student services office.
  - The library.
  - Dining locations.
  - Emergency gathering points.
- Brandi records environmental landmarks in voice notes:
  - Changes in pavement.
  - Air-conditioning sounds near entrances.
  - Echo differences under covered walkways.
  - Food smells near the student union.
  - The direction of afternoon heat.
- She learns more than one route to the dance building so construction or crowding cannot isolate her.

### Beat 4: Brandi Leads the Accessibility Meeting

- Before the first technique class, Brandi meets with dance faculty and accessibility staff.
- She refuses a conversation in which adults discuss her while she sits silently.

> “Ask me what I need. Don’t decide what I can do.”

- Brandi’s requested accommodations include:
  - Verbal descriptions of combinations rather than silent demonstration alone.
  - Counts, directional language, and named pathways.
  - Advance orientation to each studio and stage.
  - Screen-reader-compatible course materials.
  - Tactile diagrams for formations, anatomy, and production layouts.
  - Permission for Burden to rest in a consistent location at the edge of the studio.
  - An auditory orientation point when choreography requires a fixed front.
  - Consent-based physical correction rather than surprise contact.
- Brandi does **not** ask for lower technical standards, easier choreography, or exemption from performance requirements.
- The department agrees that accommodations will change access, not expectations.

### Beat 5: Mapping the Main Dance Studio

- Brandi enters the primary studio before other students arrive.
- Burden guides her to the designated resting mat near the door, safely outside all movement paths.
- Brandi walks the full perimeter with an instructor:
  - Counts the length and width.
  - Locates doors, barres, mirrors, piano, speakers, and exits.
  - Tests the sprung floor through the pressure under her feet.
  - Listens to the room’s echo from center, corners, and front.
- A low-volume auditory beacon marks the front of the room when needed.
- Any tactile markers are confined to safe perimeter or starting locations and do not create tripping hazards in active dance space.
- Once the room is mapped, Brandi can cross it without being led.

### Beat 6: First Ballet Technique Class

- Brandi and Kassie walk to the dance building together on the first morning, with Burden guiding Brandi along the route Brandi has already learned.
- They enter the same ballet class but do not automatically stand together or become permanent partners simply because they share a room.
- Brandi arrives early with Burden and establishes his resting place.
- She crosses the mapped studio independently, finds the barre, places her left hand on it, and begins her normal warm-up: posture, turnout check, slow pliés, tendus, and ankle articulation.
- Several students are **horrified** to see a blind dancer beginning barre work. Their reaction is immediate and extreme:
  - One stops warming up and stares.
  - Another backs away from Brandi’s section of the barre as if Brandi is dangerous.
  - Angry whispers spread through the room: *“This cannot be serious.”* / *“She cannot even see her line.”* / *“Why did they let her into this program?”*
  - One student complains that the class is being turned into an experiment and demands to know who will be responsible when Brandi gets hurt.
- Brandi hears every word but continues the complete warm-up without losing her counts.
- Kassie takes her own place at the barre near Brandi but does not argue on Brandi’s behalf while Brandi is working.
- The instructor orders the rest of the class to begin warming up and makes clear that staring at Brandi is not class participation.
- Brandi finishes the phrase, closes in fifth position, and says:

> “The barre has not moved. Neither have the standards.”

- Before class proceeds, the ballet professor addresses the room.
- The professor tells the students that they served on the admissions committee that reviewed and selected Brandi.
- Brandi was chosen because of her technical foundation, musical intelligence, physical discipline, audition work, and potential—not because the university needed a symbolic blind student.
- The professor is visibly and personally ashamed of the class’s reaction:

> “I helped select Brandi Jessica for this program. I am not ashamed of that decision. I am ashamed that dancers in this room watched a qualified classmate begin an ordinary barre warm-up and responded with contempt.”

- The professor makes clear that the students’ horror reveals their prejudice, not a defect in Brandi’s admission.
- The statement does not turn Brandi into someone who needs rescuing. It places institutional responsibility where it belongs: the professor and program must defend the fairness of their own admissions decision.
- Classmates introduce themselves by name so Brandi can connect voices to positions. Kassie’s is the first already-familiar voice in the studio.
- The instructor initially says, *“Watch the demonstration,”* then catches the mistake.
- Brandi responds without embarrassment or anger:

> “Describe the pathway and give me the counts.”

- The instructor repeats the combination using clear language:
  - Starting foot.
  - Direction.
  - Arm position.
  - Number of traveling steps.
  - Turn preparation.
  - Final orientation.
- Brandi uses her visual memory of ballet positions from before age seven, reinforced by years of tactile and auditory training.
- She completes the combination at the same tempo as the class.
- The first correction is given verbally. When the instructor wants to adjust Brandi’s shoulder line physically, the instructor asks permission first.
- Brandi says yes, receives the correction, and repeats the phrase.
- After class, the professor asks Brandi to remain. They sit on the floor in the middle of the empty studio with Burden beside them. Brandi recognizes the professor’s voice from her audition and recalls how Kiley Rose, Julia, and Pandora traveled to Las Vegas but let her enter the university audition alone with Burden. They also discuss the rude, ridiculous students, the professor’s admissions-committee role, and Brandi’s bilateral retinoblastoma. When the professor says they admire Brandi’s courage to undertake the program, Brandi becomes deeply emotional. See `brandi-jessica-professor-conversation-after-first-ballet-class-scene-outline.md`.

### Beat 7: Contemporary Technique Changes the Question

- Contemporary class requires floor work, weight shifts, release, improvisation, and movement that does not always face a fixed front.
- Brandi finds some elements easier than expected because they depend on internal weight and contact with the floor rather than visual line.
- Other elements are harder:
  - Fast group crossings.
  - Unpredictable improvisational traffic.
  - Silent cues from another dancer’s body.
- The class develops practical rehearsal language without making every dancer responsible for Brandi:
  - Dancers identify themselves before entering shared improvisation space.
  - Partners use agreed verbal or touch cues.
  - Brandi chooses when physical contact is useful.
  - Faculty describe visual group changes that cannot be inferred through sound.
- Brandi is not protected from collision by removing her from the exercise. She learns the traffic and participates.

### Beat 8: Dance History, Anatomy, and Music

- Brandi’s first year is not only studio work.
- Dance-history readings are provided in accessible formats.
- Visual archival footage receives description focused on movement rather than costume alone.
- Anatomy instruction uses raised diagrams and three-dimensional models so Brandi can study joints, muscle groups, alignment, and injury prevention.
- Music class becomes one of her strongest areas.
- Her ear for rhythm allows her to identify phrasing changes before some classmates who rely on watching the instructor.
- Faculty begin recognizing that Brandi’s way of processing dance is not a reduced version of visual learning. It reveals different information.

### Beat 9: Extreme Anger After the Barre Class

- The students who reacted with horror at the barre become **extremely angry** after the first technique class.
- They gather in the corridor and insist that Brandi’s admission threatens the seriousness and safety of the program.
- Their resentment is rooted in ableist assumptions:
  - They believe a dancer must be able to see demonstrations and mirrors.
  - They insist that allowing Brandi to use the barre is unsafe, even though she has trained in ballet since childhood.
  - They assume verbal description gives Brandi extra instruction and unfair attention.
  - They complain that faculty time spent describing formations will slow everyone else down.
  - They claim other dancers will have to alter spacing to protect Brandi.
  - They question whether Burden belongs near the studios.
  - They suggest that Brandi received a place only because the university wanted to appear inclusive.
- Several students discuss making a formal complaint and demanding that Brandi be removed from technique classes until she can somehow prove she is not a danger.
- One student tells Kassie that she has effectively been assigned to babysit Brandi because they share a room and program.
- Kassie answers:

> “I was assigned a roommate. I was not assigned a person to take care of.”

- Brandi overhears enough of the conversation to address it herself.

> “If you think I do not belong here, say it to me—not to my roommate.”

- A student argues that dance is visual and asks how Brandi can possibly meet the same standard.
- Brandi answers:

> “Then watch whether I meet it. You do not get to lower the standard in your imagination and blame me for it.”

- Brandi makes clear that she auditioned, was admitted, and is graded under the same technical and artistic requirements.
- She does not promise that every class will move without adjustment. She explains that access changes how information is communicated, not what she must perform.
- Kassie does not turn the confrontation into a rescue. She remains beside Brandi and supports the facts without speaking over her.
- Dance faculty address the hostility directly, with the ballet professor speaking both as Brandi’s instructor and as a member of her admissions committee:
  - Brandi was admitted on merit after the committee reviewed her application and audition work.
  - The professor stands behind the decision and refuses to treat student prejudice as a legitimate admissions concern.
  - The professor states plainly that they are ashamed of the hostility shown by members of the cohort.
  - Brandi’s years of ballet training qualify her to work at the barre.
  - There is no evidence that her presence creates a greater safety risk than any other first-year dancer learning new material.
  - Verbal description and tactile diagrams are access tools, not reduced standards.
  - No student is required to guide, monitor, or care for Brandi.
  - Any complaint demanding exclusion solely because she is blind will be rejected.
  - Repeated harassment or interference with Burden will be handled through university conduct procedures.
- Some students reconsider once they see Brandi work. Others remain cold or skeptical for part of the year.
- The arc does not require every classmate to become her friend. Brandi’s goal is equal access and professional treatment, not universal approval.

### Beat 10: A Classmate Tries to Overhelp

- One well-meaning classmate begins grabbing Brandi’s arm whenever the group changes location.
- After the third time, Brandi stops and addresses it directly.

> “Tell me the room changed. Don’t move my body for me.”

- The classmate apologizes.
- Brandi explains the preferred information:

> “Say, ‘We moved two lines toward the windows.’ Then let me move.”

- The correction is brief and not treated as a friendship-ending conflict.
- By the next class, the classmate gives the information properly and Brandi thanks her.
- This becomes part of the program’s working culture: access through communication, not control.

### Beat 11: First Composition Assignment

- In first-year dance composition, students receive an assignment to create a short movement phrase for themselves.
- Brandi builds the phrase through:
  - A recorded rhythmic structure.
  - Counts spoken into voice notes.
  - Changes in level and direction.
  - A tactile map of the floor.
  - Memories of visual line from childhood.
- The professor asks students to teach their phrase to a partner.
- Kassie is assigned elsewhere. Faculty do not pair the roommates automatically, allowing both women to build independent working relationships within the program.
- Brandi discovers that she enjoys explaining movement as much as performing it.
- She does not imitate the partner’s body visually. She communicates intention:

> “The turn is not the point. The point is resisting the turn until the music makes staying still impossible.”

- Her partner understands and performs the phrase differently—but truthfully.
- Brandi hears the difference in breath, timing, and foot pressure.

### Beat 12: The Trio Assignment

- Later in the year, Brandi must create a short trio.
- This is her first sustained experience choreographing other dancers.
- She builds a raised formation map using cord and textured markers:
  - One texture for each dancer.
  - Raised lines for travel paths.
  - Knots for pauses and level changes.
- During rehearsal:
  - Dancers state their positions at the beginning of each section.
  - Brandi listens to footfalls and breathing.
  - She asks for exact distances rather than vague terms such as *over there*.
  - A rehearsal assistant reports spacing only when Brandi requests it.
  - Brandi makes every artistic decision herself.
- If Brandi wants to adjust a dancer’s arm or torso, she asks permission before touching.
- The dancers learn that her corrections are highly precise because she listens to timing most people overlook.

### Beat 13: The First Real Artistic Disagreement

- One dancer argues that a transition will look empty if performed the way Brandi designed it.
- Brandi asks the dancer to describe what appears empty.
- The dancer explains that too much space opens between the trio members.
- Brandi does not dismiss visual information simply because she cannot verify it directly.
- She asks the group to perform the section twice:
  - Once as written.
  - Once with the dancer’s proposed spacing.
- Brandi listens, asks for descriptions, and decides to preserve the larger distance but change the timing.
- The result keeps her artistic intention while solving the visual problem.
- This is an early lesson in choreographic authority: collaboration is not surrender.

### Beat 14: First-Year Showing

- The composition class presents its work in an informal studio showing.
- Brandi performs in technique pieces and presents her trio as its choreographer.
- Burden rests at the edge of the studio during the showing.
- The trio completes its crossings without audio cues that would distract the audience; the dancers have internalized Brandi’s counts and pathways through rehearsal.
- Brandi stands with the other choreographers during feedback.
- Faculty discuss rhythm, structure, tension, and use of space before discussing access.
- Brandi is relieved that the work is treated as choreography rather than an inspirational demonstration.

### Beat 15: Kiley Rose, Julia, and Pandora Attend

- Kiley Rose, Julia, and Pandora return to Las Vegas for the first-year showing.
- Pandora describes the trio’s visual formations quietly after the performance—not during it—so Brandi can compare what Pandora saw with what Brandi intended.
- Kiley becomes emotional watching Brandi command a dance studio after everything that happened when she lost her sight.
- Julia remains practical and asks what Brandi would change in the piece.
- Brandi immediately gives three answers.
- The response makes Julia smile: Brandi is already thinking like a choreographer rather than waiting for praise.

### Beat 16: The First-Year Conversation at the Dormitory

- That night, the four women share takeout in a quiet common room at Brandi’s UNLV dormitory.
- Kassie joins them briefly, talks about her own first-year dance work, and then leaves to study with other classmates.
- Male and female residents pass through the room, greet Brandi and Kassie by name, and continue with their own evening. Kiley and Julia see that Brandi has become part of the mixed-gender floor rather than being treated as its protected exception.
- Kiley asks whether studying dance at university is what Brandi hoped it would be.
- Brandi says it is harder, less romantic, and more interesting than she expected.
- Pandora asks whether Brandi still wants to perform.
- Brandi answers yes.
- Then she adds:

> “But I don’t think performing is the only thing I want.”

- Julia asks what else she wants.
- Brandi says:

> “I came here to study dance. I think I might want to make dances too.”

- This is not yet a complete career declaration. It is the first clear articulation of the choreographer she may become.

### Beat 17: End of the First Year

- By the end of the academic year, Brandi has:
  - Completed the same core dance requirements as her classmates.
  - Established practical accessibility systems with the department.
  - Learned the UNLV campus with Burden.
  - Performed ballet and contemporary work.
  - Created and rehearsed her first university choreography.
  - Begun separating helpful collaboration from unwanted control.
- She records a private voice note before leaving the studio after her final class:

> “I know I want dance. I’m starting to understand how many ways that can mean.”

- Brandi still does not know where she will eventually live, which company she might join, or what kind of stage will become hers.
- First year gives her no destination. It gives her a direction.
