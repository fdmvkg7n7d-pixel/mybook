# Brandi Jessica in You're a Good Man, Charlie Brown — Session 3 Scene Outline

## SCENE INFORMATION

- **Characters:** Brandi Jessica Key (8), Pandora Paige Key (8), Musical Director, Cast Members (Daisy as Lucy, etc.), Maya (counselor), other campers
- **Onstage animal:** None (Burden does not arrive until age 14)
- **Locations:** Theater building — rehearsal stage, backstage, main stage — French Woods (Hancock, New York)
- **When:** Session 3, Brandi Jessica and Pandora Paige's first summer at French Woods (July 2053). Sign-up at the start of the session, rehearsals throughout, performance near the end of the session.
- **Status:** New scene outline
- **Tone:** Determined, theatrical, surprising — a blind girl in a musical without her twin, and what she brings to it

## SCENE PURPOSE

This scene captures Brandi Jessica's journey through the Session 3 production of You're a Good Man, Charlie Brown — from sign-up to rehearsal to performance. It is the first time Brandi has been in a theatrical production, and the first time she does something this big at camp without Pandora beside her.

It establishes:
- That Brandi signs up for the musical because she wants to, not to prove anything. At French Woods, every camper is welcome to be in the plays — there are no auditions. You sign up, you're in.
- That Pandora does not want to be in the play. She's just not interested. This is the first time the twins have made a different choice about a major activity at camp, and it is not a crisis. It is just a fact. Pandora has her own things. Brandi has this.
- That the Musical Director does not lower the bar for Brandi. She expects the same thing from Brandi that she expects from every other camper — learn your part, hit your marks, know your cue.
- That Brandi's blindness requires practical adjustments — blocking by touch and memory, vocal cues for entrances, a tactile marking system on the stage floor — but does not require artistic compromise. She is not cast out of pity. She is cast because she can do the work.
- That the Charlie Brown musical is about a kid who is insecure, who fails a lot, who keeps trying anyway. Brandi understands this character from the inside.
- That the production gives Brandi something Ballet and Pointe don't — a voice. In dance, she expresses through movement. In the musical, she expresses through singing and acting. The songwriting she's been doing in Songwriting class comes alive here. Charlie Brown is one of her Session 3 majors, alongside Songwriting and Painting and Drawing.
- That Brandi does the show without Pandora on stage. She finds her way the way she finds her way everywhere — by counting, by feeling, by listening. Her compass is not a person this time. It is the stage itself.
- That performing in front of an audience — including the Hayashis, Sloan and Rilee — is a different kind of confrontation than the one in Ballet class. Brandi doesn't have to say anything. She just has to be on stage.

## BEATS

### Beat 1: The Musical Announcement

- It is the first day of Session 3. The camp bulletin board in the dining hall announces: YOU'RE A GOOD MAN, CHARLIE BROWN — SESSION 3 MUSICAL. ALL CAMPERS WELCOME. NO AUDITIONS. SIGN UP BY FRIDAY.
- At French Woods, every camper is welcome to be in the plays. There are no auditions — you sign up, you're in. The directors figure out the casting.
- Pandora reads it out loud to Brandi as they walk past on their way to breakfast.
- Pandora: "A musical. Should we sign up?"
- Brandi: "What's Charlie Brown?"
- Pandora: "The cartoon. The round-headed kid with the dog. He's kind of sad all the time but he never stops trying."
- Brandi: "That sounds like me. Except for the round head."
- Pandora laughs. "And the dog."
- Brandi: "I'm getting a dog. Eventually. Burden. He's not a cartoon dog but he's mine."
- Pandora: "So should we sign up?"
- Brandi: "I want to. Do you?"
- Pandora thinks about it for a moment. She pictures herself on a stage, singing, saying lines, standing in a spotlight. It doesn't interest her. She'd rather be on the silks or the beam or the bar. She'd rather be in the studio or the gym.
- Pandora: "I don't think so. Theater's not really my thing."
- Brandi: "Okay."
- There is no argument. No disappointment. No crisis. Brandi wants to do the play. Pandora doesn't. They are twins, not the same person.
- Brandi signs the sign-up sheet by herself. Pandora stands next to her and reads the names already on the list. Then she says: "I'll come watch you. I'll be in the front row."
- Brandi: "How will you get in the front row? Everyone will be there."
- Pandora: "I'll get there early. I always get there early. I'm the early twin."
- Brandi: "You're not the early twin. You're the loud twin."
- Pandora: "I'm both."
- Brandi laughs. She signs her name and they go to breakfast.

### Beat 2: The Cast List

- At French Woods, there are no auditions — but there is casting. The Musical Director and her team assign roles based on observation, interest, and what they see in the campers during the first few days.
- The Musical Director is a woman in her forties with short gray hair and a clipboard. She has been briefed by the camp administration about Brandi Jessica. She does not care who Brandi is. She cares about casting the show.
- She watches the campers during the sign-up meeting — who reads lines with natural ease, who sings with confidence, who has the right energy for each role.
- Brandi reads a few lines when the group tries them out together. She does not need to see the script — she asks the camper next to her to read in her ear, and she repeats them with a quiet, instinctive timing that makes the room go still.
- The Musical Director watches. She sees something in Brandi — the stillness, the sincerity, the way she says Charlie Brown's lines like she's been waiting to say them her whole life. She makes her decision.
- The Musical Director decides that Brandi Jessica should be Charlie Brown.
- The cast list is posted on the bulletin board outside the dining hall. A crowd of campers presses around it.
- Brandi can't see the board. She waits while Pandora pushes through and reads it.
- Pandora, coming back: "You got Charlie Brown."
- Brandi: "The lead?"
- Pandora: "The lead. You're Charlie Brown."
- Brandi is quiet. Then her face changes — not slowly, but all at once, the way a dam breaks. Her chin trembles. Her eyes fill. She was not expecting this. She signed up because it sounded fun, because Charlie Brown sounded like her, because there are no no's only hows. She did not expect to be given the lead. She did not expect anyone to look at her — a blind eight-year-old who has never been on a stage — and say: you. You're the one.
- Brandi: "Me?"
- Pandora: "You."
- Brandi's hands go to her face. She is crying. Not the quiet, controlled crying of a girl who has been through chemotherapy and radiotherapy and surgery. This is different. This is the crying of a girl who has just been told she is enough. That someone saw her — really saw her — and decided she was the center of the show.
- Pandora wraps her arms around her. Brandi presses her face into Pandora's shoulder and cries. Not sad crying. Overwhelmed crying. The kind of crying that happens when the world gives you something you didn't know you were allowed to want.
- Pandora holds her. She does not say "it's okay" or "don't cry." She just holds her. She knows the difference between sad tears and this kind.
- After a long moment, Brandi pulls back. She wipes her face with both hands. She takes a breath.
- Brandi: "I'm going to be Charlie Brown."
- Pandora: "You're going to be the best Charlie Brown."
- Brandi: "I don't know how to be the best. I just know how to show up."
- Pandora: "Then show up. That's always been enough."
- Brandi wipes her face again. She straightens up. She takes Pandora's hand.
- Brandi: "Thank you for reading the list for me."
- Pandora: "That's what I'm here for."
- Brandi: "No. I mean thank you for always being the one who reads things for me. The list. The scripts. The signs. Everything. I know you don't have to. I know I could ask anyone. But you always just do it. You always just do it before I even have to ask."
- Pandora is quiet. This is not the kind of thing they usually say to each other. They are eight. They don't usually say the thing that's true underneath everything. But Brandi just got cast in a lead in a musical, and she is still raw, and the truth is coming out.
- Pandora: "You don't have to thank me for that."
- Brandi: "I know I don't have to. I want to. Thank you."
- Pandora squeezes her hand. "You're welcome. Now let's go eat breakfast. Future Charlie Browns need protein."
- They start walking toward the dining hall. But Brandi stops.
- Brandi: "Wait. I want to say thank you to the director."
- Pandora: "She's still by the bulletin board."
- Brandi turns around and walks back. She counts her steps — she knows the route from the dining hall to the board. She taps her cane and finds the edge of the building, then follows the wall to where the board is posted.
- The Musical Director is there, clipboard in hand, finishing a conversation with a counselor. She sees Brandi approaching with her cane.
- Musical Director: "Brandi. Did you see the list?"
- Brandi: "Pandora read it to me. I got Charlie Brown."
- Musical Director: "You did."
- Brandi stands in front of her. She does not know exactly where the director's eyes are, so she speaks to the sound of her voice.
- Brandi: "Thank you."
- Musical Director: "For what?"
- Brandi: "For choosing me. You didn't have to. You could have given it to someone who can see the stage. Someone who can see the other actors. Someone who doesn't need markers on the floor. But you chose me. And I just wanted to say thank you. Because it's the first time someone who didn't have to believe in me believed in me anyway."
- The Musical Director is quiet for a moment. She looks at this eight-year-old girl standing in front of her with a cane and red eyes from crying and the kind of gratitude that only comes from someone who knows exactly what it costs to be given a chance.
- Musical Director: "Brandi, I didn't choose you because I believe in you. I chose you because you read those lines and every person in that room went quiet. I chose you because when you said Charlie Brown's words, they sounded like they belonged to you. I don't cast out of belief. I cast out of what I see. And what I saw was Charlie Brown."
- Brandi nods. She takes this in.
- Brandi: "But you still could have picked someone easier."
- Musical Director, after a beat: "Easy isn't what makes a good show. True is what makes a good show. And you, Brandi Jessica, are the truest Charlie Brown I could have cast."
- Brandi: "Thank you. I won't let you down."
- Musical Director: "I know you won't."
- A beat. Brandi stands there. She hasn't moved. There's something else.
- Brandi: "Do you know that I'm blind?"
- The Musical Director pauses. She looks at the cane in Brandi's hand, at the eyes that do not focus, at the girl standing in front of her who just said thank you for being chosen and is now making sure the person who chose her understands exactly what that means.
- Musical Director: "Yes. I know."
- Brandi: "I mean really know. Not like you read it on a form or someone told you. Do you know what it means? That I can't see the stage? That I can't see the other actors? That I can't see the audience or the lights or the curtain or where I'm standing unless I can feel it? That I will need markers on the floor and someone to read me the script and vocal cues for every entrance? Do you know all of that?"
- The Musical Director does not flinch. She does not soften. She meets this the way she meets everything — directly.
- Musical Director: "I know all of that."
- Brandi: "And you still chose me?"
- Musical Director: "I still chose you."
- Brandi: "Why?"
- Musical Director: "Because when you stood in that room and said Charlie Brown's lines, the room got quiet. Not out of pity. Not out of surprise. Because you told the truth. And the truth doesn't need eyes, Brandi. It just needs a voice."
- Brandi is still. The words land.
- Brandi: "Okay."
- Musical Director: "Okay?"
- Brandi: "Okay. I just needed to hear you say it. That you know. That you're not going to wake up tomorrow and realize what you signed up for."
- Musical Director: "I know exactly what I signed up for. The question is — do you?"
- Brandi: "I've known what I signed up for since I was seven."
- A beat. Neither of them speaks. The counselor the Musical Director was talking to has stepped away. It is just the two of them — a director and a blind eight-year-old who has just made sure that the person putting her on stage knows exactly who she is.
- Musical Director: "Go eat breakfast, Brandi. Rehearsals start tomorrow."
- Brandi nods. She turns and walks back toward the dining hall. Pandora is waiting for her.
- Pandora: "What did you say to her?"
- Brandi: "I said thank you."
- Pandora: "For?"
- Brandi: "For choosing me."
- Pandora takes her hand. They walk into breakfast together.
- Sloan walks past the bulletin board, glances at the cast list, and stops.
- Sloan reads the list. She sees Brandi's name next to Charlie Brown. Her face does something complicated — surprise, confusion, maybe something else. She does not say anything. She walks away.
- Rilee, catching up with Sloan: "What's wrong?"
- Sloan: "The blind girl got the lead in the musical."
- Rilee: "Charlie Brown?"
- Sloan: "Charlie Brown."
- Rilee: "Can she even... do a musical?"
- Sloan does not answer. She keeps walking.

### Beat 3: First Rehearsal — Blocking

- The first rehearsal is in the theater. The full cast sits in a semicircle on the stage. The Musical Director stands in the center with her clipboard.
- Brandi is the only one with a cane. She folds it and sets it against the wall. She is on a stage she has never been on, in a room she has never mapped, surrounded by people she cannot see.
- Musical Director: "Welcome to You're a Good Man, Charlie Brown. This is a show about a kid who thinks he's a failure. He strikes out. He loses the kite. He sits alone at lunch. And he keeps showing up anyway. If you have ever felt like Charlie Brown, this is your show."
- She looks at Brandi when she says the last line. Not with pity. With recognition.
- Musical Director: "Now. Blocking. Blocking means where you stand and where you move. For most of you, that means watching where I point and going there. For Brandi, it means something different, and we are going to figure it out together."
- She does not make this awkward. She does not whisper. She says it to the whole cast, the way she would say anything else.
- Musical Director: "Brandi, how do you learn a new space?"
- Brandi: "I count steps. I map it. I feel the edges. I use my cane for new spaces. For spaces I know, I don't need it."
- Musical Director: "This is a new space. But by opening night, it will be a space you know. How do we get you there?"
- Brandi thinks. She has done this before — the barre, the beam, the pool. Every new space starts impossible and becomes hers. But this time, she won't have Pandora's voice on stage with her. She will have to find her own way.
- Brandi: "Walk the stage with me. Let me feel the dimensions. Tell me where the marks are. I'll count."
- The Musical Director nods. "Let's do it now."
- Brandi unfolds her cane. She walks the stage — edge to edge, corner to corner. She counts her steps. She feels the lip of the stage, the wings on either side, the backdrop flat at the rear. She walks it twice, three times.
- Then the Musical Director puts tactile markers on the stage floor — the same kind of raised dots that are on the barre guide in the dance pavilion. She places them at Brandi's blocking positions: center stage, stage left, stage right, upstage, downstage. Brandi runs her foot over each one. She feels it through her shoe.
- Brandi: "I'll feel these."
- Musical Director: "That's the point. They'll be there for every rehearsal and every performance. Same spots. Same feel. You'll know where you are."
- The other campers watch. Some are curious, some are impressed, some are uncertain. The Musical Director does not make a speech about inclusivity. She just moves on to the next thing.
- Musical Director: "Everyone up. We're blocking scene one."

### Beat 4: Rehearsals — Learning the Show Without Pandora

- Over the next two weeks, Brandi learns the show. She learns it the way she learns everything — by repetition, by counting, by feel, by ear.
- She memorizes her lines by having cast members read them to her, over and over, until she knows them the way she knows the route to the dance pavilion — forty-two, eighteen, seven. The words live in her body.
- She learns the songs by listening to the rehearsal tracks and singing along. She does not need the sheet music. She cannot read the sheet music. She learns by ear, and her ear is precise.
- She learns the blocking by the tactile markers on the stage floor and by the sound of the other actors' voices. She knows where she is by where they are. Charlie Brown is almost always center stage or slightly off — he's the center of the show even when he's at the edges of the group.
- She learns her entrances by vocal cues — the Musical Director arranges for another actor to say a specific word or make a specific sound that signals Brandi's cue. It is seamless. The audience will never know.
- The hardest part is the group numbers — when all the characters are on stage moving together. Brandi has to stay in formation without seeing the formation. She solves this the way she solves everything: she finds a fixed point and maps from there. In group numbers, her fixed point is the actor playing Linus — he's always near Charlie Brown, and his voice is distinctive and clear.
- During rehearsals, there are stumbles. Brandi walks into a prop table once. She misses a mark twice. She steps on another camper's foot during a dance number.
- The other camper — a girl named Daisy — yelps. Then she says: "Sorry, I should have been in my spot."
- Brandi: "No, I should have felt the marker. I'll get it."
- Daisy: "You're really good at this, by the way. Charlie Brown, I mean. The sad-face thing you do."
- Brandi: "I'm not doing a sad face. This is just my face."
- Daisy laughs. "That's exactly what Charlie Brown would say."
- By the second week, Brandi no longer uses her cane on stage. She has mapped the space. She knows it the way she knows the barre, the beam, the pool. The stage is hers.

### Beat 5: Pandora Comes to Watch Rehearsal

- One afternoon, during a late rehearsal, Pandora slips into the back of the theater. She has finished her Gymnastics minor early and has twenty minutes before Intermediate Modern.
- She sits in the last row and watches. She has never watched Brandi from an audience before. She has always been beside her — on the bunk, on the beam, in the studio. Watching Brandi from a distance is strange. It is also the first time she sees what everyone else sees: an eight-year-old blind girl standing on a stage, hitting her marks, delivering her lines, completely sure of where she is.
- On stage, Brandi walks to center stage, feels the tactile marker under her shoe, and says a line. The other actors laugh on cue. Brandi waits, still and patient, for the next line. She is not counting out loud. She is not reaching for Pandora's hand. She is just... there. Completely there.
- Pandora feels something she can't quite name. It is not jealousy. It is not sadness. It is the feeling of watching someone you love do something you can't do with them. It is pride and distance mixed together.
- She leaves before the rehearsal ends. She doesn't want Brandi to know she came. Not because it's a secret. Because this is Brandi's thing, and Pandora wants it to stay Brandi's thing.

### Beat 6: The Rehearsal Where Brandi Breaks Through

- Midway through the rehearsal process, there is a moment. It is the scene where Charlie Brown sits alone and sings about how nobody likes him — the quiet, sad heart of the show.
- Brandi sits center stage. The lights are low for rehearsal. The other actors are off stage. She is alone.
- She starts the song. And something happens that has not happened before. She stops performing the song and starts living it.
- She does not think about the cancer. She does not think about the enucleation. She thinks about the first day of Session 2, when Sloan said she shouldn't be in Ballet, and Rilee said she shouldn't be in Yoga. She thinks about walking to the barre by herself, forty-two steps, eighteen, seven. She thinks about holding warrior two while someone told her she didn't belong.
- She sings the song to the empty theater. Her voice fills the space the way her body fills the barre — centered, controlled, completely alive.
- The Musical Director stands in the back of the house. She does not stop her. She does not take notes. She just listens.
- When the song ends, the theater is completely silent.
- Musical Director, quietly: "That's the take. That's the one we use."
- Brandi: "What did I do different?"
- Musical Director: "You stopped acting like Charlie Brown and started being him. You don't have to pretend to know what it feels like to be told you don't belong, Brandi. You already know."
- Brandi sits on the stage for a moment. Then she nods. She stands up. She finds her mark by feel.
- Brandi: "Can we do it again? I want to get it right every time."
- Musical Director: "It was right."
- Brandi: "Then I want it to be right every time. That's different."

### Beat 7: Tech Week

- Tech week. The stage is dressed with the set — Charlie Brown's house, the baseball diamond, the psychiatric help booth, the doghouse. Lights are focused. Sound is balanced. The stage is no longer a rehearsal room — it is a world.
- Brandi walks the set the way she walked the stage on the first day — slowly, counting, feeling every edge. She touches the doghouse where the actor playing Snoopy sits. She feels the psychiatric help booth. She runs her hand along the prop mailbox.
- The tactile markers are still on the floor, painted over now so the audience can't see them but Brandi can still feel them through her shoes. She steps on each one. Center. Left. Right. Up. Down.
- She is ready.
- The actor playing Snoopy — a girl named Wren, from Birch Cabin — bounds up next to her in full dog ears and collar: "Brandi, can you feel my costume? The ears are so soft."
- Brandi reaches out and feels the fuzzy ears. "You sound like a real dog."
- Wren: "I've been practicing my bark. Want to hear it?"
- Brandi: "Sure."
- Wren barks. Loudly. In the middle of the tech run.
- The Musical Director, from the house: "WREN."
- Wren: "Was that too much?"
- Brandi, laughing: "No. That was perfect."

### Beat 8: Opening Night

- The theater is full. Families, campers, counselors, staff — two hundred people in the seats. The house lights dim. The audience quiets.
- Backstage, Brandi stands in the wings. She cannot see the audience. She can hear them — the murmur, the coughs, the rustle of programs. She can smell the theater — paint, dust, the faint vanilla of the lobby candles. She can feel the stage under her feet, the tactile marker at the entrance cue, the familiar terrain she has walked a hundred times.
- She is alone in the wings. Not lonely — alone. This is the first big thing she has done at camp without Pandora beside her. She takes a breath. The same breath she takes at the barre. The same breath she takes on the beam. In through the nose. Out through the mouth.
- She hears Pandora's voice from somewhere in the house — a loud, unmistakable whoop. Brandi smiles.
- Brandi enters on her cue — a vocal signal from the actor playing Linus. She walks to center stage, feels the tactile marker under her shoe, and stands in the spotlight she cannot see but can feel on her skin, warm and close.
- She delivers her first line. The audience laughs.
- She delivers her second line. The audience laughs again.
- She is Charlie Brown. She is an eight-year-old blind girl standing on a stage in front of two hundred people without her twin, and she is Charlie Brown, and there is no difference between those two things.

### Beat 9: The Performance — The Key Moments

- The show runs. Brandi hits every mark. She does not miss a cue. She does not stumble. The tactile markers guide her feet. The actors' voices guide her ears. The music guides her heart.
- In the group numbers, she dances — not the way she dances in Ballet, but the way the choreography requires: simple, joyful, imperfect, human. She spins once and catches herself on the edge of the psychiatric help booth. It looks like choreography. It is instinct.
- Wren as Snoopy does a back flip off the doghouse. The audience erupts. Wren grins and barks.
- Then the quiet moment. Charlie Brown alone. The song.
- Brandi sits center stage. The spotlight is on her. She cannot see the two hundred people in the dark, but she can feel them — the held breath, the absolute silence, the attention of an entire room focused on one small girl who cannot see them looking at her.
- She sings. The same way she sang it in rehearsal — not performing, but living. The song about being told you don't belong. About striking out and stepping up anyway. About trying and failing and trying again.
- She does not think about Sloan. She does not think about Rilee. She does not think about the Hayashis or the camp director or any of it. She thinks about the feeling — the universal, human, Charlie Brown feeling — and she sings it out into the dark.
- When she finishes, there is a beat of silence. Then applause. Not the polite kind. The kind that comes from two hundred people who were not expecting to feel something this real at a summer camp musical.

### Beat 10: After the Show

- The cast takes their bows. Brandi bows center stage, exactly on her mark. Wren bounds out next to her, grabs her hand, and raises both their arms together. The audience cheers.
- Backstage, the cast is hugging, crying, laughing. The chaos of opening night. Brandi stands in the middle of it, unable to see the celebration but surrounded by it — voices, laughter, the rustle of costumes, the thump of the curtain falling.
- Then she hears the voice she knows better than any other. Pandora has pushed through the backstage crowd.
- Pandora: "You were amazing."
- Brandi: "You came backstage?"
- Pandora: "I told you I'd be in the front row. I was in the front row. I was the one who whooped."
- Brandi: "I know. I heard you. I always hear you."
- A group of campers surrounds them. Daisy, who plays Lucy, says: "Brandi, you made me cry. I'm supposed to be the mean one and you made me cry."
- Brandi: "That's what Charlie Brown does. He makes the mean ones feel something."
- Daisy: "Is that what you do too?"
- Brandi thinks about it. "I don't know. I just show up."
- Musical Director, passing by, overhears: "That's the whole job, Brandi. Just show up."
- Wren, still in her Snoopy ears, throws her arms around both twins: "We did it! We did the show!"
- Pandora, catching Wren's enthusiasm: "You were a really good dog."
- Wren: "I practiced my bark for two weeks."
- Pandora: "It showed."

### Beat 11: Sloan and Rilee in the Audience

- Sloan and Rilee are in the audience. They are campers. They have to come to the show — it's a camp event.
- Sloan watches Brandi on stage. She watches the blind girl — the one she said shouldn't be in Ballet — walk to center stage, hit her mark, deliver her lines, sing a song that makes two hundred people go quiet, and never once look lost.
- She watches Brandi do it without her sister. She has noticed that Pandora is not in the show — the loud twin is sitting in the front row, not on stage. Brandi is out there by herself.
- Rilee leans over during the quiet song and whispers: "She's really good."
- Sloan does not answer. But she does not disagree.
- After the show, Sloan and Rilee walk back to the cabin. They are quiet for a long time.
- Sloan, finally: "She learned the whole show without seeing it. Without her sister."
- Rilee: "She learned Ballet without seeing it too. Without her sister."
- Sloan: "And Pointe. And Yoga. And the beam."
- Rilee: "Maybe she's not the one who doesn't belong."
- Sloan does not say anything. But she does not argue.
