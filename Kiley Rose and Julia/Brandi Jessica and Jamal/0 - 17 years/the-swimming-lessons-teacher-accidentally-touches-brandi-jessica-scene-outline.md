# The Swimming Lessons Teacher Accidentally Touches Brandi Jessica — Session 3 Scene Outline

## SCENE INFORMATION

- **Characters:** Brandi Jessica Key (8), Swimming Lessons teacher (male), other campers in Swimming Lessons (including some from Birch Cabin)
- **Onstage animal:** None (Burden does not arrive until age 14)
- **Locations:** Swimming pool — French Woods (Hancock, New York)
- **When:** Session 3, during Brandi's second minor period (Swimming Lessons, before lunch). After Brandi tells the Ballet and Yoga teachers about Andrew.
- **Status:** New scene outline
- **Tone:** Shock, violation of safety, the body remembering what the mind tries to carry, the pool as sanctuary broken for one moment

## SCENE PURPOSE

This scene captures the moment the Swimming Lessons teacher accidentally touches Brandi Jessica in the pool during her second minor period — and Brandi's body reacts before her mind can stop it. The touch is innocent. The reaction is not. Brandi flinches so hard she goes under and cannot find the surface — she is drowning. The teacher has to jump in and save her. His hands are on her again, but this time they are saving her life. This is the first time Brandi shows an involuntary physical response to unexpected contact, and it reveals that what Andrew did to her lives in her body, not just in her memory.

It establishes:
- That Brandi is completely at ease in the swimming pool — it has been her freest space at camp. She needs no cane, no count, no guide. The water holds her. The water is safe.
- That the Swimming Lessons teacher accidentally touches Brandi while correcting her stroke — guiding her arm, the kind of physical instruction that happens in every swim class. The touch is routine. What it triggers in Brandi is not.
- That Brandi's reaction is immediate, involuntary, and physical — she flinches hard, pulls away, goes under the water. But this time she does not resurface. She is drowning.
- That the teacher has to save her — he jumps in, grabs her, pulls her to the surface, holds her against him. His hands are on her body again, the very thing that triggered her, but now they are keeping her alive. The paradox is devastating: the man's touch that sent her under is the same man's touch that pulls her out.
- That the pool, which has been her sanctuary, is suddenly the place where she almost died — because safety is not just about the place, it's about what the body expects.
- That the Swimming Lessons teacher already knows everything about Brandi's history (from the pre-summer briefing) and immediately understands what just happened.
- That afterward, Brandi sits on the edge of the pool and cannot go back in. The water, which has never betrayed her before, has been contaminated by her own body's memory.
- That this scene takes place after Brandi tells the Ballet and Yoga teachers about Andrew — she has already said it out loud to them. But saying it out loud does not make her body stop reacting. The trauma lives in her muscles, not just in her words.

## BEATS

### Beat 1: The Pool — Before

- It is a warm morning during Session 3. Swimming Lessons is Brandi's second minor today — she chose it for the Minor 2 slot, before lunch. She has already been to Major 1 (Songwriting) and Minor 1. Now she is at the pool. The water is bright and chlorinated. Lane lines divide the water. The painted lines on the bottom are invisible to Brandi, but she doesn't need them.
- Brandi is in the water. She has always been free here. No cane. No count. No voice describing where things are. The water tells her everything — where the walls are, where the other swimmers are, where her body ends and the water begins.
- She is working on her backstroke. Her stroke is clean. Her breathing is steady. She moves through the water the way she moves through nothing else — without thinking, without counting, without fear.
- The Swimming Lessons teacher is on the pool deck, watching the class. He is a patient, experienced instructor. He knows about Brandi — he was briefed before the summer, like all the teachers. He knows about the Retinoblastoma, the enucleation, the chemo, the radiotherapy. He knows about Andrew — that the man who kidnapped and assaulted her was male. He has never brought any of it up. He has just taught Brandi to swim, and Brandi has just learned.

### Beat 2: The Correction — The Accidental Touch

- He walks along the pool deck, observing each swimmer. He notices Brandi's elbow is dropping on her backstroke — a small technical issue that happens to every swimmer.
- He kneels at the edge of the pool. He reaches out to gently guide Brandi's arm into the correct position — the way swimming teachers do a hundred times a day. A hand on the forearm. A light adjustment. "Elbow up, Brandi. Right here."
- His hand touches Brandi's forearm.
- It is an accident. Not the touch — the touch is intentional, the kind of physical instruction that happens in every swim class. What is accidental is what it triggers. His hand on her forearm, his voice close, a man reaching toward her in the water — something in Brandi Jessica's small mind snaps sideways.
- That is all it takes.

### Beat 3: The Reaction — Drowning

- Brandi flinches. Not a small startle — a full-body flinch, violent and immediate, the kind of movement that comes from a place deeper than thought. Her arm jerks. Her body twists. Her stroke breaks apart.
- She goes under. The water closes over her head.
- She is somewhere else entirely. She is seven years old. She is in a hotel room. A man's hand is on her and she did not ask for it and she cannot see and she cannot get away and —
- Her body is not swimming. Her body is fighting. Her arms are pushing, flailing, defending against something that is not in the pool. She is not treading water. She is not finding the surface. She is thrashing, and every movement is wrong for staying afloat — she is fighting the water the way you fight a person, and the water does not yield the way a person yields, and she is sinking.
- She inhales water. She chokes. Her lungs seize. She goes under again, deeper this time, and she cannot tell which way is up because she cannot see and her body does not know where the surface is and she is drowning.
- Above the water, the other campers are still swimming. A few notice — a girl is splashing too much, splashing wrong — but they don't understand yet. The teacher sees it immediately. He has been watching Brandi all summer. He knows her stroke, her rhythm, her breath. What he sees is not swimming. What he sees is a child going under.

### Beat 4: The Rescue

- The teacher does not hesitate. He blows his whistle — two sharp blasts — and shouts to the assistant instructor on the far side of the pool: "Clear the pool! Everyone out! Now!"
- The assistant instructor starts pulling campers out of the water. The children climb out, confused, a few scared. They are moved away from the pool deck, toward the benches on the far side, away from where Brandi went under.
- The teacher jumps in. He is already in his swim clothes — he is a swimming teacher, this is what he wears. He hits the water and he is at Brandi in two strokes.
- He reaches for her. His hands find her — one arm around her chest, the other supporting her head — the standard rescue hold. He pulls her to the surface.
- Brandi feels his arms around her. A man's arms. A man's hands on her body. She is still somewhere else — she is seven, she is in the dark, she is being held by someone she cannot see — and she fights him. She thrashes against the rescue hold. She pushes against his chest. She is trying to get away from the very person who is keeping her alive.
- Teacher, firm but calm, right next to her ear: "Brandi. It's me. It's your swimming teacher. I've got you. You're safe. I'm not going to let you go."
- She is coughing, choking, fighting. Water comes out of her mouth and nose. Her lungs are burning. His arms are around her. She cannot see him. She cannot see anything.
- Teacher: "You're in the pool. You're at French Woods. You were drowning. I'm bringing you to the edge. I'm not going to let you go until you're out of the water. Do you hear me?"
- She hears him. His voice is in her ear. It is his voice — the voice that has been teaching her strokes and counting her laps and saying "good job, Brandi" all summer. It is not Andrew's voice. It is a different man.
- Her body does not care. Her body is still fighting. But something in her mind — the part that counts steps, the part that maps rooms, the part that knows where she is even when she can't see — that part is listening.
- He swims her to the edge of the pool, one arm around her, the other holding the wall. He lifts her out of the water and onto the deck. She is gasping, shaking, dripping. He climbs out beside her.
- He does not hold her once she is on the deck. He lets go the moment she is out of the water. He sits a few feet away, water dripping off his swim clothes, breathing hard himself, and gives her space.
- The pool is empty now. The other campers are on the far side, sitting on benches, being supervised by the assistant instructor. A few are crying. Most are just confused. They saw Brandi go under. They saw her fighting the teacher. They don't understand what happened.
- The teacher holds up a hand to the assistant instructor: "Keep them over there. Give us a minute."
- The pool is silent. Just Brandi and the teacher on the wet deck, and the sound of water settling back to stillness.

### Beat 5: After — On the Empty Pool Deck

- The assistant instructor leads the other campers out of the pool area entirely — back toward the locker rooms, away from the deck. The pool is completely empty now. Just Brandi and the teacher.
- Brandi is sitting with her knees pulled up, the towel around her shoulders. She is still shaking. She can feel the water in her lungs. She can feel where his arms were around her chest. She can feel the brush of his hand on her shoulder that started all of it.
- The teacher sits on the deck a few feet away. He is in his swim trunks — no shirt, no shoes. The water is still dripping off him. He hasn't moved to dry off. He is just sitting there, giving her space.
- A long silence.
- Teacher: "You almost died, Brandi."
- Brandi doesn't say anything.
- Teacher: "I brushed your shoulder by accident. You flinched so hard you lost your stroke. You went under and you couldn't find the surface. You were drowning. I had to jump in and pull you out. You fought me — you fought the rescue — because you didn't know it was me. You thought it was him."
- Brandi is very still.
- Teacher: "I'm not saying this to scare you. I'm saying it because you need to know what happened. And you need to know that what happened in your body is not your fault. Your body was trying to protect you. But your body was protecting you from something that happened in the past, not something that was happening in the pool. And that difference almost killed you."
- Brandi's voice is small: "I know."
- Teacher: "Do you? Because you were drowning, Brandi. You were at the bottom of the pool, and you couldn't find the surface, and you couldn't see which way was up. You have been the safest swimmer in this class all summer. And today you almost drowned because a man accidentally touched your shoulder."
- The words land hard. Brandi flinches again — just her shoulders this time, a small tightening. But she doesn't go under. She is on dry land. She is on the pool deck. She is here.
- Brandi: "I couldn't tell which way was up. I couldn't see the surface. I never can't find the surface. The water always tells me. But this time it didn't."
- Teacher: "Because your body wasn't listening to the water anymore. Your body was listening to something else. Something that happened to you a long time ago. And your body was so busy protecting you from that that it forgot to keep you alive in the pool."
- A pause.
- Brandi: "The pool was the one place I didn't have to think about it."
- Teacher: "I know."
- Brandi: "And now I'm thinking about it. And I almost died."
- Teacher: "Yes."
- He does not soften this. He does not say "you're fine" or "it's okay." Because it is not okay. She almost died. And she needs to hear that from someone who will not lie to her.
- Teacher: "You almost died. And I had to grab you. I had to put my arms around you and hold you against me and pull you out of the water. I know that was the last thing your body wanted. I know that a man's arms around you in the water was the worst possible thing you could feel at that moment. And I'm sorry. But I wasn't going to let you drown. I'd rather you be alive and fighting me than drowning quietly."
- Brandi's eyes are wet. She is not crying. She is just very, very full.
- Brandi: "You saved me."
- Teacher: "Yes."
- Brandi: "And I fought you."
- Teacher: "Yes. Because your body didn't know I was saving you. It thought I was hurting you. That's what trauma does. It makes rescue feel like danger."
- A long silence. The pool is empty now. The water is still. The sun is lower.
- Brandi: "Will it always be like this?"
- Teacher: "No. But it will be like this for a while. Your body has to learn that not every man's touch is the same. And that takes time. And it takes feeling safe again. And I'm going to help you with that."
- Brandi: "How?"
- Teacher: "From now on, I will tell you before I come near you in the pool. Every time. Even if I'm just walking past your lane. You'll hear my voice before you feel my presence. And if I ever need to touch you — to correct a stroke, to hand you something, to help you — I will tell you first. Every time. And I want you to know — I understand that it matters that I'm a man. You don't have to say anything about that. I just want you to know that I understand, and it won't change how I teach you. It'll only change how I approach you."
- Brandi: "What if I flinch again?"
- Teacher: "Then you flinch again. And I'll be ready for it. And I'll talk to you through it. And you will not drown in my pool, Brandi Jessica. I will not let that happen."
- Brandi: "Can I come back tomorrow?"
- Teacher: "Yes. But tomorrow we're going to practice something new. We're going to practice what happens when I say your name before I come near you. So your mind knows before your body has to react. We're going to give your mind a head start."
- Brandi nods.
- Teacher: "And if you ever don't want to be in the water — for any reason, any day — you sit out. No questions. No explanations needed. The pool will be here when you're ready."
- Brandi: "Okay."
- A pause. The teacher takes a breath.
- Teacher: "Brandi, I have to tell you something. You almost drowned in my class. I have to fill out an accident report. It's camp policy. Any time a camper needs to be rescued from the water, there's a report."
- Brandi goes still.
- Brandi: "An accident report?"
- Teacher: "Yes. It documents what happened, what I did, what the outcome was. It goes to the camp director and it goes in your file."
- Brandi: "Who sees it?"
- Teacher: "The camp director. The head of aquatics. And your parents. I am required to call your mothers, Brandi. I have to tell them that you were rescued from the pool today."
- Brandi's face changes. She is not just shaken anymore — she is afraid.
- Brandi: "You have to call them?"
- Teacher: "Yes. It's not optional. A child nearly drowned in my class. Your mothers have to know."
- Brandi: "But — they'll — they'll —"
- She doesn't finish the sentence. She doesn't know how to finish it. They'll what? They'll pull her out of camp? They'll never let her swim again? They'll know that something is wrong with her that has nothing to do with swimming?
- Teacher: "I will tell them that you got water in your lungs during a stroke correction and I pulled you out. That is what happened. I will not tell them why you flinched. That is yours to tell or not tell. But they will know that you were in distress in the pool and that I rescued you."
- Brandi: "They're going to be so scared."
- Teacher: "They're your mothers. Of course they're going to be scared. But they're also going to be glad you're alive. And they're going to trust that I'm taking care of you, because I am."
- Brandi is quiet for a long time.
- Brandi: "Will they make me stop swimming?"
- Teacher: "I don't know. That's their decision. But I'll tell them that you want to come back tomorrow. And I'll tell them what I'm going to do differently — saying your name before I come near you, giving you a head start. I'll tell them you're safe in this pool and that I will not let anything happen to you."
- Brandi nods slowly. She is not okay. But she hears him.
- Teacher: "I'm going to make that call now. The assistant instructor will stay with you until I come back. You can sit here as long as you need."
- He stands up. He puts his hand on the edge of the pool — not near her, just steadying himself — and then walks toward the camp office, his footsteps squelching on the wet deck.
- Brandi sits on the edge of the empty pool. Her feet are in the water. Her towel is around her shoulders. Her cane is beside her. She listens to his footsteps fade and the water settle and the silence of a pool with no one in it.
- She almost died here. And her mothers are about to find out.
- The teacher comes back from making the call. He tells Brandi that her mothers know and that they want her to stay. He tells her to go to lunch, to go to her classes, to keep her day going. "The worst thing you can do right now is sit here," he says. "Go be with your cabin. Go do what you came here to do." Brandi nods. She picks up her cane. She goes to lunch. She goes to her afternoon classes — Major 2, Minor 3, Major 3. She is shaking and she is present. She counts her steps and she finds her places and she does not talk about what happened. She just keeps moving.

### Beat 6: That Night — Brandi and the Water

- That night, in the dark of Birch Cabin, Brandi lies awake.
- She can still feel his arms around her chest. The rescue hold. The man's arms that pulled her out of the water. She fought them. She fought the person who was saving her life. Because her body could not tell the difference between a man who was drowning her and a man who was keeping her alive.
- She almost died. That is what he said. You almost died. He did not soften it. He did not lie. And she is grateful for that, in the part of her that counts steps and maps rooms and needs the truth. But in the part of her that is eight years old and blind and in a dark bunk at camp, she is terrified.
- The pool was the one place. The one place where she was free. No cane. No count. No voice in her ear telling her where things were. Just the water and her body and the certainty that the water would hold her. And today the water did not hold her. The water let her sink. Because her body was somewhere else.
- She does not tell Pandora. She does not tell anyone. She lies still and breathes the way Kiley Rose and Julia taught her during Partner Yoga — in through the nose, out through the mouth, the breath is the only thing you control.
- She thinks about what the teacher said: "Trauma makes rescue feel like danger."
- She thinks about his arms around her. She thinks about how she pushed against his chest. She thinks about how he held on anyway. She thinks about how he said "I'm not going to let you go" and he meant it, and she was fighting him, and he did not let go.
- A man held her while she fought him, and he did not let her drown. That is not what Andrew did. That is the opposite of what Andrew did.
- She thinks about the phone call. The teacher called her mothers. Mama and Julia know she almost drowned. They don't know why. They just know their daughter went under in the pool and the teacher had to jump in and pull her out. They are probably terrified.
- She told the Ballet teacher. She told the Yoga teacher. She said Andrew's name out loud. She said what he did. And it did not stop this. Her body did not care that she had said the words. Her body still went under.
- Disclosure is not a cure. She knows that now. It is a step. But the body has its own timeline, and the body is not done yet.
- She breathes. In through the nose. Out through the mouth.
- Tomorrow she will go back to the pool. The teacher will say her name before he comes near her. She will practice hearing him before she feels him. The water will still be there.
- But she will know, now, that free is not the same as safe. And that saying it out loud did not make her body stop remembering. And that if she is going to survive this — not just the cancer, not just the blindness, but this — her body is going to need its own kind of rescue. One that does not come from words.
- Not tonight. But she will keep coming back to the pool. And the teacher will keep saying her name before he comes near her. And maybe, one day, her body will believe that a man's hands can hold her without hurting her.

### Beat 7: The Next Morning — Maya and Pandora

- It is the morning after the drowning. Before breakfast. The cabin is stirring — girls pulling on clothes, brushing hair, finding shoes. Brandi is sitting on her bunk, already dressed, her cane across her lap. She did not sleep much. Her face is tight.
- Maya, the Birch Cabin counselor, sits down on the edge of Brandi's lower bunk. She does not sit too close. She sits the way you sit next to someone who almost died yesterday — carefully, quietly, with your whole attention.
- The other girls are moving around them. Sloan and Rilee are getting dressed. Pandora is in the cabin bathroom — every cabin at French Woods has its own bathroom. The cabin is full of the ordinary noise of morning.
- Maya: "Brandi Jessica, can we talk?"
- Brandi: "About yesterday?"
- Maya: "About yesterday."
- Brandi nods.
- Maya: "Your swimming teacher called me around ten o'clock last night. He told me what happened in the pool."
- Brandi is very still.
- Maya: "He told me you got water in your lungs during a stroke correction and he had to pull you out. He told me you're okay. He told me you want to keep swimming."
- Brandi: "He told my mommies too."
- Maya: "I know. He called them as well. Camp policy. Your mothers know you're safe. They know you want to come back to the pool."
- Brandi: "Are they coming to get me?"
- Maya: "No. They called the camp director this morning. They want you to stay. They trust the camp to take care of you."
- Something in Brandi's chest loosens. Just a little.
- The bathroom door opens. Pandora walks back into the cabin, towel in hand, and stops when she sees Maya sitting with Brandi on the bunk. She can tell something is wrong — she has always been able to read Brandi's silence better than anyone.
- Pandora: "What happened?"
- Maya looks at Brandi. This is Brandi's to tell.
- Brandi: "I almost drowned yesterday. In the pool. During Swimming Lessons."
- Pandora's face changes. She moves fast — she is across the cabin in two steps, and then her arms are around Brandi, holding her tight, her face pressed against Brandi's shoulder. Brandi can feel Pandora's breath, Pandora's heartbeat, the way Pandora's arms lock around her like she will never let go.
- Pandora: "You almost — you almost —"
- She can't finish. She just holds on.
- Brandi holds her back. Her hands find Pandora's back, and she holds on too, and for a moment neither of them says anything, because they don't need to. They are identical twins. They have shared every room, every meal, every class, every night since they were born. Brandi almost died yesterday, and Pandora didn't know, and now she knows, and her body is doing what Brandi's body did in the pool — trying to protect something that cannot be protected by holding on.
- After a long moment, Pandora pulls back just enough to take Brandi's hands. She looks at Brandi's face — though Brandi cannot see her looking.
- Pandora: "Are you okay?"
- Brandi: "I'm okay."
- Pandora: "You're not okay. You're sitting on your bunk instead of going to breakfast."
- Maya watches them. She does not interrupt. This is the twin bond — the one that doesn't need a counselor.
- Pandora sits down on the bunk beside Brandi. She does not let go of her hand.
- Maya: "Brandi, I need to talk to you about what happened. Because you almost drowned, and that is a very big thing. And I need to make sure you're okay — not just physically, but in every way."

- Maya: "Are you? Because you didn't sleep last night. And you're sitting on your bunk instead of going to breakfast with everyone. And when I walked past your bed at two in the morning, you were still breathing the way you breathe when you're trying very hard not to cry."
- Brandi does not say anything for a moment.
- Brandi: "You were watching me?"
- Maya: "I was checking on you. That's my job. You almost died in the pool yesterday, Brandi. Of course I was checking on you."
- A pause.
- Maya: "You don't have to tell me anything you don't want to tell me. But I want you to know that I'm here. And I already know about your history — the camp briefed all of us before the summer. So if something happened yesterday that was about more than a stroke correction, you can tell me. And if it wasn't, you can tell me that too."
- Brandi is quiet for a long time. The cabin noise continues around them — zippers, footfalls, someone asking where the toothpaste is. Pandora's hand is still in hers.
- Brandi: "I flinched. When he touched my arm. I flinched and I went under and I couldn't find the surface. And then he jumped in and he held me and I fought him because —"
- She stops.
- Maya does not push. She waits.
- Brandi: "Because I couldn't tell the difference between someone saving me and someone —"
- She can't finish. Her hands are gripping her cane. Pandora's hand tightens around hers.
- Brandi takes a breath. In through the nose. Out through the mouth. The way Kiley Rose and Julia taught her during Partner Yoga.
- Brandi: "When I was seven, a man named Andrew kidnapped me. It was the day after Christmas. He took me from my house during the party. He beat me. And he sexually assaulted me. And I couldn't see him and I couldn't get away."

She was seven too, waiting at home, terrified that Brandi was never coming back. But hearing Brandi say it out loud, here, in the cabin, to Maya — that is different. Brandi is naming it. Brandi is choosing to say it. And Pandora's grip on her hand says: I am here. I have always been here.

She has known for as long as Brandi has. And she is holding Brandi like she can shield her from something that already happened, because that is the only thing her body knows how to do.

- Pandora: "You're telling her."
- Brandi: "I'm telling her."
- Pandora nods. She does not need to ask why. She knows what happened yesterday in the pool. She knows why Brandi needs Maya to understand.

- Brandi: "That's what the swimming teacher said too."
- Maya: "The swimming teacher?"
- Brandi: "Yes. He said trauma makes rescue feel like danger."

- Maya: "He's right. And I want you to know something. You're not the only person at this camp who has felt that way. You're not the only person who has flinched when someone reached out. It is an ordinary morning at French Woods, except that Brandi Jessica almost died yesterday, and this morning she told her counselor and her twin sister about the worst thing that ever happened to her, and she is walking to the dining hall holding both their hands, and that is also a kind of brave.

### Beat 8: The Next Day — Swimming Lessons (Minor 3) — Brandi Tells the Swimming Teacher About Andrew

- It is the day after the drowning. Brandi went to her morning classes. She had breakfast with the cabin. She held Pandora's hand under the table. She went to Major 1 (Songwriting), Minor 1, Minor 2. She did what she always does. She counted her steps. She found her places. She was present.
- She went to lunch at the cafeteria with her cabin. Birch Cabin's table. Pandora sat beside her. The food was ordinary — a sandwich, fruit, milk. Brandi ate because she was hungry, and because Maya said the cabin eats together, and because she is part of this cabin even on the hard days.
- After lunch, she went to Major 2 (Charlie Brown). She said her lines. She hit her marks. Daisy played Lucy. Wren played Snoopy. The director blocked the next scene. Brandi was there, standing where she was supposed to stand, saying what she was supposed to say, and part of her mind was in the pool. But she did it. She got through it.
- Now it is time for Minor 3. She chose Swimming Lessons for this slot — deliberately, specifically — because she needs to be in the water again. Not to prove anything. Because the teacher said she could come back, and she is going to come back.

- Brandi: "Without any single doubt."

- Brandi: "I should go. I have my third major."
- Teacher: "What's your third major?"
- Brandi: "Painting and Drawing."
- Teacher: "Then go paint something."
- Brandi: "What should I paint?"
- Teacher: "The pool."
- Brandi: "I've never seen it."
- Teacher: "That's what makes it interesting."
- Brandi almost laughs. It comes out small and broken and real — the first time she has laughed since yesterday. She picks up her cane and her towel and walks toward the painting studio, counting her steps.
- She arrives at Painting and Drawing. She sits at an easel. The teacher sets up her canvas and describes the colors on the palette. Brandi dips her brush.
- The teacher asks what she wants to paint.
- Brandi: "A swimming pool."
- She paints what she knows: the feel of the water, the sound of the lane lines, the echo of the walls, the way the surface catches the light she can no longer see but still remembers. She paints it from the inside.

## RELATION TO CONTINUITY

- This scene takes place during **Session 3**, during Brandi's second minor period (Swimming Lessons, before lunch), in the swimming pool.
- This scene happens **after** Brandi tells the Ballet and Yoga teachers about Andrew (seven days into Session 3). Brandi has already said it out loud to them. But telling the teachers did not make her body stop reacting — the trigger in the pool shows that the trauma lives in her physically, not just in what she chooses to remember or say.
- **The next day, Brandi tells the Swimming Lessons teacher about Andrew.** He is the first man she has said it to out loud — the full truth: kidnapping, beating, and sexual assault. This is significant because Andrew was a man, and the Swimming teacher is a man, and Brandi is choosing to say the words to the very person whose gender triggers her body's fear response. She tells him because he held her while she fought him and he did not let her drown, and she needs him to know why she fought him.
- The **Swimming Lessons teacher is a man**. This is critical because Andrew — the man who kidnapped, beat, and sexually assaulted Brandi — was also a man. A male teacher's accidental touch on Brandi's shoulder triggers a response that a female teacher's touch might not have. And when the teacher has to rescue Brandi by wrapping his arms around her, the gender dimension makes the rescue itself feel like the assault to Brandi's body. The teacher addresses this directly: "I understand that it matters that I'm a man."
- Brandi **actually drowns** — she goes under and cannot find the surface. Her body is fighting, not swimming. The teacher jumps in fully clothed and rescues her. Brandi fights the rescue. This is the paradox at the center of the scene: the man's touch that sent her under is the same man's touch that pulled her out.
- The pool has been Brandi's freest space at camp — consistent with established scenes. In the wants-to-be-independent scene, Brandi moves freely in the water, reading pressure and sound. In the first-day scene, she races Pandora and wins. In the Visiting Day scenes, she swims for her family. This scene shatters that freedom temporarily.
- The teacher's response — jumping in to save her, then sitting on the deck giving her space, speaking the truth without softening — is consistent with how trained professionals handle trauma responses. He does not coddle, does not make a scene, does not ask Brandi to explain. He also acknowledges the gender dimension directly, which is honest and necessary.
- The teacher's line — "Trauma makes rescue feel like danger" — is the key insight of the scene. His acknowledgment that his gender matters — "I understand that it matters that I'm a man" — is honest, direct, and does not ask Brandi to respond. His promise — "You will not drown in my pool, Brandi Jessica. I will not let that happen" — is not sentimental. It is a fact, the same way Mike Key delivers facts. This is consistent with the family philosophy: not "there are no no's," but "there are no no's, only hows." The how here is: the teacher will change how he approaches Brandi, and Brandi's body will learn to trust again.
- Brandi does **not** tell Pandora about this. She does not tell anyone at camp about the trigger. But the teacher is required to call Kiley Rose and Julia — camp policy mandates an accident report and parent notification any time a camper is rescued from the water. Kiley Rose and Julia **will find out** that Brandi nearly drowned.
- The teacher tells Kiley Rose and Julia that Brandi got water in her lungs during a stroke correction and he pulled her out. He does **not** tell them why she flinched — that is Brandi's to disclose or not. But Kiley Rose and Julia will know their daughter almost drowned in the pool, and they will be terrified.
- The scene connects to the Community Service scene (Brandi told peers about Retinoblastoma but not Andrew), the Ballet/Yoga teacher scene (Brandi tells teachers about Andrew), the Maya/Pandora disclosure in Beat 7, and the broader arc of Brandi learning to disclose on her own terms.

When Brandi tells Maya, Pandora is hearing it said out loud to someone new, not learning it for the first time.
