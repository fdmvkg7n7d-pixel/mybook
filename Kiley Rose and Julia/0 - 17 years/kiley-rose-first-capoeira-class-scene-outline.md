# Kiley Rose's First Capoeira Class — Scene Outline

> **Scope:** A scene in which young Kiley Rose attends her first Capoeira class. She is small, watchful, and not yet sure what this is — but something in the music and the movement reaches her before her mind has words for it.
>
> **Time:** Kiley Rose is approximately **six to eight years old** (~2034–2036). She is already in gymnastics and swimming. Capoeira is Mike and Terri Anne's answer to a question Kiley Rose has not yet learned to ask: *what does a body like mine get to do?*
> **Place:** A Capoeira academy — a wide, open studio with wooden floors, a high ceiling, and instruments along one wall: a berimbau, a pandeiro, an atabaque. It smells like wood and effort.
> **Characters present:** Kiley Rose, Mike Key (drops her off and stays to watch), Mestre or instructor, other children in the class.
> **Mood:** Uncertain → curious → quietly lit up.

---

## 1. Before the Class — Arriving

- **The drive:** Mike brings Kiley Rose to her first class. She is quiet in the passenger seat in the way she gets quiet when she is paying attention to something she has not named yet.
- **What she knows:** She has been told it is a Brazilian martial art. That it involves music. That it looks like dancing and fighting at the same time. None of these descriptions have fully landed yet.
- **What she carries in:** Her gymnastics background — a body already trained to be precise, to know where her limbs are, to hold a line. She does not know yet that this will matter.

### The Question — Outside the Door

- **The moment:** They are standing outside the studio door. Through the small window in the door, Kiley Rose can see the other children already inside — bigger than her, most of them. She can hear the faint knock of the atabaque drum. She does not move to open the door.
- **The question:** She looks up at Mike and asks, quietly and entirely seriously:

  *"Daddy, do you think I can do this?"*

- **What she means on the surface:** Capoeira. The class. The other children. Whether a body like hers — small, monitored, held to careful limits by doctors — can do what the discipline asks.
- **What she means underneath:** Whether she is enough. Whether her body, which has always been the subject of careful management and cautious language, is capable of something that looks like power. She has never asked this question out loud before. It has lived in her quietly, below the gymnastics and the swimming and the appointments. It comes out now because she is standing at a door she chose to walk toward, and she needs to know before she walks through it.
- **Mike hears both questions.** He does not answer the surface one and leave the deeper one alone. He crouches — the same way he will crouch on a beach years later when strangers say the wrong thing — so he is level with her. Eye to eye.

  He does not answer immediately. He looks at her the way he looks at things he wants to get exactly right.

  Then:

  *"I think you've been doing hard things your whole life. I think you just don't always call them that."*

  A beat.

  *"And I think the people who made Capoeira were told their bodies couldn't do things too. And they did them anyway. In front of everyone."*

  He holds her gaze.

  *"So yeah, bug. I think you can do this."*

- **Kiley Rose's response:** She does not say anything. She turns back to the door. She pushes it open and walks in.
- **Mike, watching her go:** He straightens up. He does not follow immediately. He stands at the door for one extra second, watching her cross the studio floor toward the other children, her chin up, her steps even.

  Then he follows and takes his place along the wall.

---

- **The studio:** Wide wooden floors. High ceiling with light coming through tall windows. Instruments along one wall — a berimbau (a single-string bow with a gourd resonator), a pandeiro (a tambourine), and an atabaque (a tall hand drum). A few other children are already there, some stretching, some watching the door.
- **Kiley's first impression:** It is louder than she expected, even before the music begins. The room feels alive in a way gymnastics studios do not.
- **Mike:** He settles along the wall with the other parents. He does not hover. He watches her face.

---

## 2. The Mestre (Instructor)

- **Who they are:** The Mestre is experienced, warm, and economical with words. They run the class with a kind of relaxed authority — no shouting, no drill-sergeant energy, but complete command of the room.
- **First address to the children:** The Mestre gathers the new students. They explain, briefly and without condescension, what Capoeira is:

  *"It was created by enslaved people in Brazil who were not allowed to fight. So they hid the fight inside a dance. They put the weapons inside the music. That is what you are going to learn. Not just how to move — how to think while you move. How to read the person in front of you. How to be exactly where you are."*

- **Their eye on Kiley Rose:** The Mestre notices her almost immediately — not because of her height, but because of how still she goes when they speak. Some children fidget. Kiley Rose listens with her whole body.

---

## 3. The Music Begins — The Ginga

- **The berimbau:** Before any movement is taught, the Mestre picks up the berimbau and plays. The sound is unlike anything Kiley Rose has heard — a single wire string stretched over a wooden bow, with a hollow gourd amplifying the vibration. It is percussive and resonant and ancient.
- **Kiley's reaction:** She does not move. She just listens. The sound goes somewhere in her chest before it goes anywhere in her head.
- **The ginga:** The Mestre introduces the ginga — the constant swaying side-to-side movement that is the foundation of Capoeira. Everything begins here. The ginga is not aggressive; it is the body saying *I am here, I am ready, I am watching.*
- **Teaching it:** The children try. Most of them are awkward at first — too stiff, too self-conscious, moving their arms and legs separately instead of as one thing.
- **Kiley's attempt:** Her gymnastics training gives her something immediately — she already knows how to shift her weight with intention, how to keep her centre of gravity low. Her ginga is imperfect but not graceless. After a few tries, something loosens in her shoulders, and the movement starts to come from her hips instead of her knees.
- **The Mestre's quiet notice:** They do not single her out. But they adjust a detail in her stance — a hand on her shoulder, repositioning her slightly — and say, simply: *"There. That's yours."*

---

## 4. The Jogo (The Game)

- **The concept:** The Mestre explains that in Capoeira, sparring is called *the game* — *o jogo*. It is not about winning or hurting. It is a conversation between two bodies. You read your partner. You respond. You never stop moving.
- **First observation:** The children watch two older students play in the *roda* — the circle. The music shifts. The two students enter, begin their ginga, and then flow into something that is genuinely beautiful: sweeping kicks that miss by intention, handstands used as attacks, cartwheels that become evasions. It is fast and slow at the same time.
- **Kiley watching:** She is absolutely still. Her lips part slightly. She has never seen anything that looks like this — a fight that looks like play, a dance that has teeth.
- **What she understands without being told:** Size is not the point. The smaller student in the demonstration is consistently the more elusive one, slipping out of reach, redirecting, using the larger student's momentum against them. Kiley Rose understands this the way she understands things before she has language for them: in her body first.

---

## 5. Her First Attempt at a Movement Sequence

- **The au (cartwheel):** The Mestre teaches a basic cartwheel used as a Capoeira evasion. Kiley Rose already knows cartwheels from gymnastics — precise, controlled, clean.
- **The difference:** In gymnastics, the cartwheel is a skill performed for an audience. In Capoeira, it is an answer to something — a way of moving out of reach while staying in the game. The Mestre shows them how to come out of the cartwheel already in their ginga, already back in the conversation.
- **Kiley's cartwheel:** Technically clean. The Mestre notes it with a nod.
- **The correction:** *"Good. Now forget the judges. There are no judges here. Do it like you're answering something."*
- **Her second attempt:** Slightly messier but alive in a different way. She comes out of it already moving, already watching. The Mestre smiles. So does Mike, from the wall.

---

## 6. The End of Class — The Roda

- **Closing ritual:** The class ends with a brief, gentle roda for the new students — just the ginga and basic movements, with the Mestre playing the berimbau. No sparring yet. Just the circle, the music, and the movement.
- **Kiley in the roda:** She is nervous and does not hide it well. But she enters the circle and does not freeze. She gigas. She stays in it. When the music stops, she is breathing harder than she expected.
- **The Mestre's closing words to the group:**

  *"Capoeira does not care how big you are. It cares whether you are paying attention. Come back next week."*

---

## 7. After — Walking to the Car

- **Mike and Kiley Rose:** They walk out together. Mike does not immediately ask how it was. He waits. He has always known that Kiley Rose answers questions on her own timeline.
- **Her verdict:** After a moment, unprompted:

  *"The music makes your body want to move before your brain decides to."*

  Mike considers this.

  *"Is that good?"*

  Kiley Rose thinks about it.

  *"Yeah,"* she says. *"I think that's the whole point."*

- **Closing image:** The two of them in the car. The berimbau sound is still in her head — she can feel the rhythm of the ginga somewhere in her hips as she sits. She does not mention this. But Mike watches her tap her knee quietly, once, twice, keeping time with something only she can hear.

---

## 8. Character Work

### Kiley Rose
- **Wants:** She does not arrive knowing what she wants. She arrives curious and slightly guarded — a child who has learned that new physical spaces sometimes involve people noticing her body for the wrong reasons.
- **Finds:** A discipline that does not notice her height as a deficit. That has, in fact, been shaped by people who were told their bodies were limited and responded by making those bodies extraordinary.
- **Key moment:** When the Mestre says *"There. That's yours."* — not praising her skill, but locating something in her movement that belongs specifically to her. She has never been told that before in a physical context.

### Mike Key
- **Function:** He brings her. He stays. He does not coach from the wall, does not compare her to other children, does not offer feedback she didn't ask for. He watches her face, not her technique.
- **His read on the class:** He chose Capoeira deliberately. He knows its history — that it was built by people who were told their bodies were not their own, who hid their power inside something that looked like play. He wanted Kiley Rose to know that history lives in her body too, if she chooses it.

### The Mestre
- **Function:** They are the voice of the discipline. Warm but not soft. They do not treat Kiley Rose's size as something to work around; they simply teach Capoeira, which already has the answer built in.
- **Key line:** *"It was created by enslaved people in Brazil who were not allowed to fight. So they hid the fight inside a dance."* — this is the scene's thematic anchor, spoken early and echoed in everything that follows.

---

## 9. Thematic Motifs

- **Hidden strength:** Capoeira's history of concealing a fight inside a dance mirrors Kiley Rose's own experience — carrying something powerful inside a body the world reads as small.
- **The music first:** The berimbau reaches her before the movement does. Her body responds before her mind decides. This is the opposite of how she usually operates in medical spaces, where she has learned to process information clinically before she allows herself to feel it.
- **Size is not the point:** The Mestre never addresses her height. The discipline addresses it structurally, without needing to name it. This is more powerful than reassurance.
- **"There. That's yours."** — The first time a physical instructor gives her something that belongs to her specifically, not as an accommodation but as a recognition.
- **The ginga:** The constant, swaying readiness — *I am here, I am watching, I am not afraid* — becomes a physical metaphor for who Kiley Rose is and who she will become.
