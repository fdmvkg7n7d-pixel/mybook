# SIMONKA'S SECOND MASTER'S THESIS PROPOSAL — THE DEFENSE

## Scene Outline — Beats, Dialogue Cues, and Thematic Notes

> **Companion to** `simonka-kata-profile.md`, `holic-club-rules-and-bylaws.md` (the Collegium's silence rule is load-bearing in this scene), and the existing Simonka/Holic outlines. **Read the profile first** for the relationship context this scene cannot say aloud.

> **Content & craft warning.** Simonka is, secretly, Holic Bartunek's pet (GG) within the Collegium. The thesis she is defending is an academic study of human-pet roleplay communities. **The scene must be readable on two layers at once:** the surface defense (a master's student presents a controversial topic to a committee) and the hidden defense (a Collegium member proposes to publish on the very thing the Collegium's silence rule forbids). **Simonka cannot, by the Collegium's rules and by her own life, disclose her own access to the field. The committee must therefore evaluate the proposal as if it were the work of an outsider.** The scene's whole dramatic engine is the gap between what she could say if she could and what she can actually say in the room. Render with care — no glamour, no thrill, no nudge-wink to the reader. The doubleness is the point.

---

**POV:** **Simonka Káta Dimitrov.** *(First sustained Simonka POV; the existing scenes have her seen from outside — by Gamaliel, by Klára, by Klárka. This is the saga's first chance to be in her head, and what is in her head is precisely **what she cannot say in the room**.)*
**Tone:** Contained. Academic in surface, geological in stakes. The register is the formal Czech-academic register of a Charles University master's defense — courteous, precise, latinate. The prose can let the formality do work: every academic locution is also a small act of concealment. *She has been preparing to speak this way about this topic for a year. The preparation has been its own form of training, parallel to GG's.*
**Location:** A seminar room at the **Faculty of Arts (Filozofická fakulta), Charles University, Prague.** Long table; five committee members on one side; Simonka on the other; a window onto a courtyard; a glass of water she will not drink; her bound proposal document on the table in front of her; the committee's five copies in front of them. The room is unremarkable. Its unremarkableness is part of the scene.
**Time in saga:** Late spring of Simonka's master's year — exact year TBD, but her early-to-mid twenties (consistent with the profile timeline: she met Holic at 23; this defense is after she has been GG for some months, probably less than a year). **Her brand is under her clothing.** The room does not know.
**Stakes:** Master's degree (formal). Reputation in her department (academic). The Collegium's silence rule (personal and dangerous; the rule predates her). Her relationship with Holic, who has not been told she is doing this (canon — see Beat 0). The integrity of her own mind, which has spent the last year writing an academic document about a life she is also living.

## CANON ESTABLISHED BY THIS SCENE

*(So later writing can rely on it.)*

- **This is Simonka's *second* master's thesis proposal. (CANON.)** Her first proposal — on a different topic [TBD; suggest: something safer, perhaps attachment in the Czech foster system, or embodied cognition in dance training, leaning into her family history without touching the secret] — was approved by her committee a year earlier, work was begun, **and Simonka withdrew it three months ago.** The withdrawal was her decision. She told her advisor only that she had found "a more pressing question" and asked for time. The advisor granted it. **The new proposal is the more pressing question.** (Whether the committee knows the topic of the withdrawn first proposal, and whether the first topic is referenced in the defense, is open — see Beat 4.)
- **The new proposal is an academic study of contemporary human-pet roleplay communities. (CANON.)** Framed in proper psychological language: *consensual non-traditional intimate role configurations, with particular attention to identity-formation, ritualized power exchange, and the construction of belonging in closed adult subcultures.* (Exact title and framing — Beat 1.) **The proposal does not name the Collegium. It names no specific community. It uses the international anglophone scholarly literature on BDSM and animal-roleplay subcultures as its anchoring frame, not personal access.** Simonka has built the proposal out of published sources — academic articles, ethnographies, conference proceedings — entirely *without* invoking insider knowledge she could not, under the silence rule, declare.
- **Her advisor likes the new proposal. (CANON.)** The advisor — *[TBD name; suggest a Czech-academic woman in her fifties, established but not famous, who has earned the right to supervise unconventional topics by surviving the political turbulence of Czech academia since the 1990s]* — has read the proposal twice and considers it methodologically sound and intellectually important. She has, before the defense, told Simonka privately that she will vote in favor and that she expects the proposal to pass. *She does not know what Simonka knows.*
- **The committee votes three to two in Simonka's favor. (CANON.)** The vote is taken at the end of the defense, in Simonka's presence, by a brief show of hands or a round-the-table polling — exact procedure per Charles University rules [TBD]. **The three in favor: the advisor, plus two others. The two against: two others.** Which two oppose, and on what specific grounds, is established below (Beat 5). The vote passes the proposal. Simonka may proceed.
- **The two dissenters' votes are recorded as part of the formal proceedings.** Their objections become part of the official record of the thesis-to-come. **If Simonka completes the thesis, both dissenters' objections will follow it through every later evaluation.** The vote is not a clean win; it is a contested permission.
- **Holic does not know Simonka has proposed this thesis. (CANON.)** She has not told him. She has been writing the proposal for a year — secretly, even from the man whose secret she is, in some sense, writing around the edges of. **The decision to keep it from him is hers and is the moral spine of the scene.** Whether and when she tells him is a future scene; this scene is the choice not to.
- **The proposal does not name the Collegium or any of its members.** Simonka has, in writing the proposal, exercised what the committee will not know is a form of disciplined silence native to her secret life. The committee reads the absence of named communities as scholarly caution. It is also Collegium silence-rule compliance. Both readings are true.
- **No one in the room knows about GG.** Not the advisor. Not the four other committee members. Not, in any way that could be spoken, even Simonka herself in the room: she is, for the duration of the defense, *only* a master's candidate in psychology.

## BEFORE THE SCENE — WHAT SIMONKA CARRIES INTO THE ROOM

*Establish the doubleness early; she will carry it for the next hour and a half.*

- **The proposal in front of her is one hundred and twelve pages.** She has read it forty times. She knows every sentence. She knows every sentence she *could not write.*
- **Her brand is under her skirt.** *H.B.* on her right buttock, placed there by Holic according to club tradition. She has dressed carefully — a long charcoal skirt, a cream blouse, a thin grey cardigan, low heels. Nothing about her dress speaks of any life other than the one she is performing in this room. The brand is invisible. **She is aware of it the way one is aware of a healed scar one has learned to live with — not painful, not absent, simply *there*, the body's permanent footnote to the conversation about to happen.**
- **She has prepared for two of the four likely committee questions she does not, herself, fully know how to answer.** The two she has not prepared for are the two she fears most — and she has not prepared for them because preparing would mean writing down sentences that, if found, would end her relationship with Holic and possibly her place in the Collegium and possibly the Collegium itself. She will have to answer them in the room, extempore, in front of five people, in a language formal enough to hold what she cannot say plainly.
- **Her advisor's confidence is its own pressure.** *She votes in favor before the defense begins.* The advisor's love of the proposal is, for Simonka, both shield and indictment: shield because it carries the vote; indictment because the advisor's certainty is grounded in a partial reading. *The advisor believes this proposal is the work of an unusually sensitive outsider.* Simonka cannot correct her without ending the thesis and possibly the friendship.
- **She has not told Klárka.** She has not told Gamaliel or Klára. She has not told Holic. She has not, in eight months of writing, spoken the word *thesis* in any house her family or her Collegium has access to. The proposal exists in Simonka's apartment and in a single email folder and in the heads of the five committee members. **The scene is the first time the proposal exists in a room with other people.**
- **She has decided in advance that if the proposal fails, she will not appeal.** She will withdraw cleanly, take her first master's-degree completion (with the original proposal she completed last year — see canon note), and reapply elsewhere or stop. **If the proposal passes, she has decided she will write the thesis even if the committee splits.** The decision to write is hers; the committee's permission is, in her private accounting, only permission to *publish*. She would write the thesis either way. She would simply, in the no-permission case, not file it.

- **Doc. PhDr. [TBD] Hájková, CSc. — the advisor.** Mid-fifties; Czech; established in clinical-developmental psychology with a side interest in identity formation in closed communities (Czech religious orders, post-Communist neighborhood associations, the LGBTQ+ scene in Prague in the 1990s). She has earned, by surviving the academy through three regime-changes, the credibility to supervise unconventional topics. - **Doc. PhDr. [TBD] Marešová — mid-career; specializes in trauma and gender; the committee's feminist anchor.** Forties; published; combative when she chooses to be. **She is the second dissenter.** Her objection (Beat 5b) is the one Simonka has feared most — the objection on *power and consent*, framed from a position the proposal cannot, without revealing itself, properly answer. ## BEAT 0 — THE WALK TO THE ROOM

*Very short. Establish doubleness.*

- Simonka leaves her apartment at 8:30. The defense is at 10:00. The walk to the Faculty of Arts takes nineteen minutes. She walks. She does not take the tram.
- She passes a café where she has, twice in the past year, sat with Holic. She does not stop. She does not look in.
- She passes a bench in the small park near the river where she once sat in GG's collar for an hour, in private, in winter, fully clothed, simply to know what it felt like to wear it in a public place without anyone knowing. **She has carried that knowledge into the proposal.** The proposal does not say so.
- She arrives at the faculty building at 9:42. She does not enter immediately. She stands outside for four minutes — the four minutes the advisor has, twice in her career, called *the candidate's last private minutes* — and she does what she has trained herself to do in the long preparation for this hour: she **folds GG away.** Not erases — *folds.* GG is in a drawer in her chest, the drawer closes, the drawer is invisible to the room she is about to walk into. Only the master's candidate goes through the door.

## BEAT 1 — THE OPENING

*The advisor convenes. Simonka delivers her opening statement. Formal, prepared, fifteen minutes by Charles University custom.*

- The advisor opens the proceedings in formal Czech. Names the candidate. Names the proposal. Names the committee. Reads the standard procedural sentence. *(The Czech-academic formality should be rendered in the prose — full titles, full constructions; do not anglicise the rituals.)*
- **The proposal's working title is read aloud for the first time in the room.** Suggested form:

> *"Závazek a péče: konstrukce identity a vztahových rolí v současných subkulturách dobrovolného mocenského směňování (na příkladu zvířecí role-play)."*
> ("Bondage and Care: The Construction of Identity and Relational Roles in Contemporary Subcultures of Voluntary Power Exchange, with reference to Animal Role-Play.")

- The Latin echo in *Závazek a péče* — *bondage and care* — is, to anyone who knows the Collegium's motto *Per vincula, cura*, **an exact translation**. Simonka chose the title knowing this. No one in the room knows the Collegium's motto. **Holic, if he ever sees the title page, will know in one second what she has done.** *(This is canon and is important: she has put the Collegium's own theological self-justification in her thesis title, in Czech, where the Collegium will not see it but where the title will, for the rest of her academic life, secretly contain her.)*
- Simonka delivers her opening statement. Fifteen minutes. **Render only its shape, not its full text** — the prose should let the reader feel the formal containment without reproducing a lecture. Three movements:
  1. **The field.** The international scholarly literature on consensual adult BDSM and animal-roleplay subcultures — Newmahr, Weiss, Hoff, Bauer — established, peer-reviewed, methodologically serious. The proposal stands on real shoulders.
  2. **The gap.** The Czech-language literature is almost nonexistent. Central European scholarship has, for political and cultural reasons traceable to the post-1989 transition, lagged. The proposal proposes to begin to fill it.
  3. **The method.** Mixed: discourse analysis of published community-produced texts (manuals, forums, memoirs in translation); semi-structured interviews with consenting adult practitioners, conducted under standard ethics-board protocols, recruited through general-public channels (no insider access claimed); a small theoretical contribution to the *belonging-through-bondage* question.
- She does not look at her advisor while she speaks. She looks at the table. **Looking at her advisor would be a tell.** She has decided this in advance.

## BEAT 2 — THE FRIENDLY QUESTIONS

*Procházka and Černý first. The committee custom is to begin with the kinder questions and proceed to the harder ones. Simonka knows this; the easier questions are no comfort, because they are only what is in front of her.*

### 2a — Procházka

- Procházka asks a methodological clarifying question: *how* she intends to recruit interview participants without using insider networks. The question is a softball; it gives her the opportunity to demonstrate she has a plan.
- She answers cleanly. The recruitment plan is, in fact, what she has spent the most time on. **Public-facing channels:** a discreet recruitment notice in two adult-community publications with established readership; a snowball methodology with explicit ethical guardrails; no compensation beyond travel expenses; full informed consent; the right of withdrawal at any stage. She names the IRB process. She names the timeline. She has the document memorised.
- **What she does not say:** that she could, with a single phone call to a man whose name is engraved on her hipbone, recruit thirty interview subjects in twenty-four hours. She does not say it. She does not let herself think it in the room.
- Procházka nods. He has heard what he came to hear.

### 2b — Černý

- Černý asks a question from sociology: how she plans to handle the *informant-vs-anthropologist* problem — the standard methodological worry that participants in any closed adult subculture will perform for the researcher rather than reveal what is actually there.
- **This is the question Simonka has, secretly, the most to say about, and can say almost none of.** She knows from her own life that the performance/reality distinction is, in this field, not a distinction at all — *the performance is the reality*. GG is not a mask over a real Simonka; GG is, on her weekends with Holic, what real means.
- She answers in the proper register: *the standard mitigations — triangulation across multiple participants, attention to discrepancies between published community texts and elicited testimony, theoretical framing that does not assume an underlying authentic self to be revealed.* She invokes the right names. She does not invoke the only authority that would let her answer the question honestly.
- Černý nods. He is satisfied. He is the easiest yes in the room.

## BEAT 3 — THE ADVISOR'S QUESTION

*Hájková asks one question. She has earned the right to ask it. She uses it to give Simonka a chance to demonstrate something the dissenters will need to see.*

- The advisor asks Simonka to describe, in her own words and not in the proposal's careful prose, *why* she wants to write this thesis.
- This is a kindness. It is also a test — *can the candidate speak about the question in a voice that is not the proposal's voice and still hold the centre?*
- **Simonka pauses for one beat — longer than her usual pause. The advisor sees the pause and reads it (correctly) as the candidate gathering herself to speak from somewhere unrehearsed.** The advisor does not know that the pause is, in fact, Simonka deciding *how close to the truth she will let herself sail.*
- Simonka speaks. Less formally than in her opening. **The shape of what she says** (do not render in full; let the prose suggest):

> *"There is a kind of belonging that the disciplines I was trained in do not have language for. I was raised in a ballet academy. I watched, for eighteen years, what it means to give one's body over to a form, daily, for a purpose larger than the body — and to find, inside the discipline, not the absence of freedom but a particular kind of freedom. The international literature on the communities I want to study takes seriously what other literatures sometimes do not: that bondage can be a form of care, that submission can be a form of agency, that bodies organized inside structures of power can be doing something other than suffering. I think Czech psychology needs the language for this. I want to help build it."*

- **The room receives this.** The advisor's face does not change; she has heard it before; she knew Simonka could deliver it.
- **Klimeš writes one word on his pad.** Simonka cannot see what it is. She does not look.
- **Marešová's pen has been still for ninety seconds.** Simonka registers the stillness without looking at her either.
- *(The reader is to understand: Simonka has just given the **only** answer she could give that does not lie and does not disclose. The ballet framing is real — she did watch eighteen years of bodies giving themselves to form. The bondage-can-be-care framing is real — it is, in fact, the Collegium's own creed, and she believes it, in the limited and contested way a thoughtful person can believe something whose costs she has experienced from the inside. **Every sentence she has spoken is true and none of it is the whole truth.** The doubleness of the scene lands here with the most weight.)*

## BEAT 4 — THE QUESTION OF THE WITHDRAWN FIRST PROPOSAL

*Brief. One of the dissenters — most plausibly Klimeš, opening his criticism by establishing context — raises it.*

- Klimeš asks why the first proposal was withdrawn. The question is procedurally permitted. The advisor has already informed him that Simonka's first proposal — *[TBD topic; suggest: attachment formation in Czech residential foster care, with reference to the post-Velvet Revolution reorganization of the child-welfare system]* — was approved and three months underway when she withdrew it.
- **Simonka has prepared an answer for this.** The answer she gives is true: she found a question she could not put down, and she did not want to write a thesis on a topic she could put down. She does not name the new question as having grown out of personal experience. She frames it as a scholarly *encounter* — reading the international literature, recognising a gap, realising she was the candidate to fill it.
- Klimeš asks one follow-up: *had she discussed the change with the advisor before withdrawing?* The advisor answers, briefly, that yes — at length, and that she had not pressed against the change because the new proposal struck her as the stronger work.
- **The question lands.** It will become part of the room's understanding of Simonka, and the saga must hold it: *she is a candidate who, once, chose a safer topic and walked away from it.* The walking-away is, to two of the people in the room, a credit; to the other two, a warning. (The advisor and Černý read the walking-away as scholarly courage. Klimeš and Marešová read it as evidence of a candidate who has not settled, who may yet walk away from this proposal too, who chooses topics for reasons not declared.)

## BEAT 5 — THE TWO HARD QUESTIONS

*The scene's centre. Klimeš first; Marešová second. The order matters: Klimeš's methodological objection is the easier of the two to answer in form; Marešová's is the one that nearly breaks the candidate.*

### 5a — Klimeš, on method

- Klimeš lays out his objection precisely. *(Render with the specificity of a real academic objection.)*

> **The shape of what he says:**
>
> The proposal claims to use snowball recruitment through public-facing adult-community channels. The claim is **structurally insufficient.** In a closed subculture of this kind, the *snowball* will inevitably propagate through pre-existing trust networks — which means the candidate will either (a) end up with a sample that is, in fact, a single community's self-presentation, or (b) be quietly refused access by every community she approaches, and end up with a thesis built almost entirely on published texts. **The proposal's methodology has not adequately specified which of these two failure modes it is prepared to absorb.** Without that specification, the thesis cannot be evaluated for what it claims to do.

- Klimeš is correct. The methodological problem is real. **Simonka has known about it since she began the proposal.** She has, with her advisor, drafted the specific contingency language; she has *deliberately* kept it slightly vague in the written proposal because the most rigorous mitigation she could imagine is the one she cannot disclose.
- She answers carefully. She acknowledges the problem. She names her contingency: *if the snowball methodology produces too narrow a sample, the thesis will reframe as primarily textual-analytic, with the interviews repositioned as case material rather than empirical sample. The thesis would then make a smaller but defensible claim.* She offers to write the contingency more explicitly into the methodology section before the final filing.
- **Klimeš writes one more word on his pad.** He does not visibly soften. He does not visibly harden. He notes the answer and waits for Marešová.
- *(Internal beat for Simonka: she knows that Klimeš's methodological objection is the cover for a deeper objection she has heard him express in seminars about other topics — that the field itself is not, in his view, a proper object of Czech psychological study at all, that it belongs to sociology or anthropology, that letting it into the department is a category error that will, over years, soften the discipline. **He will vote against not because the methodology cannot be fixed but because he does not believe the methodology should be attempted from within the field she is attempting it from.** She cannot answer this objection in the room because he has not, in the room, named it. The unspeakable in the room runs in both directions tonight.)*

### 5b — Marešová, on power and consent

- Marešová's question is shorter than Klimeš's. It is also harder.

> **The shape of what she says:**
>
> "The proposal frames the communities it studies as *consensual non-traditional intimate role configurations*. The framing is, in the international literature, contested. There is a substantial body of work — particularly in feminist psychology, and particularly in the post-#MeToo decade — that argues that consent under conditions of internalized power asymmetry is not the same kind of consent as consent under conditions of equality. The proposal does not engage that critique. It cites the literature that takes the communities' self-description at face value, and it omits the literature that asks whether the self-description is itself a product of the structures being described. **I would like to know whether the omission is an oversight or a position. If it is a position, I would like to know how the candidate intends to defend it.**"

- **This is the question Simonka has feared most.** Marešová is not wrong. Simonka knows from her own life that the question is real — that she has, in moments she does not name in the room, wondered whether her own consent to GG is the consent of a free adult or the consent of a woman who has been shaped, by training her predecessors did not name as training, to want what is being done to her. The yellow she once said to Holic during aftercare lives in this question.
- She answers. The answer is the hardest performance of her academic life.

> **The shape of what she gives back:**
>
> "It is a position, not an oversight. The proposal cites the contested literature — footnote forty-three — and declines to adopt its frame as the proposal's primary frame, for the following reason: the post-MeToo critique, important as it is, operates principally at the level of *judgment about* the communities studied, not at the level of *description of* them. A thesis that begins from the position that the participants' consent is suspect will not be able to hear what the participants say about their own lives, because every utterance will be pre-categorized as the symptom of a structure the researcher has already named. The proposal takes the methodological view that *first* the field must be heard in its own terms; *then* the critical question of consent under asymmetry can be addressed with the data in hand. The thesis will, in its concluding chapters, return to Marešová's question explicitly. I think the question is the most important question in the field. I do not think it is the question one can answer before doing the listening."

- The answer is good. The advisor's hand, on the table, opens once and closes — Hájková is signalling, in the small Czech-academic gesture of approval, that the candidate has held the line.
- **Marešová's face does not move.** She has heard what she came to hear. She has decided.
- *(Internal beat for Simonka: she has just defended, in formal Czech academic prose, the methodological position underwriting her own life with Holic. She has said it cleanly. She believes it — half of her believes it. The other half is GG in her chest drawer, and GG is silent in the room as she was trained to be silent in the kennel yard. **The two halves of Simonka have, for one sentence, spoken from the same mouth without contradicting each other. The cost of having done so will land later, alone.**)*

## BEAT 6 — THE VOTE

*The committee's custom is to vote in the candidate's presence by a polled round-the-table. Render with discipline; this is not a courtroom verdict, it is an academic procedure.*

- Hájková says, in the proper Czech formula, that the discussion is closed and the committee will now vote.
- She asks Simonka to remain seated. *(In some defenses the candidate is asked to step outside; Hájková has chosen, deliberately, to keep her in the room. **The keeping is itself a vote.** Hájková is signalling to the committee that she is not afraid of the candidate seeing the count.)*
- The poll proceeds. The Czech formula for each vote is a single word: *přijímám* (I accept) or *nepřijímám* (I do not accept).
- **Černý:** *Přijímám.*
- **Marešová:** *Nepřijímám.* She does not elaborate. She does not need to; the objection is in the record.
- **Procházka:** *Přijímám.*
- **Klimeš:** *Nepřijímám.* He does not elaborate either. He folds his hands on his pad.
- **Hájková:** *Přijímám.*
- **Three in favor. Two against. The proposal is accepted.**
- Hájková states the result aloud in formal Czech. She names the vote. She names that the proposal is approved with the requirement of the methodological specification Klimeš has requested (the explicit contingency language). She names the date by which the methodology revision must be filed. She closes the proceedings.
- **No one applauds.** Two of the committee members have voted against; the academic register does not permit celebration of a contested vote.
- Simonka stands. She thanks the committee in the proper Czech form. She thanks Klimeš and Marešová specifically, by name, for their questions. **The thanking is sincere and is also a discipline of the role.** They incline their heads in return.

## BEAT 7 — THE CORRIDOR

*Short. The five minutes after the door of the seminar room closes behind her.*

- Hájková follows Simonka into the corridor. The other four remain in the room — by custom, the committee debriefs briefly without the advisor present.
- Hájková does not say *congratulations.* She says, in Czech, *"Three to two. We will need to be careful."* She means: *the dissenting record will follow this thesis for the rest of its life; the methodology revision is real work; the political weather around this topic in the department is not over.*
- Simonka nods. She does not, in the corridor, trust herself to speak more than necessary. She says, *"Thank you."*
- Hájková puts a hand briefly on Simonka's arm — the only physical contact between them in the entire scene — and says, *"You answered Marešová well."* It is a compliment. It is also, somewhere beneath itself, a question Hájková is not quite asking — *how did you know to answer her that way.*
- Simonka does not answer the question that was not asked. She says, *"I had thought about it for a long time."*
- Hájková nods. She returns to the seminar room.
- Simonka stands alone in the corridor for one beat. Then she walks to the stairs and goes down.

## BEAT 8 — OUTSIDE

*One short coda. The first minute after the building.*

- Simonka steps out onto the small plaza in front of the Faculty of Arts. The spring sun is full. The cobblestones are warm through her shoes.
- She does not go home. She does not go to a café. She walks, without deciding to, toward the river.
- At the embankment she stops. She stands at the railing for some time. She does not cry. She does not feel triumphant. She feels — and the prose can let her name the word in her own head, because she has earned it — *unfolded*.
- **GG is no longer in a drawer.** GG has, in the corridor with Hájková, in the answer to Marešová's question, in the careful naming of the Collegium's own creed in formal Czech academic prose, *unfolded back into the rest of her*. The two halves of Simonka are, on the embankment, the same Simonka. They will not always be. Today they are.
- She thinks of Holic. She does not call him. She has not decided how to tell him. She has only just, in the last hour, finished deciding *what* to tell him she has done. The *how* will be another scene.
- She thinks of her advisor. She thinks of Marešová, briefly, with respect.
- She thinks of her great-grandfather Mike Key, whom she never met, whom she has been told stories about her whole life — the man who walked into the bedroom and chose protection over punishment. *She wonders whether the thesis she has just been permitted to write is, in some way she does not yet have the language for, an act in the long Key tradition of refusing to look away from the people the world has decided not to see.* She does not finish the thought. The thought is too large for the embankment.
- She turns from the railing. She begins to walk home. The proposal in her bag is heavier than it was this morning, and lighter.

## WHAT THE COMMITTEE DOES *NOT* DO

- **They do not vote unanimously.** This is the scene's load-bearing structural fact. The proposal passes contested.
- They do not, in the formal record, document anything beyond the vote count and the methodological revision requirement. The qualitative content of the objections will live in the committee members' heads and in their later conversations; it will not be written into the formal proposal-approval document.

The unspeakable in the room runs in three directions: Klimeš's deeper objection to the field's belonging in the department; Marešová's deeper objection to a candidate she may already suspect of insider involvement she cannot prove; Simonka's deeper truth that she is the insider neither of them has the right under university rules to ask about.

## TONE GUARDRAILS

- **No exoticism.** The defense is a defense. The committee is a committee. The Czech academic register is the Czech academic register — formal, latinate, precise, occasionally beautiful, never picturesque. The prose holds the formality without commenting on it.
- **No glamour around the doubleness.** Simonka is not *clever* for having written this proposal. The proposal is, simultaneously, a real scholarly contribution and a small disciplined silence in a system whose rules she has agreed to. The prose must not make the scene a thriller. *The doubleness is the moral content; treating it as a thrill would betray the moral content.*
- **No nudge-wink to the reader.** The reader knows about GG. The committee does not. The prose must not, by phrasing or framing, let the reader feel privileged-by-knowing. The reader is not winked at. The reader is simply, like Simonka, asked to hold two registers at once.
- **No internal monologue about the brand.** Simonka does not, during the defense, think about *H.B.* on her hipbone. The brand is named once in Beat 0 and not again. *Thinking about it during the defense would be the prose's failure to trust the reader to remember it.*
- **No vindication.** The vote is contested. It is not a triumph. Marešová and Klimeš are not antagonists; they are colleagues whose objections are partly right. The prose must let the dissent stand as serious. *A clean win would betray the scene.*
- **No Holic.** Holic is, throughout the entire scene, **named in Simonka's thought twice and named in the prose's narration once** (the embankment beat). He is otherwise not present. The defense is hers, not theirs. The prose holds the line.

- This scene is **the first sustained Simonka POV** in the saga. The existing scenes have shown her from outside (Klárka, Gamaliel, Klára, Holic). What we now know — that her interior is built out of *folding GG away* and *unfolding her back in* — is canonical from this point forward.

Simonka's academic work is no longer abstract psychology; it is specifically the academic study of human-pet roleplay communities. **Every later scene with Simonka now lives in the shadow of this work** — both the work itself and the silence she has agreed to hold around her insider access.
- The Collegium's silence rule **gains a new texture from this scene**: Simonka has, by writing the proposal at all, made a private decision about how to comply with the rule that no one else in the Collegium would necessarily endorse. She has reasoned: *I name no one. I describe no community by its identifiers. I treat the field as a generic field. I write a thesis. The silence rule is about disclosure of specific persons, places, and practices; the silence rule is not, in my reading, an embargo on academic study of the general phenomenon.* **Whether the Collegium would accept her reading is one of the saga's most dangerous open questions.** Holic might. Augustyn Reinholt (the Steward) might or might not.
