# SCENE OUTLINE: SIMONKA'S PAINTED PIECE — THE ONE TIME SHE DANCES

**Part of the Prague Academy / Key family saga.**
**Placement:** The Senior Showcase — final night of the Annual End-of-Year Recital, the June Simonka is seventeen, near the end of her time at the academy and the year before she leaves for Charles University. This is the recital *before* the canon beat in which, in her first university year, she skips the recital for exams and breaks the "every year" pattern. In other words: this is the last time she is fully a child of the academy — and the only time she is ever onstage.

**Characters in the scene:** Simonka Káta (seventeen, Grade 12, the non-dancer, the daughter who watches), Klára Key (mother, artistic director), Klárka Pavlînka (older sister, ~eighteen/nineteen — **graduated from the academy a year ago and now in her first year teaching there**, so she is present as family, helper, *and* junior faculty rather than as a performing student; the sisters last danced "together" as students in Klárka's Grade 12 / Simonka's Grade 11 year), Gamaliel Galvin Key (father, backstage/stage manager), and Simonka's **dance partner** for the piece — **[name & surname TBD]**, a senior academy student paired with her for the duet. (Whether their connection is purely artistic or quietly romantic is left open — see Open Items.)

**Note (canon reconciliation):** The piece is now a **painted duet**, not a solo. It is still Simonka's "body as text" concept; she has simply built it for two figures, with one partnered move at its center — see `simonka-painted-piece-rehearsal-five-weeks-out-scene-outline.md`, the earlier rehearsal scene where Klára helps the pair with that exact move.

**Characters referenced but not present:** Patti Anne and Aleksander (in the audience, visiting from Canada), the academy's senior dancers, the dogs at home, the future Simonka the audience does not yet know is coming (Charles University, Holic, the secret world) — none of it written on her yet.

**Setting:** Backstage at the academy on the final night of the recital — the dressing room off the Mike Key Studio. Mirrors framed in bulbs. The smell of rosin, hairspray, and now, faintly, cosmetic paint. A folding table laid with sponges, brushes, a row of theatrical body paints, a bowl of warm water. Onstage, later: the Senior Showcase, the front row where Simonka has sat every year of her life — empty tonight, because for once she is not in it.

**Tone:** Tender, quiet, a little astonished. A family that has spent seventeen years making peace with the fact that one of their daughters would never dance — and then, on the last possible night, she asks. Not because she changed her mind about who she is. Because she finally found the one piece only she could do. The mother and the sister, both dancers, kneel and stand around her with brushes instead of giving corrections. The body that was always the watcher becomes, for nine minutes, the watched. And everyone in the family understands that this is not Simonka becoming a dancer. It is Simonka saying goodbye in the only language the academy ever taught her.

## BEFORE THE SCENE

- Simonka has worked front-of-house at the recital every year of her life. Programs. Ushering. The back row. Watching the audience as much as the stage — reading the parents' faces, the held breath, the way a father's hand finds a mother's hand when their child comes onpointe. This is where her psychology began. She has never once been *in* the recital. The family stopped expecting it years ago, and stopped being sad about it not long after. It is simply true: Klárka dances, Simonka watches. That is the shape of them.
- This is the last recital before Simonka leaves for Charles University. Everyone feels the ending of something, though no one says it directly. After tonight she belongs to lecture halls and libraries, not studios.
- **The ask came weeks earlier, and it surprised everyone.** Simonka went to her mother — not her father, not her sister first — and said she wanted to do a piece. One piece. The final-night Senior Showcase. Klára's first instinct was to ask if she was sure, and her second instinct was to not ask, because she had watched Simonka think things through her whole life and knew that by the time her younger daughter said a thing out loud, the thinking was already done.
- **What Simonka proposed is not a ballet.** She does not have a lifetime of training in her body and she knows it; she will not embarrass the work by pretending. What she proposed is a short movement-and-stillness piece — closer to living installation than to dance — built around the one idea she has spent her whole childhood circling: *the body is a text. People read it before you say a word.* She wants to be painted. Not decorated — written on. The paint is the piece. And she wants **two** figures, not one — because reading is something that happens *between* people.
- **The concept (Simonka's, in her words):** Two figures begin as blank slates — skin painted a flat, uniform, unreadable neutral, so that the audience cannot immediately read age, mood, status, history. Bodies with nothing written on them yet. Over the nine minutes, under changing light, the two move through a small sequence of plain, pedestrian gestures — the way a person stands when they are waiting, when they are afraid, when they are loved, when they are alone — approaching and reading each other, and the painted marks (revealed by light, by the turn of a body) gradually make them *legible* to the audience and to one another. By the end they are fully readable: two people the audience suddenly feels it *knows*, though neither has spoken and they have barely "danced." The point — her psychology-student's thesis made flesh — is that we are reading each other constantly, and we do not get to choose when the reading begins.
- **The one partnered move.** At the center of the piece is a single supported move — the moment the two figures finally *touch* and one reads the other up close (a slow supported lean/lift in which Simonka must give her full weight to her partner and stay completely still and "blank-faced" while he turns her to the light). It is the only technically demanding thing in the whole piece, and it is the thing Simonka cannot reliably do — not because of the lift, but because her partner's hands at her ribs make her flinch and laugh (see the rehearsal scene five weeks out). Conquering that move — learning to be *touched and read without defending herself* — is, quietly, the entire emotional argument of the piece.
- **Why she needs Klára and Klárka.** Two reasons, and only one of them is technical. The technical one: she cannot paint her own back, and the design has to survive nine minutes of movement and stage heat without smearing — that takes hands that understand how a body moves, where it folds, where it sweats. The other reason is the real one. Simonka has spent her life being the one outside the work, and for this — the one time — she wants the two dancers she loves most *inside it with her.* She wants her mother's hands and her sister's hands on her before she walks out. She does not say this part out loud either. She does not have to.

## THE SCENE

### The Dressing Room, Two Hours Before Curtain

- The junior and intermediate nights are done. Tonight is the Senior Showcase — the last night, the one Patti Anne and Aleksander flew in for. Out front, Gamaliel is greeting families at the door in three languages, the way he and Klára always have. He will come backstage soon.
- In the dressing room, the senior dancers are in their own ritual — buns, hairspray, ribbons sewn and resewn, the particular silence of dancers warming up. And in the corner, at a folding table, something the academy has never seen before: a recital piece being *painted on*.
- Simonka sits on a stool in a simple flesh-toned base garment — modest, practical, the kind of dance underlayer worn for any costume; the paint goes over skin and base alike. She has pinned her hair up herself. She looks, for once, nervous in the particular way she has spent her whole life *observing* in other people from the back row. She notes this about herself, dryly, out loud: *So this is what it feels like.* Klárka laughs, because of course Simonka would be analyzing her own stage fright while she has it.
- Klára lays out the paints. She is the artistic director; she has approved every costume in this building for twenty years, and she approved this one too, carefully, asking the questions a director asks — sightlines, lighting, what reads from row twelve, what reads from the front row where the family always sits. But right now she is not the director. She is a mother with a sponge in her hand, about to put a base coat on the daughter she was sure she would never paint for a stage.

### The Base Coat — Klára

- Klára starts the neutral base — the "unwritten" layer. Long, even strokes down Simonka's arms, across her shoulders, the practiced economy of a woman who has spent her life being efficient with bodies.
- It is intimate in a way none of them expected. Klára has not done something like this since the girls were small — sunscreen at the beach, paint on a school-play cheek, the everyday tending of a child's body. Simonka is seventeen, a year from leaving for university, and her mother is touching her shoulders with a sponge, and they are both quiet because they both feel it.
- Klára, working: *You know you never have to do this. Not even now. You could still pull it. Front of house always needs hands.* (One last out — offered honestly, not as discouragement.)
- Simonka: *I know. I'm not doing it because I want to be a dancer.* A beat. *I'm doing it because I finally have something to say up there. I never did before. I wasn't going to go out and do a bad* port de bras *just to make you happy. You'd have hated that more than me not dancing.*
- Klára, after a moment, smiling at the mirror: *Yes. I would have.* The honesty between them is its own form of love. Klára never wanted a daughter who performed to please her. She got a daughter who performs only when she has something true — and she is, quietly, undone by it.

### The Marks — Klárka

- Klárka takes over for the detail work — the marks that will be revealed by light and movement: the lines along the spine, the figures at the ribs and the backs of the knees, the small written places. She is the dancer; she knows where a body bends, where paint will crack, where to leave a margin so a fold of skin doesn't smear the line. She works fast and sure — the body-literacy of a girl who lived at the barre her whole life, and now, in her first year teaching, the steady hands of someone who corrects other people's bodies for a living.
- This is the inversion of their whole childhood. For as long as either of them can remember, Klárka was the one being prepared — costumed, pinned, sent onstage while Simonka watched from the wings or the house. Now Klárka is on her knees behind her little sister with a fine brush, *preparing her.* Neither says it. Both feel it.
- Klárka, lightly, to break the weight of it: *Hold still. You move worse than the six-year-olds.* (Sisterly — and now literally the teacher voice, the one she's been using on real students all year, aimed for once at the one sibling who never enrolled.)
- Simonka: *I've watched you do this to a hundred kids. I never thought I'd be the kid.*
- Klárka, quieter, painting the line down her sister's spine: *You were never the kid. That's what made you* you. A real thing said sideways, the way sisters say real things. *Everybody in this building dances. You're the only one who ever* watched *us. We needed one of those too.*
- A pause. Simonka, who reads people for sport, lets herself be read for once and doesn't deflect it. *Thank you for doing this with me.* It lands. Klárka keeps painting so she doesn't have to make a face. (Optional beat: a single drop falls and Klárka has to fix a line — she blames the brush.)

### Gamaliel Comes Backstage

- Gamaliel comes back from greeting families. He has translated for three sets of grandparents and walked Patti Anne and Aleksander to their seats — front row, where the family always sits, the seat beside them empty tonight for the first time in seventeen years because the family member who fills it is the one going onstage.
- He stops in the dressing-room doorway. He sees his wife and his elder daughter, both dancers, both with paint to the wrist, kneeling and standing around his younger daughter — the one who never danced — making her ready for a stage.
- He doesn't say much. Gamaliel, who saved Klára when he was eight, who carried a secret at fifteen, who built a school out of grief and twenty-five million dollars — is not a man who needs a lot of words for the big moments. He just looks at the three of them for a moment too long.
- Gamaliel: *Five minutes to places.* And then, because he has to say *something* true or he won't be able to do his job: *Your great-grandfather caught your mother and me in a kitchen and decided to protect us instead of stopping us. I think about that every recital.* He looks at Simonka, painted, unrecognizable and completely herself. *He'd have liked this. He liked the ones who did it their own way.*
- (Mike Key's name does the work it always does in this family: it makes the present moment part of a line that started long before anyone in the room.)

### Places

- The senior dancers file toward the wings. Klára wipes her hands and becomes the director again — final bow, students, the machine of the night. Klárka, on staff now and shepherding her own little students through their nerves backstage, hands them off and slips into the wings to watch as family. But first, the three of them — mother, sister, the painted daughter — have one second together.
- Klára does the thing she does for every dancer before they go on, the academy's ritual: a hand briefly to the back of the neck, a single word. She has done it for Klárka a hundred times. She has never once done it for Simonka, because Simonka was never going on. She does it now.
- Klára, low: *Go let them read you.* (Simonka's own thesis handed back to her as a blessing.)

### The Piece (kept brief — this outline is about the family, not the choreography)

- Lights up on two figures the audience cannot read. Nine minutes. The plain human gestures — waiting, fear, being loved, being alone. The two approaching, reading each other. The light finding the marks. The slow arrival at legibility.
- **The partnered move lands.** The supported lean/lift at the center — the one she could not do five weeks out because his hands made her flinch — she gives him her full weight, stays blank and still, lets herself be turned to the light and *read*, and does not flinch. The family in the front row are the only people who know what that stillness cost her. (Optional: a held breath from Klára, who coached the move; a flicker of a private, victorious look between Simonka and her partner the instant after.)
- From the wings, Klárka watches her sister the way Simonka watched her for seventeen years. The roles, for nine minutes, are reversed. (And there is an extra ache for Klárka: she graduated a year ago, danced her own last student recital then, and has spent this whole year on the *other* side of the work — teaching, correcting, sending other people's children onstage. She knows exactly what a final recital feels like, and tonight she is in the wings for the sister who never went on. The watcher is being watched by the teacher who used to be the dancer.) (Optional callback: Klárka remembers the night two years ago when she pulled Simonka onto this same floor in an empty studio and made her dance once, in private — see `simonka-and-klarka-dance-together-grade-eleven-scene-outline.md` — and understands that she was the first person to teach her sister she *could* be seen. Tonight, painted and still, Simonka is doing in public what Klárka coaxed out of her in private.)
- From the front row, Patti Anne — who fostered a broken child named Klára and watched her become this woman with paint on her hands — watches a great-granddaughter she helped make possible, and does not entirely keep it together. Aleksander's hand finds hers. (Mirror of the gesture Simonka spent years cataloguing from the back row — now happening *for* her.)
- The piece ends not on a flourish but on stillness: the two fully-written bodies, simply standing, fully readable, looking back at the people who have been reading them the whole time. Then the light goes.

### After

- No triumphant "she's a dancer now" beat. That would betray her. The opposite: Simonka comes off, and the first thing she wants is the warm water and the sponges and her clothes — to wash it off, to be unwritten again, to go back to being the one who watches.
- Klára and Klárka help her get the paint off in the dressing room, the reverse of the way they put it on — and *that*, the unpainting, is where the quiet emotional landing is. Three women, warm water, the marks dissolving, the night ending.
- Simonka, scrubbing a forearm, matter-of-fact, already herself again: *Okay. I did it. Once.* A beat, with a small smile. *Front of house next year.*
- And the family knows she means it — and knows that next year she will be at Charles University and will, in fact, miss the recital for exams, and that *this* was the night the academy got the only performance it would ever get from its watcher. Nobody in the room knows that yet. The reader does. That is the ache under the scene.

## CONNECTION TO CANON

- **Honors, does not overwrite, the core trait.** Simonka remains the daughter who does not dance. This is explicitly a one-time exception, motivated by *her* idea (the body as text), performed in her own non-balletic register, and immediately followed by her returning to the watcher role. It strengthens "the quiet divergence" rather than reversing it.
- **Fulfills the user-specified beat:** Klára (mother) and Klárka Pavlînka (sister) help Simonka with the body paint for her piece.
- **Sets up the established future scene:** This is the *last* recital before Simonka, in her first Charles University year, skips the recital for exams and breaks the "every year" pattern (per `annual-end-of-year-recital.md`). This scene gives that later break its weight — the academy's one and only Simonka performance, never repeated.
- **Reinforces the "watching bodies → psychology" throughline** named in both `simonka-kata-profile.md` and the recital canon: her single stage piece is literally an argument about reading bodies, the same instinct that takes her to Charles University.
- **Pairs with the rehearsal scene five weeks out** (`simonka-painted-piece-rehearsal-five-weeks-out-scene-outline.md`), in which Klára helps Simonka and her partner with the one partnered move — the move Simonka keeps failing because her partner's hands at her ribs make her flinch and laugh, and over which she feels like a failure.
- **Uses established family ritual:** Gamaliel greeting families in multiple languages; Klára's final-bow/artistic-director role; the front-row family seat; Patti Anne and Aleksander visiting from Canada; the recurring invocation of Mike Key as the family's moral origin point.
