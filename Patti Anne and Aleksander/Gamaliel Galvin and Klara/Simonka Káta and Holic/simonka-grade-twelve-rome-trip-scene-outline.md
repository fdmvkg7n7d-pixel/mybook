# Simonka Káta – Grade Twelve Rome Trip Scene Outline

## Scene Goal
Establish Gamaliel Galvin's overprotective fatherhood, Simonka's quiet rebellion against the ballet-family legacy, and the origin of her later comfort with non-traditional boundaries — through a school trip where her father becomes her roommate.

## Characters
- **Simonka Káta** – 17, Grade 12, Charles University-track psychology student at a Prague international school. Not a dancer. Anxious about being seen as "the ballet school kid."
- **Gamaliel Galvin Key** – 43, father, former dancer, co-director of Prague ballet academy. Speaks fluent Italian from touring years.
- **Klára Key** – mother (present via phone/video). Does not come on trip.
- **Ms. Nováková** – history teacher, trip leader
- **Klárka Pavlînka** – 18, Simonka's older sister (referenced, not present — at academy intensive in Vienna)

## Premise
Simonka's Grade 12 class takes a 5-day art history trip to Rome (Colosseum, Vatican Museums, Forum, Keats-Shelley House). 

**Gamaliel comes along with the class and shares a hotel room with Simonka because they are family and always see each other in the morning.**

It's not a school policy or a trauma rule — it's simply how the Prague household works. Gamaliel and Klára have raised Simonka and Klárka in the ballet academy apartments where mornings are shared: coffee in the kitchen, dogs at the door, stretching in the living room. When Simonka's school announced the Rome trip, Gamaliel volunteered as parent chaperone (he speaks Italian and is cleared to work with minors), and he booked the twin room because he and Simonka both prefer waking up and seeing a familiar face first thing — the same way they do at home.

The school books them a twin room at Hotel Artemide (Via Nazionale) — two single beds, shared bathroom. Officially "parent chaperone accommodation." Unofficially, the other 17-year-olds think it's weird and sweet.

## Narrative Beats

### 1. Departure – Václav Havel Airport
- Simonka with rolling suitcase, psychology textbooks packed "just in case." Gamaliel with a small dance-stretching kit out of habit.
- Klára's goodbye FaceTime: "Send me photos of the Forum. Both of you. And don't forget — morning coffee together, even in Rome." It's their family ritual.
- Ms. Nováková thanks Gamaliel for volunteering as the Italian-speaking parent chaperone.

### 2. Hotel Check-in – The Twin Room
- Awkward moment: receptionist assumes "honeymoon?" Gamaliel corrects in Italian: "mia figlia."
- Simonka claims the bed by the window, immediately creates a "study corner" with highlighters. Gamaliel unpacks, hangs his clothes, places Willow's old photo on nightstand.
- Ground rules they negotiate (adult-to-adult, not parent-child):
  - Knock before entering bathroom
  - Lights out 11pm unless studying
  - No ballet corrections in the room

### 3. Day 2 – Vatican City (full day)

**Morning – St. Peter's Basilica**
- The class enters St. Peter's Square at 8am before crowds. Gamaliel, who toured Italy with ballet companies in his 20s, quietly translates the Latin inscriptions for Simonka.
- Inside the Basilica, Simonka is overwhelmed by scale — not by art, but by *people watching people*. She notices how tourists perform reverence for photos. This clicks for her psychology interest: performance of faith.
- Gamaliel lights a candle with her (not for religion — Klára's family is culturally Catholic, Gamaliel is not practicing) — "for your grandma Patti Anne," he says. They stand together in the side chapel, morning ritual transplanted to Vatican.

**Midday – Vatican Museums & Sistine Chapel**
- Gamaliel doesn't hover — he walks three paces behind Simonka, letting her be with friends.
- In the Raphael Rooms, Simonka freezes during Ms. Nováková's lecture on "The School of Athens" — overwhelmed by the expectation she should "feel" art like a dancer. Gamaliel notices, quietly hands her water, says in Czech: "You don't have to perform for the paintings. Just look."
- In the Sistine Chapel, silence is enforced. Simonka looks up at Michelangelo's Creation, then down at the crowd craning necks. She whispers to Gamaliel (breaking the rule): "Everyone's looking up, but no one's seeing each other." Gamaliel smiles — this is the first time he hears his daughter think like a psychologist.

**Afternoon – Vatican Gardens (special parent-chaperone access)**
- Because Gamaliel speaks Italian, the Swiss Guard lets the parent chaperones take a short guided walk in the Gardens while students sketch. Simonka is invited to join him.
- They sit on a stone bench. Gamaliel tells her about dancing *Romeo and Juliet* at Teatro dell'Opera di Roma at age 22, and how he hated the Vatican back then — "too much gold, too much power." Now, with his daughter, he sees it differently.
- Simonka takes a photo of them — morning faces, Vatican backdrop — and sends it to Klára with caption "morning coffee was espresso today."

**That night in the hotel:** Simonka admits she chose psychology because she wants to understand *why* people need to perform — in ballet studios, in churches, everywhere. Gamaliel: "Your mother and I built a school for bodies. Maybe you'll build one for minds."

### 4. Day 3 – Night Walk, Trevi Fountain
- Curfew is 10pm. Simonka's friends sneak out for gelato. Gamaliel knows, says nothing, but walks with her at a distance.
- At the fountain, Simonka makes a wish (not said aloud). Later she tells Gamaliel: "I wished I wasn't afraid of wanting strange things." Foreshadowing her later Holic dynamic — the comfort with rules, boundaries, and being watched over.
- Back in room: Gamaliel is asleep, reading glasses on. Simonka covers him with blanket — role reversal.

### 5. Day 4 – The Conversation
- Klára video-calls, asks for the morning photo: "Did you two have coffee together?" They hold up their hotel mugs and laugh.
- After call, Simonka asks: "Dad, why do we always need to see each other in the morning? Even on trips?" 
- Gamaliel: "Because we're family. At home, your mom and I see you and Klárka first thing — dogs, kitchen, barre warm-up. In Rome, it's the same, just a different kitchen. I didn't want your first morning in a foreign city to be waking up alone in a hotel with strangers down the hall."
- Simonka understands: it's not overprotection, it's continuity. The same reason they share the academy apartment, the same reason they eat together — family sees family first.

### 6. Return – Prague
- On the flight, Simonka fills out her Charles University application essay: "On watching bodies in Rome."
- Gamaliel watches her type, doesn't read over shoulder.
- Klára meets them at arrivals with Kleopatra the Golden Retriever. Family hug.

## Thematic Resonance
- **Inheritance vs. Choice:** Simonka sees her father's presence modeled as *consistent care* — a direct thematic precursor to her later comfort with Holic's collar, rules, and doghouse. Gamaliel offers safety without shame.
- **The Ballet Family's Non-Dancer:** Rome is where Simonka stops apologizing for not dancing.
- **Family Morning Ritual:** The Keys do mornings together at home; Rome proves the ritual travels. Sharing a room isn't strange to them — it's how they maintain family continuity.
- **Why the Room Share Matters Later:** When Holic later asks Simonka to sleep in a heated doghouse, share a space, follow bedtime rules — it's not new. Her father already taught her that intimacy can be structured, safe, and familiar. Holic eroticizes that template.
