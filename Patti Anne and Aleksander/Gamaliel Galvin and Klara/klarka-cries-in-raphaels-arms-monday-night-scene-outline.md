# Klárka Cries in Raphael's Arms — Monday Night Scene Outline

## Placement

**When:** Monday night, after Klárka and Raphael leave Simonka Káta's apartment with the diary.

**Where:** Klárka's bedroom, or another private room where Raphael can stay with her after the apartment visit. This happens **the same night** as the diary discovery — not Tuesday.

**Immediate lead-in:** Klárka has found Simonka's diary on the kitchen table with the sticky note **Přečti mě** / **Read me**. She has read enough to understand the central fact: Simonka did not merely consider Holic Bartunek's offer. Simonka chose to become Holic's pet.

---

## Scene Purpose

This scene is the emotional collapse that Klárka delayed during the apartment visit.

At Simonka's apartment, Klárka had to function: find the diary, decide whether to read it, decide whether to take it, leave the apartment, keep moving. On the walk home, she could still hold herself together because the body can stay disciplined while it is in motion.

But Monday night, alone with Raphael and the diary, the fact becomes unbearable:

> Simonka chose this.

Klárka cries in Raphael's arms because her fear, anger, sisterly protectiveness, and helplessness finally catch up with her.

---

## Core Emotional Conflict

Klárka is not only horrified by Holic.

She is horrified by Simonka's agency.

If Simonka had been forced, Klárka could imagine rescue. If Simonka had been tricked, Klárka could imagine intervention. If Simonka had written *help me*, Klárka could know what role to play.

But Simonka wrote, in one form or another:

> I chose him.
>
> I chose to be his pet.

That makes Klárka feel trapped between two incompatible truths:

1. **Simonka is an adult and made a choice.**
2. **Klárka thinks the choice is stupid, dangerous, humiliating, and impossible to accept.**

The tears come from that contradiction.

---

## Characters in the Scene

### Klárka Pavlînka

- Older sister by fifteen months.
- Ballet teacher, trained to control her body, breath, and emotions.
- Has spent the whole evening trying to stay functional.
- Now feels helpless because sisterly protection cannot undo an adult choice.
- Her first honest reaction is anger: **Simonka made a stupid choice.**
- Beneath the anger is grief: **Why wasn't home enough? Why wasn't I enough?**

### Raphael

- Klárka's partner.
- Present as witness and comfort, not as investigator.
- He has not read the diary and does not demand details.
- His role is to hold Klárka without taking control of the secret.
- He lets Klárka call the choice stupid without correcting her too quickly.
- He understands that she needs space to be angry before she can be fair.

### Simonka Káta — Absent Presence

- Not physically present.
- Present through the diary.
- Her choice dominates the room.
- Her absence is part of the pain: Klárka cannot argue with her, protect her, or ask why.

### Holic Bartunek — Offstage Force

- Not present.
- Exists in the scene as the man Klárka blames.
- Klárka may hate him before she fully understands him.
- He represents the world Simonka chose over the safety and familiarity of family.

---

## Scene Structure

## Beat 1 — Arrival Back From Simonka's Apartment

Klárka and Raphael return from Simonka's apartment.

The diary is with them. It may be in Klárka's bag, under her arm, or pressed against her body. The sticky note is still on it:

> **Přečti mě.**

Klárka is quiet. Too quiet.

Raphael does not crowd her. He follows her lead. If she goes to her room, he goes with her only if invited or if she clearly wants him there. His presence remains careful.

**Key image:** Klárka carrying the diary as if it weighs more than it should.

---

## Beat 2 — Klárka Opens the Diary Again

Klárka does not intend to read much more.

She may tell herself she only needs to check one thing:

- Where Simonka is.
- Whether Simonka is safe.
- Whether Simonka accepted Holic's offer.
- Whether the diary says what happened after the one-week decision period.

She opens the diary again because partial knowledge is worse than no knowledge.

Raphael remains nearby but does not look over her shoulder.

**Important boundary:** Raphael does not read Simonka's diary. Klárka reads. Raphael witnesses Klárka reading.

---

## Beat 3 — The Sentence That Confirms the Choice

Klárka finds the page where Simonka records the decision.

The exact wording can be decided later, but the emotional function is clear. Simonka writes that she returned to Holic and accepted the role.

Possible diary phrasing:

> I went back to Holic.
>
> I said yes.
>
> I am GG now.

Or:

> I chose to be his pet.

Or:

> I know how this sounds, but I chose it.

Klárka reads the line once and does not absorb it.

She reads it again and understands.

She reads it a third time because she wants the sentence to become something else.

It does not.

---

## Beat 4 — First Reaction: Anger Before Tears

Klárka's first reaction is not crying.

It is anger.

She shuts the diary or presses her hand flat against the page. Her body becomes rigid, controlled, dancer-still.

She says something like:

> That's stupid.

Or:

> Simonka, that is so stupid.

Or, in Czech:

> To je tak hloupé.

This is not meant as the narrator's final judgment of Simonka. It is Klárka's immediate sister-reaction: scared, furious, protective, unable to understand how someone so intelligent could choose something that looks to Klárka like surrender.

Raphael does not correct her.

That matters.

If he corrects her too soon, Klárka will feel alone. If he lets the anger exist, she can move through it toward the grief underneath.

---

## Beat 5 — Klárka Tries to Explain Why It Hurts

Klárka speaks in fragments.

She may pace, sit on the bed, stand by the window, or hold the diary closed in both hands.

She says some version of:

- Simonka has a home.
- Simonka has parents who love her.
- Simonka has Charles University.
- Simonka has the academy, even if she chose a different path.
- Simonka has Klárka.
- Simonka had somewhere to go besides Holic.

The emotional center:

> Why would she choose that when she has us?

This is not rational. Klárka knows love does not automatically answer every need. But in this moment, sister-love feels rejected.

---

## Beat 6 — The Word “Pet” Breaks Her

Klárka can say Holic's name with anger.

She can say *decision* with control.

But she struggles with the word **pet**.

The word reduces Simonka, in Klárka's mind, from sister/student/psychologist/daughter/person into something owned, named, handled, and called.

Klárka may say:

> She chose to be his pet.

Then repeat it, more quietly:

> His pet.

That repetition breaks her composure.

The tears begin here.

---

## Beat 7 — Raphael Asks Permission Before Touching Her

Raphael sees the change before Klárka fully feels it.

He does not immediately grab her. He asks, softly:

> Can I?

Or:

> Klárko, come here.

If the scene wants to emphasize his restraint and respect, he asks permission. This mirrors his larger role: he does not take what is not offered, whether that is the diary or Klárka's body in distress.

Klárka nods, reaches for him, or collapses toward him.

---

## Beat 8 — Klárka Cries in Raphael's Arms

Raphael holds her.

Klárka finally cries.

This should feel like the release of everything she suppressed:

- The Friday conversation with Klára.
- The confrontation with Simonka.
- The weekend uncertainty.
- The decision to go to the apartment.
- The empty apartment.
- The diary on the kitchen table.
- The first partial reading.
- The walk home.
- The discovery that Simonka said yes.

Klárka is not graceful when she cries. She is not performing grief. She is an older sister whose body has run out of discipline.

Raphael holds her without trying to solve the problem.

---

## Beat 9 — What Klárka Says While Crying

Klárka may speak into Raphael's chest or shoulder. Her words are broken, repetitive, not polished.

Possible lines:

> She chose him.

> She chose that.

> How could she choose that?

> She's too smart for this.

> It's stupid. It's so stupid.

> I hate him.

> I hate that he gets to call her something else.

> I hate that she answered.

> I don't know if I'm allowed to hate it.

The final line is especially important because it reveals the deeper conflict: Klárka knows Simonka's consent matters, but emotionally she is nowhere near accepting it.

---

## Beat 10 — Raphael's Response

Raphael should not over-explain.

He should not lecture Klárka about consent, kink, adult autonomy, or open-mindedness in this moment. Those conversations may happen later. Right now, Klárka is in acute emotional distress.

His response is simple:

> I know.

Or:

> I'm here.

Or:

> You don't have to understand it tonight.

He may eventually say something that helps locate the next step:

> Not tonight. Tonight you breathe.

Or:

> We do not decide everything tonight.

This gives Klárka permission not to solve Simonka's life before morning.

---

## Beat 11 — Klárka's Helpless Question

After the first wave of crying passes, Klárka asks the question beneath everything:

> What do I do now?

Or:

> What does an older sister do with this?

This is the heart of the scene.

Klárka knows how to teach. She knows how to correct posture. She knows how to run an audition. She knows how to carry a family legacy inside a ballet studio.

She does not know how to respond to a younger sister who has voluntarily entered a human-pet dynamic with a man Klárka does not trust.

Raphael does not have a perfect answer.

He may say:

> You love her. You keep the door open. You don't disappear.

This answer does not fix anything, but it gives Klárka something she can do.

---

## Beat 12 — The Diary Remains Visible

The diary should stay physically present in the room.

It may sit on the bed, the floor, a desk, or the nightstand. The sticky note remains visible.

> **Přečti mě.**

By the end of the scene, the diary is no longer just Simonka's confession. It is now Klárka's burden.

She has read enough to know Simonka chose Holic. She may not have read every detail. The unread pages still matter.

**Key final image:** Klárka in Raphael's arms, exhausted from crying, with the diary nearby and the note still visible.

---

## Optional Czech Dialogue

Use Czech sparingly for intimacy and family weight.

- **To je hloupé.** — That's stupid.
- **Simonko, co jsi udělala?** — Simonka, what did you do?
- **Proč?** — Why?
- **Já tomu nerozumím.** — I don't understand this.
- **Nechci tomu rozumět.** — I don't want to understand this.
- **Jsem tady.** — I'm here.
- **Dneska nemusíš vědět všechno.** — Tonight you don't have to know everything.

---

## What This Scene Establishes

1. **Monday night is the emotional breaking point.** Klárka does not wait until Tuesday to collapse. The discovery and breakdown happen the same night as the apartment visit.

2. **Klárka knows Simonka chose.** This changes the arc from uncertainty to painful knowledge. Klárka is no longer only wondering what happened. She now knows the decision.

3. **Klárka's anger is protective, not analytical.** Calling the choice stupid is her frightened older-sister reaction. It does not have to be morally final or fully fair.

4. **Raphael proves himself trustworthy.** He comforts Klárka without reading the diary, demanding details, or taking control.

5. **Simonka's agency complicates everyone else's response.** The family cannot simply rescue her if she does not ask to be rescued.

6. **The diary becomes a burden Klárka now carries.** Simonka chose to disclose through writing, and Klárka must decide what to do with that knowledge.

7. **The Wednesday audition becomes even harder.** Klárka now has to carry confirmed knowledge of Simonka's choice into the professional responsibilities waiting later that week.

---

## Continuity Notes

- This scene should be placed after the Monday evening apartment visit.
- It revises the earlier uncertainty: Klárka reads far enough on Monday night to learn that Simonka accepted Holic's offer.
- Klárka may still not know every detail of the Holic/GG dynamic, but she knows the essential fact: Simonka is Holic's pet by choice.
- Raphael should remain a witness and comforter, not a co-reader of Simonka's private diary.
- The tone should be emotional, intimate, and non-explicit. The focus is Klárka's sisterly devastation, not the mechanics of Simonka and Holic's relationship.
