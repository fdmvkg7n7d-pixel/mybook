# SCENE OUTLINE: KLÁRA ASKS KLÁRKA / KLÁRKA ASKS SIMONKA / THE APARTMENT

*This scene outline covers a sequence of events across one week — from the Friday of Simonka's Holic/GG decision period through the Wednesday audition — in which Klára asks Klárka Pavlínka what she knows about her younger sister, Klárka confronts Simonka directly, Klárka defies her mother and goes to Simonka's apartment with Raphael, and Klárka finds a diary on the kitchen table with a sticky note that says *Přečti mě* (Read me). The diary is Simonka's written voice — the only way she could say what her mouth could not. Whether Klárka reads it, and what happens next, remains the central open question.*

---

## TIMING & CANON BACKGROUND

- **When:** Friday evening through Wednesday morning. One week.
  - **Friday:** Simonka's one-week decision period is ending. She has visited Gamaliel Galvin and Klára for a meal and told them *nic není* — nothing is wrong. That evening, Klára asks Klárka what she knows. Klárka confronts Simonka. Neither conversation breaks through.
  - **Saturday–Sunday:** The weekend passes. Simonka's decision deadline arrives. Klárka does not know what Simonka chose. She tries to prepare for Wednesday's audition. She cannot.
  - **Monday afternoon:** At the ballet academy, Klárka tells Klára she is going to Simonka's apartment. Klára tries to stop her. Klárka refuses to obey. Klára tells her to take someone. Klárka brings Raphael.
  - **Monday evening:** Klárka and Raphael arrive at Simonka's apartment. It is empty. Simonka is not there. On the kitchen table, in the center, is a diary. On the diary is a sticky note: *Přečti mě.* Read me.
  - **Tuesday:** [TBD — the day between the diary and the audition. Does Klárka read it? Does she tell Klára what it says? Does she confront Simonka?]
  - **Wednesday morning, 10:00:** The audition. Klárka leads her first solo evaluation at the Mike Key Studio, carrying whatever the diary gave her or didn't, and she must be present, professional, and precise — because the applicant deserves that, and because the name on the studio wall demands it.

## BEAT 0 — BEFORE: KLÁRKA IN THE MIKE KEY STUDIO

*This is where Klárka is when the evening begins. It is not a separate scene — it is the starting position from which everything else pulls her away.*

**The studio:**

The Mike Key Studio is empty. It is evening. The last class of the day has been over for an hour, maybe two. The lights are on but not full — the kind of low, warm light that makes a studio feel like a church after Mass has ended. The barre casts its familiar shadow. The sprung floor holds the memory of a hundred feet that passed over it today.

Klárka is sitting on the floor. Not stretching — that was hours ago. She is sitting cross-legged with the applicant's file open in her lap and a pen in her mouth, which is where the pen goes when her hands are busy holding the pages still. She is reading the file for the third time this week. Not because she hasn't memorized it — she has, almost — but because Wednesday is five days away and this is the first solo audition she has ever been trusted to run, and the weight of it is not only professional. It is familial. This studio is named after Mike Key — the man her mother never met as an adult, the grandfather whose name lives in the wood and the mirrors and the silence of this room. Klárka is about to decide who enters that legacy. The pen stays in her mouth. The pages stay open.

**What she is looking at:**

A new incoming student. A young dancer — age, background, training history, all TBD. Someone who wants to join Gamaliel Galvin and Klára's ballet academy, which means someone who wants to enter the world Klárka's parents built from a dead man's faith and a grandmother's twenty-five million dollars. Klárka is not evaluating a dancer. She is evaluating whether this person belongs inside her family's dream.

She has her criteria written out. She has notes from conversations with Gamaliel and Klára about what they look for — not only technical facility, but the thing that cannot be taught: the quality of attention, the way a child listens when given a correction, the relationship between discipline and curiosity. Klárka grew up watching her parents make these decisions. Now she is making one herself. Alone.

**The interruption:**

From the kitchen — distant, but clear in the way sound travels through a house built around studios — Klára's voice:

> *Klárko?*

Not urgent. Not alarmed. The ordinary pitch of a mother calling a daughter in the evening. But Klárka hears something underneath it — or she doesn't, and she will realize later that it was there all along. She closes the file. She puts the pen behind her ear, or on the floor beside her, or in her pocket. She stands up — the way dancers stand, from the floor, without using hands, because the floor is home.

She walks toward the kitchen, leaving the applicant's future on the studio floor behind her.

## BEAT 1 — AFTER THE MEAL: THE KITCHEN, KLÁRA ALONE

The Friday meal is over. Gamaliel is in the next room — cleaning up, or walking Kleopatra, or on a call about the academy. Simonka has already left for the evening — or she is upstairs in her old room, or in the bathroom, or somewhere just out of reach. The point is: she is gone from the table, and Klára is alone in the kitchen with the remains of the meal and the knot in her chest that has been tightening all evening.

**What Klára is feeling:**

Klára knows what it looks like when a child is hiding something. She knows because she was that child. At seven, she told Gamaliel about her father's abuse in words too small for what they described. At fifteen, she hid a secret relationship with her foster brother for months because she believed the truth would cost her the only family she had. She sat across from adults she loved — Mike, Patti Anne, Aleksander — and lied with her face, and she knows exactly how that feels from the inside: the effort of keeping the body still when the mind is screaming, the way denial tastes in the mouth, the specific quality of silence that is not peace but compression.

Simonka is doing what Klára once did. Sitting at the table. Saying *nic není*. And Klára cannot reach her.

**The decision to find Klárka:**

Klára does not go to Gamaliel with this — not yet. She goes to Klárka because Klárka is Simonka's older sister. Fifteen months apart. Close enough to be each other's first friend and first rival. Klára is gambling on the sister bond: the hope that Klárka, who has known Simonka since the moment she arrived in the world, might have seen something Klára has not.

She does not know yet that this gamble will not pay off.

**Czech flavor:** Klára moves through the kitchen putting things away. Kleopatra follows her, golden head against her thigh. Klára scratches behind the ears without thinking — the same unconscious gesture she has performed with every dog since Willow. She calls Klárka's name from the kitchen. Not formally. Just *Klárko?* — the vocative, the way a mother calls a daughter in a Prague kitchen when the evening is still ordinary but about to stop being so.

---

## BEAT 2 — THE KITCHEN: KLÁRA ASKS KLÁRKA

Klárka comes into the kitchen. She is still carrying something from the studio — not the file, she left that behind, but the residue of it: the posture of a person who was deep in concentrated work five minutes ago and has not fully transitioned out of it. Her sleeves may still be pushed up from sitting on the studio floor. There may be a pen behind her ear. She is not relaxed the way she normally is in this kitchen. She was working. Her mind was in Wednesday.

**The opening:**

Klára does not sit Klárka down with the formality of a serious conversation. She continues putting things away — or she stops, hand on the counter, and asks without preamble. The Czech mother's directness: no setup, no soft landing, just the question delivered in the same tone she would use to ask about the laundry.

> *Klárko, co se děje se Simonkou?* (Klárka, what is going on with Simonka?)

Or, more precisely:

> *Něco není v pořádku. Vím, že není. Ty a ona — vy jste blízko. Víš něco?* (Something is not right. I know it isn't. You and her — you're close. Do you know something?)

**Klárka's response:**

Klárka pauses. Not because she has a secret to protect, but because she has been ambushed by a question she cannot answer while she was still half-inside the audition preparation. The gear-shift is visible: her face moves from *I was working on something important* to *my mother is asking me about my sister and I don't have the answer* in the space of a single breath.

Her face does something — the particular expression of an older sister who is about to discover that the distance between *fifteen months ahead* and *knowing everything* is much farther than she assumed.

She says she doesn't know.

Not defensively. Not dismissively. Klárka is the daughter who grew up inside the academy, who teaches there now, who carries her mother's name forward — she is serious, and careful, and she treats Klára's worry as real. She repeats it: *Nevím, Mami. Opravdu nevím.* (I don't know, Mom. I really don't know.)

**The detail that hurts:**

Klárka tries. She tries to think of something useful. She runs through what she knows: Simonka has been spending time in the city more than usual, she comes home later, she is quieter, she changed something about her routine recently — or did she? Klárka cannot be sure. The more she tries to assemble evidence, the more she realizes how little she has. Simonka has been *there*, in the house, at the table, in her room — but present in body only, like someone standing in a photograph they do not belong to.

**The frustration — Klárka's, not Klára's:**

Klárka expected to have an answer. She is the older sister. Fifteen months of trail-breaking, of reaching everything first, of being the one Simonka follows — she assumed that if anyone would know, she would. The realization that Simonka has been living an entire hidden life right next to her — that Simonka's silence is not absence but concealment — is a new and uncomfortable feeling for Klárka.

She does not say *she's fine, don't worry.* That would be a lie, and Klárka is a ballet teacher; she can see when a body is holding something it shouldn't.

She says: *Je jiná. Ale nevím proč.* (She's different. But I don't know why.)

**Klára's interior:**

Klára hears this and recognizes it from the other side — from being the girl who said *nic není* to Mike Key and meant *everything is, but I'm too afraid to say it.* She does not push Klárka further. Klárka has told her the truth: she does not know. Pushing will not produce a different answer.

But Klára does not let the moment pass without one more thing. She touches Klárka's arm — the same quiet, physical gesture that has been the language of this family since Mike held Gamaliel on the kitchen floor — and says:

> *Když něco zjistíš — cokoliv — řekneš mi. Ano?* (If you find out something — anything — you'll tell me. Yes?)

Klárka says yes. She means it.

And then Klára adds — gently, because she is a mother and she sees the pen behind Klárka's ear and knows where her daughter was before she called her:

> *A soustřeď se na středu. To je tvoje. Simonka je její. Nezaměňuj to.* (And focus on Wednesday. That's yours. Simonka is hers. Don't confuse the two.)

It is not unkind. It is the specific wisdom of a woman who once carried a secret so heavy it made a seven-year-old boy vomit, and who knows that the world does not stop because someone you love is hiding. Klárka has a job to do on Wednesday. Klára is telling her: *do not let your sister's silence eat your preparation.* Klárka is needed in the studio on Wednesday. Whatever Simonka is carrying, she is carrying it with or without Klárka's knowledge. The audition will not wait.

**The weight of the exchange:**

What passes between them is not only a mother asking a daughter about a sister. It is Klára Drahoslav — the girl who kept the secret of abuse inside a child's body, who hid a relationship from the adults who loved her, who understands from her own marrow what it costs to carry something alone — recognizing that her youngest daughter is carrying something, and being unable to help her set it down. The echo is explicit in Klára's mind: *I was Simonka. I sat at that table and said nothing was wrong. I know what she is doing. I just don't know what she is hiding.*

This is one of the saga's generational mirrors: the mother who was the girl with the secret now watches her daughter sit in the same silence, and the difference is that Klára had Gamaliel to tell, and Mike to catch her, and a psychologist to guide her toward disclosure. Simonka has no one. Or she has people — Gamaliel, Klára, Klárka, all of them right here — and is choosing not to reach for them. And Klára cannot force her hand. No one could force Klára's hand either. The parallel is exact and painful.

## BEAT 3 — THE HALLWAY: KLÁRKA FINDS SIMONKA

Klárka does not go back to the studio. She does not go back to the applicant's file. She stands in the kitchen for a moment after Klára leaves — staring at nothing, or at Kleopatra, or at the counter where Klára's hand rested. Wednesday is five days away. The file is still on the studio floor. Klárka should go back to it. She should follow her mother's advice: *soustřeď se na středu.*

She does not.

Because Klára's question is still in the room like a smell, and Klárka's inability to answer it is not a fact she can sit with and work around. It is a hole. It is the first time in her life that her sister has been a question mark instead of a sentence, and Klárka — who has spent her whole life being fifteen months ahead, being the one who knows first — cannot stand at a desk and read about foot turnout while her sister is somewhere in this house carrying something that made her say *nic není* in a voice Klárka has never heard before.

**The search:**

Klárka walks through the house looking for Simonka. Not urgently — she is not running — but with the determination of someone who has just been told *your sister is not okay and you should know why, but you don't* and cannot tolerate not knowing anymore.

She finds Simonka in her old room — the room she grew up in, the room she still keeps even though she has her own apartment in the city near Charles University. The room with the east window, or the room next to Klárka's, or wherever the saga has placed the second daughter's space. Simonka is sitting on the bed, or standing at the window, or on the floor with her back against the wall — somewhere that lets the audience see her posture before she sees Klárka.

**What Klárka sees before Simonka sees her:**

Simonka is still. Not ballet-still — not the trained, intentional stillness of someone who has learned to hold a position. Something else. A held stillness. The kind that comes from trying very hard not to move because moving might crack something open. Her hands are in her lap, or her arms are crossed, or she is holding something small — her phone, turned face-down; a pen; nothing at all. She is looking at something the viewer cannot see, or at nothing, or at the wall, or out the window at the Prague evening.

Klárka watches her for a moment. This is the sister who has been fifteen months behind her for her entire life. The sister whose first steps she witnessed, whose first day of school she remembers, whose every milestone she reached first and then turned around to watch Simonka reach after. She has never looked at Simonka and not understood what she was seeing. Until now.

**The entrance:**

Klárka does not knock, or she knocks once and does not wait. She comes into the room and sits down — on the bed beside Simonka, or on the floor across from her, or wherever places them at the same level. She does not start with *Mami se ptala* (Mom asked). She starts the way sisters start when the pretense has run out:

> *Simonko.* (The vocative. Not *Simi*, not a childhood nickname. The full name, said gently.)

Simonka looks at her. For one second, something flickers — not guilt exactly, but the flash of a person who was deep inside a private thought and has been pulled out of it. The mask reassembles quickly, but Klárka is a ballet teacher. She watches bodies for a living. She saw the crack before it closed.

**The approach:**

Klárka does not ask *what's wrong* the way the adults did at the table. She tries a different door:

> *Mami je z tebe nervózní. Já taky. Ne proto, že bys něco udělala špatně. Prostě — nejsi ty.* (Mom is worried about you. Me too. Not because you did something wrong. Just — you're not you.)

She tries warmth. She tries the older-sister privilege of *I have known you since before you had words, and I can tell when the words you're using aren't yours.* She tries lightness, almost:

> *Neboj se. Nejsem tady, abych tě préskala. Jen mi řekni, jestli jsi v pořádku. Opravdu v pořádku.* (Don't worry. I'm not here to interrogate you. Just tell me if you're okay. Really okay.)

**Simonka's response — the first deflection:**

Simonka gives Klárka the same thing she gave the table: a smile that does not reach her eyes, and the same words, maybe in Czech, maybe in English, maybe in the private shorthand that sisters use when they want to end a conversation without ending the relationship:

> *Jsem v pohodě, Klárko. Opravdu.* (I'm fine, Klárka. Really.)

**Klárka pushes — gently, once:**

> *Lžeš.* (You're lying.)

Not angry. Not even accusatory. Said the way you say a simple fact to someone you have known since birth. Klárka is not Mike — she does not have forty years of reading faces behind her. But she has twenty-three years of reading *Simonka's* face, and that is its own kind of expertise.

**Simonka's second deflection — the harder one:**

Simonka's expression changes. Not to anger. To something more complicated — the look of someone who is being seen and cannot afford to be. She holds Klárka's gaze for a moment, and in that moment the audience should feel how close Simonka is to telling the truth, and how far.

Then she says something that is technically honest but structurally evasive:

> *Je to složité. Ale nic špatného se neděje. Slibuji.* (It's complicated. But nothing bad is happening. I promise.)

This is, in its own way, the most painful thing she could say. Because *nothing bad is happening* is not a lie from Simonka's perspective — Holic is consensual, she chose the collar, she chose the doghouse, she is deciding whether to choose it again. She does not consider herself in danger. She considers herself in the middle of a decision. But Klárka cannot know that *complicated* means *I spent the weekend as someone's pet and I have four days left to decide if I want to do it forever.*

The word *složité* (complicated) becomes the lock on a door Klárka does not have the key for.

**Klárka's surrender:**

Klárka could push harder. She chooses not to. Not because she does not care, but because she knows Simonka — knows that pushing will produce a wall, not an opening. Simonka is the daughter who chose psychology over ballet, who watches and studies instead of performing, who has trained herself to analyze people from the outside. You cannot interrogate someone who understands interrogation better than you do.

Klárka does the only thing she can: she stays. She sits with Simonka in the room they grew up in, in the Prague evening, in the house where their parents built an academy out of a dead man's faith and a grandmother's twenty-five million dollars, and she does not leave.

> *Dobře. Ale jsem tady. Kdybys to složité chtěla říct nahlas — jsem vedle.* (Okay. But I'm here. If you ever want to say that complicated thing out loud — I'm next door.)

**Simonka's interior — what the audience sees but Klárka does not:**

Simonka hears this and something in her chest tightens — not rejection, not relief, but the ache of being offered a door she cannot walk through yet. She is still carrying the doghouse. The collar. The bowls. The name GG. The branding iron. The other pet. The warm bath. The *yellow*. The one-week clock, now at three days and counting. She is standing in her childhood bedroom and in Holic's mansion at the same time, and the distance between those two places is the distance she has to close before she can tell Klárka anything at all.

She says: *Vím.* (I know.)

It is not an opening. It is an acknowledgment — the minimum truth she can give her sister without lying.

## BEAT 4 — AFTER: KLÁRKA RETURNS TO KLÁRA

Klárka leaves Simonka's room. She walks back through the house to wherever Klára is — the kitchen, the living room, the hallway outside the studio where the evening light comes through the tall windows and the barre casts a long shadow across the floor.

**The report:**

Klárka tells Klára the truth: she talked to Simonka, and Simonka said she is fine, and it is complicated, and nothing bad is happening, and Klárka does not believe her but she does not know how to prove it.

**Klára absorbs this.**

She does not look surprised. She looks like a woman who has been here before — who sat in a room with a psychologist at fifteen and was told *you need to tell them*, and who knows that no amount of love can force a person to open a door they have decided to keep closed. Mike could not force Klára to speak until she was ready. Klára cannot force Simonka.

**What Klára says:**

> *Bude to v pořádku. Nebo nebude. Ale je to její cesta, Klárko. Můžeme jenom čekat s otevřenýma rukama.* (It will be okay. Or it won't. But it's her path, Klárka. We can only wait with open hands.)

It is the hardest thing a mother can say about a child she is worried about. It is also the truest thing Klára can offer — because Klára was the child who was waited for. Mike waited. Patti Anne waited. The whole Key family waited, with open hands, until Klára was ready to step into them. Klára is extending to Simonka the same grace that was once extended to her.

Then she adds — practical, maternal, the way Klára speaks when she is trying to give someone a place to put their worry so it does not eat them:

> *Teď běž. Máš přípravu. Středa přijde bez ohledu na to, co Simonka řekne nebo neřekne.* (Now go. You have preparation to do. Wednesday comes regardless of what Simonka says or doesn't say.)

**Klárka's interior:**

Klárka accepts this, but she does not like it. She is the older sister. She is supposed to know. She is supposed to be ahead, to have broken the trail, to be the one Simonka follows. Instead she is standing beside her mother in a Prague hallway, watching her sister disappear into a silence she cannot penetrate, and she is — for the first time in her life — the one who is behind.

She goes back to the Mike Key Studio. She picks up the file from the floor. She sits down and tries to read it. The words are there. The criteria are there. The applicant's history is there. Klárka's eyes move across the page. Nothing goes in.

She is a ballet teacher. She can hold position when her muscles burn. She can stand at the barre when everything in her wants to sit down. She will be ready on Wednesday. She will be professional. She will evaluate this dancer with the precision and care that her parents taught her and that her great-grandfather's name on the studio wall demands.

But tonight, she sits on the studio floor with an open file in her lap and a pen that does not move, and she thinks about her sister saying *Je to složité*, and she does not know what it means, and it is eating her alive.

## BEAT 5 — THE FINAL IMAGE: SIMONKA ALONE

After Klárka leaves. After the house settles. After Gamaliel comes back, and Klára does not tell him what she asked Klárka, because some worries belong to the mother and the sister before they belong to the father.

Simonka is alone in her room.

The Prague night is outside the window — the city she grew up in, the city her parents chose because it takes ballet seriously without making it precious, the city where she met Holic Bartunek by accident in Old Town Square and followed him into a world no one in this house can imagine.

She is thinking about GG. About the collar. About the heated doghouse and the two bowls and the sound of Holic's voice when he said *Good Girl.* About the other pet. About the bath. About *yellow.* About the brand — H.B., right buttock, very hot iron — that she has not yet received but already feels like a question burning on her skin.

She is thinking about the driver who will come for her tomorrow, or the next day, or the day after. Who will ask her to take her clothes off and get into the limousine. Who will take her back to Holic's world, where she is not Simonka Káta Key-Dimitrov, daughter of Gamaliel and Klára, sister of Klárka Pavlínka, psychology student at Charles University — but GG. Good Girl.

She has three days left to decide.

Her phone is face-down on the bed. Kleopatra is not in this room — she is downstairs with Klára, or in the hallway, or wherever the golden dog has settled for the night. Simonka is alone in the way that a person is alone when they are carrying a decision that belongs to no one else.

The camera — or the reader's eye — holds on her for a moment. Not on her face. On her hands. The hands that are sitting in her lap, still, holding nothing. The hands that will either put the collar back on or leave it on the floor of Holic's mansion. The hands that no one in this house knows are shaking.

**Cross-cut — briefly, possibly:** Through the wall, or down the hall, or across the courtyard of the house-attached-to-the-academy, the Mike Key Studio light is still on. Klárka is still sitting on the floor with the applicant's file. Two sisters. Two rooms. Two secrets — except Klárka's secret is not a secret at all; it is a Wednesday audition she cannot prepare for because her mind keeps sliding toward her sister's face when she said *složité.*

## PART TWO: MONDAY — THE APARTMENT

*Monday. Three days after the Friday conversations. Two days before the Wednesday audition. Simonka has not been home since the weekend. The weekend came and went — and Simonka's one-week decision period ended, and no one in the family knows what she chose. Klárka has spent Saturday and Sunday trying to prepare for Wednesday and failing. Her pen moves across the applicant's notes but her mind is somewhere else. Now it is Monday. Classes are running. The academy is full of children at the barre. And Klárka has made a decision of her own.*

---

### BEAT M1 — THE ACADEMY, MONDAY AFTERNOON: KLÁRKA TELLS KLÁRA

**Where:** The ballet academy — between classes, or during a break, or in the office that Gamaliel and Klára share. The building is alive with the sound of piano and feet and the particular echo of a school that trains bodies. Through the walls, someone is playing a Chopin nocturne for a centre exercise. The world is ordinary. The academy does not know that one of the founder's daughters has disappeared into a decision no one can see.

**The conversation:**

Klárka finds Klára. Not at home — at the academy. This matters. Klárka is not coming to her mother in the kitchen of the family house, where these conversations have always happened. She is coming to her in the place where they are both professionals: Klára as the founder, Klárka as the teacher. The shift in location is deliberate. Klárka is not asking permission as a daughter. She is informing as an adult.

> *Mami, jdu k ní. Dnes večer. Jdu do jejího bytu.* (Mom, I'm going to her. Tonight. I'm going to her apartment.)

**Klára's reaction — she tries to stop Klárka:**

Klára's face changes. Not to anger — to something harder: the particular tightness of a mother who has been fighting the impulse to do exactly this for three days and has been choosing not to. Klára knows Simonka's apartment near Charles University is Simonka's space. It is the address where Simonka lives when she is not at the family house — the place where she studies, where she keeps her own life, where the academy and the family name and the weight of being a Key-Dimitrov do not follow her through the door. Klára respects that boundary because Klára once needed a boundary like that — a room with a cracked door, a dog on the bed, a space that belonged to her before it belonged to anyone else.

> *Nepůjdeš. Je to její byt. Její prostor. Nemáš právo tam jít bez jejího pozvání.* (You're not going. It's her apartment. Her space. You don't have the right to go there without her invitation.)

**Klárka pushes back:**

> *Nemůžu jenom čekat, Mami. Čekala jsem celý víkend. Nic. Žádná zpráva, žádný hovor. Nic. Středa je za dva dny a já nemůžu myslet na tu pitomou audienci, když nevíš — když my nevíme — jestli je v pořádku.* (I can't just wait, Mom. I waited all weekend. Nothing. No message, no call. Nothing. Wednesday is in two days and I can't think about that stupid audition when we don't know — when we don't know if she's okay.)

**Klára holds:**

> *Řekla složité. Řekla nic špatného. To musí stačit, Klárko. Někdy stačit musí.* (She said complicated. She said nothing bad is happening. That has to be enough, Klárka. Sometimes it has to be enough.)

**Klárka breaks through — the thing she has not said until now:**

> *Ale nestačí, Mami. Ne mně.* (But it's not enough, Mom. Not for me.)

This is the moment where Klárka stops being the daughter who follows her mother's wisdom and becomes the sister who acts on her own instinct. Klára chose to wait — chose open hands, chose Mike's example, chose to trust that Simonka would come to them when she was ready. Klárka cannot make that choice. She is not Klára. She does not have Klára's history of being the one who was waited for. She has only the present fact of a sister who has been gone for three days and an apartment in the city with a door she can knock on.

**Klára's surrender — reluctant, pained:**

Klára does not give permission. She does not say *go.* She stops arguing. There is a difference. The silence between them is the sound of a mother recognizing that her daughter is going to do something she disagrees with, and that the disagreement cannot prevent it. Klára has raised Klárka to be exactly this — someone who sees a closed door and decides whether to knock. The irony is that Klára does not want her to knock on this one.

> *Vezmi si někoho s sebou. Nejdi sama.* (Take someone with you. Don't go alone.)

It is not blessing. It is damage control — the same instinct that made Mike call the OPP instead of going himself, the same instinct that made Patti Anne send Aleksander to the hallway when Klára disclosed the relationship. If Klárka is going to walk into whatever Simonka is hiding, Klára wants someone beside her.

**Raphael:**

### BEAT W1 — WEDNESDAY MORNING: ARRIVAL

## WHAT THIS SCENE ESTABLISHES

1. **The generational echo of secrecy.** Klára was the girl who said *nic není* to the people who loved her most. Now Simonka sits at the same table and does the same thing. The difference is that Klára's secret was abuse — something being done to her — while Simonka's secret is a choice she is making. But the mechanics are the same: the closed face, the denied worry, the room full of people who would help if she would only say the words. Klára sees herself in Simonka and cannot reach her.

2. **Klárka's position as the older sister who does not know.** The profile establishes that Klárka is close enough to be asked about Simonka, but Simonka is secretive enough that Klárka cannot explain her emotional shift. This scene dramatizes that asymmetry. Klárka is not a bad sister. She is an older sister who has discovered that being first does not mean being told. This is a new and humbling experience for her, and it will shape her relationship with Simonka going forward.

3. **Simonka's isolation is chosen, not imposed.** Unlike Klára's childhood secrecy — which was driven by fear of the system and fear of losing her family — Simonka's secrecy is driven by something else: the knowledge that her family cannot understand what she is choosing, and the desire to make the choice without their judgment. She is not hiding because she is afraid. She is hiding because she is *free*, and freedom tastes like something her family might try to take away. This is a crucial distinction.

4. **The weight of *složité*.** The word *complicated* becomes Simonka's shield. It is honest — her situation is genuinely complicated — and impenetrable, because *complicated* can mean anything and therefore means nothing to the person hearing it. It is the most sophisticated deflection in the scene: not a lie, not a wall, just a door that only opens from one side.

5. **Klára's restraint is Mike's inheritance.** Klára does not push. She waits. She offers open hands. This is what Mike did for her — he sat on her bed and told her she was not losing this family, and then he waited. He did not demand the truth. He made the truth safe to tell. Klára is doing the same thing for Simonka. She cannot guarantee the outcome, but she can guarantee the posture: *I am here, and I will not make you sorry for telling me, whenever you are ready.*

6. **The countdown is invisible.** The audience knows that Simonka has days left. Klára and Klárka do not. Every gentle word, every open hand, every moment of waiting is happening against a clock only Simonka can see. The dramatic irony gives the scene its tension: the reader is screaming *ask her about the collar, ask her about the doghouse, ask her about the man in Old Town Square* — and the characters cannot, because they do not know those things exist.

7. **The hands.** The final image — Simonka's hands in her lap, holding nothing — echoes the physical vocabulary of the saga: Klára's fingers in Willow's fur during the pre-cruise disclosure, Gamaliel's hands on Mike's floor, Mike's hand on the back of a chair turned into a barre. The body tells the truth the voice will not. Simonka's hands are still. Her hands are shaking. Both are true.

8. **Klárka's audition as counterpoint to Simonka's secret.** The Wednesday audition gives Klárka her own stakes, her own weight, her own storyline that does not depend on her sister. It also creates a structural mirror: both sisters are making a decision about who enters a world. Simonka is deciding whether to enter Holic's world permanently. Klárka is deciding whether to let a new student enter her family's world. Both decisions are about thresholds. Both decisions are made alone. But Klárka's decision is professional, trusted, blessed by her parents — she has support, she has authority, she has a studio named after her great-grandfather. Simonka's decision is none of those things. The contrast is exact: one sister stands at the front of a room deciding who comes in; the other stands at the threshold of a mansion deciding whether she will kneel.

9. **Klára's advice — *soustřeď se na středu* — is both wise and insufficient.** Klára tells Klárka to focus on Wednesday because Wednesday is something she can control. It is good maternal advice. It is also a dodge — a way of saying *I don't know how to help Simonka either, so let me help you instead.* Klárka follows it. She goes back to the studio. She prepares. She conducts the audition with clarity. And then, when it is over, the worry is still there, on the shelf where she put it, waiting. The advice held for five days. It did not solve anything. It was never meant to.

10. **The diary is Simonka's voice when her mouth cannot speak.** The *Přečti mě* sticky note is the most important sentence in the entire sequence. Simonka spent a week saying *nic není* — nothing is wrong — and then went home and wrote *Read me* on a diary and left it in the center of a kitchen table. The gap between what Simonka says and what Simonka writes is the gap between the woman who can kneel for Holic and the woman who cannot face her sister. The diary is not a betrayal of her secrecy. It is the culmination of it — the moment where the pressure of holding the secret alone produces its own release valve, not in speech but in writing, not to her mother's face but to her sister's hands.

11. **Klára's attempt to stop Klárka — and Klárka's refusal to obey.** This is the first real disagreement between Klára and Klárka in the saga. Klára's instinct is restraint: respect the boundary, wait, trust the process. Klárka's instinct is action: go, look, find out. Neither is wrong. Klára's instinct comes from experience — she knows what it cost her to be pushed before she was ready. Klárka's instinct comes from love — she cannot stand another day of not knowing. The collision between them produces the Monday apartment visit, which produces the diary, which produces the *Read me*, which changes everything. Klára tried to protect Simonka's boundary. Klárka broke through it. And Simonka was the one who left the door open.

12. **Raphael as witness.** Raphael's presence at the apartment is not incidental. He is the first outsider to stand in the space where Simonka's secret life meets her family's worry. He does not read the diary. He does not need to. He sees Klárka's face when she finds it, and that is enough to make him a witness to something that will reshape the family he is trying to enter. His role in this scene is to be present without consuming — to stand in the doorway of a kitchen that is not his and hold the silence for the woman he is with.
