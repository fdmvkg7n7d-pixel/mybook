## SCENE PURPOSE

Klára asks Rabbi Löwy to find her great-grandmothers in the Bohemian records — and he does. The scene turns the saga's longest-carried inheritance from **memory into document**: the line she has carried since childhood (quietly, portably, hers) gets names, dates, addresses, and — because this is Bohemia — transport numbers.

Three things at once:

1. **For Klára:** the completion of what the drawer always was. She has kept her inheritance the way she kept everything — light, portable, undocumented. This is the day it becomes *heavy on purpose*: paper, ink, the registry's permanence. The girl who owned nothing she couldn't carry chooses to own something that cannot be moved.
2. **For the halachic question [long-flagged]:** the records may establish her **maternal line** — and with it, whether Klára is halachically Jewish already. [The saga has kept this deliberately open. This scene is where it can close — see "The Finding," below.]
3. **For the family ahead:** whatever is found becomes the daughters' inheritance too — the line Klárka and Simonka stand in, written down at last.

## SCENE ARC

### 1. The Search (montage-able; weeks, compressed)

- Löwy works the way the community has always worked: the **birth, marriage, and death registers** of Bohemian Jewish communities (the *matriky* — held today in the Czech National Archives), the federation's own books, the **Terezín transport lists**, the Yad Vashem central database, pages of testimony.
- Beats available [choose in drafting]: microfilm and digitized scans side by side; an archivist colleague of Löwy's [one-scene character, TBD]; the specific Bohemian town's register reached at last — a rabbi's narrow brown ledger from the 1920s, photographed page by page.
- Gamaliel's role: logistics, drives, coffee — and **not reading anything before Klára does.** House rule, never stated.

### 2. The Summons

- Löwy calls them in. On his desk, a folder. He does not slide it across. He says [working line]: *"I will tell you what is in it, and then it is yours. Or you take it home unopened and read it when you want. Both are done here. There is no wrong order."* — the drawer-keeper offered, one last time, control of the pace. 
- Klára: *"Tell me."* [She has spent years earning the ability to say that.]

### 4. Klára with the folder

- However the telling goes, there is a moment where the folder is finally in her hands and she reads the names herself — **out loud, once, both of them.** The first time the great-grandmothers' names have been spoken by their line in [decades — TBD].
- Gamaliel's place in the beat: beside her, silent. [He is, by now, a Jew himself — the convert watching the born daughter of the line read her own citizenship. Neither of them says the obvious thing. The obvious thing is the scene.]
- What Klára does after [proposal, very her]: she does not frame the documents. Copies go into the drawer — the drawer's *first contents that are answers instead of questions* — and the originals' scans go to [the daughters' boxes — the files she keeps for Klárka and Simonka, TBD whether established elsewhere].

## SAGA CONNECTIONS

- **The drawer's last conversion:** questions in, answers out. The girl who kept things light ends the scene owning the heaviest objects in the saga — and chooses to.
- **The halachic close [if accepted]:** Klára Jewish by birth; the daughters likewise through her. Gamaliel remains the family's one convert — the chooser among the born — which sharpens, not blurs, what his choosing meant.
- **Terezín, rhymed:** the teenage trip planted it; the records pay it off. Story → name → place, across two visits and ten years.
- **Löwy's offer, honored:** made at the first meeting, never repeated, redeemed on her clock. Both of Klára's Prague rooms — Dvořáková's and Löwy's — turn out to run on the same rule: *her pace, always.* [Dvořáková echo: the work in Czech and the work in the registers likely intertwine — a session after the finding is its own candidate beat.]
