# SCENE OUTLINE: THE APARTMENT — KLÁRKA AND RAPHAEL FIND SIMONKA'S DIARY

*Klárka Pavlínka defies her mother and goes to Simonka's apartment with Raphael on Monday evening. Simonka is not home. Klárka lets herself in with the key Simonka gave her. The apartment is empty. On the kitchen table, in the center, sits a diary with a sticky note that says Přečti mě. Klárka reads part of it. She takes it home. The family now holds a fragment of the truth.*

---

## TIMING & CANON BACKGROUND

- **When:** Monday evening. Three days after the Friday conversations at the family house. Two days before Klárka's Wednesday audition. Simonka's one-week Holic/GG decision period has ended. The family does not know what she chose.
- **Where:** Simonka's apartment — a flat in Prague, near Charles University, where Simonka lives when she is not at the family house. The apartment is Simonka's own space: her books, her desk, her psychology research, her life outside the academy and the Key-Dimitrov name.
- **Who:** Klárka Pavlínka (older sister, early twenties, ballet teacher at the academy), Raphael (Klárka's partner). Simonka Káta is absent.
- **The key:** Klárka has a key to Simonka's apartment. Simonka gave it to her — an act of trust between sisters, an unspoken agreement: *my door is always open to you.* Klárka is not breaking in. She is using an invitation that was given freely, at a time when Simonka may or may not have intended it to be used like this.
- **What the audience knows:** Simonka spent last weekend as GG — Holic Bartunek's consensual human pet. She wore his collar, slept in his doghouse, ate from bowls, answered to *Good Girl*, and was given one week to decide whether to become his pet permanently. The week is over. The diary on the kitchen table contains Simonka's account of all of it — or some of it, or as much of it as she could bring herself to write. The sticky note says *Přečti mě*: Read me.

---

## BEAT 1 — THE ACADEMY, MONDAY AFTERNOON: KLÁRKA TELLS KLÁRA

*This beat is summarized here for context. It is detailed in the parent outline.*

**Where:** The ballet academy, between classes.

**What happens:** Klárka tells Klára she is going to Simonka's apartment tonight. Klára tries to stop her — *Je to její prostor. Nemáš právo* (It's her space. You don't have the right). Klárka refuses to obey — *Ale nestačí, Mami. Ne mně* (But it's not enough, Mom. Not for me). Klára relents but insists Klárka not go alone. Klárka says Raphael will go with her. Klára's final instruction: *Když najdeš něco — cokoliv — zavoláš mi. Okamžitě* (If you find something — anything — you call me. Immediately).

**What Klárka carries into the evening:** Her mother's reluctant permission, her own determination, the applicant's file still unprepared, and the key to her sister's apartment, which has been on her keyring for as long as Simonka has lived there.

---

## BEAT 2 — THE WALK: PRAGUE EVENING

**Where:** The streets of Prague, between the academy and Simonka's apartment. Evening. The city is doing what Prague does in the evening — trams, windows lighting up, the last of the daylight catching on rooftops and cobblestones and the Vltava somewhere in the distance. The air is cold. Autumn or winter — whichever season holds this chapter.

**Klárka and Raphael:**

They walk together. Or they take the tram. Or Raphael drives, or they take a taxi. The mode of transport matters less than the space between them — the silence of two people heading toward something one of them does not fully understand.

Raphael knows only what Klárka has told him: her sister is hiding something, she hasn't been home, she hasn't called, the family is worried. He does not know about Holic. He does not know about GG. He does not know that the Key-Dimitrov family carries secrets the way other families carry heirlooms — passed down, inherited, hidden in plain sight.

**What Raphael says — or doesn't:**

He may ask: *Řekneš mi, co se děje?* (Will you tell me what's going on?) Klárka may answer honestly — *Nevím. To je ten problém.* (I don't know. That's the problem.) He may put his hand on hers. He may say nothing at all and simply walk beside her, because sometimes the most useful thing a person can do is occupy the space next to someone who is about to enter a room alone.

**Klárka's interior during the walk:**

She is thinking about the key. The key on her keyring — next to her apartment key, next to her studio key, next to whatever else Klárka Pavlínka carries on a daily basis. The key to Simonka's apartment. Simonka gave it to her. When? The day Simonka moved in? A birthday? A random Tuesday when Simonka said *tady, pro jistotu* (here, just in case) and pressed it into Klárka's palm without ceremony? Whenever it was, the gesture meant something: *I trust you inside my life when I am not there.* Klárka is about to test that trust. She is not sure whether she is honoring it or breaking it.

She is also thinking about Wednesday. The audition. The applicant's face in the file — a face she has memorized but cannot quite see, because every time she tries to focus on it, Simonka's face appears instead, saying *Je to složité* in a voice that was almost steady.

She is thinking about her mother's face when she said *Nepůjdeš.* The tightness of it. The fear underneath the command. Klára, who was the girl who kept secrets, was now the mother trying to protect her other daughter's right to keep them. The irony is not lost on Klárka.

---

## BEAT 3 — THE BUILDING: ARRIVING AT SIMONKA'S APARTMENT

**The street:**

Simonka's building is in a Prague neighborhood that is student-friendly — near Charles University, near tram lines, near the cafés and bookshops where psychology students sit with laptops and pretend to study while actually watching people. The building is not fancy. It is not the family house. It is not the academy. It is Simonka's. That is the point.

**The entrance:**

Klárka and Raphael stand at the front door of the building. The street is quiet. The building door requires a code or a buzzer — Klárka knows the code, because Simonka gave it to her, because this is what sisters do: they share the small passwords that let each other in.

They enter. The hallway. The stairs or the lift. The door to Simonka's flat.

**The door:**

Klárka stands in front of the door. She has the key in her hand. She can feel the shape of it — smaller than her studio key, newer, on a ring that Simonka chose (something simple, or something with a charm, or something completely ordinary). The key is real. The door is real. The apartment on the other side is Simonka's.

Raphael stands behind her. Close enough to be present. Far enough to give her room.

**Does she knock?**

Klárka knocks. Once. Out of habit, or out of respect — because even though she has a key, knocking is what you do before you enter someone's space. The knock lands on the door and dies. No sound from inside. No footsteps. No voice.

She knocks again. *Simonko?* Quiet, almost a whisper, as if she is afraid of waking someone who is not there.

Nothing.

She puts the key in the lock.

**The threshold:**

The door opens. Klárka steps inside. Raphael follows. The apartment is dark — not the dark of a place that has been empty for days, but the dark of a place where no one is home right now. A lamp may be on, or the kitchen light, or nothing at all. The air inside is still. It smells like Simonka — or like Simonka's absence: coffee, books, the faint trace of whatever shampoo she uses, the specific silence of a space that belongs to someone who is not in it.

> *Simonko? Jsi doma?* (Simonko? Are you home?)

The question is a formality. The apartment answers before Simonka can: she is not here.

---

## BEAT 4 — THE APARTMENT: EMPTY

**Raphael's position:**

Raphael stands in the entryway for a moment. He takes off his shoes — the Czech way, because you do not wear shoes inside someone's apartment, even when that someone is not home, even when you are there because you are worried. He places them neatly by the door. He waits. He is not going to walk through Simonka's apartment like he belongs there. He will follow Klárka. He will be where she needs him. He will not look at things she does not ask him to look at.

**Klárka moves through the apartment:**

She does not search. Not yet. She walks — the way you walk through someone's space when you are checking, not snooping. She moves through the living area first: the couch, the small desk with Simonka's laptop (closed, dark), the bookshelf with psychology textbooks and research papers and a framed photo that Klárka does not stop to look at because she already knows what it shows (their parents at the academy, probably, or the two of them as children, or the family at Christmas — some image of the world Simonka came from, placed in the apartment where she lives now).

The bathroom: empty. A towel draped over the rod. A toothbrush in a cup. The ordinary evidence of a life that is being lived, not abandoned.

The bedroom: the bed is not made, or it is made loosely, or the covers are pulled up without care — the particular dishevelment of a person who makes their bed sometimes but not always, and who definitely did not expect anyone to be checking. A book on the nightstand, spine up, pages open to wherever Simonka stopped reading. A charger cable plugged into the wall but not connected to anything — Simonka took her phone with her. A sweater over the back of a chair. The window with curtains that may or may not be fully closed.

Simonka is not here. But Simonka was here — recently enough that the apartment still holds her, recently enough that her life is still in the process of being lived, not packed up. She did not leave. She is simply somewhere else.

**The kitchen:**

Klárka turns the corner into the kitchen and stops.

---

## BEAT 5 — THE KITCHEN TABLE: THE DIARY

**What Klárka sees:**

A small kitchen. A window. A counter with a coffee maker, a mug in the sink or on the draining board, the ordinary clutter of a person who drinks coffee and does not always wash the cup immediately. A table — small, two or three chairs, the kind of kitchen table where one person eats breakfast alone and reads the news on their phone before leaving for the university.

In the center of this table — not pushed to the side, not tucked under a magazine, not hidden in a drawer — is a book.

It is a diary.

Not a notebook — not one of the composition books Simonka uses for class, not a research journal, not a legal pad. A diary: the kind with a cover that is soft or hard or leather or plain, the kind that is specifically for private thoughts, the kind that announces itself by existing. It is closed. It is sitting in the exact center of the table, where no one entering the kitchen could miss it.

**The sticky note:**

On the cover — or on the first page, or stuck to the front cover where it cannot be overlooked — is a sticky note. A small square of colored paper. Yellow, or pink, or blue — whatever color Simonka grabbed when she wrote it. The adhesive is still good. The edges are flat. The note was placed carefully, not slapped on in a hurry.

Simonka's handwriting. Two words:

> **Přečti mě.**

Read me.

**Klárka's reaction:**

She stops moving. Her whole body stops — the way a body stops when it recognizes that the thing in front of it is not ordinary. Not the diary itself — diaries exist, they are not strange. But the placement. The note. The deliberate centering of a private object in a public space. The invitation written in handwriting Klárka has known since Simonka learned to hold a pen.

This is not a diary Simonka forgot.

This is a diary Simonka left.

**Raphael sees her freeze:**

He is behind her, in the doorway of the kitchen. He cannot see what Klárka sees — not clearly, not yet. He sees her back go still. He sees her hand rise and stop in the air, halfway toward the table, as if the diary is hot or fragile or both. He sees the shape of her shoulders change — the particular change that happens when someone realizes they have been handed something they were not expecting to carry.

He may say her name. *Klárko?* Quiet. Testing. The way you say someone's name when you want to know if they are still in the room with you or if they have already gone somewhere else.

**Klárka picks up the diary:**

Her hand completes the motion. She picks it up. The diary is not heavy — it is paper and a cover and a sticky note and whatever Simonka wrote inside. But Klárka holds it the way you hold something that has been left for you: carefully, because the person who left it chose to trust you with it, and that choice has weight.

She reads the sticky note again. *Přečti mě.* Simonka's handwriting. The same hand that held a pen to write *Je to složité* in the air between them on Friday — except on Friday the words were a deflection, and now the words are an invitation.

**The difference between *složité* and *Přečti mě*:**

On Friday, Simonka said *Je to složité* and meant *I cannot tell you.* On Monday, Simonka wrote *Přečti mě* and meant *I am telling you.* The distance between those two statements is the distance between a woman who cannot speak and a woman who found another way to open her mouth. Simonka could not say it to Klárka's face — could not sit across from her sister and form the words about Holic, about the collar, about the doghouse, about GG. But she could sit alone in this apartment and write it down, and then she could leave it on the kitchen table with a note that says *Read me,* and then she could walk out the door and let the diary do the work her voice could not.

This is not surrender. This is strategy. Simonka is a psychology student. She understands that the medium changes the message. Speaking requires facing the other person's reaction in real time. Writing allows the words to arrive without the writer present to see them land. *Přečti mě* is Simonka choosing the method that lets her be honest without being present for the consequences. It is not cowardice. It is the most controlled form of disclosure available to someone who has spent a week controlling everything.

## BEAT 6 — READING: KLÁRKA READS PART OF THE DIARY

**Klárka sits down.**

She pulls out a kitchen chair and sits. The diary is in her hands. Raphael is still in the doorway. He can see that something has shifted — that Klárka has crossed from *checking* into *reading*, and that this is no longer a visit but an event.

He may ask: *Co to je?* (What is it?) Klárka may answer without looking up: *Její deník. Nechala ho tady. S vzkazem.* (Her diary. She left it here. With a note.) She may not explain further. The diary is not his. The diary is not hers either — but the note says *Read me*, and Klárka is going to obey.

**She opens it.**

The cover lifts. The first page, or the last page, or wherever Simonka's account begins. The handwriting is Simonka's — familiar, slightly rushed in places, careful in others. Klárka has seen this handwriting her whole life: on birthday cards, on notes left on the fridge, on shopping lists, on the margin of a textbook. But she has never seen this handwriting say *these* things.

**What Klárka reads:**

She does not read the whole diary. She reads part of it. Enough.

[TBD: The exact contents of what she reads are TBD and may be written as a separate diary document. But the shape of what Klárka absorbs in this beat is as follows:]

She reads about **a man named Holic Bartunek**. A meeting in Old Town Square — by accident, by fate, by whatever word Simonka chose for the moment a stranger looked at her and saw something that made him stop walking. A first date at a restaurant called Marie B. He ordered her meal for her. She did not know it was a test.

She reads about **a mansion**. A tour. A room dedicated to a dead Wolfdog — photographs on the walls, an urn in the center, an electric dog collar that Holic explained was a training signal, a boundary, a form of care. He asked her if she wanted to try it on. She said yes.

She reads about **the collar**. About taking her clothes off. About sitting on the floor. About being led outside to a doghouse — heated, prepared, waiting. About the name he gave her: **Good Girl. GG.**

Klárka stops.

She does not stop because she is disgusted. She does not stop because she is angry. She stops because she has reached a point in the reading where the words on the page stop being information and start being her sister. Her sister — the girl who was born fifteen months after her, who took her first steps while Klárka watched, who chose psychology over ballet, who said *nic není* at the dinner table with a voice that was almost steady — is describing what it felt like to kneel on the floor of a stranger's mansion and wear a dead dog's collar, and Klárka cannot take the next sentence yet.

**Raphael in the doorway:**

He watches her face change. He cannot see what she is reading. He can see what it is doing to her: the way her jaw tightens, the way her breathing shifts from regular to controlled, the way her fingers press harder against the pages as if she is holding the book still because her hands want to do something else. He has never seen Klárka's face like this. He has seen her focused, he has seen her tired, he has seen her frustrated by a student who cannot hold a relevé. He has not seen her read something that makes her look like the floor has rearranged itself beneath her chair.

He does not ask again what it says. He moves to the counter, finds the coffee maker or the kettle, and makes something — not because anyone wants coffee, but because his hands need to do something useful while Klárka's hands hold something that is breaking her open. The small domestic act of heating water in someone else's kitchen while the person you love reads her sister's secrets. This is what Raphael can offer. It is not enough. It is not nothing.

**Klárka continues — but not all the way:**

She reads more. She reads about the bowls — water and food. About the training stages. About the name training, the collar training, the position training, the bowl training. She may read about the toilet training, or she may skip ahead — her eyes moving faster than her comprehension, scanning for the shape of the thing rather than every detail, because the shape is already enough and every additional sentence adds weight she is not sure she can carry home.

She reads about **the one-week decision**. Holic gave her a week to decide whether she wanted to become his pet permanently. He let her stand up, go home, think. She was supposed to return by the end of the week with an answer.

She reads about **the Friday meal** — the meal at Gamaliel and Klára's house, where Simonka sat at the table and said *nic není* while carrying the entire weight of the doghouse and the collar and the name GG inside her body. The diary describes what it felt like to sit across from her parents and lie. Klárka reads this and recognizes it — not the specific content, but the act: the effort of sitting still when the truth is screaming. She knows what that costs. She has watched her mother's face do the same thing for years, without knowing what her mother was hiding.

She may read about **the return to Holic** — the driver, the limousine, the undressing, the club member, the branding iron, the other pet, the *yellow*, the warm bath. Or she may stop before she reaches that part. The diary is thick. Simonka wrote a lot. Klárka reads until she hits a threshold inside herself — a point where the next sentence will change something she cannot change back, and she is not ready.

**The threshold:**

Klárka stops reading. Not at the end — at a point in the middle where she has enough to understand the shape of Simonka's secret but not enough to know its full dimensions. She knows: there is a man named Holic Bartunek. He has a mansion. He gave Simonka a collar and a name and a doghouse and a week to decide. Simonka chose something — Klárka does not know what. The diary describes the decision period but may or may not include the outcome.

This is partial knowledge. The worst kind. Enough to terrify. Not enough to act on. Enough to know that her sister has entered a world Klárka cannot imagine. Not enough to know whether Simonka is safe, whether she wants to be there, whether she needs help or needs to be left alone.

**She closes the diary.**

Her hand comes down on the cover. Not a slam — a controlled press, the way you close something when the act of closing it is the only way to stop it from consuming you. The diary shuts. The sticky note — *Přečti mě* — stares up at her from the cover.

She has obeyed. She has read. She has read enough. The rest will wait.

## BEAT 7 — RAPHAEL

**Raphael asks — once:**

The kettle clicks off, or the coffee maker finishes, or he simply stands at the counter with two mugs he has no intention of filling. He looks at Klárka. He sees what the reading has done to her posture — the way she is holding the diary against her chest now, not like a book but like something she is protecting, or something that is protecting her.

> *Klárko.*

She looks at him. Her eyes are not wet. They are the eyes of a person who has just absorbed something too large for tears — something that has gone past sadness into a kind of stillness that is harder to reach and harder to break.

> *Co píše?* (What does it say?)

**Klárka's answer:**

She does not tell him everything. She cannot — not because she does not trust Raphael, but because the words are still forming inside her, and speaking them too soon would make them smaller than they are. She gives him the shape:

> *Potkala někoho. Muže. Je... je to složité, Raphaeli. Dělá věci, které — nečekla jsem.* (She met someone. A man. It's... it's complicated, Raphael. She's doing things I — I didn't expect.)

She does not say *collar.* She does not say *doghouse.* She does not say *Good Girl.* Those words belong to the diary and to Simonka and to the space between sisters that even Raphael cannot enter yet.

Raphael nods. He does not push. He puts down the mugs. He comes to the table and sits across from Klárka, and for a moment they are two people sitting at Simonka's kitchen table in an empty apartment with a closed diary between them, and the Prague night is outside the window, and somewhere in this city Simonka is doing something her sister has just read about and cannot fully understand.

> *Půjdeme domů?* (Should we go home?)

Klárka nods.

---

## BEAT 8 — LEAVING: KLÁRKA TAKES THE DIARY HOME

**The decision:**

Klárka picks up the diary. She does not put it back on the table. She does not leave it where she found it. She puts it in her bag — or under her arm, or in her coat, or wherever it goes when a person decides to carry their sister's secrets out of their sister's apartment.

The sticky note is still on the cover. *Přečti mě.* Klárka has read part of it. The rest is still inside, waiting. The diary will come home with her. It will be in Klárka's room tonight. It will be in her hands or in a drawer or on her desk, and the unread pages will be as present as the ones she has already seen.

**Raphael does not comment on this.**

He watches her take the diary. He understands — without being told — that the diary is leaving with them. This is not a discussion. Klárka is taking it. He does not ask whether she should. That question belongs to the space between Klárka and Simonka, and Raphael is not standing in that space. He is standing beside it, holding the door.

**They leave the apartment:**

Klárka checks the kitchen one more time — the table, the counter, the window. The apartment is empty and dark. The diary is no longer on the table. The sticky note is no longer visible from the doorway. The kitchen looks the way it would look if no one had been here at all — except for the coffee maker that Raphael touched and the two mugs he moved and did not fill, and the chair that is pulled out slightly from the table where Klárka sat and read about her sister's other life.

She closes the door behind her. She locks it with the key — because you do not leave your sister's apartment unlocked, even when you have taken something out of it that was not given to you in person.

The key goes back on her ring. The diary goes home.

---

## BEAT 9 — THE WALK HOME: WHAT KLÁRKA CARRIES

**The Prague street at night:**

They walk. The tram rattles past. The city is cold and ordinary and does not know that a woman is walking through it carrying a diary that contains her sister's secret life. The streetlights come on, or they are already on, and the cobblestones are wet or dry, and Prague is beautiful in the way it is always beautiful — in the way that cities are beautiful when they do not care about the people walking through them.

**Klárka's interior:**

She is not crying. She is not shaking. She is holding the diary against her body — inside her coat, or in her bag pressed against her ribs, or in her hand with her fingers wrapped around the cover — and she is thinking.

She is thinking about Simonka at the dinner table saying *nic není* while the diary in her bag held the words she could not say.

She is thinking about the collar. The dead Wolfdog's collar. The electric dog collar that Simonka chose to try on. The collar that Klárka has now read about but has never seen, and cannot imagine on her sister's neck.

She is thinking about the doghouse. Heated. Prepared. Waiting. A doghouse for a human being. For her sister. For the girl who was born fifteen months after her and who grew up in the same room, the same house, the same academy, and who has somehow ended up kneeling in a stranger's yard answering to a name that is not her own.

She is thinking about *Good Girl.* Two words. A name and a verdict. Simonka — who speaks three languages, who studies psychology at Charles University, who watches people for a living — answering to *Good Girl* the way Kleopatra answers to her own name: by coming, by sitting, by waiting for the next instruction.

She is thinking: *Is she safe?*

She is thinking: *Does she want to be there?*

She is thinking: *She said nic špatného se neděje — nothing bad is happening. Was that true? Is this what 'nothing bad' looks like?*

She is thinking about her mother. Klára, who said *nepůjdeš* — don't go. Klára, who tried to protect Simonka's boundary. Klára, who carried her own secrets for years and knows what it costs to have them read without your permission. Klára is going to ask what Klárka found. Klárka is going to have to tell her.

She is thinking about Wednesday. The audition. The applicant. The Mike Key Studio. Two days from now. She is supposed to stand at the front of a room and evaluate a dancer with clarity and precision and care, and she has just read that her sister spent a weekend in a doghouse.

**Raphael walks beside her.**

He does not speak. He does not reach for the diary. He keeps pace with her stride. His presence is not a comfort exactly — it is a fact. He is here. He saw the kitchen table. He saw her face when she read. He did not read the diary himself, and he will not ask to. But he was there. He witnessed the moment the diary changed from a book on a table into the heaviest thing Klárka has ever carried.

## BEAT 10 — THE CALL: KLÁRKA TELLS KLÁRA

**When:** Later that evening. Klárka and Raphael are back at the family house, or at Klárka's own place, or wherever Klárka goes when she needs to make a phone call she has been dreading since she left the academy.

**The call:**

Klárka calls Klára. Or she finds Klára in the house. Or Klára is waiting — because of course Klára is waiting, because she has been waiting since Klárka walked out of the academy this afternoon, and she has been watching the clock, and she has been sitting with Kleopatra at her feet and a mug of something she has not touched, and she has been telling herself that Klárka will call, that Klárka will call, that Klárka will call.

**Klárka speaks first:**

> *Mami.*

One word. But Klára hears it — the tone of it, the weight behind it, the way *Mami* sounds different when the person saying it is carrying something they did not have when they left the house this morning.

> *Našla jsem něco.* (I found something.)

**Klára asks:**

> *Co?* (What?)

**Klárka tells her:**

She does not read the diary over the phone. She describes it. The diary on the kitchen table. The sticky note. *Přečti mě.* She tells Klára that she read part of it. She tells Klára what it contains — the shape of it, not every word. A man. Holic Bartunek. A collar. A doghouse. A name — Good Girl. A decision. A week.

She does not tell Klára about the bowls, or the toilet training, or the branding iron, or the other pet, or the *yellow* in the bath. Those details are still inside the diary, still unread or read but not spoken aloud, and Klárka is not going to be the person who puts them into her mother's mouth. Not tonight. Not over the phone. Not like this.

**Klára's reaction:**

Silence. A long silence. The kind of silence that happens when a woman who was once a child carrying a secret hears that her child has been carrying a secret, and the weight of both secrets — hers and Simonka's — lands on her at the same time.

Klára does not say *I told you not to go.* She does not say *you should have waited.* She does not say any of the things she might be entitled to say, because what Klárka is describing — a collar, a doghouse, a name, a man — is beyond the scope of *I told you so.* Klára's objections were about boundaries. Klárka's discovery is about something else entirely.

> *Přivezla jsi ho domů?* (Did you bring it home?)

> *Ano.*

> *Kde je Simonka?*

> *Nevím. Byla tam* — Klárka's voice breaks slightly, or it doesn't, but it comes close — *jenom deník.*

Klára does not respond immediately.

When she speaks again, her voice has changed. Not harder. Quieter. The voice of a woman who has just learned that her daughter is living inside something she cannot reach, and that the diary on the kitchen table was the closest Simonka could come to asking for help — or to offering an explanation — or to saying *this is who I am now, and I needed someone to know.*

> *Přečteš mi ho. Zítra. Ne po telefonu.* (You'll read it to me. Tomorrow. Not on the phone.)

It is not a question.

---

## BEAT 11 — KLÁRKA ALONE: THE DIARY IN HER ROOM

**After the call. After Raphael has gone or is asleep or is sitting quietly in another room. After the house is dark and Kléopatra has settled and Gamaliel has asked no questions because Klára has told him nothing yet.**

Klárka is alone with the diary.

She puts it on her desk. Or on her nightstand. Or on the floor beside her bed, where she can see it if she opens her eyes. The sticky note is still on the cover. *Přečti mě.* She has read part of it. The rest is inside — the pages she skipped, the sentences she could not finish, the details that wait like stones at the bottom of a lake.

She does not open it again tonight. She has carried enough for one day. Wednesday is in two days. The applicant's file is somewhere in this room, unopened since Friday. The audition will happen whether Klárka is ready or not. The diary will be here when she wakes up.

**The final image:**

Klárka lies in bed. The diary is on the nightstand. Her hand rests near it — not touching, but close enough that she can feel the edge of the cover with her fingertips. The room is dark. The house is quiet. Somewhere in Prague, Simonka is wherever Simonka is — with Holic, or alone, or in the doghouse, or in a mansion, or in a limousine, or somewhere Klárka cannot imagine because the diary only told her part of the story and the rest is still waiting to be read.

Klárka closes her eyes.

She does not sleep.

The diary does not move.

## WHAT THIS SCENE ESTABLISHES

1. **The diary as Simonka's chosen voice.** Simonka could not say *I am someone's pet* to her sister's face. She could write it in a book and leave the book on a table with a note that says *Read me.* The diary is not a violation of Simonka's privacy — it is Simonka's deliberate, controlled disclosure. She chose the medium, the timing, and the audience. *Přečti mě* is the most intentional sentence Simonka has produced all week.

2. **Partial knowledge is worse than no knowledge.** Klárka read enough to know the shape of Simonka's secret — collar, doghouse, Good Girl — but not enough to know the outcome. She does not know whether Simonka accepted. She does not know about the branding, or the club, or the other pet, or the full extent of the training. She has the outline of a world she cannot fully see, and the outline is terrifying enough that filling in the details might break something.

3. **The key as trust tested.** Simonka gave Klárka a key. Klárka used it. The question of whether this was a violation of trust or an honoring of it hangs over every beat. Simonka gave the key meaning *come in if you need to.* Klárka came in because she needed to. The *Přečti mě* note retroactively validates the intrusion: Simonka left the diary for someone who had the key. She may have left it for Klárka specifically.

4. **Raphael as witness, not reader.** Raphael saw the diary. He saw Klárka's face. He did not read it. His role in this scene is to occupy the space beside Klárka without consuming the thing she is carrying. This is the test of who Raphael is to the Key-Dimitrov family: not someone who reaches for what is not his, but someone who stands in the doorway and holds the silence while the woman he loves reads her sister's life.

5. **Klára's silence on the phone is the loudest moment in the scene.** When Klára hears what Klárka found — *a man, a collar, a doghouse, a name* — she does not say *I told you not to go.* She does not say *you should have waited.* The boundary she tried to enforce at the academy no longer exists. The diary has replaced it with something that cannot be un-seen. Klára's silence is the sound of a mother realizing that her daughter's secret is not the kind of secret Klára's was — it is not abuse, it is not fear of the system, it is something Klára has no framework for understanding, and she is going to have to build one by tomorrow.

6. **The Wednesday audition now has impossible stakes.** Klárka has to walk into the Mike Key Studio in two days and evaluate a dancer while carrying a diary that describes her sister kneeling in a doghouse. The audition is still happening. The applicant still deserves a fair evaluation. Klárka will do her job — because she is her parents' daughter, because the name on the wall demands it, because the discipline of the barre does not care about your personal life. But the cost of that professionalism has just become enormous.

7. **The diary is not finished.** Klárka read part of it. The rest is still inside. The unread pages are a ticking clock of their own — not Holic's one-week deadline, but a slower, more personal countdown: how long can Klárka live with partial knowledge before she has to open the cover again and read the sentences she skipped? What is in those pages? The branding? The club? The other pet? The *yellow*? The outcome of Simonka's decision? Every page Klárka did not read is a page that could change everything.
