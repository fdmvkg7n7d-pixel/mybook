# PATTI ANNE PICKS UP GAMALIEL GALVIN

## A Prose Scene

**When:** Gamaliel Galvin is a child — old enough to have real ballet training in his body, young enough that a weekend at his grandparents' mansion still feels enormous. This can sit before the Klára disclosure scene, or elsewhere in his early childhood timeline.

**Where:** Mike and Terri Anne's mansion. Late Sunday afternoon, after Gamaliel has stayed over for the weekend.

**Who:** Patti Anne, Gamaliel Galvin, Mike, and Terri Anne.

**Core image:** Patti Anne arrives to pick up her little man and finds evidence of the weekend everywhere: damp pool towels, a half-finished game of Jenga, snacks, laughter, and Gamaliel demonstrating ballet movements for Mike with the seriousness of a child who wants his grandfather to understand the thing he loves.

---

Patti Anne knows before she reaches the front door that Gamaliel has had a good weekend.

There are signs.

One of his shoes is not where it should be. His little overnight bag is in the foyer, zipped only halfway, one sleeve of a shirt caught in the teeth because Gamaliel has never once packed a bag as carefully as he stands at the barre. A damp towel is folded over the banister in the way Terri Anne pretends not to allow and Mike always pretends not to notice. Somewhere deeper in the mansion, someone is laughing.

Not polite laughter. Not adult laughter.

Gamaliel laughter.

Patti Anne stops with her hand still on the doorframe.

There are days when being a mother feels like counting all the ways the world might bruise your child before dinner. And then there are days like this, when she walks into her parents' house and hears her little man laughing from another room, and the whole weight of her body changes.

"Hello?" she calls.

Terri Anne appears first, coming from the direction of the kitchen with a dish towel over one shoulder and the expression of a woman who has hosted a grandchild for forty-eight hours and would do it again tomorrow.

"He's in the family room," Terri Anne says. "With your father."

Patti Anne narrows her eyes. "Why does that sound ominous?"

"Because there are wooden blocks involved."

Patti Anne smiles despite herself.

She follows the sound down the hall.

The mansion has never been quiet, not really. Even when only Mike and Terri Anne are home, it holds the echo of eight daughters, their friends, their partners, their children, every birthday and Christmas and crisis the Key family has ever dragged through the front door. It is too large to be called cozy, but somehow it is. The rooms are wide, the ceilings high, the windows tall enough to make the afternoon light look theatrical — and still the house feels lived in because Mike and Terri Anne have spent decades letting children be children inside it.

In the family room, Jenga blocks are scattered across the low table.

Mike is sitting back in an armchair, sleeves rolled up, hair still slightly damp from the pool. Gamaliel is cross-legged on the rug in front of him, also damp-haired, wearing clean clothes but with the unmistakable post-swim looseness of a child who has spent half the afternoon in water. His cheeks are flushed. His eyes are bright. He has a Jenga block in one hand and the severe concentration of a surgeon.

"Careful," Mike says.

Gamaliel does not look up. "I am careful."

"You said that last time."

"Last time you bumped the table."

"I breathed near the table."

"You breathed aggressively."

Patti Anne presses her lips together in the doorway so she will not laugh too loudly and ruin the tower.

Gamaliel slides the block free.

The tower wobbles.

Mike freezes.

Gamaliel freezes too, but in a different way — dancer-still, every muscle suddenly listening. Patti Anne recognizes it instantly. That is not ordinary stillness. That is ballet. That is the barre. That is a child who has already learned that balance is not luck; it is negotiation.

The tower settles.

Gamaliel places the block on top.

Mike exhales.

"There," Gamaliel says, with the quiet satisfaction of a boy who has known all along that the tower would obey him.

Mike looks over and sees Patti Anne in the doorway.

"Your son cheats," he says.

Gamaliel whips around. "I do not."

"He wins seventy percent of the time," Mike tells Patti Anne. "Approximately. Suspiciously."

"That's because Grandpa keeps taking the wrong blocks," Gamaliel says.

"The wrong blocks," Mike repeats, offended. "There are wrong blocks now?"

Gamaliel gives him a look Patti Anne has seen on ballet teachers' faces when correcting posture. Patient, firm, a little disappointed.

"Yes."

Patti Anne laughs then. She cannot help it.

Gamaliel turns fully toward her, and the seriousness breaks open into joy.

"Mom!"

He is on his feet in one motion, all knees and elbows and dancer's balance, and then he is across the room and against her. Patti Anne catches him with both arms.

"Hi, little man."

He smells faintly of chlorine and shampoo and the cookies Terri Anne lets him eat too close to dinner. Patti Anne kisses the side of his head because she can, because he is still young enough to allow it in front of Mike, because one day he may not be.

"Did you have a good weekend?" she asks.

Gamaliel pulls back, scandalized by the smallness of the question.

"We swam," he says.

"I can tell."

"Grandpa raced me."

Patti Anne looks at Mike. "Did he?"

Mike lifts both hands. "I was merciful."

"He was not," Gamaliel says immediately. "He tried to win."

"Trying and succeeding are different legal categories," Mike says.

"I beat him twice."

"He beat me twice," Mike confirms, with the solemnity of a man giving testimony under oath. "Then we played Jenga and he beat me there too. Repeatedly."

"About seventy percent," Gamaliel says, proud but precise.

"He has been saying seventy percent all afternoon," Terri Anne says from behind Patti Anne. "He likes the number."

"It's accurate," Gamaliel says.

Patti Anne smooths his damp hair back from his forehead. "Of course it is."

There is a half-second where she sees him exactly as he is: her first child, her solemn boy, her little man who keeps statistics for Jenga and treats balance like a sacred responsibility. The sight of him here — safe, spoiled, chlorinated, triumphant — loosens something in her chest.

"Show your mother," Mike says.

Gamaliel's face changes.

Patti Anne knows that change too. That is the look he gets when ballet enters the room. Not performance exactly. Something more intimate. As if he is about to show them a language and hopes they will listen properly.

"Grandpa wanted to see what we do at school," Gamaliel says.

"I asked one question," Mike says. "One."

"He asked what a tendu was," Gamaliel says, as if this explains everything.

"A reasonable question."

"Then I had to show him."

"Naturally."

Gamaliel steps away from Patti Anne and moves to the open space between the rug and the windows. He glances at the floor, judging the surface. Not slippery enough to be dangerous, not smooth enough for real work. Patti Anne watches him make the calculation with a seriousness that makes her throat ache.

He places one hand lightly on the back of a chair, turning it into a barre.

Then he stands in first position.

The room shifts.

It always does when Gamaliel dances, even when he is only demonstrating. His little body gathers itself into order. Feet turned out, spine lifted, shoulders low, chin easy because some teacher has said that word to him a hundred times: *easy, Gamaliel, easy*. He looks suddenly older and younger at the same time — older because discipline has entered him, younger because he is still small enough that his seriousness feels almost too large for his bones.

"This is first," he says.

Mike nods gravely, as if being briefed on something of national importance.

"Then tendu." Gamaliel slides one foot along the floor, pointing through the toes. "You don't just stick your foot out. You brush."

"Brush," Mike repeats.

"Yes."

"Not stick."

Gamaliel looks relieved that this has been understood. "Not stick."

Patti Anne bites the inside of her cheek.

He shows them tendu to the front, side, back. He shows plié, careful not to let his knees fall wrong. He shows a small port de bras, arms moving with the soft precision that always surprises Patti Anne, even though she has seen him do it before. There is strength in him, yes, but also tenderness. Ballet has taught him that holding something beautifully is not the same as holding it tightly.

Mike watches without joking now.

That is one of the things Patti Anne loves most about her father: he knows when a child's game has become a child's offering. He can tease about Jenga. He can complain about losing. But when Gamaliel shows him ballet, Mike gives him the full attention of a man being trusted with something real.

"And this," Gamaliel says, stepping away from the chair, "is a balance."

He lifts one foot to the ankle of the standing leg. Not high. Not showy. Just enough. His arms come up. His face goes calm.

The mansion holds still around him.

One second.

Two.

Three.

Patti Anne forgets to breathe.

Then Gamaliel lowers his foot and looks at Mike, waiting.

Mike applauds once, then again, then with enough seriousness that Terri Anne joins in from the doorway. Patti Anne claps too. Gamaliel flushes, pleased and embarrassed and trying not to look either.

"Very good," Mike says.

Gamaliel's eyes shine.

"Really?"

"Really."

The word lands. Patti Anne sees it land. Praise from a ballet teacher is one thing. Praise from Mike Key is another. Mike is not careless with approval. He gives it warmly, but he gives it honestly, and children know the difference.

Gamaliel returns to Patti Anne's side and leans into her, suddenly shy now that the demonstration is over.

"Grandpa tried first position," he murmurs.

Patti Anne looks at Mike.

Mike looks at the ceiling.

Terri Anne says, "It was brave."

"Was it good?" Patti Anne asks.

Gamaliel considers this with devastating fairness.

"No."

Mike puts a hand over his heart.

"But he tried," Gamaliel adds quickly.

"Thank you," Mike says. "My legacy is restored."

Patti Anne laughs again, and Gamaliel laughs with her, and for a moment the whole weekend seems to gather itself into the room: the pool water flashing under the mansion lights, Mike pretending not to race and racing anyway, Terri Anne bringing towels, the Jenga tower leaning and surviving, Gamaliel winning about seventy percent of the time and believing that number matters, a grandfather turning a chair into a barre because his grandson needed a place to show him the shape of his days.

Eventually Patti Anne says, "We should get your things."

Gamaliel's face falls only a little. Not because he does not want to go home. He does. Patti Anne knows that. But leaving Mike and Terri Anne's house after a weekend is always a small grief. The mansion is too full of pleasures: the pool, the games, the kitchen, the rooms that remember every Key child who ever ran through them.

"Can I finish the game?" he asks.

Patti Anne looks at the tower. It is unstable in the way Jenga towers become unstable when a child has outplayed his grandfather for too many rounds.

"One turn each," she says.

Gamaliel nods, satisfied by the fairness.

Mike gestures grandly toward the table. "Champion first."

Gamaliel kneels on the rug again. Patti Anne sits on the arm of the sofa and watches him study the tower. Terri Anne disappears toward the foyer to make sure the overnight bag is actually packed. Mike leans forward, elbows on knees, pretending strategy, but Patti Anne knows him. He is watching Gamaliel more than the game.

Her father loves her son.

It is a simple fact. It should not still surprise her. But sometimes love is most visible in its smallest forms: a man letting a child beat him at Jenga but not too obviously; a grandfather getting into the pool; a chair held steady while a boy demonstrates tendu; applause given at exactly the right volume.

Gamaliel slides out another block.

The tower wavers.

Mike's eyes widen.

Patti Anne holds her breath.

Gamaliel places the block on top.

The tower stands.

"Seventy percent," Gamaliel says softly.

Mike shakes his head.

"Suspicious," he says.

But he is smiling.

## Notes for the Saga

1. **This scene shows ordinary safety.** Before the heavier Klára material, this gives Gamaliel a weekend of uncomplicated childhood with Mike and Terri Anne: swimming, games, snacks, and being adored.

2. **Mike and Gamaliel's bond is physical and playful.** They swim together, compete, tease, and then shift naturally into Mike taking Gamaliel's ballet seriously.

3. **Jenga becomes a small metaphor for Gamaliel.** Balance, patience, choosing the right piece, staying still when the tower wobbles — all of it echoes ballet and later emotional burdens.

4. **The "seventy percent" detail belongs to Gamaliel's precision.** He does not merely say he won a lot. He says about seventy percent, because the number matters to him.

5. **Patti Anne's nickname appears in a light context.** "Little man" is not only used in crisis. It belongs to daily motherhood — pickup, damp hair, hugs, and pride.
