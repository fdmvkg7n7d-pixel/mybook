# SCENE OUTLINE: KLÁRKA ASKS KLÁRA TO TAKE FELICIAN'S AUDITION

**Part of the Simonka/Holic/GG arc.**
**Placement:** Wednesday morning, Prague — the same day Klára calls Patti Anne. Klárka is in the studio, preparing for auditions, but she is too upset to go through with it.

**Characters in the scene:** Klárka Pavlînka (older sister of Simonka, teacher at the academy), Klára Key (mother, academy co-director), Felician (little girl auditioning), Jelka and Celestina (Felician's parents — present for the audition)

**Characters referenced but not present:** Simonka Káta (the source of Klárka's distress), Gamaliel (father, at home or in the office), the wider family

**Setting:** The Mike Key Studio, Prague — the first studio of the academy. Morning light through the high windows. The barre. The piano. The floor that has held every dancer who has passed through this school. An audition room. A little girl in her first recital shoes. A mother and father watching from the benches. And Klárka, standing in the doorway, unable to walk in.

**Tone:** Tender, heavy, real. The academy does not stop because the family is breaking. It cannot stop. The children who come here need it. The parents who bring them here trust it. And Klárka — the older sister, the teacher, the one who should be handling this — is too upset to do her job. Her mother sees it. Steps in. Not as a rescue. As a continuation. The way the Key family has always done: the door opens, the light stays on, the work goes on.

---

## BEFORE THE SCENE

- It is Wednesday morning. Klára has just finished the call to Patti Anne. She is sitting at the kitchen table, coffee cold, dogs at her feet. Gamaliel has gone to the academy early. Klárka is already there, preparing for auditions.
- The auditions have been scheduled for weeks. Little girls and boys, ages ten and up, trying out for the academy's youngest program. Felician is one of them.
- Klárka knows Felician. She has seen her in the pre-ballet classes. She is small, serious, the kind of child who watches the advanced dancers through the studio windows and mimics their combinations in the hallway. Jelka and Celestina are proud, nervous parents — the kind who arrive early, who hold their child's hand too tightly, who want this so badly it hurts.
- Klárka was supposed to run the audition. She is the teacher. It is her responsibility.
- But Klárka cannot do it. Her sister is gone into a world she does not understand. Her mother called her grandmother across an ocean. Her father is holding the weight of a family he built with his own hands. And Klárka — the oldest daughter, the teacher, the steady one — is standing in the doorway of the Mike Key Studio, unable to walk in.

---

## THE SCENE

### Klárka in the Hallway

- Klára arrives at the academy. She walks through the courtyard, past the dark studios, toward the Mike Key Studio. The door is open. Light spills out.
- She finds Klárka in the hallway, leaning against the wall, arms crossed, face pale. The child who looks so much like her mother — the same Czech cheekbones, the same dancer's posture — but with Gamaliel's eyes.
- Klára stops. She knows that face. She has worn that face.
- *Klárko.* (The diminutive, the way Patti Anne uses it for Klára, the way Klára now uses it for her daughter.)
- Klárka looks up. Her eyes are red but dry. She has been crying and then she has stopped crying because there is no time to cry.
- *Mami. I can't do it.*

### The Request

- Klára does not ask what she can't do. She knows.
- *The audition.*
- *Felician is in there. With her mothers. Jelka and Celestina. They are so excited. They brought her a new leotard. It is pink. She has been running her combinations in the hallway, counting her steps out loud, serious as a surgeon. I can hear her.*
- Klára steps closer. *And you can't go in.*
- *I can't.* Klárka's voice is small. The teacher, the steady one, the oldest daughter — reduced to a girl who cannot walk through a door because her sister is gone and the world is too heavy.
- *Will you do it for me?*
- Klára does not hesitate. *Yes.*

### The Studio

- Klára opens the door. The studio is bright, warm, the floor polished. The piano sits in the corner. The barre runs along the wall.
- Inside, there is a screen in the corner — the same screen every girl uses to change behind. Felician will go in there alone. She will remove her leotard and tights. She will come out in her underwear and her soft shoes.
- Jelka and Celestina stand when Klára enters. They know her — the co-director, the teacher, the mother of Klárka. They are nervous. This is their child's first real audition.
- *Good morning,* Klára says. She is warm. She is the woman who has taught children since she was sixteen, since she and Gamaliel first opened the storefront, since the Mike Key Studio was a painted name on a window and a dream.
- **Parents are not allowed inside the audition room.** This is academy policy. The children get nervous enough when other children are watching — parents in the room would make it worse. So the parents wait outside, and the children go in alone. Felician is still dressed in her pink leotard and tights, standing between her mothers in the hallway outside the studio door.

### The Conversation in the Hallway

- Klára steps out of the studio and closes the door behind her. The audition room is not open to parents — academy policy. The children get nervous enough when other children are watching; parents in the room would make it worse. So the parents wait outside, and the children go in alone.
- Felician is standing between her mothers in the hallway. She is still dressed — pink leotard, white tights, soft shoes, dark hair in a proper bun. She is ten years old. She is not nervous, or she is hiding it well. She is the kind of child who watches the older dancers through the studio windows and mimics their combinations in the hallway.
- Klára addresses all three of them, but especially the mothers.
- *Before Felician goes in, I need to explain something.*
- *The academy follows the Vaganova tradition. When the children audition, they do not audition in their leotards or tights. They audition in their underwear only.*
- She says it plainly. Not apologetically. Not defensively. Just as a fact of the school, the way she would explain why the floor is rosin or why the barre is wood.
- *The reason is simple. We need to see the body. The turnout at the hip. The arch of the foot. The alignment of the spine. The proportions. A leotard hides too much. Tights hide the knees and the ankles. We cannot assess what we cannot see. This is not about modesty or immodesty. It is about seeing the instrument.*
- Jelka nods immediately. She is the pragmatic one — the mother who has always trusted the academy's methods, who knows that ballet demands honesty about the body. *I understand. It is fine.*
- Celestina does not nod. She hesitates. She looks at Felician, then at Klára, then at Jelka. Her discomfort is visible, but she does not make a scene.
- Celestina: *She has to take everything off?*
- Klára: *Not everything. Underwear only. Nothing more, nothing less. She will go behind the screen inside the studio. No one else will see her undress. She will change alone. When she comes out, she will be in her underwear and her soft shoes. The faculty will see her body as it is — and then we will see what it can do.*
- Celestina: *She is ten.*
- Klára: *I know. And at ten, the body is already telling us what it can become. We do not look at it as anything other than what it is — a dancer's body, in progress, being evaluated by professionals who have seen thousands of bodies and know what to look for. The gaze is clinical. It is professional. It is unsentimental. It is not cruel, and it is not kind. It is honest.*
- Jelka: *It is fine, Celestina. I am fine with it. She is fine with it. It is just clothes. It is just the academy. It is just what we do.*
- Celestina is quiet. She looks at Felician. Felician is not embarrassed — she has been through pre-ballet classes, she has seen older girls undress for auditions, she knows this is the way of the school. She looks at her mother and says, small but steady: *Mami, it is okay.*
- Celestina nods. Reluctantly. But she nods.
- Klára: *Felician will go in alone. Parents are not allowed in the audition room — the children get nervous enough without us there. She will change behind the screen. I will be inside with her and the other faculty. When she is ready, she will come out, and we will begin.*
- Felician takes a breath. She is ten. She is brave. She walks through the studio door.
- *We are so sorry to be early,* Jelka says. *Felician was too excited to stay home.*
- Celestina: *She has been practicing all week. She says she wants to be like Klárka.*
- Klára looks at Felician. Felician looks back. There is a moment — the kind of moment that only exists between a teacher and a child who wants to dance: the silent recognition that this is real, that the floor is real, that the barre is real, that the thing they both love is standing between them, waiting.
- *Hello, Felician,* Klára says. *Ready?*

### The Audition

- Klára does not run the audition the way Klárka would have. Klárka is formal, precise, the Vaganova-trained teacher who runs a tight room. Klára is different. She is the woman who was rescued at eight, who found her name in the stars, who learned to dance because dancing was the first thing that made her body feel like hers and not someone else's.
- She runs it like a real audition. Felician takes center. She moves through her combinations — tendus, jetés, a short adagio at the barre, a turn sequence across the floor. Klára watches the feet, the placement, the musicality, the discipline. The things a teacher watches in a ten-year-old who is no longer a beginner but not yet a dancer. But she also watches the girl's face. The way Felician locks in when the music starts. The way she corrects her own posture without being told. The way she looks at Klára for approval — not her mothers. This is no longer about the parents. This is about the work.
- Jelka and Celestina watch from the bench. They are holding hands. They are both crying, quietly, the way parents cry when they see their child do the thing they have dreamed of.
- Klára sees this. She knows this. She has been the parent on the bench. She has held a hand and watched a child dance and felt the world shrink to the size of a studio floor.
- *Her feet are clean,* Klára says to the mothers. *Her placement is solid for her age. But more importantly — she listens to her body. She corrects herself without being told. You do not teach that. She either has it or she does not. She has it.*
- Jelka: *She talks about this school all the time. Klárka is her hero.*
- Klára smiles. It is a small smile. It is real. *Klárka is a good teacher. She will be proud of Felician.*

### The Results — Inside the Studio

- The audition ends. Klára has seen enough. Felician is standing in the center of the studio, breathing hard but composed, the way a ten-year-old who has just been asked to give everything gives everything.
- Klára kneels to Felician's level. She does not soften the delivery. She does not need to. The truth is kind enough on its own.
- *Felician. You have clean feet. Your placement is solid for your age. You listen to your body — you correct yourself without being told. You do not look at the mirror. You look at the work. You have the thing. The thing you cannot teach.*
- Felician stares. Her mouth opens. She does not speak.
- *You are accepted.*
- Felician makes a small sound — not a cry, not a cheer, just the sound a child makes when something they wanted with their whole body becomes real.
- Jelka, in the hallway outside the open door, hears everything. She covers her mouth. Celestina reaches for her hand. They have been standing just outside, listening, the way parents who are not allowed in the room listen anyway.
- But Klára is not done.
- *And I want you to know — the academy will be offering you a full scholarship.*
- She says it before they can ask, before they can worry about cost, before Celestina can start calculating tuition and uniforms and shoes and the long years ahead.
- *Full tuition. Full uniform costs. Full shoe costs. Through grade twelve.*
- Jelka: *A full scholarship?*
- Klára: *The academy does not give these lightly. We give them when we see something that cannot be allowed to slip away because of money. Felician has the kind of body, the kind of musicality, the kind of discipline that is rare at any age. She is ten. She has been training for a few years. She is already correcting herself. That does not happen by accident.*
- She looks at Celestina, who is still holding Jelka's hand, eyes wet.
- *We will take care of her. The academy will take care of her. From today, through grade twelve. Every class, every exam, every performance, every pair of shoes. You will not pay a single dollar. All we ask is that you bring her, that you let her work, and that you trust us with her body and her future.*
- Celestina: *I — I do not know what to say.*
- Klára: *You do not have to say anything. Say yes when you are ready.*
- Felician says it first. *Yes.*
- One word. Small. Certain. The word that changes everything.

### The Tour

- Klára does not let the moment end in the studio. She opens the door wider and gestures into the hallway.
- *Would you like to see the new building? This is where Felician will be learning.*
- Jelka and Celestina look at each other. This is more than they expected. They nod.
- Felician is already halfway out the door, still in her underwear, still in her soft shoes, still glowing. She does not think about changing. She is ten years old and she has just been handed her future, and she wants to see it.
- Klára leads them down the hallway, past the older studios, toward the new wing of the academy. The floors are polished wood. The walls are lined with mirrors and barres and the names of students who have passed through. Felician walks with her head high, her mothers behind her, the sound of her soft shoes on the floor like a metronome counting the beginning of something.
- **The New Building — The Tour.**
- Klára opens the doors to the new wing and the three of them step inside. It is impressive — modern, bright, built with the kind of intention that only comes when you have spent your life building something from a storefront into an institution.
- **Inside swimming pool.** Klára shows them first — a long, heated pool at the far end of the building, surrounded by glass walls that let in the Prague light. *For recovery. For cross-training. Dancers need it.* Jelka nods. Celestina looks at Felician, who is already imagining herself in the water.
- **Massaging rooms.** Next to the pool — small, quiet rooms with tables, oils, the kind of space where tired bodies come to be put back together. Klára explains: *We take care of the bodies that work for us. This is not optional. This is the academy.*
- **Fifteen dance studios.** Klára counts them off as they walk past — wood floors, mirrors, barres, pianos in the corners, the smell of rosin and sweat and the particular quiet of a room that has held a thousand rehearsals. Felician is wide-eyed. She has never seen this many studios. She has never imagined this much space for dancing.
- **Sixteen classrooms.** Academics are part of the program — the academy does not produce only dancers. It produces educated people who happen to dance. Klára points out the desks, the whiteboards, the books on the shelves. *Your body is one thing. Your mind is another. We train both.*
- **The dormitories.** This is where Felician will stay. Klára opens the door to the girls' wing — bright, clean, beds arranged neatly, lockers for each student. *Felician will have her own bed. Her own locker. Her own space.* Then Klára shows them the fingerprint scanner on the door frame. *Boys cannot get into the girls' dormitory. Girls cannot get into the boys'. Gamaliel and I insisted on fingerprint technology. Every student is scanned before they enter. No exceptions.* Celestina relaxes. This is the thing she was worried about and did not want to say out loud. Jelka nods. *Good. Safe.*
- **The common room.** Between the two dormitory wings — a large, comfortable space with games, books, a television, couches, a kitchenette. *They can gather here. Play. Study. Rest. But they cannot cross into each other's sleeping areas.* Felician is already looking at the board games. She likes this. She wants to be here.
- **The bathroom.** About halfway through the tour, Felician shifts. She is ten. She needs to go. But she does not say anything — she is still in her underwear, still glowing from the audition, still trying to be brave. Jelka notices. She leans in. *Do you need to—?* Felician shakes her head, embarrassed. Celestina looks at Klára, uncertain. Klára catches the glance and smiles. *The water is running throughout the pipes. Every floor has bathrooms. She can go. No one is watching. No one is judging. This is a school, not a stage.* Felician exhales. Relief. She asks quietly. Klára points her to the nearest bathroom. Felician runs.
- **The broken floorboard.** Before Felician returns, Klára leads Jelka and Celestina into one of the dance studios. She is showing them the space, the mirrors, the piano, the barre — and then she stops. She looks down. A floorboard near the center of the room is cracked. Broken. Not fixed.
- Klára's face changes. The warmth is gone. She is the co-director of this academy, the woman who built it with her own hands from a storefront to this, and she is **very angry** with the workers. *This should have been fixed. I told them. I said it twice. I will not have my dancers on broken wood.* Her voice is sharp. Not at the mothers. At the situation. At the people who did not do their job.
- Felician comes back from the bathroom. She sees Klára's face. She is ten. She has never seen a teacher look like this. She asks, small and careful: *Why are you angry?*
- Klára turns. She sees the child. She softens. Not because she is less angry — because she is a mother, and mothers do not let their anger scare the children who are looking at them for the first time. *I am not angry at you, Felician. I am angry at the floor. It is broken. It needs to be fixed before you dance on it. I will not let you dance on broken wood.*
- Felician nods. She understands. The floor is broken. The teacher is angry. But the teacher is not angry at her. This is a distinction that matters.
- But Felician is still looking at Klára's face, still uncertain, still the kind of ten-year-old who has never seen a grown woman so visibly upset about a piece of wood. Jelka notices. She kneels to Felician's level, the way a mother kneels when the world is too big for a small child to carry alone.
- Jelka: *Mami, the lady is angry because she cares. The floor is where you dance. If it is broken, you could fall. You could hurt yourself. She is angry because she does not want you to get hurt. That is what a good teacher does.*
- Celestina adds, softer: *She is angry at the workers because they did not fix it. They were supposed to. And when someone is supposed to do something and does not do it, the person who is in charge gets angry. That is not your fault. That is their fault. She is not angry at you. She is angry for you.*
- Felician absorbs this. She is ten. She has just been accepted. She has just been offered a full scholarship. She has just been told she has *the thing.* And now she is being told that the woman who said it is angry because she cares — because a broken floor is a dangerous floor, and no one in this academy lets danger stand.
- Felician nods again. This time, she means it. She understands. The floor is broken. The teacher is angry. But the teacher is angry because she wants Felician to be safe. Because she wants Felician to dance on wood that will hold her. Because she built this place with her own hands and she will not let it be less than what it deserves to be.
- She looks at Klára. Klára looks back. The anger is gone from her face now — replaced by something softer, something that looks like the woman who once stood in this exact position, age eight, in snowy boots, learning for the first time that someone can be angry on your behalf without being angry at you.
- **On the way, across the courtyard, through the glass doors — Simonka Káta.**
- She is walking toward the exit. She has just come from a meeting with her master's professor at Charles University — a man in his fifties, a thesis advisor who works in developmental psychology and meets with Simonka regularly to discuss her research. He is a father, too: his two daughters are students at the academy. Klára has seen them in the studios. She knows their names, their levels, their faces. They are good dancers, serious, the kind of children whose parents take ballet seriously enough to pay attention to who teaches them and why.
- Simonka has just asked him to let her change her thesis.
- The request is not casual. She has been carrying it for weeks, maybe months. She has rewritten it in her head a dozen times before she brought it to him. Today, she brought it to the meeting. She laid it out. She asked.
- And then — because she trusts him, because he is the one adult in her life outside her family who has authority without judgment, who listens without rescuing — **she tells him about the world she has entered.**
- She does not give him the graphic details. She is twenty-three, not a child, and she knows how to separate the personal from the academic. But she tells him what matters: she has found a community. A space. A dynamic that she chose, that she understands, that is not what the world would assume if they knew. She tells him about the Collegium. About the consent structures, the safewords, the care, the discipline, the way the world inside those walls is organized around trust and boundary and the deliberate surrender of power — not the loss of it, the *surrender* of it, which is entirely different.
- She tells him because she needs someone to know. Not her family — they would not understand. Not Klárka — she has hidden this from her sister completely. Not her mother, who would hear it through the lens of her own trauma and see a daughter walking into the same dark. She tells her thesis advisor because he is a psychologist. Because his job is to understand human behavior, not to judge it. Because he is a father, too, and he knows what it means for a child to choose a life the parent did not plan.
- He listens. He does not flinch. He does not reach for her hand or tell her she is confused or suggest she talk to a counselor. He is a man who has spent his career studying how people become who they are, and he knows that sometimes the most important thing a person can do is name what they have chosen out loud.
- When she is done, he is quiet. Then he says: *Thank you for telling me.*
- And then: *This changes the direction of your thesis, does it not?*
- She nods. It does. She wants to study what she has entered — not from the outside, not from the clinical distance of someone observing a phenomenon, but from the inside, as someone who has lived it. She wants to understand the psychology of consensual power exchange, the architecture of trust, the way the body and the mind negotiate surrender and safety at the same time.
- He did not say yes. He did not say no. He asked her to sit with it. To think it through. To bring him a proposal — not a feeling, a proposal. She left with the notebook heavier than it was when she walked in.
- Simonka is carrying a notebook, a tote bag, the look of a student who has just been given too much to think about and is processing it on her feet. Her professor may still be behind her, or he may have already left. The academy is small enough that paths cross, that a father's work life intersects with a daughter's, that a mother's world overlaps with the world of the child who has chosen to step out of it.
- She sees her mother. She sees the little girl in underwear. She sees the two women who are clearly parents. She sees the way Klára is walking — the teacher's walk, the tour-guide walk, the mother who is doing her job even when her own daughter is walking out of her life.
- For a second, Simonka stops.
- Klára sees her. They lock eyes across the glass.
- Neither speaks. Neither waves. The distance between them is thirty feet and a world of choices neither can undo.
- Simonka keeps walking. She pushes through the glass doors and disappears into the Prague afternoon.
- Klára does not stop. She does not look away from Jelka, Celestina, and Felician. She keeps walking. She keeps talking. She points out the studios, the practice rooms, the performance space, the place where Felician will spend the next eight years of her life.
- But the moment has landed. The family is breaking, and the academy is opening, and both things are true at once.

### Simonka's Afternoon — Holic's Place

- After she leaves the academy, Simonka does not go home to her apartment. She goes to Holic's.
- She arrives and sees **three box vans** parked outside. They are large, commercial, unmarked. The back doors are open or partially open, and inside each van are **cages** — transport cages, built for human pets, lined with padding, secured with latches that lock from the outside. She does not ask what they are for. She knows what they are. She has learned, in this world, not to ask until she is told.
- She walks to the **large fenced area** behind Holic's mansion — the Collegium's outdoor space, the yard where the human pets are kept, where the rules are different from the world outside the fence.
- She drops her backpack on the ground. The notebook from Charles University is still inside it. The proposal she hasn't written yet. The thesis she wants to change. All of it, set down.
- Then she gets undressed. Not in a rush. Not in shame. In the way a person undresses when they are entering a space where clothes do not matter — where the body is not something to be covered or hidden, but something to be placed, deliberately, into the care of the person who owns it.
- She folds her clothes. She places them on top of the backpack. She steps into the fenced area in her underwear, the way she was taught, the way the Collegium requires. The air is cold on her skin. She does not flinch.
- She knows her safewords — *yellow* to slow down, *red* to stop. She has them ready on her tongue, the way she has been taught to keep them ready. But she does not use them. She will not use them. Not during what comes next. Not if she can help it. Because what she wants — what she *needs* — is to please Holic. To impress him. To prove she can take it. To be the good girl, the one who stays, the one who does not break the frame.
- The next twenty-four hours are full. She is busy — with women and with men, with Holic and with others from the Collegium, in the fenced yard and in the rooms beyond it. The time stretches and compresses the way it does in this world: hours that feel like minutes, minutes that feel like hours. She is tired. She is sore. She is exactly where she chose to be.
- She does not use her safewords. Not once. Even when her body wants to. Even when her mind wanders to places she does not want it to. Even when the cages in the box vans are a thing she has to step past. She holds the frame. She is GG. She is Holic's. She is the one who stays.
- When it is over — twenty-four hours later, her body exhausted, her mind quiet — Holic does not let her pack up and leave on her own. He leads her out to the yard. He shows her to her outside doghouse. It is warm, prepared for her, exactly as it was during the weekend trial. He tells her it is time for a good nap. She goes inside. She sleeps. Deep, exhausted, earned rest. The frame holds, but it holds gently. This is care, not just intensity. She is GG. She is Holic's. And when she wakes, the world outside the fence will still be there. But for now, she rests.

### After the Audition

- Jelka and Celestina come into the studio. Felician runs to them — still in her underwear, still in her soft shoes, her hair still in its bun. She is so excited, so buzzing, so glowing with the kind of exhilaration that only comes when a child has just been told they have the thing — that she forgets to change. She doesn't think about it. She just runs.
- Klára watches the family embrace. She does not interrupt. She is the woman who knows what it means to be held by someone who chose to hold you.
- **Thirty minutes have passed.**
- When Felician finally remembers, she laughs — not embarrassed, just the laugh of a ten-year-old who got carried away by joy. *Oh! Right!* She turns and runs behind the screen. The soft shoes come off. The leotard and tights go back on. She emerges, composed again, ready to face the world as an academy student.
- Klára opens the studio door. The hallway is not empty. A group of summer campers — ten, eleven, twelve years old, heading to or from their own classes — is walking past the studio door. They see Klára and they see Felician.
- Felician, newly dressed, newly accepted, newly a scholarship student, walks out beside her mothers. She is still glowing. The campers see it. They know what that look means. Some of them have seen it in the mirror after their own auditions. Some of them never will.
- Klára says, to no one in particular, to everyone in the hallway: *Felician has been accepted. Full scholarship. Through grade twelve.*
- The campers look. Some clap. One of the older girls — a twelve-year-old who has been at the academy for two years — says, matter-of-factly: *Good. You deserved it.* Felician beams.
- The hallway breathes again.
- Klára turns to Klárka, who is still standing at the end of the hall, still listening, still waiting.
- *How was it?* Klárka asks.
- Klára: *She has good feet. And she loves it. And she is ours now — through grade twelve, on a full scholarship. She is going to be fine.*
- Klárka nods. She is not crying anymore. She is just tired.
- *Thank you, Mami.*
- *You do not have to thank me. This is what we do. The academy does not stop because the family is breaking. It keeps going. Because the children who come here need it to.*
- Klárka looks at her mother. She sees the woman who raised her, who built this school, who has carried a hundred children through a hundred auditions and a hundred fears and a hundred firsts. And she sees, for the first time, that her mother is carrying something too.
- *Are you okay?* Klárka asks.
- Klára: *I am here. That is enough for today.*

## WHY THIS SCENE MATTERS

- **The academy continues.** The family is in crisis, but the school does not stop. The children who come here need it. The parents who bring them here trust it. Klára steps in not as a favor but as a duty — the duty of a woman who has spent her life building something that exists outside of herself.
- **Klárka's vulnerability.** The oldest daughter, the teacher, the steady one — she is the one who breaks first. Not because she is weak, but because she loves her sister and cannot fix what is happening. Her asking for help is its own kind of courage.
- **Felician's audition.** A small, ordinary moment in the middle of an extraordinary crisis. But it matters. It is proof that life goes on, that children still need to dance, that the world does not stop spinning just because one family is hurting.
- **Klára as teacher and mother.** She runs the audition the way she runs her life: with precision, with warmth, with the understanding that every child who walks into this studio is someone's everything. She sees Felician the way she saw Klárka when she was a child — small, scared, wanting to be good at something.
- **The tension between the two mothers.** Jelka is fine with the undressing.
