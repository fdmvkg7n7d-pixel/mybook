# SCENE OUTLINE: GAMALIEL & KLÁRA — THE BANANA SPLIT CONVERSATION

*The night of Mike's funeral. **Just after** the celebration of life has emptied out. Gamaliel and Klára, **still in the ballet outfits they wore for the pas de deux** — they never changed out of them — are in Mike and Terri Anne's family kitchen, eating Mike's favorite dessert — a banana split with chocolate mint ice cream — and saying out loud to each other what they could not say in front of the family during the day.*

---

## TIMING WITHIN THE SAGA

- **The same day as the funeral.** Not weeks later. The cars have left. The cousins are gone. The marley flooring is rolled up in the car downstairs. The pas de deux is two hours old in their bodies.
- **Gamaliel and Klára are sixteen.** They disclosed their relationship to Patti Anne and Aleksander when they were fifteen (pre-cruise), but they are still keeping it quiet from the rest of the extended family and, most importantly, Klára's social worker. (The FaceTime conversation between Rachel Darcy and Patti Anne, where Rachel reports her observations of the funeral pas de deux, hasn't happened yet; it will happen weeks from now.)
- They are at the mansion because **Terri Anne asked Patti Anne to leave them with her for the night.** Patti Anne agreed. The official reason is *"They're exhausted, let them sleep here, I'll drive them home in the morning."* The actual reason — which Terri Anne does not say out loud, and which Patti Anne does not require her to say — is that Terri Anne understands what those two danced today, and she knows that what they need tonight is each other's company in the only adult's house in the family where they have ever been seen for what they are.
- It is **late** — past 11 p.m. The mansion is quiet in the particular way large houses are quiet after they have been full and then emptied. Terri Anne is upstairs in her own bedroom, in the room she shared with Mike for forty years, alone for the first time in it tonight.

---

## SETTING

**The family kitchen at Mike and Terri Anne's mansion.** The big kitchen, the one with the long maple island and the row of stools — the same kitchen the celebration of life centred on a few hours ago. The same kitchen where, weeks from now, Terri Anne will walk in on Patti Anne and Rachel after a tea-and-FaceTime aftermath, but that is the future, and Gamaliel and Klára do not know it yet.

Right now:

- The caterers have been gone for two hours. The dishes are done. The kitchen island is wiped down. Terri Anne washed the last few platters by hand herself before going up to bed, because she said she needed to.
- **The over-the-stove light is on.** Mike's habit. Terri Anne left it on, the way Mike always did.
- **Gamaliel and Klára are still in their ballet outfits from the pas de deux.** Neither of them changed after dancing — there was no moment in the afternoon when changing felt right, and as the hours wore on and the cousins left and the catering trays got packed up, the moment for changing never came. So they are in what they danced in.
  - **Klára** is in a simple, long-sleeved **black leotard** with a thin wrap-front **black practice skirt** that Gamaliel can lift cleanly, soft pink **tights** rolled down once at her hips because she has been sitting in them for hours, and her **pointe shoes off** — placed neatly at the foot of her stool with the ribbons still tied in their bows. She is in her **stocking feet**. Her hair is still in the rehearsal bun she did this morning, though several pins have given up over the course of the day and a few strands have come loose around her face. She has not retouched her stage makeup. The mascara along her lower lashes is slightly smudged from the lift toward Mike's empty chair; nobody has been there to fix it for her.
  - **Gamaliel** is in a fitted **black long-sleeved dance shirt** and **black ballet tights**, his **ballet slippers off** and set under his stool beside Klára's pointe shoes. He has put a borrowed grey cardigan of Mike's over the top because the mansion got cooler after sunset and Terri Anne handed it to him on his way through the front room without comment. The cardigan is two sizes too big. The sleeves are pushed up. It smells like Mike's aftershave. Gamaliel has not commented on this either.
- **They never changed because changing felt wrong.** Taking off the costume of the only piece they will ever choreograph for their grandfather felt, to both of them independently, like a betrayal of the dance. They have not said this to each other. They both know it.
- **Willow** is asleep on the rug by the door—she stayed at the mansion through the day because Patti Anne brought her along, and now she is one of the things keeping the kitchen warm. (Klára's second dog, Kleopatra, is not born yet.)

They came down together. Neither of them announced it. They had gone to their separate rooms upstairs — Klára to her east-facing one, Gamaliel to the old-study one down the hall — and at almost exactly the same moment had decided, *no, not yet, not sleep, not without saying it to each other first.* They met in the hallway. They came down to the kitchen because the kitchen is the room Mike was always in.

---

## BEAT 1 — KLÁRA OPENS THE FREEZER

Klára opens the freezer for ice water. She sees, on the second shelf, what she expected to see: a fresh container of **chocolate mint ice cream**, half full, with the corner of the lid pried up the way Mike used to pry it up because he could never wait the extra second to break the seal cleanly.

She closes the freezer. She opens it again. She just looks at it.

She says, without turning around: *"There's chocolate mint."*

Gamaliel, at the island, looks up from the spot on the maple he has been staring at.

He says: *"Of course there's chocolate mint."*

She turns. They look at each other across the kitchen for a long second.

She says: *"He'd want us to."*

He says: *"Yeah, he would."*

So they do.

---

## BEAT 2 — BUILDING THE BANANA SPLIT

They do not discuss it past that. They just start.

**Mike's banana split, made by his grandchildren at his kitchen island, the same night he was buried:**

- **The banana** — Gamaliel cuts it lengthwise on Mike's old wooden cutting board, the one with the burn mark from a saucepan in 2003 that nobody ever replaced. His hands shake slightly. He does not say so.
- **Three scoops** — Klára does the scooping, which is a thing she has watched Mike do a hundred times. Chocolate mint goes in the middle. Vanilla on one side. Strawberry on the other. Mike's order. They do not have to talk about the order.
- **Chocolate sauce** — Gamaliel finds the squeeze bottle in the door of the fridge. Cold, not heated. Mike said cold sauce was honest sauce after midnight, and it is almost midnight, and they are not going to argue with him tonight.
- **Whipped cream from the can** — Klára does the architecture. It is, intentionally, too tall. Mike's whipped-cream towers were always too tall. That was the point of them.
- **Maraschino cherries** — two each, the way Mike insisted, because a single cherry is a coward's dessert. Gamaliel says the rule out loud, quietly, the way you quote someone you have just buried.
- **No nuts.** Mike said nuts were a distraction from the ice cream and the ice cream was the point.

They build *one* banana split. Not two. They are sixteen and in love and have been sharing food since they were eight, and tonight especially this is the kind of dessert that wants to be shared. They put it between them on the island with two spoons.

Klára says, almost as soon as it's built: *"He'd be furious we didn't make him one too."*

Gamaliel gets up, gets out a second oval dish — the chipped one from 1998 — and builds a small one for Mike's place. Half a banana, one scoop of chocolate mint, a token of sauce, two cherries this time (they have plenty), the can-whipped-cream pile a deliberate small tower. He sets it at the stool at the end of the island where Mike always sat.

He sits back down. He says: *"There. Now he can be furious about the size of it instead."*

Klára almost laughs. She does not quite get there. But almost.

---

## BEAT 3 — THE FIRST BITE

They eat in silence for a minute. The chocolate mint melts first, as it always does. Klára notices this. Gamaliel notices that she notices this. They both, separately, think the same thing: *Mike would have said something about it.* Neither says so.

Klára speaks first.

> *"He's in the ground."*

She says it flat. It is the first time either of them has said the literal sentence aloud — the dirt, the box, the actual physical fact. They have said *gone* and *died* and *we lost him*, but they have not said *in the ground*.

Gamaliel nods. He is looking at the small banana split they made for Mike. He says, after a moment: *"Yes."*

Klára says, quieter: *"He's the only adult in this family who knew about us. And he's in the ground."*

> *Pause.*

Gamaliel says, almost a whisper: *"I know."*

That sentence sits on the island between them, beside the dessert.

---

## BEAT 4 — THE DANCE

Klára puts her spoon down. She wraps her hands around her own elbows the way she does when she is bracing.

> *"Did we just tell the whole family?"*

Gamaliel knows immediately what she means. *The dance.* The lift toward Mike's empty chair. The trust Rachel Darcy could read across a room because Rachel Darcy is a dancer and Rachel Darcy is not stupid.

He says: *"I think we told some of them, yeah."*

Klára: *"Who?"*

He thinks. *"Rachel for sure. Mom — maybe. Maybe she already knew. I don't know. The aunts who don't dance probably didn't see anything except a sad ballet."*

Klára: *"And the social worker isn't an aunt who doesn't dance. The social worker isn't at this funeral at all. The social worker doesn't know."*

Gamaliel: *"Not unless one of the aunts tells her. Which they won't."*

Klára looks at him. She wants him to be right. She has spent her entire life knowing that adults who *say* they won't do something to a foster child sometimes still do it, even when they love her, even when they think they are protecting her.

She does not say that part. She does not need to. Gamaliel knows. He has known since he was eight years old, since the day she told him about her father and he carried the secret until he vomited and then told Mike.

He says, *"If anyone tries to move you, I go with you."*

Klára shakes her head. *"You can't. You're sixteen. You don't get to. And if you tried, you'd lose your mother, and your half-brother and half-sister, and the Academy. I'm not letting you do that."*

*"It wouldn't be losing them. It would be choosing you."*

She looks at him hard. *"Don't do that thing where you make it sound like a small thing."*

He looks back at her, and he does not make it sound like a small thing. He says: *"Okay. It would be losing them. And I would do it."*

She does not answer that. She picks her spoon back up. She takes a bite of the chocolate-mint-soaked banana. She lets the weight of what he just said sit in the kitchen with them, in the house where Mike died, on the night of the day he was buried.

After a minute she says, very quietly, *"Mike said I wasn't losing this family. He said it to me. In my bedroom. After he caught us."*

Gamaliel says, *"I know. He told me later."*

She says, *"Did he tell you what to do when he was gone?"*

Gamaliel thinks about this. He says: *"No. I don't think he thought he was going to be gone."*

---

## BEAT 5 — PRAGUE

They do not stay in the fear long. They are sixteen and they have a plan and they have already said it out loud to one adult — Terri Anne, in the study, this afternoon. Saying it again, here, at the kitchen island, hours later, is easier.

Klára says: *"We just have to get to eighteen. We just have to make it to graduation. Then we go to Prague and nobody can move me anywhere I don't want to go."*

Gamaliel says: *"Two years."*

She nods. *"Two years."*

He says: *"We can do two years."*

She nods again. *"We can do two years."*

And then, because she is Klára and she has been carrying it since the lift toward Mike's empty chair: *"I want to name a studio after him. In Prague. The first one we open. Mike Key Studio. Just — that."*

Gamaliel sets his spoon down for good. He puts his elbow on the island, his chin in his hand, and looks at his foster sister with the look that has been on his face since he was eight years old in a temporary foster home reaching for her.

He says: *"Yes. That."*

Klára: *"You don't want to think about it?"*

Gamaliel: *"I've already thought about it. The whole time during the lift. I had the same thought you did."*

Klára does not respond. She just looks at him. Something inside her — the part of her that has spent sixteen years being half a step ahead, ready to leave, ready to be moved, ready to lose — quiets for a second.

---

## BEAT 6 — THE SMALL BANANA SPLIT IS MELTING

The small banana split at Mike's place — the one they made for him with the two cherries — has been melting steadily through the conversation. The whipped cream is gone. The chocolate mint is a green pool. The banana is collapsing into it.

Klára looks at it. She says, *"Should we eat his?"*

Gamaliel thinks about it. He says: *"I think we should drink it."*

She blinks at him. Then she understands. She gets two small glasses from the cupboard — the ones Mike used to use for orange juice — and they pour the melted banana split between them, into the glasses, and drink it.

It is too sweet. It is exactly right. It tastes like everything Mike loved about that dessert, distilled down to the last form a banana split can take before it stops being a banana split at all. Klára drinks hers in three swallows. Gamaliel drinks his in two.

They wash the glasses and the oval dishes by hand. Klára dries. Gamaliel puts away. They leave the kitchen the way Mike would have left it: the over-the-stove light on, the cherry stems in the compost, the cutting board back in its slot beside the sink.

---

## BEAT 7 — TERRI ANNE COMES DOWN — MATCHA

They are about to head upstairs when they hear it: the soft sound of slippered footsteps on the back stairs. They both look up. Klára freezes for a half-second the way she still freezes around the sound of an adult arriving unannounced. Gamaliel reaches for her hand without looking.

**Terri Anne appears in the doorway.** She is in her dressing gown — the long pale-green one Mike used to call her *"librarian armor."* Her hair is down for the first time today. Her face is the face of a woman who has just buried her husband and has been awake for the last three hours pretending to be asleep so that her two oldest grandchildren could have her kitchen.

She does not pretend she hasn't heard them. She says, simply: *"I'm going to make matcha. Sit down."*

It is not a question. It is a grandmother sentence, delivered by a sixty-something woman who knows that everyone in this kitchen tonight has gone past the point where words help — and who knows that the only thing left to offer is the same thing she has offered her husband and her eight daughters for forty years: *a hot drink, made by hand, made for you, made in this kitchen.*

### What Terri Anne makes

Not Mike's tea. Not the Earl Grey. That belongs to the four-o'clock weeks-from-now conversation she will eventually walk in on between Patti Anne and Rachel. Tonight she makes something else, something quieter, something Mike never drank because Mike thought all green tea tasted *"like a lawn."*

**Matcha. Powdered green tea. The traditional way.** She takes down the small ceramic tin Mike used to tease her about. *"What do you need a tea you can't see through for?"* She gets out three matcha bowls — small, hand-thrown, mismatched, from a Toronto pottery studio she has visited twice a year for a decade. She gets the **chasen** — the bamboo whisk she replaces every couple of years and never throws the old one out — and the **chashaku** — the slim bamboo scoop.

She boils water. She lets it cool to the right temperature — not boiling, never boiling for matcha — counting the seconds in her head the way she has always counted, the way her own mother taught her.

Two scoops of bright green powder into each bowl. A small pour of cooled water. Then the whisk: a fast, precise *M* motion of the wrist, the chasen breaking the powder into a smooth jade froth. She has done this thousands of times. Tonight her hands are absolutely steady — it is the only part of her body that has been steady all day.

She does it three times. Three bowls. Three places at the island. She slides one to Klára. One to Gamaliel. One she keeps for herself. She sits on the stool at the end of the island — **not Mike's stool; the stool next to it.** Mike's empty stool stays empty. The chipped oval dish from 1998 is still at his place, washed clean now, drying.

### The first sip

Gamaliel takes his bowl in both hands the way Terri Anne taught him to, years ago, the first time he watched her do this. Klára does the same. Terri Anne nods once at both of them — the smallest correction-by-approval — and they all sip at the same time.

The matcha is bitter and grassy and bright. It is the opposite of the dessert that just left their mouths: bitter where the banana split was sweet, hot where the melted glass was cold, green where everything else was sugar. It cuts through. It is, somehow, exactly what the night has been waiting for.

For a long second nobody says anything.

Then Terri Anne says: *"He hated this stuff."*

And — for the first time tonight — **all three of them laugh.** Small, real, helpless laughs. The kind that escape because they need to. Klára laughs into her bowl. Gamaliel laughs and then puts his hand over his face. Terri Anne laughs and then her eyes fill and she does not wipe them.

She says, *"He called it lawn water. Forty years. He never drank a sip of it. I would make it for myself and he would walk through the kitchen and say, 'how's the lawn,' and I would tell him to leave."*

Klára says, in a small voice, *"He would have liked the banana split, though."*

Terri Anne looks at her. Her face does something soft. She says: *"He would have eaten the whole thing and yelled at us for using the small dish."*

Gamaliel: *"I knew it."*

### What Terri Anne tells them

She does not stay long. She does not ask what they were talking about for the last hour. She does not need to.

She does say, once — looking at both of them across the rim of her bowl — *"You did right by him today."*

She does not specify which *today.* It covers the funeral, the eulogies, the pas de deux, the conversation in Mike's study about Prague, and this. All of it. The whole long day, from the Mass at the church through to the empty banana split dish on his stool. *You did right by him today.*

Neither Gamaliel nor Klára knows what to say back to that. They both nod. Klára's eyes do not quite meet Terri Anne's, because if they do, she will cry, and she has spent the entire day not crying in front of any adult, and she is not going to break that streak ten minutes before bed.

Terri Anne sees it. She looks at her matcha. She gives Klára the privacy of not making her look up.

---

### THE OFFER — TWENTY-FIVE MILLION DOLLARS

Then Terri Anne sets down her matcha bowl with both hands. She straightens slightly. Her face changes — not hardening, but *settling*, the way her face used to settle before she said something she had already decided on hours earlier.

She looks at Gamaliel. Then at Klára. Then at both of them.

She says:

> *"I want to give you twenty-five million dollars to start your academy in Prague."*

The kitchen goes still in a way that even the banana-split silence did not. Willow lifts her head off Terri Anne's slipper and puts it back down. The over-the-stove light hums faintly.

Gamaliel and Klára do not, at first, react. Sentences with the word *million* in them do not land all at once when they have been said in a kitchen, by a grandmother in a dressing gown, on the night of a funeral. The number has to travel from her mouth, across the maple island, past Mike's empty stool, into their understanding.

Klára gets there first. Her hand finds the edge of the island. Her knuckles whiten.

Gamaliel says, carefully: *"Grandma."*

Terri Anne raises one hand — the same gesture she has used for forty years to mean *let me finish before you argue.*

> *"I have thought about this. I have not just thought about it tonight. I have been thinking about it since the lift. I knew, when I watched Klára extend her arm toward your grandfather's chair, that you two were going to need money to do what you are going to do, and that nobody else in this family is going to be in a position to give it to you the way I am. Your grandfather and I were careful with what we had. There is more than enough. This is the part of it I want you to have."*

She looks at both of them again. Then she says the second part, which is — for her — the more important part:

> *"It is not a loan. It is not an investment. It is not something you owe me back. It is a gift, and I do not want to hear about it again as a debt, ever. Do you understand me?"*

Klára nods, automatically, the way you nod when an adult uses a tone you have learned not to interrupt.

Gamaliel says, slowly: *"That's — Grandma, that's so much. That's more than we need. We can build the whole thing on a quarter of that. We don't —"*

Terri Anne cuts him off, gently. *"I know it's more than you need. That's the point. I don't want you to scrape. I don't want you to take on debt. I don't want Klára worrying about how the rent gets paid the month somebody is sick or the month a pipe bursts or the month one of you breaks an ankle. The extra is so that you have somewhere to fall. Your grandfather and I had somewhere to fall every time something went wrong. I want you to have that too."*

She pauses. She lifts her matcha again. She drinks. Then she says, quieter:

> *"And I want him to have done this for you. So I am doing it for him."*

### What Klára does

Klára does not say anything for a long second. She is sixteen and has spent her entire life knowing the price of every roof over her head, every meal in front of her, every shoe on her foot. She has been counting since she could count. The idea that twenty-five million dollars is being handed to her without strings, by a grandmother who has known her since she was eight, the night after her grandfather was buried — does not have a shape yet.

She says, very small: *"Why us?"*

Terri Anne answers without hesitating. *"Because you danced for him. Because you are going to build something. Because Mike loved both of you. Because I love both of you. And because I trust you with this. Those are the four reasons. Which one would you like me to say first?"*

Klára shakes her head — *no, I heard you* — and finally, *finally*, the tear that she has been holding back for fourteen hours falls. Just one. She does not wipe it.

Gamaliel, beside her, reaches under the island and squeezes her hand.

### What Gamaliel does

Gamaliel looks at his grandmother and says, *"Can we name the school after him?"*

Terri Anne smiles in the small, tired way she has been smiling at her grandchildren for forty years. She says, *"That is a separate conversation. You can name *a studio* after him if you want. The school you can name what you want it to be called. Not all good things have to wear Mike's name. Some of them just have to exist because he was here."*

Gamaliel nods. He files this away — *Mike Key Studio inside a school that has its own name* — and resolves to think about it tomorrow, and the next day, and for the next two years.

### How the offer lands

The number does not feel real yet. It will not feel real for several days. Klára will go to bed tonight and lie awake for an hour and the number will not feel real. Gamaliel will get up tomorrow morning and the number will not feel real.

But the *gift* feels real. The fact that their grandmother, on the night she buried her husband, sat down at her kitchen island and gave them the rest of their lives — that part is already real, and will not stop being real, ever.

Klára says: *"I don't know how to say thank you for this."*

Terri Anne says: *"You already did. This afternoon. In the front room. With the lift."*

---

### Finishing

They drink the rest of their matcha slowly. Nobody speaks. The over-the-stove light is on. Willow has come back down at some point during the matcha and is sprawled across Terri Anne's slippered feet under the island — Willow remembers, somehow, that Mike used to do this to her, and is reassigning the role.

When the bowls are empty, Terri Anne stands and washes the three matcha bowls and the chasen and the chashaku by hand, the way matcha tools have to be washed — by the person who used them, the same night they were used. Klára dries. Gamaliel puts away. Terri Anne returns the chasen to its small stand to dry properly. She returns the chashaku to its drawer. She wipes the island one more time.

She kisses Klára on the forehead. She kisses Gamaliel on the forehead. She says, *"Bed. Both of you. I love you."*

She does not say *I will see you in the morning,* because at this hour neither of them is sure what the morning will look like, and Terri Anne does not make promises about mornings she has not seen yet — not anymore, not after today.

She goes upstairs first. They follow a few minutes later.

---

## BEAT 8 — THE HALLWAY

They go upstairs together. The mansion creaks the way old houses creak. It is past midnight now. Mike has been in the ground for less than twelve hours.

At the top of the stairs they stop. Klára's room — the one Terri Anne has kept for her, by the east-facing window seat — is to the right. Gamaliel's room — down the hall, in what used to be a small study — is to the left.

Willow has followed them up. She walks past both of them and goes into Klára's room and gets up on the bed without being told.

Gamaliel and Klára look at each other in the hallway. They are both still in the ballet outfits they wore for Mike — Klára in her black leotard and practice skirt, Gamaliel in his black dance shirt and tights under Mike's borrowed grey cardigan. Klára's bun has half collapsed. Klára's mascara has run a little and she has not fixed it because there has been nobody to fix it for. They are sixteen.

They do not kiss. They do not need to tonight. Klára touches Gamaliel's forearm. He touches the back of her hand. They look at each other for two seconds. Klára says, *"Two years."*

Gamaliel says, *"Two years."*

She goes right. He goes left. The doors are left cracked, the way every door in this family is left cracked.

Downstairs, in the dark kitchen, the over-the-stove light is on. The maple island is clean. There is nothing on it but a slight ring where the melted glass sat, which Klára will wipe away in the morning before Terri Anne comes down.

Upstairs in her own bedroom, Terri Anne sits on the edge of her bed for a long minute before lying down. She is the surviving matriarch of this family, and tonight she did two things: she let two sixteen-year-olds have her husband's kitchen to themselves for an hour, and then — when she heard them finish the dishes — she came down and made them matcha. She believes she got both halves right. She turns out her bedside lamp.

The mansion is dark, except for Mike's over-the-stove light.

---

## WHAT THIS SCENE ESTABLISHES

- **Gamaliel and Klára never changed out of their ballet outfits.** They eat Mike's banana split, drink the melted version from his orange-juice glasses, and walk upstairs to bed in the same clothes they wore for the pas de deux that afternoon. Klára in her black leotard and practice skirt and stocking feet; Gamaliel in his black dance shirt and tights, with **one of Mike's own grey cardigans** over the top (handed to him by Terri Anne on his way through the front room, without comment). This is canon: the body of the dance does not leave the body of the dancer for the rest of the day.
- **Mike's banana split is now associated with the conversation Gamaliel and Klára have, alone, at his kitchen island, on the night of his funeral.** It is *their* dessert in this saga — the inheritance they take from him the same day he is buried.
- **The banana split recipe is canon:** banana split lengthwise, three scoops with **chocolate mint in the middle** (Mike's iron rule), vanilla and strawberry flanking, chocolate sauce (cold after midnight is honest sauce), can whipped cream piled too tall on purpose, **two cherries (a single cherry is a coward's dessert)**, no nuts.
- **Klára's dog, Willow, is present throughout.** She stays in the kitchen keeping them warm and sprawls over Terri Anne's slippers during the matcha. (Her second dog, Kleopatra, is not born yet.)
- **They build a small extra one for Mike's place** at the island. When it melts, they pour it into Mike's old orange-juice glasses and drink it. That ritual — *the melted banana split poured into juice glasses and drunk* — is now a thing the saga can use again.
- **Gamaliel says out loud that if the system tries to move Klára, he goes with her.** Klára refuses to let him say it lightly. She makes him own the weight of it. He does. That sentence is now between them on the record.
- **Klára names what she wants to do in Prague: name the first studio after Mike.** *Mike Key Studio.* Gamaliel says yes immediately, and tells her he had the same thought during the lift toward Mike's empty chair. Two minds, same vow, mid-dance.
- **"Two years."** They say it to each other twice. It becomes the small phrase they will use to themselves between now and graduation when things are hard.
- **Terri Anne stays upstairs through the banana split, then comes down and makes them matcha.** Her first act as the surviving matriarch is to leave them the kitchen; her second is to join them in it. The two halves of the same instinct.
- **Matcha (powdered green tea, traditional preparation) is now canon for Terri Anne.** Three bowls, hand-thrown, mismatched. Bamboo chasen, bamboo chashaku. *Mike hated it* — called it *"lawn water"* for forty years — and that joke becomes the first time anyone in the kitchen laughs since the funeral. The matcha is the deliberate counterpoint to Mike's banana split: bitter where his dessert was sweet, green where his was sugar. Two grief inheritances, his and hers, served in the same kitchen on the same night.
- **Terri Anne sits at the stool next to Mike's, not on Mike's.** His stool stays empty. The chipped 1998 oval dish stays at his place, washed and drying.
- **Terri Anne's verdict: *"You did right by him today."*** The whole long day, summed in five words, said once.
- **Terri Anne offers Gamaliel and Klára twenty-five million dollars to start their ballet academy in Prague.** Not a loan. Not an investment. A gift. *"It is not something you owe me back."* The number is deliberately more than they need — *"the extra is so that you have somewhere to fall."* She has been thinking about it since the lift toward Mike's empty chair. She frames it: *"I want him to have done this for you. So I am doing it for him."* Gamaliel asks if they can name the school after Mike; she answers, *"A studio, yes. The school you can name what you want."* The Prague academy now has a working name (**Mike Key Studio**, for the first studio) **and** a funding source.\n- This scene is the **very first night** of the post-Mike era for both Gamaliel and Klára. Everything that comes after — Patti Anne's FaceTime with Rachel weeks later, the cruise, the eventual post-cruise conversation, graduation, Prague — flows downstream from this kitchen.
