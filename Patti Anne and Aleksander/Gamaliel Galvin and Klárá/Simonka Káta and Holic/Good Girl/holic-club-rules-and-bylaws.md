## Tone & Boundaries (for any scene that uses these rules)

- **Frank and adult.** The club is a consensual adult kink community organized around human-pet dynamics: collars, kennels, leads, bowls, branding, pet-space rules, and sex between consenting adults. The document does not euphemize this.
- **Non-graphic on sex acts.** Consistent with every Simonka file, sexual acts are not depicted explicitly; scenes fade out at the threshold. The weight is psychological, social, and ethical.
- **Consent is the spine.** Every member and pet is an adult and a volunteer. The drama of this world comes from what happens when consent culture is *tested* — which only works if the text takes consent seriously.
- **The rules are real on paper and unevenly enforced in practice.** That gap is the point. A scene should never present the rulebook as a guarantee; it is an aspiration the club fails or honors under pressure.

## 0. NAME, SELF-IMAGE & THE ANNUAL SHOW

### The name

- **Full name:** **Collegium Fidelium** — Latin, "The Society of the Faithful." *(Latin register; refined gentleman's-club self-image.)*
- **What members call it:** **"the Collegium."** In English, occasionally **"the Society."**
- **The cover.** *Collegium* is an ordinary, respectable word for a guild or association (Roman social and trade clubs were *collegia*). To an outsider it sounds like a wine circle, an academic society, or a hunting club. It is designed to mean nothing alarming.
- **The buried meaning.** *Fidelium* = "of the faithful." The club's chilling elegance is that the **"faithful" are the pets** — *fidelity* being the dog's cardinal virtue. A dignified fraternal order named, in plain Latin, for the loyalty of the creatures it keeps. Members understand this; outsiders never would.

### Motto

- **Per vincula, cura** — "Through the bond, care." *(Alt: "Through the leash, care," since* vinculum *= a bond, a tie, a chain, a leash.)*
- The motto encodes the club's central self-justification — the same *control-is-care* rationalization the saga interrogates (see §10.3, §21.4, §19). The name and motto carry the theme: the Collegium genuinely believes its bonds are kindness.

### The annual show

- **Name:** **the Concursus** (full: **Concursus Fidelium**). *Concursus* means both **a gathering/assembly** and **a contest/competition** — precisely a show where the faithful are brought together and judged.
- The Concursus is the Collegium's flagship event and organizing purpose; much of the year's training (and the kennel-yard endurance conditioning, §21.3) bends toward it. *(Full treatment in §IV / §13.)*

### Naming alternates (if any of the above needs to change)

- **Club:** *Collegium Canorum* ("of the hounds/songbirds" — punnier, less clean); *Societas Fidelis*; *Ordo Fidelium* ("the Order of the Faithful" — more cultish); plain English front: **"The Kennel Society."**
- **Show:** *Exhibitio Fidelium*; *the Exhibition*; *the Showing*; *Dies Fidelium* ("the Day of the Faithful").

## II. THE CORE RULES (STATED ETHICS)

*These are the rules the club recites about itself. They are genuine commitments on paper. §VII covers where they break.*

### 3. The Silence Rule *(the club's signature discipline)*

3.1. **A pet in pet space does not speak.** While in pet space, a pet communicates only through posture, position, and trained behavior. For most pets, collaring marks entry into pet space; for permanently collared pets like GG, pet space is marked by name, posture, place, commands, and handling rather than by the collar appearing or disappearing.

3.2. **The only permitted speech is a safeword.** A pet's voice is reserved exclusively for **yellow** and **red**. Nothing else.

3.3. **Stated logic (protective):** by keeping a pet silent in all things, the safeword can never be lost in chatter. The two words always carry absolute weight because they are the only words a pet ever says.

3.4. **Lived consequence (control):** a pet can say *stop* but never *explain.* There is no sanctioned way for a pet to say *yes, but,* or *wait, what is happening to me,* or to give testimony, or to express nuance. The vocabulary of a collared pet is two words, both of which mean some version of *less.*

3.5. **The way to restore a pet's full voice is to release them from pet space** (see §V, Collaring, and §VI, Release-to-Speak). For most pets this means removing the collar. For GG, whose dog collar stays on permanently, this means explicitly returning her to Simonka/person-register while the collar remains on.

### 4. Safewords

4.1. **Yellow** = slow down / check in / something is wrong, attend to me.

4.2. **Red** = full stop, immediately and without question.

4.3. Safewords are **universal and binding on everyone in the Collegium** — every owner *and* every handler (§21.5), not only a pet's own owner. Any owner or handler who hears any pet's *red* must stop and ensure the pet is safe. This is what (in the club's own account) keeps a pet safe even when their owner is not present — e.g., while owners meet and pets are in the kennel yard.

4.4. A safeword may **never** be punished, mocked, or treated as failure. A pet who uses a safeword has done the single thing the silence rule exists to protect.

4.5. **Limitation the club does not advertise:** a safeword can only address a harm the pet *perceives.* It cannot reach a harm the pet does not know is being done to them (see §VII and the Spark violation).

### 5. Consent

5.1. All dynamics rest on **negotiated limits** agreed before pet space begins.

5.2. **Consent is ongoing.** A pet retains the right to withdraw at any time, via safeword or — out of pet space — in plain words. For most pets, being out of pet space means decollared. For GG, the dog collar remains on permanently, so plain-word withdrawal must still be available while she is collared but addressed as Simonka.

5.3. Consent given to one owner does **not** transfer to another. A **shared scene** (a pet interacting with an owner other than their own, or two pets together) requires explicit, separate negotiation and the consent of every owner and pet involved.

### 6. Medical Disclosure

6.1. **No substances, conditions, injuries, or medications may be hidden** in any scene, and most strictly in shared scenes.

6.2. Before any pet interacts with another owner's pet, **full disclosure** of anything affecting capacity, perception, or safety is mandatory — disclosed to every owner and pet in the scene.

6.3. **Violating disclosure is, by the club's own logic, among the gravest breaches it recognizes,** because it makes consent and safewords meaningless: you cannot consent to, or safeword, what you were not told. *(Whether the club actually enforces this when tested is a later thread.)*

### 7. Owner Responsibility

7.1. An owner is accountable for **their pet's** safety, limits, hydration, rest, and aftercare.

7.2. An owner is equally accountable for **their own conduct toward other owners' pets.**

7.3. An owner who brings a pet into a shared scene is responsible for ensuring every disclosure rule (§6) has been honored on their side.

7.4. An owner may not delegate away responsibility. Leaving one's pet with another owner does not transfer the duty of care; it extends it.

### 8. Aftercare

8.1. **Every scene must end in care:** warmth, water, rest, and a verbal debrief.

8.2. Aftercare is the owner's duty and is **not optional**, regardless of how the scene went.

8.3. Aftercare is also where a pet, brought back toward themselves, may raise concerns — including a **yellow about something larger than the scene** (the meeting, the club, the dynamic). A good owner hears this; a controlling owner reassures it into silence. The difference is the difference the club claims to know.

## III. RITUALS & MARKS

### 9. The Collar

9.1. **Standard convention:** collar on = pet space. The pet assumes their pet name and the silence rule binds.

9.2. **Standard convention:** collar off = the person returns, with full voice and full standing.

9.3. The collaring at a threshold (e.g., arriving at a meeting) is a formal transformation for most pets, often the last exchange between owner and pet "as equals" before the role begins.

9.4. **GG exception — permanent dog collar.** Once Simonka chooses the permanent GG life, Holic's dog collar stays on permanently. It is not removed when she speaks as Simonka. For GG, movement into and out of pet space is marked by name, posture, place, command, handling, and explicit release — not by taking the dog collar on and off.

### 10. The Electric Collar

10.1. Electric shock collars are **permitted within agreed limits** — negotiated intensity, agreed circumstances, and the absolute override of the safeword.

10.2. The club regards Holic's use of an electric collar as **restrained by club standards.** *Most owners in the club use harsher methods or equipment.* This is canon and important: Holic is severe by ordinary standards and comparatively mild *within this world* — which indicts the world, not absolves Holic.

10.3. An electric collar may **never** be used as anger, punishment-in-cruelty, or outside negotiated limits. The club's stated philosophy (Holic's words): *control can be care, if the one holding it knows the difference.*

### 11. Branding

11.1. The club marks pets permanently with a **very hot branding iron.**

11.2. **The brand is the owner's initials.** (Simonka's: **H.B.**, for Holic Bartunek.)

11.3. **Required placement: the right buttock.** Uniform across the club.

11.4. Branding is **ceremonial and weighty within the hierarchy** — a permanent claim, performed (per canon) with another member and the branding iron present, often as part of a pet's formal entry into club life.

11.5. A fresh brand marks a pet as new; senior pets bear older marks. The brand's age is, in practice, a visible timestamp of how recently a pet chose this life.

### 12. Names

12.1. In pet space a pet answers only to their **pet name** (Simonka's: **Good Girl / GG**).

12.2. The pet name functions as both identity and praise; "good girl" / "good boy" is reward as well as address.

## IV. THE ANNUAL HUMAN PET SHOW — THE CONCURSUS

13.1. The **Concursus** (full: *Concursus Fidelium*) is the Collegium's **public-facing event** (public within the membership and invited guests, not the world). Its organizing purpose; much of the year's training bends toward it. The name means both *gathering* and *contest* — the faithful assembled and judged.

13.2. Pets are kept together in a **large fenced area** during the show.

13.3. Because of the fenced-area format, certain routines that would otherwise be private are **deliberately not private.** Per canon, this includes toilet training: a show pet is expected to **go on the ground in front of the other pets** as part of shared pet-space rules. Owners train for this in advance.

13.4. Show preparation covers **posture, stillness, obedience, endurance, presentation, and conduct around other owners and pets** — including fenced-area group behavior.

## V. THE MONTHLY MEETING

*The private machinery behind the public show. (Full treatment in `holic-club-monthly-meeting-scene-outline.md`.)*

### 14. Where the pets are during the meeting

14.1. **While the club meets, the pets are kept outside in a large fenced area.** The formal meeting is **owners only.** Pets do not attend the business of the club.

14.2. This is the same kind of **large fenced area** used to hold pets together at the annual show (§13.2) — collared, in pet space, managed as a group, subject to the non-private fenced-area routines (§13.3). For much of the year the area's two uses rhyme: the show displays pets in it; the monthly meeting *parks* pets in it.

14.3. **Why it matters (the structural point, stated plainly):** the people whose bodies, marks, and dynamics are the entire subject of the meeting are **not in the room.** Owners speak, vote, and rule indoors; pets wait on the grass behind a fence. The silence rule (§3) and the no-vote rule (§1.2) reach their logical conclusion here — a pet has no words *and* is not even present for the words spoken about them.

14.4. **Sequence.** Most pets are collared at the threshold (§9.3), then **taken out to the fenced area** for the duration of the owners' meeting. GG arrives already wearing her permanent dog collar (§9.4) and is shifted into pet space by Holic's handling and address. Pets are **brought in (or the owners come out) only for the parts that require them** — presentation of new pets, the training/demonstration block, and later the social/scene portion. Pure governance and discipline happen while the pets are outside.

### 15. Standing agenda

1. **Arrival & entry into pet space** — most pets are collared at the threshold; GG arrives already wearing her permanent dog collar. Pets enter pet space, then are taken out to the **fenced area.**
2. **Steward opens (owners only, pets outside)** — minutes, dues, calendar (the show looms), welcomes.
3. **Presentation of new pets** — pets brought in (or owners go out to the area); a new pet is formally introduced to the membership; the brand acknowledged; the owner speaks for the pet. The pet then returns to the area.
4. **Training & demonstration block** — pets brought in or worked in the area itself; skill-sharing; show-prep; fenced-area group routines.
5. **Club business / disciplinary matters (owners only, pets outside)** — handled by the Circle while pets remain in the fenced area.
6. **Social / scene time** — the unstructured adult portion. *(Non-graphic; scenes fade away.)*
7. **Aftercare & return from pet space** — pets brought back out of pet space; most pets are decollared; GG's permanent dog collar stays on; owners accountable for safe departure.

## VI. DISCIPLINE & ENFORCEMENT

### 16. Jurisdiction

16.1. The **Circle** hears and rules on violations. Serious matters are raised in the disciplinary block of the monthly meeting — **while the pets are outside in the fenced area** (§14).

16.2. Penalties available: **warning, sanction, probation, suspension, or expulsion.** The Circle sets the penalty.

### 17. Release-to-Speak

17.1. Because of the silence rule (§3), **a pet in pet space cannot give testimony.** A pet who has been harmed, or who witnessed harm, has no words for it while in pet space — only *yellow* and *red.*

17.2. For most pets, the mechanism that frees a pet's full voice is **removing the collar.** Decollared, the pet is no longer a pet but a **person**, and a person may speak freely.

17.2a. **GG exception:** because Holic's dog collar stays on permanently (§9.4), removing the collar is not the mechanism for restoring Simonka's voice. She must be explicitly released from pet space and addressed as Simonka while still collared. This exception is structurally important: the club's ordinary collar logic does not fully fit her.

17.3. **Compounded by the fenced area (§14):** a pet whose own dynamic is being discussed is not merely silent but **absent** — waiting outside, behind a fence, while owners deliberate indoors. To be heard, a pet would first have to be brought in and released from pet space. For most pets that means decollaring; for GG it means being named and treated as Simonka while the collar remains on.

17.4. **Open question (TBD for the saga):** is release-to-testify an *established, codified* mechanism the club has used before, or an *unprecedented act* the Circle must improvise under pressure? This choice determines how radical it is to bring a pet in from the fenced area and release them in front of the membership.

> *This rule's real test — whether the club will actually bring a pet in and release them to hear them, versus letting an owner speak "for" the pet — belongs to the later rule-breaking thread, not to this foundation document.*

### 18. The Standard the Club Claims

18.1. **The disclosure rule is the keystone.** The club's own logic: every safeword, every negotiated limit, every collar depends on disclosure being honored. *"A pet cannot consent to what he was not told."* Break disclosure and the entire structure is theater.

18.2. By the club's own standard, a **disclosure violation in a shared scene is the gravest ordinary breach** — graver than excess, because excess can be safeworded and a hidden harm cannot.

## VII. THE FAULT LINES (BUILT-IN, NOT YET SPRUNG)

*This section names the weaknesses **designed into** the rules — so no scene mistakes the rulebook for a guarantee. These are the pressures that the **later rule-breaking thread** will test. They are stated here as latent structure, not as events that have already played out.*

19.1. **Enforcement will be uneven.** The rules are genuine on paper but depend on people to apply them. **Status protects bad actors.** Senior owners are deferred to; the Circle is not immune to its own hierarchy. Whether the club enforces against a connected member is exactly what a future reckoning will reveal.

19.2. **The pressure the rules ignore: a pet's hunger to belong.** A new pet's desire to "earn her place" and impress her owner is a force the rulebook does not account for. It can override a pet's own safety — a pet may decline to safeword *not* because nothing is wrong but because using it feels like failing. (Canon already establishes this in GG.)

19.3. **The silence rule's failure mode.** Designed to protect the safeword, it also guarantees a pet cannot report, explain, or testify. When a harm is one a pet cannot perceive, or one a pet won't name for fear of disappointing, two words are not enough — and two words are all a pet in pet space has. The §17 release-to-speak mechanism is the only release valve, and its reliability is untested.

19.4. **Decisions are made about the absent.** Because pets are held in the fenced area during governance and discipline (§14), the people most affected by a ruling are not present to hear it, react to it, or be asked about it. Combined with the no-vote rule (§1.2) and the silence rule (§3), this means a pet can be discussed, judged, and disposed of without ever being in the room. The fence is the most literal form of the club's central contradiction — and the place a pet would have to be brought *in* from before they could ever be decollared to speak (§17.3).

19.5. **The disclosure keystone is the most exposed.** Because every safeword and every consent depends on disclosure being honest (§6, §18), a **hidden** harm is the one thing the entire safeguard architecture cannot catch in the moment. This is the fault line most likely to be exploited later. *(The specific case that springs it — the Spark violation — is handled in its own scene, not in this rules document.)*

19.6. **The best-of-a-bad-standard problem.** Holic is, by the club's own measure, a *good* owner: restrained electric-collar use, real aftercare, a sincere belief in disclosure. He is also a man who owns, brands, and electric-collars a woman and trains her to crave his approval to the point she may not safeword. He is the best of this world — and that is an indictment of the world's standard, not an absolution of Holic. Scenes should sit in that discomfort rather than resolve it.

## VIII. THE VENUE & THE FENCED AREA

### 20. The venue — a working farm

20.1. The club meets at a **working farm / barn complex** — a private agricultural property, not a clubhouse and not Holic's mansion. Real outbuildings, real barns, real land, repurposed for the club's use. The agricultural character is functional, not decorative: the place already had pens, fencing, drainage, water lines, and outbuildings built to hold animals, and the club adapted them.

20.2. **Location:** in the same **affluent district as Holic's mansion** — the wealthy rural-adjacent belt outside Prague where large private land holdings, estate farms, and gentleman's farms sit behind gates. The venue is **a short drive from Holic's**, which reinforces that this is a world of money and proximity: the club, the mansion, and the limousine all move within the same small, insulated, well-off geography. The drive there is brief; the threshold is social, not distance.

20.3. **Why a farm fits the club's logic (state plainly, don't moralize in-text):** a farm is a place organized around animals — their housing, feeding, marking, breeding, showing, and management. The club's human-pet framework maps onto agricultural infrastructure with almost no modification. The barn, the pen, the brand, the show ring, the feed bowls — all of it has a literal farm antecedent. The venue makes the club's central metaphor concrete: people kept and managed as livestock are.

20.4. **The barns and outbuildings** house the indoor functions: the great room / meeting hall (a converted main barn or farmhouse), the den/kennel wing, scene rooms, storage for equipment. *(Specific layout TBD.)*

### 21. The fenced area — an engineered kennel yard

21.1. The large fenced area where pets are held during the owners' meeting (§14) is a **purpose-built kennel yard** — engineered, not improvised. Secure fencing, controlled access, drainage, hard standing and ground, and the fixtures of a professional animal-holding facility, adapted to human pets.

21.2. It is the **same enclosure used to hold pets at the annual show** (§13.2): dual-purpose, show ground and holding yard at once. On show day it is staged and watched; on meeting nights it is the place pets simply wait.

21.3. **Conditions are deliberately harsh.** Despite being engineered, the kennel yard is **not** built for the pets' comfort. **Exposure is treated as part of the discipline** — endurance, stamina, and obedience training for the demands of the annual show, where pets are kept outdoors for long stretches. A cold Prague night in the kennel yard is, in the club's framing, *conditioning,* not neglect. Pets are expected to endure it.

21.4. **The contradiction this creates (a fault line, not a contradiction the club admits):** the club's stated ethics include an **aftercare mandate** and a self-image built on *care* (§8, §10.3 — "control can be care"). The harsh kennel yard sits in direct tension with that. The club squares it by reclassifying exposure as *training* rather than *harm* — the same move it makes everywhere (the brand is "tradition," the electric collar is "a signal," the harsh yard is "conditioning"). Each reclassification lets a cruelty pass as a discipline. **This is exactly the kind of self-justification the later rule-breaking thread will pressure.** See §19.

21.5. **Supervision — the handlers.** The kennel yard is watched by **handlers**: non-owner staff of the Collegium whose job is to manage the pets while the owners meet. A handler is not an owner and does not own any pet; the role is custodial — keeping order in the yard, enforcing the club's procedures, and, critically, **standing as the safeword authority while the owners are inside.**

21.5.1. **A pet can always safeword to a handler.** This is absolute and is the club's stated proof that the harsh yard (§21.3) is still "care": even with the owners shut inside, every pet retains *yellow* and *red,* and a handler is bound to honor them exactly as an owner would (§4.3 — safewords bind all members; handlers are held to the same standard). A pet who says *red* to a handler is removed from the yard; *yellow* gets a check-in.

21.5.2. **The catch the club does not advertise.** The safeword's existence is not the same as a pet's willingness to use it. A pet who wants to please her owner — to be found to have endured, to have been *good* in the yard — will decline to safeword to a handler even when she could and arguably should. The mechanism is intact; the pressure not to use it (§19.2) is exactly as intact. The handler is a real safeguard that a status-hungry or owner-pleasing pet will route around by choice. *(Canonically true of GG.)*

21.5.3. **The handler as a club role (new).** Handlers are staff, not members — paid or junior figures, present at meetings and the Concursus, managing the yard, leading pets to and from the hall, assisting at rituals. They have authority over pets *in custody* but no standing in governance (no vote, not on the Circle). Names/structure TBD.

21.6. **What it does to GG, on the pivot Tuesday:** branded days earlier, still healing, dressed carefully to be *seen,* she is instead put out into a cold engineered yard to endure — while the owners deliberate indoors about pets, including her. The yard is where the club's reality outruns its language: whatever "control is care" meant in Holic's mansion, the kennel yard on a cold night means something harder. *(Dramatized in `holic-club-monthly-meeting-scene-outline.md`, Beat 3.)*
