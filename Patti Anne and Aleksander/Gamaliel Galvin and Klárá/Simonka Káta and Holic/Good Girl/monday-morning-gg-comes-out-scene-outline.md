# MONDAY MORNING — GG COMES OUT

## Scene Outline — Beats, Dialogue Cues, and Thematic Notes

> **Companion to** `holic-asks-gg-the-morning-after-scene-outline.md`, `gg-walks-out-to-the-doghouse-scene-outline.md`, and `holic-club-rules-and-bylaws.md`. **Read those first.** This scene is the Monday morning after the Sunday-night blindfold test. GG has been blindfolded since the test began. She has slept in the doghouse (or wherever the Sunday night test placed her). It is now morning. The arc closes the loop: *blindfold on → blindfold off; GG in → GG out; commanded → released.*

> **Content & craft warning.** This scene depicts the tail end of a consensual adult human-pet arrangement: Simonka, in GG-form, blindfolded, collared, crawls to the kitchen and eats from her bowls; Holic walks her; he administers the pill; he releases her from GG-form and tells her to remove the blindfold. The prose must hold the same discipline as every other Simonka/Holic outline: no titillation, no glamour, no shame, no psychologising. **The body does what the body does. The pet does what the pet does. The household has trained for this.** What is new is the *coming out* — the first time in this arc that Holic explicitly tells GG she is finished, that she may return to Simonka, that the blindfold can come off. *That moment is the whole scene.*

---

**POV:** **GG → Simonka.** *(The consciousness in the room begins as GG — sensory, obedient, blindfold-dark — and becomes Simonka again at the moment of release. The prose should let the shift happen without announcing it. The last paragraph is the first paragraph of Simonka thinking again.)*

**Tone:** Morning. The quiet of a house that has been living in two registers all weekend. The blindfold test has been going on since Sunday evening. GG is tired but functional; she has slept in the dark and woken in the dark and the world has been a series of sounds, smells, touches, commands. **This morning is the first morning the blindfold comes off.** The prose may render the physical fact of light returning — the flinch, the squint, the slow recognition of the room.

**Location:** **Holic's mansion — kitchen and back yard.** The kitchen: tile floor, the same bowls as always, placed on the same mat, the same dog food (brand TBD; see OPEN ITEMS). The counter at standing height; the table at sitting height; neither matters to GG. The floor is the floor. The back yard: the engineered kennel yard, the iron fence, the path from kitchen door to fence gate and back. Monday morning light.

**Time in saga:** **Monday morning.** The blindfold test began Sunday evening (~6:00 PM pickup, then the demonstration with Bruno, then the limousine ride to the mansion, then the commands and guidance through the house). GG has been blindfolded continuously since then. She has slept — in the doghouse or wherever Holic directed. She wakes Monday morning still in the blindfold. The 24-hour test (if it was 24 hours) has ended, or Holic has decided it has ended. The pill is due. The release happens now.

## THE BEATS

### Beat 0 — Waking

GG wakes in the dark. She has been sleeping somewhere in the house — likely the doghouse or a floor space Holic designated. The blindfold is on; it has been on for hours, maybe since Sunday evening. She knows where she is by the smell of the sheets/blanket, the sound of the house waking (a boiler clicking on somewhere, a clock ticking, birds outside), the position of her body on the floor.

She does not take the blindfold off. That is not her decision. The test is still running, or she does not know whether it has ended.

She waits.

### Beat 1 — The Command

Holic's voice. From the doorway, or from somewhere in the room. A simple command:

*"Up. Kitchen."*

Or perhaps nothing so direct. Perhaps just a tap on the floor beside her, or the sound of his footsteps, and GG — trained now for weeks, months — reads the cue and begins to move.

She rises. She orients herself by the wall, by the memory of the corridor layout. She crawls.

### Beat 2 — The Crawl to the Kitchen

GG moves through the mansion on hands and knees. The blindfold limits her to touch and sound: the texture of the hallway floor changing from wood to tile as she enters the kitchen area; the sound of the refrigerator; the smell of the food before she can see it.

Holic is already in the kitchen. He has prepared her bowls — the same ones, the same food, placed on the same mat in the same spot near the floor. He has done this before; the routine is established. The prose renders the preparation as *fact*: Holic, in a robe (colour TBD), standing at the counter, opening the can or bag, placing the food in the bowls, setting them down. No ceremony. No commentary. The household's *cura* side, performed in the kitchen on a Monday morning.

GG arrives at the kitchen. She stops at the threshold — or perhaps Holic gives a soft sound, a click of the tongue, and she comes to the bowls.

### Beat 3 — The Meal

GG eats. The prose renders this with the same clean discipline as the doghouse outline: the body doing what the body does, the bowl on the floor, the blindfold still on, the eating without sight. The sounds of chewing, swallowing, the occasional pause. The smell of the food (which she has learned to recognise as hers).

Holic watches, or does not watch. He may be at the table with his own breakfast — coffee, toast, something human. The two meals happen in the same room, in different registers. **The prose does not judge the juxtaposition.** It renders it.

When GG is finished, she sits back. She waits. The meal is complete; the next thing is Holic's call.

### Beat 4 — The Walk

Holic stands. He gives the cue — a word, a sound, a touch on the collar — and GG rises. He leads her out the back door, into the yard.

The walk is short. The kennel yard is enclosed: iron uprights on stone footings, the fence holding the world out. The path goes from the kitchen door along the side of the mansion to the back gate and back again. GG follows at heel, blindfolded, on hands and knees or on all fours (the posture depends on the household's established gait for walks; the outline leaves this to the prose).

The morning air is cool. GG can smell the yard — grass, the fence, the doghouse in the corner, the faint residue of Sunday night. She can hear the street beyond the fence — a car passing, a distant tram. The blindfold makes every sense sharper, as the test intended.

Holic walks beside her. He does not speak. The walk is not a training exercise; it is the morning's routine, the *walk* that follows the *meal*, as it does every morning in this household.

### Beat 5 — The Pill

They return to the kitchen. Holic has the pill ready — a small cup of water, the pill between his fingers. This is the daily pill (birth control, per the morning-after outline's canon: *"The pill, taken in Beat 5 of the morning-after, is still doing its small daily work"*).

GG takes the pill. The prose renders this as the household renders it: a small physical act, no drama. Holic tilts her chin, places the pill, offers the water. GG swallows. It is done.

**This is the last act of GG-care before the release.** The pill is the boundary object — it belongs to Simonka's body but is administered to GG. The prose may note this without commenting on it.

### Beat 6 — The Release

Holic sets the water down. He stands. He speaks.

The exact words matter. The outline proposes:

> *"You can come out of Good Girl now. Take your blindfold off."*

Or something closer to the household's established register:

> *"That's enough, Good Girl. You may come out now. Remove the blindfold."*

Or simpler still — the household's language tends toward the spare:

> *"Out. Take it off."*

The prose should choose the phrasing that best fits Holic's voice as established across the arc. **The words themselves are not dramatic. The drama is in what they mean.** For the first time in this scene — perhaps for the first time in this arc — Holic is not giving a command to GG. He is *releasing* Simonka.

GG sits. She reaches up. Her fingers find the edge of the blindfold. She pulls it off.

### Beat 7 — Light

The light hits her. The kitchen window is east-facing (TBD — adjust to match the mansion's actual orientation). Monday morning sun, pale and new, comes through the glass and onto the tile floor and into her eyes.

She blinks. She squints. The room resolves: the bowls on the mat, Holic at the counter, the robe on the hook by the back door, the collar still around her neck.

She is still collared. The blindfold is off, but the collar is on and will stay on. The release is partial by design: **Holic has told her to take the blindfold off. The collar is not available for removal.**

The prose holds this distinction. The blindfold was the test. The collar is the life. *She can come out of the blindfold — and later out of full GG-form — but not out of the permanent thing that marks her as GG.*

### Beat 8 — Simonka Returns

The consciousness in the room shifts. Simonka opens her eyes properly. She looks at Holic. She looks at the bowls. She looks at her own hands — on the floor, still in the posture of the pet.

She does not stand immediately. The prose may let her stay on the floor for a moment — not because she is commanded to, but because *she is not yet ready to be upright.* The transition from GG to Simonka is not instantaneous. It is a process, and this scene captures the first step of it.

Holic does not rush her. He stands at the counter, drinking his coffee, waiting. He has done this before — perhaps not this exact sequence, but the *waiting* part of it. He knows that GG does not become Simonka on command. She becomes Simonka when she is ready.

### Beat 9 — The Assessment

Simonka is upright now, or getting there. She is seeing the room, seeing Holic, being a person again. Holic speaks — not as an owner giving a command, but as a man making an observation about the pet he has just released.

**First statement: the natural skills.**

Holic tells Simonka that he is very impressed with her natural skills. The prose should render this without flattery — Holic is not a man who gushes. His praise is factual, delivered in the same register as his commands, but the content is evaluation.

Suggested phrasing (adjust to fit Holic's established voice):

> *"I am very impressed with your natural skills, Simonka."*

Or more sparely:

> *"You have natural skill. I noticed it this weekend. I am impressed."*

Or in the household's most minimal register:

> *"Your natural skills are very good. I am impressed."*

**The weight of this moment.** This is the first time Holic offers an explicit assessment of Simonka-as-GG to Simonka-as-Simonka. Throughout the test — the blindfold, the commands, the walk, the kennel — she has performed without feedback, without knowing how she is being judged. Now, released, she learns: *he was watching, and what he saw, he liked.*

**Simonka's response.** The prose may let her receive this without knowing how to receive it. She has spent the weekend being GG — a creature who does not receive compliments, who does not process evaluation, who simply obeys. Now Simonka is back, and Simonka hears praise for the things GG did. **The dissonance is the point.** She may nod. She may say nothing. She may flush — not with embarrassment, but with the strange feeling of being seen across the boundary between her two lives.

**Second statement: the 2 AM crawl.**

Then Holic tells her something else. He tells Simonka that he knows that around two o'clock, she crawled to the kennel with her blindfold on.

Suggested phrasing:

> *"I know that around two o'clock, you crawled to the kennel with your blindfold on."*

Or:

> *"Around two in the morning, you crawled to the kennel. Blindfolded. I know."*

**Why this matters.** Simonka did not know he was watching. During the blindfold test, she was in the dark — literally and figuratively. She thought she was alone in her obedience, that the household was sleeping, that the night belonged to her and the kennel and the fence and the dark. **But Holic was watching.** Or he has other means of knowing — cameras, a staff member who reports, his own quiet surveillance of the grounds. The prose need not reveal how he knows. **That he knows is enough.**

**The two statements together.** Holic gives her praise ("impressed with your natural skills") and surveillance ("I know what you did at two o'clock"). The combination is deliberate — even if Holic does not consciously intend it that way. He is telling her: *I saw you. All of it. The skills, the obedience, the midnight crawl to the kennel. I saw you, and I am impressed, and I know.*

**Simonka's response to the second statement.** This is harder to receive than the praise. The crawl to the kennel was perhaps the most private moment of the weekend — the thing she did alone, in the dark, without anyone watching (or so she thought). Now Holic tells her he was watching. The prose should render her reaction without interpretation: a pause, a stillness, the kind of silence that is not agreement or disagreement but the processing of a boundary shift. *The kennel was hers, and it was his to watch.*

### Beat 10 — Closing Image

Simonka rises. She stands. The collar is still on. The blindfold is in her hand, or on the floor beside the bowls. She looks at Holic.

The prose closes on one of two options:

**Option A:** She says nothing. She walks to the hook by the door, takes her robe (or the household garment she wore before becoming GG), and goes to her room. The collar stays on. The morning is over; the day begins. The scene closes on Simonka walking down the corridor, upright, collared, silent.

**Option B:** She says something — small, quiet, not about the weekend, not about the test, not about anything that can be put into words yet. *"Thank you."* Or *"I'm going to shower."* Or nothing at all, just a look. Holic nods. The scene closes on the nod.

**Recommendation: Option A.** The silence is more truthful to where Simonka is in her arc. She has just been released from a blindfold test and a weekend of GG-life. She is not ready to talk. The prose should let her be quiet. *The words will come later — in the thesis, in the doghouse, in the moments where she finally has the language for what she has chosen.* Not here. Not yet.

## SAGA-LEVEL NOTES

- **This is the first scene in which GG comes out of the blindfold by Holic's permission rather than by her own initiative.** The doghouse scene had Simonka *asking* to go to the doghouse; this scene has Holic *telling* her to come out. The two directions of agency — GG-initiated and owner-initiated — now both exist in the household's grammar. **The saga should track the ratio of who initiates what.**
- **The pill is the last act of GG-care before the release.** This is structurally significant: the household's care for GG's body continues to the very moment she stops being GG. The pill crosses the boundary. **It belongs to Simonka's biology but is administered to GG's body.** Future scenes should remember this as the moment where the two lives are most entangled.
- **The collar stays on.** This is the scene's most important unresolved tension. Simonka is released from the blindfold, from the crawl, from the bowls, from the posture — but not from the collar. *She can come out of Good Girl, but she cannot come out of the thing that makes her GG.* This is the household's architecture, and the scene should let the reader feel its weight without naming it.
- **The silence of the closing image.** Simonka does not speak, or speaks very little. This is consistent with the arc's discipline: the prose renders what is done, not what is felt. *The feeling is in the doing.* But this is also consistent with Simonka's psychology: she is a person who processes in silence, who writes the slip of paper later, who thinks the thing before she says it. The scene closes on the silence; the words come later.
