# The Shattering: Klárka Witnesses Good Girl

The morning air at the Holic mansion was deceptively sweet, smelling of damp earth and manicured boxwood. Klárka had come with a light heart, a simple desire to see Simonka, perhaps to share a coffee and a quiet conversation. The gates had been open, a silent invitation that she had followed without hesitation.

But as she rounded the corner of the sweeping driveway, the world stopped.

The scene in the front yard was not a conversation. It was a display.

Simonka—her Simonka, the brilliant scholar, the poised dancer, the woman of steel and grace—was stripped of everything. She was tied to the heavy wooden pole in the center of the lawn, her body arched, her limbs bound in a way that left her utterly exposed. She was not alone. She was in the midst of a sexual act, a raw, public performance of submission that looked less like love and more like ownership.

Klárka froze. The air seemed to vanish from her lungs. She didn't scream; she couldn't. She simply watched, her eyes wide and trembling, as the image burned itself into her retinas.

Then came the voice. Holic.

He stood nearby, his posture casual, his expression one of bored authority. He didn't look at Klárka at first; he was looking at the woman on the pole.

"You see, Klárka," Holic said, his voice cutting through the silence with a terrifying lack of empathy, "Good Girl likes sleeping with different people. It’s what she was made for."

The words were the catalyst. The psychic shock hit Klárka like a physical blow to the stomach. The breakfast she had eaten an hour ago—the toast, the fruit, the tea—surged back up her throat. She doubled over, the sound of her own retching loud and violent in the quiet morning air. She vomited onto the pristine grass, her body shaking with a sudden, uncontrollable nausea.

As the wave of sickness passed, the horror rushed in to fill the void. Klárka collapsed to her knees, the grass staining her clothes, and began to cry. It wasn't a soft weeping; it was a panicked, breathless sobbing, the kind of sound a wounded animal makes when it realizes there is no escape.

From the pole, Simonka’s head snapped toward her cousin. 

For a moment, the "Good Girl" mask slipped. Simonka saw the wreckage of Klárka—the vomit on the lawn, the heaving shoulders, the utter devastation in her eyes. A flash of visceral agony crossed Simonka's face. She knew. She knew that in this moment, she was the monster in Klárka's eyes. She knew that her own submission had just become Klárka's trauma.

Simonka couldn't move. She couldn't reach out and hold her. She was bound, exposed, and owned.

"Holic," Simonka gasped, her voice strained and raw. "Let me call him. Please. Let me call Raphael."

Holic glanced at Klárka, who was now curled into a ball on the grass, sobbing so hard she was choking. He sighed, a sound of mild annoyance, as if Klárka were a spilled glass of wine that needed to be cleaned up.

"Fine," Holic murmured. "Get the cleaner to come and take her away. She's ruining the view."

He stepped forward and unlocked a small, secure box attached to the post, handing Simonka her phone. 

With trembling fingers, Simonka dialed Raphael's number. She didn't wait for him to speak.

"Raphael," she whispered, her voice breaking, "please. Come to the mansion. Now. Klárka is here... and she's broken. Please, just come and take her away from here."

As she hung up, Simonka looked back at Klárka. She wanted to tell her she loved her, to tell her it wasn't what it looked like, to apologize for the image she had just seared into her cousin's mind. But as Holic stepped back toward her, the "Good Girl" persona settled back over her like a shroud, leaving her silent and bound while Klárka continued to sob in the dirt.
