# GG WALKS OUT TO THE DOGHOUSE

## Scene Outline — Beats, Dialogue Cues, and Thematic Notes

> **Companion to** `holic-invites-prostitute-gg-watches-scene-outline.md` **and** `holic-asks-gg-the-morning-after-scene-outline.md`. **Read both first.** This scene begins moments after the morning-after scene's Beat 6 (the scene closes by name and posture; the **dog collar stays on permanently**; Holic invites Simonka to breakfast in her real name). **This outline supersedes Beats 7–8 of the morning-after outline** — there is no breakfast room. Simonka asks for something else.

> **Content & craft warning.** This scene depicts a consensual adult human-pet routine in its fullest established form: Simonka, by her own initiative, asks to return to GG-form, walks outside to her doghouse in the mansion's fenced yard, urinates by a fence pole, and eats from her bowls. **The scene is canonical pet-life — household-routine activity — but it is happening for the first time at the pet's request rather than at the owner's instruction.** That distinction is the scene's whole content. Render with the same disciplines as the prostitute and morning-after outlines: no titillation, no glamour, no shame, no psychologising. The body does what the body does. The pet does what the pet does. The household has trained for this. *What is new this morning is not the doing; what is new is the asking.*

---

**POV:** **Simonka Káta / GG.** Continuity from the morning-after outline. *(The POV-name slides across the scene: Simonka asks; the collar is already on and stays on; Holic shifts her back into GG-register by name, permission, posture, and route. The prose can let the slide happen without announcing it.)*
**Tone:** Morning air. Quiet outdoor light. The register shifts from the bedroom's interior-domestic to the kennel yard's exterior-routine. Less held than the prostitute scene; less interior than the morning-after; closer to *clean physical fact* than either. **The scene's beauty — and it has a quiet beauty that the prose must permit — is the ordinariness of pet-life performed in cool spring sun by a person who has, an hour ago, chosen it.** The prose holds the cleanness; it does not romanticise it.
**Location:** Holic's mansion — the back corridors, the back door, and **the engineered kennel yard** (per the Collegium's canonical infrastructure; see `holic-club-rules-and-bylaws.md`). The yard is on the mansion grounds, smaller than the show-venue yard at the Collegium farm, intended for daily household pet-life. **The doghouse** — GG's, named hers since the beginning of the arrangement — is at the back of the yard. **The fence** is iron uprights set on stone footings, painted dark. **The fence poles** are vertical bars at intervals; one of them, near the doghouse, will matter in Beat 5.
**Time in saga:** Hours after the prostitute scene; minutes after the morning-after scene's Beat 6. **No other scene has occurred between the two outlines.** The morning is the same morning. The pill, taken in Beat 5 of the morning-after, is still doing its small daily work.

## CANON ESTABLISHED BY THIS SCENE

*(So later writing can rely on it.)*

- **Simonka asks for the doghouse. (CANON.)** Between the morning-after scene's Beat 6 (scene closed by name, collar still on, breakfast invited) and what would have been Beat 7, she stops in the bedroom and asks Holic if she may go to her doghouse instead. **This is the first time in the history of the GG arrangement that Simonka has, of her own initiative, asked to be in GG-form when Holic has not directed her there.** The asking is the household's first instance of *pet-initiated GG*.
- **The household had not developed a phrase for this request. (CANON.)** Until this morning there has been no occasion. Simonka has to make a phrasing on the spot. What she says (Beat 1) is, by default and going forward, the household's working phrase for the request.
- **Holic accepts. (CANON.)** He has, in the morning-after conversation, named the doghouse as a thing. He cannot, having said so, refuse her access to it now. **He accepts without performing surprise.** Whether the acceptance is also relief — that she is, by asking, doing what he had hoped she would have done last night — the prose refuses to decide.
- **The collar stays on permanently. (CANON.)** Holic does not refasten anything; there is nothing to refasten. **The scene is, in effect, reopened — not by putting the collar on, but by Holic accepting Simonka's request and addressing her as Good Girl again.** The permanent collar is the continuous claim; the shift into GG-register happens through name, posture, route, and permission.
- **They walk together to the back of the mansion. (CANON.)** Through the corridors, past the kitchen wing, to the back door. **Simonka is in Holic's robe; the collar is on her; her feet are bare.** The household staff are at work in the kitchen and elsewhere; they do not appear to her; the route is uneventful. *(The household has been built so that GG can move from the bedroom to the yard without crossing public spaces of the mansion. This is canonical infrastructure.)*
- **The robe comes off at the back door. (CANON.)** GG steps out of it. She is naked except for the collar. **The robe is hung on a hook by the back door — a hook that exists for this purpose, with two robes already on it (one of GG's, one of Holic's).** She steps into the yard. *Inside the fence, she is GG; outside the fence, she is whoever the day calls her.* The fence is the household's smallest legible threshold.
- **Holic goes back inside to fetch the bowls. (CANON.)** Two bowls — water and food. The food is GG's dog food (per the profile canon, established from the beginning of GG). The bowls live in a designated low cupboard in the back kitchen. Holic carries them himself; he does not delegate. *(Owner-fetches-bowls is its own piece of household-canon: the owner does the pet's mealwork. This is, in the Collegium's vocabulary, part of the *cura* of *Per vincula, cura* — the care side of the motto.)*
- **GG urinates at a fence pole while Holic is inside. (CANON.)** She has not, since the previous afternoon, urinated. The need is real and not stage-managed. The pole — one of the vertical iron bars near the doghouse — is what she squats beside, for balance and orientation. *(The household's toilet-training canon, established in the profile, includes outdoor toileting without privacy. This morning's toileting is consistent with that canon — but is the first time the saga has staged it in scene.)*
- **Holic sees, in passing, on his return with the bowls. (CANON.)** He does not stop. He does not look longer than necessary. He continues to where the bowls go and sets them down. **The not-stopping is the household's correct response to a pet's toileting** — and is, this morning, also Holic's correct response to a moment that, in any other register, would be one of the most exposed things he has seen her do. *He does not, ever, refer to it.*
- **GG eats and drinks from the bowls. (CANON.)** She does so on her hands and knees, in the pet-form she has been trained in. The bowls are at ground level. She eats the dog food (Holic's chosen brand). She drinks the water. **The eating-and-drinking are her morning meal, and they replace the breakfast she has declined.** *The household has not, in any prior scene, depicted GG taking a meal from the bowls in the daytime; this is the first.*
- **Holic stays in the yard with her for some minutes after the bowls are down. (CANON.)** He sits on a low stone bench near the doghouse. He does not interact. He is present in the way an owner is present to a pet at meal — *available, watchful, not intervening.* After some time, he goes back inside. **GG stays.**
- **GG remains in the yard for the rest of the morning. (CANON.)** What she does — sit, lie in the doghouse, walk the perimeter, doze — is open. The prose may render some or none. **The point of the scene is not what she does during the morning; the point is that she has, by asking, made the morning hers in the GG-register, in a way she had never previously known the architecture permitted.**

## BEFORE THE SCENE — WHAT SIMONKA CARRIES INTO IT

*Brief — she has been in continuous on-page time since the morning-after Beat 6.*

- **The collar is, at the start of this scene, on Simonka's neck.** Holic has not removed it and will not remove it. He has called her *Simonka*. He has said *Come and have breakfast.* She has said *Yes.*
- **Between his sentence and the next thing she says, a small interior thing happens that the morning-after outline did not name and this outline does:** Simonka realises, in the half-second between his invitation and her acceptance becoming the morning's plan, **that she does not want breakfast in person-form.** She wants something else. The wanting arrives before the language for the wanting. She sits with the wanting for one beat. The wanting clarifies into the doghouse.
- **The wanting is not a refusal of him.** It is not a withdrawal. It is, she understands as it clarifies, *a use of what he told her last night was a thing.* She is, in the half-second of clarification, doing the small private accounting she did not do on the platform: **the doghouse is a thing. She is allowed to choose it. She is, this morning, choosing it.**
- **She has not, until this morning, ever wanted the doghouse outside of a doghouse-weekend.** She does not, herself, fully understand why she wants it this morning. *She does not require herself to fully understand. She has only to ask.*
- **There is also, very faintly, a piece of the wanting that is for Holic.** She knows, on some level she does not narrate even to herself, that *walking to the doghouse this morning will register as an answer* to the question he asked her in bed about why she did not, last night, ask. **The walking is, in pet-form, the thanks she withheld in person-form.** She did not, in the morning-after scene's Beat 6, say *thank you for asking.* She did not say it because the thanks would have rewarded a question he should not have needed to be moved to ask. **The doghouse-walking is the same thanks, given by a different route.** She does not quite admit this to herself. The prose registers it; she does not.

## WHAT HOLIC CARRIES (briefly — seen from outside)

- He has just closed a scene. He has invited her to breakfast. He has not, in the dark hour before she woke, anticipated that she would ask him for the doghouse this morning.
- **When she asks, what shows in his face is the small adjustment of a man who has been ready to do one thing and now will do another.** Not surprise — *adjustment*. He is the man who has trained himself, over years of owning pets, to absorb a pet's request without reacting to it. He absorbs hers.
- *(Internal, not on page: he is, somewhere under the adjustment, **gratified**. She has, by asking, demonstrated that the morning's conversation took. The gratification is real and the saga must hold it — and it is one of the morally complicated facts of this scene. **His pleasure in her well-formed asking is not the same as her well-formed asking being a good thing.** The prose holds both. The gratification is not visible on his face; it is the saga's underground knowledge of him.)*

## BEAT 1 — THE ASKING

*Short. The smallest hinge in the entire GG arc so far.*

- They are standing in the bedroom. The collar is already on her neck. He has said *Simonka.* She has said *Yes.* He has said *Come and have breakfast.* She has said *Yes.*
- She does not move toward the door.
- She says, quietly — and the prose must let the reader hear that she is composing the phrasing as she speaks, because the household has never developed it:

> "May I — may I go to the doghouse instead."

- The repetition of *may I* is the household's first instance of *the new word being made.* She does not have a smooth version yet. She has the version that the moment produces. *(The household, from this morning forward, has a phrase. It is this one. Future scenes in which GG asks for the doghouse may use the same construction or refine it; the saga should keep the construction recognisable.)*
- **She does not say *please*.** She does not say *if that is allowed*. She does not say *just for the morning*. **She makes the request bare.** The bareness is correct — the household's grammar does not include performance.
- Holic does not answer for one beat. The beat is the *adjustment* described above.
- He says: *"Yes. Of course."*
- *(The *of course* is the small generosity of his answer. He could have said *yes* alone. He chose the additional two words. The two words are the household's smallest acknowledgement that the request was legitimate and that he is glad she made it. **They are also the only two words of celebration he will allow himself in the scene.** From here on, he treats the morning as routine — which is itself the household's correct response to a pet-request honoured.)*
- He looks at her — neither at her face only, nor at her body only, but at *her*, the way one looks at a person whose request one has just received and whom one is about to return to pet-register at their request. *The look is brief and is, this morning, the kindest thing he does.*
- He does not touch the collar. It is already there. Instead, he gives the shift its words.
- He says: *"Come, Good Girl."*
- The pet name has returned. The scene has reopened. Simonka has, in the time it takes him to say her pet name, become GG again. **This time at her own request.**

---

## BEAT 2 — THE WALK THROUGH THE MANSION

*Brief. Render the route as routine.*

- They leave the bedroom. He walks first; she follows, half a step behind, the way the household has trained. **The robe is on her over the collar — the household's transitional dress for moving GG between interior locations.** Her feet are bare. The floor is cool.
- The corridors. The household's morning sounds — distant kitchen, a phone somewhere ringing once and being answered, a door closing in another wing. They pass no one. The route has been built so that they pass no one; the household's infrastructure has, from the beginning, included this discretion. *(GG has walked this route many times. This morning it is the same route.)*
- They reach the back wing. The back door is wood, glassed in the upper half, framed in iron. Beyond it, in the yard, the morning light is full now.
- He opens the door. He steps through. **He does not hold the door for her.** *(He does not hold doors for pets. The household's grammar. Holding the door would be the wrong gesture inside the role she has just asked to resume.)* He stands by the door and waits.
- GG steps through.

---

## BEAT 3 — THE ROBE OFF

*The threshold beat. Short.*

- The robe hook is to the right of the back door, outside, on the stone wall, sheltered by the eave. Two robes already hang on it: his daytime one, and one of hers (size, fabric, the household's standard for this hook). The hook is a household provision. *Of course it is.*
- GG, on the stone step just outside the door, lets the robe fall from her shoulders. She catches it; she does not drop it. She hangs it on the hook with the small precision the household has trained.
- **She is naked except for the collar.** The morning air is cool on her skin. The spring sun is on her shoulders. She is, for the first time this morning, fully GG in the household's visible register.
- *(Internal, half a sentence: she is aware that this is the first time she has been naked outdoors in this household since her last doghouse-weekend, and that the last doghouse-weekend was over a month ago, and that the body remembers the cool air on the skin the way the body remembers the collar. **The remembering is its own quiet small thing. She does not narrate it. She steps into the yard.**)*

---

## BEAT 4 — THE YARD, THE DOGHOUSE

*Short. Establish the geography.*

- The yard is paved with stone slabs for some distance from the door, then becomes packed earth with patches of grass. The fence — iron uprights, dark — encloses an area roughly twenty metres by fifteen. The doghouse is at the back, against the far wall of the mansion, set on a small wooden platform to keep it dry. It is sized for an adult human pet on hands and knees; it has straw bedding inside, replaced weekly by the staff. **It is hers.**
- GG walks across the yard on her feet — *the household permits walking upright across the yard between the door and the doghouse; the four-point posture is for stationary positions, meals, and specific trained sequences*. She is not in a hurry. The cool stone gives way to the earth. She passes a low stone bench set against the inside of the back fence — *the bench is for Holic when he sits with her in the yard. It has been in the yard since the yard was built.*
- She reaches the doghouse. She does not yet enter it. She turns and looks back at the door.
- **Holic is no longer at the door.** He has gone inside — to fetch the bowls. *She has not, in her own mind, instructed him to. She did not need to. The household's choreography has, from the moment the collar went back on, taken over.*
- She stands by the doghouse for a moment in the morning sun. **She is alone in the yard for the first time this morning.** She is alone with the collar on, in the open air, in the household's smallest possible legal version of the GG-life. *The aloneness is not loneliness. It is the morning's quietness. She lets it be what it is.*

---

## BEAT 5 — THE FENCE POLE

*The scene's hinge. Render with the discipline named in the warning. No titillation. No shame. The body does what the body does.*

- She becomes aware, while standing by the doghouse, that she has not urinated since yesterday afternoon — sometime before she came to the mansion. The need has been present, low, for some time. It clarifies now, in the morning sun, with the air on her skin and the body returning to its own count.
- **The household's toilet-training canon** (per profile) has included outdoor toileting without a privacy focus from the beginning of GG. She has, on doghouse-weekends, done this — usually under instruction, usually with other pets nearby at the Collegium farm. **She has never, in this household's home yard, done it alone, in the daytime, at the morning of her own decision.**
- She walks the short distance to the nearest fence pole — one of the vertical iron uprights, near the corner where the doghouse-side of the fence meets the back wall. The pole is roughly an inch and a half thick, set into a stone footing. *The pole exists, in the yard's architecture, for the architectural purpose of holding the fence up. The household has not designated it as a toileting pole. **It is simply the nearest one.***
- She squats. **The pole, behind her, gives her balance.** *(The practical mechanics: in the trained pet-squat, one or two hands may rest on a vertical surface for stability. The fence pole is suitable. This is body-knowledge, not erotic content. The prose renders it as body-knowledge.)*
- She urinates.
- The stream is what it is. The earth at the base of the pole receives it; the earth has received this before; the staff hose the yard clean each evening. **The whole act takes some seconds.** The prose does not measure them.
- *(Internal, half a sentence — and this is the only interior beat the prose permits during the act, because the act itself is the scene's centre and any further interiority would betray it: **she is, in the seconds at the pole, neither ashamed nor proud nor performing nor enduring. She is simply, in the most literal sense, in her body in her yard at the morning of her choosing.** The household's training has, by removing privacy from this act, removed shame from it. **What is left after the removals is the act itself — and this morning, for the first time, the act itself is hers in a way nothing in her GG-life has previously been hers.** She does not articulate this. The body knows.)*
- She finishes. She rises. *(The rising is uncomplicated; the legs do their work; the body returns to its standing posture.)* She walks the short distance back to the doghouse.

---

## BEAT 6 — HOLIC RETURNS WITH THE BOWLS

*The scene's most disciplined beat: how the household acknowledges what just happened without referring to it.*

- The back door opens. Holic, in his daytime robe, comes through it carrying the two bowls — one in each hand, water in the right, food in the left. He has, from the door, the line of sight to the corner where GG just was. **He saw the latter portion of the act in passing. He has not slowed his step.**
- He walks across the yard to the bowl-place — a flat slab near the doghouse, designated for this purpose. He kneels — *the household requires the owner to kneel when placing the pet's bowls; this too is part of *cura*, the care side of the motto* — and sets the bowls on the slab. Water bowl on the right, food bowl on the left.
- **He does not look at the fence pole.** He does not look at the wet earth. He does not look at GG with anything other than the routine attention of an owner placing a meal. *His not-looking is the household's correct response. It is also, the prose can let the reader feel, the upper limit of what his frame permits him to do.* He could not, even if he wished to, acknowledge what he saw — because acknowledgement would itself violate the household's grammar of pet-toileting. **The not-acknowledging is its own kind of presence.** He is here. He saw. He is treating what he saw as routine. Routine is, in this household, a form of respect.
- He stands. He says, in the household's standard sequence-marker for a meal: *"Good Girl. Eat."*
- GG, who has reached the bowls, lowers to her hands and knees. She drinks from the water bowl first, then eats from the food bowl. The eating is the eating. The drinking is the drinking. *The prose renders this at the same plain register as the toileting beat — as body-fact, not as event.*
- **Holic, while she eats, does the small staff-job of fetching a small hose-and-bucket from the back wall and rinsing the wet earth at the corner.** *(This is, the saga's underground knowledge: the household has a small rinse-bucket at the back wall for exactly this purpose; the staff use it daily on doghouse-weekends; Holic, this morning, uses it himself. **He does the rinse himself rather than calling the staff** — and the doing-it-himself is the morning's second of-course-ness, the smaller cousin of his *yes, of course* in Beat 1. He is not making the staff handle this morning's particular rinse. He is doing it. The doing is his own private acknowledgement of the morning's significance, performed in the form of an ordinary maintenance task.)*
- The rinse takes thirty seconds. He returns the bucket to its hook. He walks to the stone bench. He sits.

---

## BEAT 7 — THE BENCH

*Brief. Holic sits with her while she finishes the meal. He does not interact.*

- He sits on the stone bench. He does not bring a book. He does not bring his coffee. He sits with his hands on his knees, in his robe, in the morning sun, and watches the yard — *not GG specifically, but the yard, of which she is a part*.
- *(The not-watching-her-specifically is the household's correct sit-with-a-pet posture. The owner is present; the owner is not surveilling. **Holic's sit on the bench is the daytime equivalent of his arm across her shoulder in the bed an hour ago.** The two postures are the same posture in two registers.)*
- GG finishes the food. She drinks the rest of the water. She sits back on her heels by the bowls. *(The sitting-back-on-heels is the household's post-meal posture for an unscheduled meal — it indicates the pet is finished and awaits the next instruction or the absence of one.)*
- Holic does not give an instruction. He sits.
- **The yard is quiet.** A bird, somewhere over the back wall. A tram, two streets away. The morning continuing.

---

## BEAT 8 — HOLIC GOES INSIDE; GG STAYS

*Short. The scene's parting.*

- After some minutes — five, ten; the prose does not measure — Holic stands. He crosses the yard to where GG sits by the bowls. He puts his hand once, briefly, on the top of her head. *(The hand on the head is the household's standard pet-acknowledgement. It is the gesture he made in the bedroom last night when he came to her on the platform. It is one of the household's most stable gestures and its meaning is consistent: *I am here, you are here, this is correct.*)*
- He says: *"I will be inside. Come back when you wish."*
- *(The sentence is, structurally, what he said in the bedroom this morning when he closed the prostitute-scene with **Come and have breakfast**. The structural rhyme is the household's smallest piece of grace. **The morning has, by this sentence, given GG the same standing it has given Simonka: an invitation to rejoin him on her own clock.** That GG has the same standing as Simonka in this regard — for the first time in the saga — is canonical from this scene forward.)*
- He walks across the yard. The back door opens and closes. The yard is hers.

---

## BEAT 9 — THE YARD ALONE

*Short. Coda. Render briefly; the morning belongs to her now and the prose does not need to follow her through it.*

- GG remains by the bowls for some time. Then she rises, walks to the doghouse, looks into it. She does not enter immediately. She walks the perimeter of the yard once — slowly, on her feet, the household's permitted upright walking. She passes the fence pole. *The earth at the base of the pole is, by now, rinsed; only her body remembers what passed there.*
- She reaches the doghouse again. She crouches at the entrance. She enters — knees first, then the rest of her — and curls into the straw. The straw is fresh; the staff replaced it earlier in the week; it smells of nothing in particular except straw.
- She lies on her side. She closes her eyes.
- **The doghouse contains her.**
- *(Internal, the morning's last and quietest beat: she is, in the doghouse, more at rest than she has been at any point since the prostitute walked into the mansion last night. **The rest is not contentment. The rest is the body's recognition that the morning has been, on her own clock, completed.** She had a question to answer. She has answered it. Not in words — Holic's two questions in bed were answered in words, partially. The third answer — *do you know that you may choose this* — has been answered this morning by the morning itself. She knows. She has chosen. She has eaten. She has been left in the yard with the option to come back. **The whole of GG is, this morning, finally a thing she is doing rather than a thing being done to her.** Whether this is liberation or further capture, the saga refuses to decide. **It is, in this moment in the doghouse, simply hers.**)*

---

## BEAT 10 — CLOSING IMAGE

*One short coda. Match the saga's other closings in length and register.*

- *Late morning. In the yard, the doghouse contains GG. The straw rises and falls with her breath. The bowls are at the slab, empty, the food bowl licked clean to the household's standard. The fence pole stands where it has always stood; its base is rinsed; its iron is warming in the sun.*
- *Inside the mansion, Holic, in his daytime robe, sits at his own breakfast table, alone. He has eaten. He is reading the paper. He has not, this morning, eaten the breakfast he prepared to eat with Simonka, because the morning produced a different breakfast for both of them — Simonka has eaten from her bowls; Holic has eaten from his plate; the two meals were taken in the same household at the same hour in different forms.*
- *The kitchen staff have come and gone. They saw, on their morning round, that the back-door hook held GG's robe and not Holic's, and they understood from the configuration that GG was in the yard, and they have, accordingly, left the back wing alone for the morning.*
- *Somewhere in Prague, in a different apartment, **the woman in the dark dress is waking up.** She does not know that the household she visited last night has, this morning, produced a quiet thing that her doorway-look made possible. She does not know that the look has been put on a shelf in another woman's interior and that the shelf is the foundation of a thesis that does not yet exist. She knows only that her morning's small administrative tasks await her. She drinks water. She begins the day.*
- *GG, in the doghouse, breathes the slow breath of a pet at rest. The morning continues. The mansion holds. Simonka — under the collar, under the straw, in the yard, in the doghouse — sleeps for an hour without knowing she is doing so.*

## WHAT SIMONKA / GG DOES *NOT* DO

- She does not, when Holic invites her to breakfast as Simonka, accept it as the morning's only option.
- She does not, when she asks for the doghouse, perform the asking — no plea, no ornament, no apology.
- She does not, walking through the mansion, look at any door she passes.
- She does not, at the back door, hesitate.
- She does not, in the yard, ask Holic anything.
- **She does not, at the fence pole, look up to see whether he is watching.**
- She does not, when he returns with the bowls, react to the not-acknowledgement.
- She does not, while eating, leave any of the food.
- She does not, when he leaves, ask him to stay.
- She does not, in the doghouse, regret the choice.
- **She does not, ever, tell anyone outside this household that she has, this morning, walked of her own initiative to the doghouse.** *(Not Klárka. Not her mother in Prague's English-speaking expatriate circle. Not anyone in her university life. The morning, even more than the prostitute scene, becomes part of her shelf.)*

## WHAT HOLIC DOES *NOT* DO

- He does not, when she asks for the doghouse, ask why.
- He does not, when she asks, say *are you sure*.
- He does not, when she asks, suggest a compromise (*breakfast first, then the doghouse*).
- He does not, when refastening the collar, speak more than the household's standard words.
- He does not, in the corridor, look back at her over his shoulder.
- He does not, in the yard, hold the door.
- **He does not, when he sees her at the fence pole in passing, slow his step or alter his face.**
- He does not, returning to the bowls, give her any instruction beyond the standard *Eat*.
- He does not, while she eats, watch her.
- He does not, when he leaves the yard, look back from the door.
- **He does not, later in the day or in the week, tell anyone — including the other Collegium members at the next monthly meeting — that GG asked for the doghouse this morning.** *(The asking is between him and her. The household's discretion goes both ways. The Collegium would, the prose can let the reader sense without entering his head, be very interested in this development; he chooses not to share it. The not-sharing is one of the saga's small better things about him.)*

## TONE GUARDRAILS

- **No exoticism.** A yard, a fence, a doghouse, two bowls, a stone bench. The mansion is a mansion in a Czech city. Cool spring air. Birds, a tram. Render at the plain register of a yard one knows well.
- **No titillation.** This is the load-bearing tone discipline of the scene. The nakedness in the yard is the household's established practice; the prose renders it as *practice*, not as spectacle. **The toileting at the fence pole is rendered with the same plainness one renders any animal's toileting in literary prose: the body does what the body does; the prose is present; the prose does not lean in.**
- **No shame.** The household has, by long training and by full consent, removed shame from the toileting. The prose must not reinstall the shame the household has trained out. *Any prose that lingers on embarrassment would be the prose forcing onto Simonka a register she has, with full agency, declined.*
- **No glamour around GG's asking.** Simonka asking for the doghouse is not *brave*. It is also not *broken*. It is the household's first instance of pet-initiated GG, and the saga must hold it as exactly that — *neither a triumph nor a defeat, but a development*. Whether the development is, in the long run, good for her is one of the saga's running questions, and the scene must not, in either direction, decide it.
