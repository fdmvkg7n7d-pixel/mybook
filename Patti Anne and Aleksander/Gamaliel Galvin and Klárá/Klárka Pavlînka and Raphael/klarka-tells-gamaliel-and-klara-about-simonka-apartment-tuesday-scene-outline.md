# Klárka Tells Gamaliel Galvin and Klára What She and Raphael Found

## Tuesday Scene Outline

## Placement

**When:** Tuesday, the day after Klárka Pavlînka and Raphael went to Simonka Káta's apartment on Monday night.

**Where:** **Gamaliel Galvin's office** at the Prague academy / family property. The room belongs to the academy's adult responsibilities — schedules, student files, finances, letters, old photographs, and the practical machinery of the school Gamaliel and Klára built. This makes the family crisis feel trapped inside a professional room: Simonka's private life is being spoken aloud in the place where her father usually solves academy problems.

**Continuity Lead-in:**

- Monday afternoon: Klárka told Klára she was going to Simonka's apartment.
- Klára tried to stop her, then told her not to go alone.
- Klárka brought Raphael.
- Monday evening: Klárka and Raphael found Simonka's apartment empty.
- On the kitchen table, they found Simonka's diary with a sticky note: **Přečti mě** / **Read me**.
- Monday night: Klárka read enough to understand that Simonka chose to become Holic Bartunek's pet.
- Klárka broke down in Raphael's arms.
- Tuesday: Klárka now has to tell both parents what she and Raphael found.

---

## Scene Purpose

This scene moves the Simonka/Holic secret from **Klárka's private burden** into the **family circle**, inside **Gamaliel Galvin's office** rather than the kitchen. The setting matters: the office is where Gamaliel is used to having answers, making plans, and solving institutional problems. But this is not an academy problem. It is his daughter.

Until now, Gamaliel Galvin has been outside the knowledge. Klára knew something was wrong and knew Klárka went to the apartment, but she did not know the full shape of what Klárka found. Raphael witnessed the discovery but did not read the diary.

By the end of this scene:

1. Gamaliel and Klára know Simonka left a diary for Klárka to read.
2. They know Simonka is involved with Holic Bartunek.
3. They know Simonka chose to become Holic's pet.
4. They understand that Simonka's choice appears to be voluntary, which makes intervention complicated.
5. They must decide what to do next without turning love into panic or control.

---

## Core Emotional Conflict

The family is not only reacting to what Simonka chose.

They are reacting to the fact that they did not know.

Klára thought she had recognized the signs of secrecy because she once carried secrets herself. Gamaliel thought his daughters were safe inside the life he and Klára built. Klárka thought being fifteen months older meant she would always be close enough to see when Simonka was disappearing.

All three are wrong.

Simonka had an entire hidden world. The diary proves it.

---

## Characters in the Scene

### Klárka Pavlînka

- Tired from Monday night.
- Has cried already, but may still look controlled from the outside.
- Carries the diary physically into the conversation.
- Feels guilty for entering Simonka's apartment, guilty for reading the diary, and guilty for not knowing sooner.
- Still believes Simonka's choice is stupid, dangerous, and devastating.
- Has to describe the discovery without fully collapsing again.

### Gamaliel Galvin

- Simonka's father.
- Until this scene, he may know only that Klára was worried and that Klárka went to check on Simonka.
- His first instinct may be action: find Simonka, call her, go to Holic, bring her home.
- He must struggle against the urge to become forceful, because Simonka is an adult and because the diary indicates choice rather than abduction.
- His pain may be quieter than Klára's at first, then suddenly sharper.

### Klára

- Simonka's mother.
- Already knew something was wrong.
- Already tried to stop Klárka from going to the apartment.
- **Cries softly while Klárka speaks.** This is not a dramatic breakdown or an interruption. The tears come quietly as she listens, because every fact Klárka gives confirms that Simonka was farther away than Klára understood.
- May feel the double wound: she wanted to respect Simonka's boundary, but the boundary concealed something enormous.
- Her own history with secrecy, rescue, and being protected by Mike Key shapes her reaction.
- She may be the one who understands fastest that forcing Simonka home could damage trust permanently.
- Her crying should coexist with restraint: she is devastated, but she is still listening carefully enough to stop Gamaliel from acting too fast.

### Raphael

- Optional physical presence.
- He was there when the diary was found, so Klárka may mention him as witness.
- He does not need to attend the full conversation unless Klárka asks him to.
- If present, his role remains limited: he confirms what he saw, then steps back.
- He should not become the speaker for Klárka or the interpreter of Simonka's diary.

### Simonka Káta — Absent Presence

- Not present.
- Present through the diary, the sticky note, and the facts Klárka reports.
- The family's central challenge is deciding how to love someone who is absent and has chosen something they do not understand.

## Scene Structure

## Beat 1 — Tuesday Morning: Gamaliel Galvin's Office Is Too Normal

The scene opens in **Gamaliel Galvin's office**.

Possible details:

- A desk with academy paperwork, student files, tuition records, audition notes, or schedules.
- Framed photographs from the academy's early years.
- A photograph of Mike Key, or another quiet reminder of the family legacy that made the academy possible.
- Ballet music faintly audible through the wall or floor.
- Young dancers, teachers, and ordinary academy life continuing outside the office door.
- Coffee or tea on the desk, untouched.

The office is too normal. It is built for decisions, not for this.

Klárka enters carrying the diary.

The diary should be visible immediately. It is not just a prop; it is the reason the conversation exists.

**Key image:** Klárka standing inside her father's office with Simonka's diary in her hands, while academy life continues outside the closed door.

---

## Beat 2 — Klára Already Knows This Is Bad

Klára sees Klárka's face and the diary.

She is already fragile before Klárka starts speaking. Her fear has had all night to gather itself, and the office setting makes it worse: this is Gamaliel's room, the place where problems are usually named and managed, but Klára can already feel that this problem will not be managed cleanly.

She does not begin with accusation. She may say only:

> Klárko.

Or:

> Pojď sem. Sit down.

Klára has been waiting all night or since the early morning. If Klárka called her Monday night, Klára knows part of the situation already. If Klárka did not call, Klára has been waiting for this exact moment.

Either way, Klára knows from Klárka's posture that the answer is worse than ordinary worry.

---

## Beat 3 — Gamaliel Is Brought In

If Gamaliel does not already know, Klára calls him into the room.

This is important: Klára chooses not to protect Gamaliel from the knowledge. Simonka is his daughter too.

Possible line:

> Gamalieli, musíš sem přijít. Teď.
>
> Gamaliel, you need to come here. Now.

Gamaliel arrives expecting a serious family matter, but not this.

He may look first at Klára, then at Klárka, then at the diary.

He understands before he is told that the object matters.

---

## Beat 4 — Klárka Starts With the Facts

Klárka tries to be precise because precision keeps her from breaking down.

As she speaks, **Klára begins to cry softly**. The tears should be quiet enough that Klárka can continue. Klára may wipe them away with her fingers or let them fall without making a sound. She does not sob. She does not stop the report. She listens.

She reports what happened in order:

1. She and Raphael went to Simonka's apartment Monday evening.
2. Simonka was not there.
3. The apartment did not look ransacked or chaotic.
4. On the kitchen table, in the center, was Simonka's diary.
5. On the diary was a sticky note in Simonka's handwriting.
6. The note said: **Přečti mě.**

Klárka may place the diary on **Gamaliel's desk** at this point.

No one touches it immediately.

The diary sits between them like a fourth person. Klára looks at it through tears. Gamaliel looks at it as if it is evidence of a world that entered his office without permission.

---

## Beat 5 — Klára Asks the Boundary Question

Klára may ask, quietly:

> Did you read it?

Klárka answers:

> Yes.

This is not a simple answer. It contains guilt, justification, fear, and obedience to Simonka's note.

Klárka explains:

> She left it for someone to read. She wrote it on the note. It was in the middle of the table.

Klára may close her eyes, tears still moving quietly down her face, because she understands the difference between snooping and being handed a written disclosure.

Klára tried to protect Simonka's privacy on Monday. Now she has to accept that Simonka deliberately opened the door.

---

## Beat 6 — Raphael's Role Is Clarified

Gamaliel may ask:

> Raphael read this too?

Klárka answers firmly:

> No.

She explains that Raphael was there, but he did not read the diary. He saw the apartment, the table, the note, and Klárka's reaction. He respected the boundary.

This matters for Gamaliel and Klára's understanding of Raphael. He was not consuming Simonka's secret. He was protecting Klárka while she received it.

If Raphael is present, he can confirm only what he saw:

> I saw the diary on the table. I saw the note. I did not read it.

Then he falls quiet again.

---

## Beat 7 — Klárka Names Holic Bartunek

Klárka gives the first major fact from the diary:

> There is a man. Holic Bartunek.

The name changes the room.

Until this point, the diary is an object. Now there is a person outside the family attached to Simonka's absence.

Gamaliel may repeat the name:

> Holic Bartunek?

Klára may ask:

> Who is he?

Klárka answers only what she knows from the diary:

- Simonka met him in Prague.
- He took her to Marie B.
- He brought her to his mansion.
- He introduced her to a human-pet dynamic.
- He gave her the name GG / Good Girl.
- He gave her time to decide.

Klárka should avoid giving every detail at once. The information comes in controlled pieces because the parents have to absorb it.

---

## Beat 8 — The Word Klárka Does Not Want To Say

Klárka has to say the central fact:

> Simonka chose to become his pet.

This line should be difficult for her.

She may say it once and then correct herself into more precise language:

> She chose it. She wrote that she chose it.

This distinction matters. Klárka is not claiming to understand Simonka's desire. She is reporting Simonka's own words.

Possible line:

> She didn't write that he forced her. She wrote that she went back.

That makes the room worse, not better.

---

## Beat 9 — Gamaliel's First Reaction: Action

Gamaliel's first instinct is to act.

Possible reactions:

- He stands up.
- He asks for Holic's address.
- He wants to call Simonka immediately.
- He wants to go to the mansion.
- He wants to bring his daughter home.

Possible line:

> Where is he?

Or:

> Give me the address.

Or:

> I am going to get her.

This is not portrayed as villainous or controlling. It is a father's panic translating itself into motion.

But the scene must make clear that immediate force is dangerous because Simonka is an adult and because the diary frames the choice as voluntary.

---

## Beat 10 — Klára Stops Gamaliel From Moving Too Fast

Klára intervenes even though she is crying.

This is important: her softness does not mean helplessness. She can have tears on her face and still be the person who understands the danger of panic.

She may physically touch his arm or say his name sharply:

> Gamalieli.

This is the same woman who once needed adults to act for her when she was a child, so her restraint is not passivity. It costs her.

She tells him they cannot storm into Simonka's life without understanding what Simonka asked for.

Possible line:

> She left a diary. She did not leave a rescue note.

Or:

> Jestli tam půjdeš takhle, ztratíme ji.
>
> If you go like this, we lose her.

This line should hurt because it is true.

---

## Beat 11 — Klárka's Anger Surfaces Again

Klárka may react badly to Klára's restraint.

She may say:

> So we do nothing?

Or:

> She is living as his pet and we are supposed to respect it?

Or:

> Mami, it is stupid. It is so stupid.

This connects directly to Monday night's breakdown in Raphael's arms.

Klára does not shame her for saying it. Klára understands that Klárka's anger is fear.

But Klára may answer:

> We do not have to like it to be careful.

This becomes the family's immediate rule.

---

## Beat 12 — The Parents Ask What Else Is In The Diary

Gamaliel and Klára need to know whether Simonka is safe.

Questions they may ask:

- Did Simonka say she was afraid?
- Did she say Holic hurt her?
- Did she mention safewords or consent?
- Did she say where she is now?
- Did she ask Klárka to come get her?
- Did she ask the family not to interfere?
- Did she describe this as something she wanted?

Klárka may not have every answer because she has not read every page.

This is important. The family is working with partial knowledge.

Klárka can say:

> I read enough to know she chose. I did not read everything.

Or:

> I couldn't finish it last night.

This admission reveals that Klárka is still overwhelmed.

---

## Beat 13 — Decision: Read More Together, But Carefully

The family must decide what to do with the diary.

Possible outcome:

- Klárka reads selected passages aloud.
- Klára reads silently while Gamaliel waits.
- Gamaliel asks to read it himself, but Klára cautions against consuming Simonka's private words too quickly.
- They agree to read only enough to answer immediate safety questions.

Recommended emotional choice:

They agree not to pass the diary around like evidence. It is Simonka's voice, not an object for inspection.

Klára may say:

> We read what we need to keep her safe. We do not punish her with her own words.

This gives the family an ethical boundary.

---

## Beat 14 — Raphael Leaves Or Is Asked To Step Out

If Raphael is present, this is a natural point for him to leave the room.

Klárka may look at him, and he understands.

He has done his part:

- He went with Klárka.
- He witnessed the apartment.
- He comforted her Monday night.
- He confirmed he did not read the diary.

Now the parents and daughter need a private family conversation.

Raphael may say quietly:

> I'll be nearby.

This reinforces his role as supportive but not intrusive.

---

## Beat 15 — Gamaliel's Grief Lands

After the first wave of action passes, Gamaliel's grief arrives.

He may sit down heavily. He may put his hand over his mouth. He may stare at the diary without touching it.

His pain is not only that Simonka chose Holic. It is that he did not see his youngest daughter carrying such a secret.

Possible line:

> She ate dinner with us.

Or:

> She sat at our table and we did not know.

This echoes the Friday meal, where Simonka said **nic není** while carrying the secret.

Klára may answer:

> I knew something was wrong. I did not know this.

The parents share the wound of not knowing.

---

## Beat 16 — Klárka's Guilt

Klárka confesses her own guilt.

Possible lines:

> I should have known.

> She is my sister.

> Fifteen months. I was only fifteen months ahead of her. How did I not see this?

Klára may respond firmly:

> Because she hid it.

Or:

> Because she is allowed to have a life we do not see.

This is painful but necessary. It prevents Klárka from making herself responsible for Simonka's adult choices.

---

## Beat 17 — Immediate Plan

The family needs a practical next step.

Possible plan:

1. Do not storm Holic's mansion.
2. Do not call police unless evidence of coercion or danger appears.
3. Read enough of the diary to assess safety.
4. Try to contact Simonka directly with a non-threatening message.
5. Make it clear she can come home or call any time.
6. Do not shame her or call her choice stupid in the message.
7. Keep Wednesday's audition in mind but do not pretend Klárka is unaffected.

Possible text to Simonka:

> Simonko, našli jsme deník. Četli jsme jen tolik, abychom pochopili. Milujeme tě. Nejsi v problémech. Prosím, ozvi se nám, až budeš moct.
>
> Simonka, we found the diary. We read only enough to understand. We love you. You are not in trouble. Please contact us when you can.

This message preserves connection without immediate accusation.

---

## Beat 18 — The Wednesday Audition Is Remembered

At some point, someone remembers that Klárka has a solo audition to run on Wednesday.

This should feel almost absurd — the ordinary professional world continuing in the middle of family crisis.

Gamaliel or Klára may say:

> Klárko, the audition—

Klárka may cut them off:

> I know.

She does know. That is the problem.

The scene does not need to solve the audition issue, but it should sharpen its emotional stakes. Klárka now has to teach and evaluate while carrying confirmed knowledge of Simonka's choice.

---

## Beat 19 — Closing Image

The scene ends with the family gathered around the diary.

Possible final images:

- Klára sitting in one of the office chairs, crying softly, one hand near the diary but not on it.
- Gamaliel behind or beside his desk, both hands clasped, fighting the urge to act.
- Klárka standing or sitting across from him, no longer the only person carrying the secret.
- Raphael visible beyond the office door or waiting nearby, not listening but present if Klárka needs him.
- The muffled sound of the academy continuing outside Gamaliel's office.

The diary remains on the table.

The sticky note still says:

> **Přečti mě.**

The family has read enough to be changed.

They have not yet read enough to know what to do.

---

## Key Dialogue Possibilities

### Klárka

> We found her apartment empty.

> The diary was on the kitchen table. In the middle. She wanted it found.

> Raphael didn't read it. He only saw it.

> There's a man. Holic Bartunek.

> She chose him.

> She chose to be his pet.

> I know how it sounds. I know. But she wrote it that way.

> I couldn't finish it.

> I should have known.

### Klára

> Did you read it?

> She left the note. That matters.

> We do not know enough to panic in the wrong direction.

> She left a diary, not a rescue note.

> If we go to her with shame, she may never tell us anything again.

> We read what we need to keep her safe. We do not punish her with her own words.

### Gamaliel Galvin

> Where is she?

> Who is Holic Bartunek?

> Give me the address.

> She sat at our table.

> She is my daughter.

> If she chose this, why did she hide it?

> What does she need from us?

### Raphael, if present

> I saw the diary. I saw the note. I did not read it.

> I was there for Klárka.

> I'll be nearby.

---

## What This Scene Establishes

1. **Gamaliel Galvin is now inside the secret.** He can no longer be protected from what is happening with Simonka.

2. **Klára becomes the emotional and ethical brake.** Her history makes her want to act, but also teaches her that the wrong kind of rescue can become another form of control.

3. **Klárka is no longer alone with the diary.** The burden moves from one sister to the family, though Klárka still feels responsible.

4. **Raphael's trustworthiness is reinforced.** He was present, but he did not violate Simonka's written privacy.

5. **Simonka's consent complicates the family response.** They cannot treat the situation as a straightforward abduction or emergency unless the diary gives evidence of coercion or danger.

6. **The family chooses caution over panic.** This does not mean approval. It means they understand that shame or force may push Simonka further away.

7. **The diary remains unfinished.** They know enough to hurt. They do not yet know enough to act cleanly.

8. **Wednesday's audition remains on the horizon.** Klárka's professional duty continues, creating pressure between family crisis and academy responsibility.

---

## Continuity Notes

- This scene happens on **Tuesday**, the day after the Monday night apartment visit and Klárka's breakdown in Raphael's arms.
- The scene happens in **Gamaliel Galvin's office**.
- Klárka tells **both** Gamaliel Galvin and Klára what she and Raphael found.
- **Klára cries softly while Klárka speaks**; the crying is quiet, continuous, and restrained, not a dramatic interruption.
- The scene should stay non-explicit. The focus is the family's emotional and ethical response, not graphic detail from Simonka's diary.
- Simonka is an adult, so the family must balance concern with respect for agency.
- Klárka may still personally call the choice stupid, but the family's outward response to Simonka should avoid shaming language.
- This scene can lead into a later Tuesday scene where Klára and/or Gamaliel read more of the diary, contact Simonka, or decide whether and how to approach Holic Bartunek.
