# THE MORNING AFTER — PALMER PETER, CATRIN, AND RHIAN

## Scene Outline — Beats, Dialogue Cues, and Thematic Notes

> **Companion to** `palmer-peter-meets-catrin-rhian-scene-outline.md`. Read that file first. This scene picks up the morning after, in the same house, in the same world that has just been remade.

> **Content & craft warning.** Same as the meeting scene. This is the scene where the **terms of the rest of Catrin and Rhian's lives** are laid out by the man who took them. The prose must render that without comfort, without redemption-by-restraint, and without giving Palmer Peter any moral credit for the things he chooses not to do. *Patient ownership is still ownership* — the meeting-scene canon line — governs every beat here.

---

**POV:** **Catrin**, continued. *(Rhian, however, **drives** more of this scene — she is the one who asks the questions whose answers set the terms. Catrin's POV watches her sister become, in a single morning, the household's negotiator. This is the first scene in which the lifelong division of labour between the twins inside Palmer Peter's house is established: Rhian speaks to him; Catrin watches him; both pay.)*
**Tone:** Held. Flat. The light is brutal. The night scene's darkness was a kind of cover; the morning scene removes it. Everything is visible now — the room, the courtyard, the man, the two clothes folded on the chair, the absence of a phone. Where the meeting scene was a slow death, this scene is the first day of the long life that follows it. **The register must shift accordingly: less stricken, more bureaucratic.** The terror of this morning is not that anything is happening for the first time — it is that *the rest of life is being scheduled*.
**Location:** Palmer Peter's house in Saudi Arabia — *which the twins are about to discover is also the home of five other women.* Three sub-locations across the scene: **the room** (where they slept / did not sleep, in the guest wing — physically separated from the residences of the existing household), **a small adjoining room or courtyard** (where breakfast is brought, still in the guest wing), and **the *majlis*** (the same room as the meeting — and the choice to bring them back into that room is itself a statement, which the prose should let land).
**Time in saga:** The morning immediately following the meeting. *Fajr* has been called and is over. The first sunlight is full in the courtyard. The marker call against Huw is, at this hour, **being executed in parallel by Palmer Peter's lawyer at Huw's hotel.** Neither twin knows this yet. The collapse of Huw's firm is hours, not days, away. **The five women already in Palmer Peter's household do not yet know either** — he has not told them. He will tell the twins about the household before he tells the household about the twins. The asymmetry is canonical and the morning carries it.
**Ages:** Catrin and Rhian are eighteen (CANON, established in the meeting scene). Palmer Peter is in his early-to-mid twenties.

> **HOUSEHOLD CONTEXT — READ THIS BEFORE THE BEATS.** Per the meeting outline's revised canon: at this point Palmer Peter has **two legal wives (Theadora, Dakota)** and **three long-term partners (Addyson, Aurora, Madison)** already living in his compound, all of whom entered consensually. Catrin and Rhian have spent the night in a **guest wing physically walled off** from the household residences. The five women do not know about the twins. The twins do not know about the five women. *This morning's scene resolves only one of the two ignorances — Palmer Peter tells the twins. The five women learn later, in scenes still to be written.* **Note:** This is the household's state *at this scene*. The profile's currently-canonical *final* breakdown (wives Aurora, Addyson, Rhian, Theadora; partners Catrin, Dakota, Madison, Margurie, Kourtney) is the eventual state, reached through later category changes. See the meeting outline's revised Profile Revisions §9.

## CANON ESTABLISHED BY THIS SCENE

*(Read this list as the operational architecture of Catrin and Rhian's life in the household from this morning forward. The meeting scene was the moral hinge; this scene is the **floor plan**.)*

- **Palmer Peter speaks to them for the second time in the scene, and the first time at length.** What he says here becomes the terms of their life. The terms are not negotiated. They are *delivered*. **(CANON.)**
- **The terms (delivered in this scene):**
  1. **They are not free to leave the country.** Their passports have already been taken. Palmer Peter says this without flourish; he does not threaten; he simply states it as a fact about the day. (Whether he has the legal authority to do this in Saudi Arabia is irrelevant — he has the *practical* authority; the passports are in a drawer in another room and they are not in the twins' hands.) **(CANON.)**
  2. **They are not free to leave the house unaccompanied** for the foreseeable future. The reason given is *for your safety;* the reason understood by all three people in the room is *because you would not come back.* **(CANON.)**
  3. **They will not be touched.** Palmer Peter says this — without euphemism, without performance, without invoking faith. He says it in two sentences. He does not say *yet*. He does not say *until*. He says it as a present-tense fact: he will not touch them. *He does not say for how long.* **(CANON.)** *(He is, in this moment, telling the truth of what he intends today. The "for how long" question is the question their whole lives are about to be organised around.)*
  4. **They will not be made to convert.** He says this too, and means it today, and the saga must hold the line that nothing in this scene *makes* them convert. (Whatever later conversion either of them comes to is now permanently shadowed by the fact that they live in his house and eat at his expense and have no passports.)
  5. **They will not be married to him without their consent** — and *until* their consent, they will not be his wives in any legal or religious sense. He uses the word **wives** for the first time. He uses it in the negative. *"You are not my wives. You will not be my wives unless you ever choose it."* The sentence is the second deepest cruelty of the scene, because it gives them a door that is also a lock: the door is theirs to open, and only theirs, and they will live the rest of their lives next to it. **(CANON.)** — **And in the same breath he discloses, for the first time, that he already has wives. (CANON.) See Beat 5b.**
  6. **They will have what they need.** Clothing. Books. A doctor if they need one. Privacy from him within the house, within the limits of his architecture. He does not call any of this *kindness*. He calls it *provision*. The word is exact and it is the word he means.
  7. **They will be allowed to speak to their mother.** Once a week. The first call is today. The call will not be monitored, but it will be placed from a phone in the house, not from a phone in their hand. **(CANON.)** *(The mother's existence and the call structure are established here. Whether the call is, in fact, monitored is one of the saga's permanent open questions; the twins will never quite know. The not-knowing is part of the architecture.)*
  8. **They will not speak to their father.** Not today. Not ever — at Palmer Peter's instruction. He does not explain. The reader is meant to understand, eventually, that he is *protecting them* from the man who sold them, *and* protecting himself from the man's eventual attempts to undo what cannot be undone, *and* exercising ownership. The three motives cannot be separated. The prose must not try to separate them.
- **Palmer Peter discloses the existing household to Catrin and Rhian. (CANON.)** Inside the same prepared statement that delivers the terms of their lives, he tells them — without preamble, without warning, in the same flat register he has used for the passports and the not-touching — that **he has two wives and three other partners, all already living in this compound, all of whom entered freely.** He names them. He says how long each has been with him. He says how many children he already has, by which mothers. *He does not apologise for any of this. He does not soften it. He does not explain it.* He delivers the information the way he delivers everything in this scene: as a fact about the day they are now inside. **(CANON — see Beat 5b for the texture.)**
- **The household, the morning after, is being walled off in two directions.** The twins are in a guest wing the existing household cannot see; the existing household is in residences the twins will not see today. Palmer Peter tells *both halves* about each other on his own clock — the twins this morning, the household later. The architecture of the disclosure mirrors the architecture of the compound: separation, with him as the only connecting corridor. **(CANON for today; the existing-household disclosure is its own unwritten scene.)**
- **The twins receive the terms.** They do not accept them — accepting is a category that no longer applies to anything in their lives. They *receive* them. The distinction is the entire moral content of the scene.
- **Rhian asks two questions; Catrin asks one.** What each twin asks defines who they will be inside this house for the rest of their lives. (See Beats 5 and 6.) **(CANON.)**
- **Huw's collapse is happening offstage as the scene runs.** The twins do not know. Palmer Peter does not tell them. They will learn within twenty-four hours — by which route, and from whom, is open and important. **(CANON for the offstage timing; open for the route of the news.)**
- **The "he waits" canon from the meeting outline is reinforced here, not introduced.** It is established as *the form of his ownership*, not as a softening of it. The prose must not allow the reader to thank him for it.

## BEFORE THE SCENE — WHAT CATRIN CARRIES INTO THE MORNING

*She did not sleep. She has been counting arches in the plaster since* Fajr.

- **She has not yet cried.** Rhian cried in the *majlis*, silently, while their father was still in the room; Catrin has not. She is waiting — without knowing she is waiting — for a moment in which crying would *do something*. She has not, in eighteen years, learned to cry for any other reason. (This is who she is. The saga's people-watcher does not cry except where it would help. Tonight there is nothing it would help.)
- **She has spent the night in two parallel thoughts.** One is the practical thought: *what do we do.* The other is the larger thought: *what has happened.* The two thoughts do not connect to each other. She cannot use the practical thought to address the larger one, or the larger one to organise the practical one. She has been moving between them the way one moves between rooms in a house in the dark.
- **She has not been able to look at Rhian** for most of the night, even with Rhian three feet away on the other bed. She does not understand why. She will, by the end of this scene, understand: she cannot yet look at her sister because looking at her sister would mean acknowledging *that the same thing has happened to both of them*, and the only way she has survived the night is by not yet letting that acknowledgement complete.
- **She is, despite everything, hungry.** This will shame her later. It does not shame her now. It does not yet occur to her *to* be shamed by it. The body is, even tonight, a body.

---

## WHAT RHIAN CARRIES (briefly — not POV but load-bearing)

- **She did sleep.** A little. Broken. Her body overruled her mind for perhaps an hour and a half. She woke before dawn and has not slept since.
- **She has been doing arithmetic.** Not on her father's books — on **this house**. Counting doors. Counting staff voices in the corridor. Estimating the size of the courtyard from what she could see through the slatted window. Trying to build, in her head, a map of where she is. She has not told her sister she is doing this. She has been doing it because doing it is what she does, and because the alternative is to lie still in the dark and think about her father, and she has decided — in the small hard way Rhian decides things — that she will not think about her father today.
- **She has, in the half-light before sunrise, made one decision.** *I will speak to him.* She has not told Catrin. She does not know, yet, what she will say. She knows only that the man who took them yesterday did not address them, and that someone in this room is going to have to address him, and that her sister — who reads people but does not, by temperament, *push* them — is not going to be the one. So it will be her.

---

## WHAT PALMER PETER CARRIES (briefly — seen from outside, throughout)

*The POV is still Catrin's. The reader still does not enter his head. What is visible:*

- He has, almost certainly, slept. (This too is its own small horror — that he could.)
- He has prayed *Fajr* at its proper time.
- He has, in the hours before the twins are brought to him, **had a phone call with his lawyer** about the execution of the marker against Huw. The call ended a few minutes before they enter the room. He does not mention the call. He does not mention Huw. The fact that he has spent the morning destroying their father, and is about to receive them across a table without saying so, is the third deepest cruelty of the scene.
- He has prepared what he will say. He has not written it down. He has rehearsed it once, while dressing. He will deliver it almost exactly as rehearsed. **The fact that it was rehearsed is what makes it terrible** — he has spent his morning preparing to speak to two women he acquired the night before. The rehearsal is the form his decency takes. The decency does not absolve him. The two facts have to sit together.

## BEAT 1 — THE WAKING

*Short. The first beat is the room they did not sleep in, in full morning light.*

- Catrin has been on her back since *Fajr*. She has not slept. She is aware that the light at the bottom of the window has moved from very thin to full, and that the courtyard outside is now in some kind of sun.
- Rhian, on the other bed, is sitting up. She is dressed in the clothes that were left on the chair — modest, loose, in a colour neither of them would have chosen, but well-fitted in a way that confirms what Catrin suspected the night before: *someone measured us by eye in the* majlis *and acted on the measurement*.
- **Rhian says, the first words spoken between them since the night call to prayer:** *"Get up. Get dressed. We are not meeting him in what we slept in."*
- Catrin — who has not, in eighteen years, taken instruction from her sister in any matter larger than which jumper to borrow — gets up and gets dressed. The clothes fit. The fact of their fitting is small and exact and terrible.

**Internal beat (Catrin):** *Her sister has, sometime in the night, become the elder of the two of them. She does not know how this happened. She does not, yet, know whether she resents it. She does what her sister says.*

---

## BEAT 2 — THE FETCHING

- A soft knock at the door. Not the staff member from the night before — a woman this time. Older. She speaks English carefully, as if she has practised the sentences she is about to say.
- *"Please. Breakfast is ready. I will take you."*
- She does not give her name. She does not meet either twin's eye for longer than is necessary. Catrin will, over the next weeks, come to understand that this woman *knows* — knows what was done in this house last night, knows what the two young women in front of her are now, and is conducting herself with the small care of a person who has decided that her job is to make the practical mechanics of their lives as gentle as the mechanics will permit, and who has also decided that this is the entirety of what she will allow herself to do. **She is the saga's first portrait of a person inside Palmer Peter's household who is, simultaneously, kind and complicit.** There will be others. (See open items.)
- Catrin and Rhian follow her down a corridor — pale walls, a single window onto the courtyard, a turn — to a small room with a low table set for two. *Not three.* Palmer Peter is not at the table. The twins are to eat alone. The woman pours tea, indicates the food (flatbread, dates, a soft white cheese, a small bowl of something green Catrin does not recognise), and withdraws.
- **They do not eat for a long time.** Then Rhian, without speaking, takes a piece of the bread, breaks it, hands half to Catrin, and begins. Catrin eats. They do not look at each other while they eat. The shame Catrin had been waiting to feel about being hungry arrives now and she puts it down.

**Thematic note:** *Provision* has begun. He has not had to say the word yet. The bread is the word.

---

## BEAT 3 — THE WALK BACK

*Briefer. The geometry of the house begins to declare itself.*

- The woman returns. *"He is ready. Please."* She leads them — same corridor, different turn — to a door Catrin recognises before it is opened. The smell of cardamom is already coming under it. **They are being taken back to the** majlis.
- Catrin understands, in the half-second before the door opens, that this is deliberate. *He is receiving them in the same room he took them in.* The choice could have been any of several rooms in the house. He has chosen this one. She does not know whether to read it as cruelty (he wants them to remember) or as honesty (he refuses to soften the address by changing the address) or as something colder than either (he simply uses the *majlis* for matters that are the *majlis*'s, and what he is about to do is one of them). She will, over the years to come, settle on the third reading. She will hate herself, on some days, for the small admiration the third reading contains.

---

## BEAT 4 — THE ROOM IN THE MORNING

*The shock of seeing the room of last night in daylight. Use this beat to remap the space.*

- The cushions have been straightened. The tray has been cleared and replaced — fresh *qahwa*, fresh cups, fresh dates. **There are three cups, not four.** Huw's place is gone. The space where he sat last night between his daughters has been physically erased — the central cushion replaced or rearranged so the geometry does not invite a middle.
- **Palmer Peter is already seated.** He is in the same place he sat last night. He is dressed in the same register (*thobe*, *ghutra*) but a different garment — fresh, deliberate. He does not stand when they enter.
- **He gestures to two cushions** — the same cushions Catrin and Rhian sat on the night before. Not a different arrangement. The same. The twins will spend the rest of the conversation sitting in the seats they were sold from.

**Internal beat (Catrin):** *She understands, sitting down, that this is the room their lives are now organised around. She will never enter this room again without the night returning to her in full. He knows this. He has chosen the room anyway. **It is the room because he is the room.***

---

## BEAT 5 — HE SPEAKS

*The center of the scene. Most of the clock-time is here. Render it with no music.*

- He does not greet them. He does not ask if they slept. He does not begin with *I know this is difficult*. He does not begin with *I am sorry* — he is not, and he will not lie. He begins with the first practical fact, the way one begins a meeting.

**Dialogue cue (Palmer Peter, the shape — render close to this, not in elaborate prose):**

> "Your passports are in this house. They will not be returned to you for the foreseeable future."

- Pause. He does not soften it.

> "You will not leave the house unaccompanied. The staff will arrange anything you need from outside."

- Pause.

> "I will not touch either of you. You are not my wives. You will not be my wives unless you ever choose it."

- Pause. **This is the sentence that does the most damage** — and the prose must let it land without commentary. The reader has to absorb, simultaneously, the *limit* of what he is saying (he will not force them; he will not marry them by decree) and the *cage* of it (the door is theirs and they will live the rest of their lives next to it). He uses the word *wives* deliberately. He does not name what they *are*, because there is no honest noun for it; he names what they are *not*.

> "You should know that I already have wives. Two. And three other women I am committed to. They live here. In other houses on this compound. They have been with me, some of them, for years. They have children with me."

- **This is Beat 5b.** It is the sentence the entire morning is shaped around, and the only one of the seven that he has **rehearsed but does not pre-pause for** — because the rehearsal cannot fully prepare a person for saying these words to two eighteen-year-olds at breakfast. He does not name them, in this first delivery — names will come; today, not yet. He gives only: *two wives. Three others. Years. Children.* The prose must render the four pieces of information in the order he gives them, because the *order* is what tells the twins the architecture they have walked into.
- Catrin will, in the years that follow, be unable to recover what the surface of his face looked like as he said it. She will remember only that his coffee cup was on the low table beside him, and that he did not pick it up during the sentence, and that he picked it up immediately afterward.
- **Rhian's face does, finally, move.** Not much. A small tightening at the jaw. Not at the existence of the household — Rhian was already, in some chamber of her mind, prepared for him to have *people* — but at the **specificity** of *children.* That word lands separately. *Children* means *this is a house with children in it.* Catrin sees her sister's face move and feels her own go cold in a new place.
- He does not stop on the disclosure. He moves immediately into the next term, because to stop would be to invite a question he is not yet willing to take. The prose lets the disclosure pass under the next sentence the way a stone falls past the mouth of a well.

> "You will not be made to convert. Your beliefs are your own. The same is true of your sister."

- He looks, for the first time, at Rhian, then at Catrin, then back at Rhian. He has been giving them the terms; now he is checking that they have heard. Rhian's face does not move. Catrin's, she fears, does — she does not know what it does; she cannot feel it.

> "You will have what you need. Clothing, books, a doctor, time alone in your rooms. Anything reasonable. Ask the woman who fetched you. Her name is [TBD]. She will see to it."

- Pause. The hardest one.

> "You will speak to your mother once a week. The first call will be today. The phone is in this house. The call will not be monitored."

- He does not look at them when he says *not monitored*. (The reader is meant to notice. The twins will, later, notice too.)

> "You will not speak to your father. Not today. Not in the future. This is not a discussion."

- He stops. He is finished. He has, in the saga's measurement, spoken eight sentences. Each sentence has structured a permanent feature of their lives. **The middle sentence — the one about the household — is the one the next several weeks will be organised around.**
- He picks up his coffee cup. The signal is clear: the room is now *open* to whatever they wish to ask. He has, in his way, opened the floor.

**Internal beat (Catrin):** *She understands that he has rehearsed this. She knows it the way she knows when her own father was reading from notes at a school presentation — the small pre-pause before each sentence, the absence of a single visible hesitation. He has spent the morning preparing to deliver this to them. She cannot decide whether the preparation is decency or its opposite. She files the question. She will spend years on it.*

## BEAT 6 — RHIAN'S TWO QUESTIONS

*This is the scene's other center. Rhian — who decided in the small hours that she would be the one to speak to him — speaks.*

### 6a — The first question (the practical one, the numbers one)

- Rhian does not pause. She does not visibly gather herself. She asks, in the same flat register he used:

> "Where is our father?"

- This is the first time anyone in the room has named Huw since last night. Palmer Peter does not flinch. **He does not lie.** *(This is important and must be canon: he does not lie to them, today or ever. He withholds; he refuses; he is silent; he does not lie. It is one of the small architectures of his ownership — the lie would degrade the transaction, and the transaction is precisely what he is unwilling to degrade.)*
- He answers. He gives them the time-frame in business terms — *the position is being closed today; my lawyer is at his hotel this morning; the firm will not survive it.* He does not say *I have destroyed him*. He says *the firm will not survive it*. The passive voice is its own kind of truth-telling.
- He does not soften it. He does not add *I am sorry that this affects you*. He does not add *he brought it on himself*. He says only what he has said.
- **Catrin watches her sister receive this.** Rhian's face still does not move. The face that read paper for their father for the last two years has now read the paper of their father's end, and the reading takes one beat, and the face holds.

### 6b — The second question (the harder one, the one Catrin would not have thought to ask)

- Rhian takes one breath. Then, in the same voice:

> "Our mother. Does she know?"

- *Now* he pauses. For the first time in the scene. The pause is short — perhaps a second — but it is a pause, and the pause is information.
- He answers: *No. Not yet.* He explains, in two sentences, that the news of the firm will reach her by the end of the day, probably from a colleague of Huw's or from the press; that her husband may or may not call her himself first; **that the existence and whereabouts of her daughters are not yet known to anyone outside this house and his lawyer.**
- **Rhian does not ask the next question.** Which is: *and what will she be told.* She does not ask it because she has already understood — in the silence that followed the previous answer — that the answer is *whatever you and I decide she will be told.*
- Catrin understands it half a second after Rhian. The two of them — eighteen years old, sitting on the same cushions they were sold from — have just been handed the smallest, sharpest piece of agency they will be granted in this room: **they will be allowed to compose, with him, the story their mother is told.** The agency is a trap. It is also, in the only sense the morning permits, *real*. They can choose what their mother knows. Whatever they choose, they will have chosen.

**Internal beat (Catrin):** *She watches her sister sit with this and not yet take it up. Rhian will not decide for them at the table. She will decide with Catrin, alone, later. The two of them have, without speaking, agreed on this already.*

---

## BEAT 7 — CATRIN'S ONE QUESTION

*She had not planned to speak. She had not, in the night or the morning, decided what she would say if she did. The question comes out of her mouth before she knows it is hers.*

- She does not ask about Huw. She does not ask about their mother. She does not ask about the country, or the house, or the woman in the corridor, or the bread. **She does not ask about the wives or the partners or the children — yet. That set of questions is too large for this morning; she and Rhian will have to dismantle it together, over weeks. She asks instead the question that the disclosure has, in fact, made *worse*. Because *now* — knowing he had five women, knowing he had children, knowing he had a household that did not require them — the question has a sharper edge than it would have had an hour ago.**

> "Why did you say yes?"

- **This is the question Palmer Peter has not rehearsed for.** The reader must feel that. He has rehearsed the terms. He has rehearsed the answer about Huw. He has rehearsed the answer about their mother. **He has rehearsed the disclosure of the household.** He has not rehearsed the question of his own *yes*. And he understands, in the half-second before he answers, that Catrin is asking it *in the new light* — not *why did you take women your father offered*, but *why did you take women your father offered when you already had wives*. The first version of the question has a defence. The second has none.
- He does not answer immediately. He does not look away. He sets his coffee cup down — the first movement of his hands he has allowed himself in some minutes — and he looks at her.
- **What he says is, in some form:** *"Because the alternative was to leave you in that room with him."*
- Pause. He does not elaborate. He does not say *I am the lesser evil*. He does not say *I will be good to you*. He does not even claim that what he did was *right*. He has named, in one sentence, the only honest defence available to him: that his *yes* removed them from the man who had just sold them. **It is true. It is also wholly insufficient. The prose must let both be true at once — and must let the reader notice that the answer does not address the harder version of Catrin's question.** *He did not have to bring them home. He could have paid for a hotel. He could have arranged a flight. He could have done any of a dozen things that would have removed them from Huw without making them his.* The answer he gives is true. It is also *not the answer to the question Catrin asked.* And the saga must hold that he knows it.

- **Catrin does not reply.** She does not nod. She does not look away. She looks at him for one long beat — the people-reader doing her one job — and what she sees, she will spend years deciding what to do with. The prose should not tell the reader what she sees. The reader sees only that she sees something. Whatever Catrin reads in his face in this beat is the seed of every later thing she will or will not become to him, and the saga has to **keep that seed under the soil** — never harvested into a sentence — for the seed to do its work.

**Thematic note:** This is the only beat in either scene in which Palmer Peter is in any way *answerable* to either twin. He has been opaque throughout. For one sentence, in answer to one question from the quieter twin, he is — slightly, briefly — *legible*. The legibility is not a redemption. It is information. The information is: *he knew what he was doing, he chose it, and he is prepared to say so to her face.* Whether that makes him better than the worst version of himself, or simply more honest about being it, the saga refuses to decide for the reader.

---

## BEAT 8 — THE CALL

*Functional. Render the small terrible logistics of the first phone call home.*

- The conversation is over. Palmer Peter stands. He does not say *you may go*; he simply stands, and the standing ends the meeting. He says, before leaving the room:

> "The phone is in the small room next to yours. The woman will bring it when you are ready. Take whatever time you need."

- He leaves. The woman from the corridor is there before either twin has stood — she has been outside the door the whole time, or has been summoned by a signal Catrin did not see. She walks them back to the small room (not the *majlis*; not their bedroom; a third room, neutral, with a window onto a different part of the courtyard).
- The phone is a landline handset on a low table. Not a mobile. The number, when Rhian asks, is to be dialled by the woman — *"please tell me the number"* — and the handset given to them when the call connects. This is, the twins both understand, the *not monitored* qualifier doing its real work: the call may be silent on the line and audible to the household; or it may be, as Palmer Peter said, unmonitored; they have no way to tell, and they will not, for years, find out. **The architecture of not-knowing has begun.**
- **They do not call yet.** Rhian, who has been the engine of the morning, runs out of engine here. She sits on the floor next to the low table — not on the chair, on the floor — and puts her hand on her sister's ankle. Catrin sits in the chair. They do not speak. The woman, after a moment, withdraws and closes the door.

- **The decision they have to make:** what their mother is told. (Truth? Half-truth? The truth Huw has already given her, if he has called? A version that lets her sleep tonight? A version that lets her come to them, if she can?) The scene does **not** stage this conversation. The scene ends with the decision still to be made, the phone still uncalled, the two of them in a small room they did not choose, with a number to dial and no agreed sentence to say into it.

## BEAT 9 — CLOSING IMAGE

*Brief. Match the meeting scene's coda in length and register.*

- The courtyard outside is in full sun now. Through the window of the small room, Catrin can see a single tree — an olive, she thinks, though she has never seen an olive — and beneath it a section of tiled ground that catches the light. Somewhere in the house, very faintly, the *adhān* for *Dhuhr* is being called. She has learned the word in the eight hours since *Fajr*. She has not learned the others yet; she will.
- Rhian's hand is still on her ankle. Catrin puts her own hand down and covers her sister's hand with hers. It is the first touch between them since the meeting ended last night. Neither speaks.
- The phone is on the table. The number is in Rhian's head; it has always been in Rhian's head; their mother has had the same number since they were small. Eventually one of them will dial it. Not yet.
- **Closing line (the shape):** *They sat in the small room and the sun moved across the floor, and they did not speak, and the phone did not ring, and somewhere in the house a man who had bought them and would not touch them was, perhaps, eating, or reading, or praying* Dhuhr; *and somewhere else in the same house — in rooms they had not yet seen, in courtyards they had not yet walked — five women they had not yet met were, perhaps, also eating, also reading, also praying or not, also moving through a morning that did not yet know what had been added to it; and the day went on.*

## WHAT PALMER PETER DOES *NOT* DO

*(Mirror to the meeting scene's discipline. The negatives have shifted, because the day has shifted. What he does not do in the morning is different from what he did not do in the night.)*

- He does not greet them.
- He does not ask if they slept.
- He does not apologise.
- He does not name what he did last night. He does not call it *the matter*, the way he did in front of Huw. He does not need to call it anything; it is what the room is.
- He does not promise them anything beyond what he intends to do today. He does not say *eventually*. He does not say *one day*. The absence of a future tense is one of the scene's quietest cruelties.
- He does not threaten them. He does not need to.
- He does not raise his voice.
- He does not soften his voice.
- He does not say *I will be good to you*. (This is the line every lesser version of this scene would give him. He does not give it to himself. The saga refuses to give it to him.)
- He does not lie. *(Canon, established in this scene.)*
- He does not, when Catrin asks him *why did you say yes,* perform an answer. He gives the one sentence that is true. He gives no more.
- He does not stay for the phone call. He does not ask, afterward, what was said.
- He does not — and this is the largest, most load-bearing absence — **claim that what he is doing this morning makes up for what he did last night.** He does not invoke the morning against the night. The two are separate. The night is a debt the morning does not pay.
- **He does not name the wives or partners.** He gives the count, the duration, and the existence of children. He does not give Theadora, Dakota, Addyson, Aurora, or Madison a name in this room. The names will come later, on his clock, and partly through the women themselves; he does not deliver them today. *(Whether this is a small mercy or a small further dehumanisation of the existing household — the rendering of them, to the twins, as a number — is one of the saga's permanent open readings.)*
- **He does not tell the twins when they will meet the others.** He gives them no date. The disclosure is, today, *information only*. The household will become visible to the twins on his clock, not theirs.
- **He does not tell the existing household about the twins** before — or during — this scene. The disclosure runs in one direction only. Theadora, Dakota, Addyson, Aurora, and Madison will learn later, separately, by means he chooses. He is, this morning, **the single point of contact between two halves of a household he has split into a known half and an unknown half** — and he is, deliberately, the only person who can see both halves at once.

## TONE GUARDRAILS

*(Carry forward from the meeting scene, with two additions specific to the morning.)*

- **No exoticism.** Same as the meeting scene. The *qahwa*, the *adhān*, the woman in the corridor — these are ordinary. The morning happens in a real house in a real city. Nothing about the country causes what is happening. Palmer Peter causes what is happening.
- **No glamour around the calm.** Palmer Peter's seven-sentence delivery is not *impressive*. It is not *measured restraint*. It is a man who acquired two human beings last night reading a prepared statement to them at breakfast. The prose must let the reading sit as what it is.
- **No interiority for Palmer Peter.** Catrin POV continues. The reader is allowed to see, in Beat 7, that he has not rehearsed for her question — but the reader is not allowed inside him to feel what the unrehearsed moment is *like* for him. The closing of that door is the scene's most important craft discipline.
- **(NEW for this scene) No false agency.** Where Catrin and Rhian appear to be *given* small choices — what their mother is told, when they make the call — the prose must hold the line that these choices exist *only inside the larger non-choice*. Letting them choose how to phrase the news to their mother is not freedom. It is the smallest possible adjustment of how their captivity is presented to the world. The saga has to let the small choices feel like choices to the twins, because they are real choices about a real call — and it has to let the reader see, simultaneously, the cage that frames them. Both registers, on the page, at the same time.
- **(NEW for this scene) No "he is doing his best."** The morning is, in some sense, the *best version* of how this morning could have gone — and that fact is itself part of the horror, not a mitigation of it. The prose must never let the reader settle into *well, given the circumstances, he is being decent*. The circumstances are circumstances **he made**.
- **The romance is in the noticing.** (Carried from the meeting outline. It still applies. **Nothing in this scene is romance.** Any later scene that wants to be romance is on its own, and will have to walk a very long way back from this morning.)

### The household — open items added by the household-disclosure beat (5b)

- [ ] **When and how Catrin and Rhian first meet each of the five existing women.** Each meeting is its own scene; each woman's response will be different. *Recommend* Theadora and Dakota (the wives) first, separately; the three partners (Addyson, Aurora, Madison) in some order after. *Strongly recommend* none of these meetings happens this day. The twins need time. The women need to be told first.
- [ ] **When and how Palmer Peter tells the five women about the twins.** Five separate disclosure scenes — at minimum. (He may, alternatively, tell them as a group; whether he does, and why, is character-defining.) Each will respond differently: **Theadora and Dakota** (the wives) have the most standing — the marriage contracts give them legal and religious footing the others do not. **Addyson** — the long spine, the first relationship, mother of the eldest of all his children — has the most *historical* standing, even as a partner; her response is one of the saga's most important to pin. **Aurora** has children already and is the most exposed to immediate household reshuffling. **Madison** is the quietest variable. *The scene where Addyson learns is, structurally, the second-most-important unwritten scene in the entire arc after Patti Anne's discovery — precisely because she is the woman whose relationship the most resembles a marriage but who has no marriage contract to fall back on.*
- [ ] **Whether any of the five women refuses to accept the twins' arrival.** If so — what does she do? Leave? Stay? Stay and become an ally to the twins? Stay and become an enemy? Each branch is a major arc.
- [ ] **The architecture of "splitting" the compound.** How long Catrin and Rhian remain in the guest wing before being moved to the two newly-finished residences. Whether the wall between guest wing and residences is taken down — or whether it is kept up, and the two halves of the household remain partially separate for years. (The "separate homes, one shared heart" architecture of the profile suggests *one shared heart* eventually wins; this scene's canon suggests the shared heart was never the centre Palmer Peter promised it was.)
- [ ] **The existing children's exposure to the twins.** At time of this scene there are already children in the compound (Aurora's first, Addyson's eldest several, Dakota's one, possibly Madison's). The twins' arrival is also their arrival into a household with children. The first time a child of the household sees one of the twins is its own scene-seed.

## PROFILE REVISIONS — UPDATE

*This scene compounds — does not replace — the revisions flagged in the companion outline. In particular:*

- The profile's "Conversion to Islam — at twenty" section currently reads as a calm, considered adult choice grounded in his temperament. **It survives, but its meaning is now contaminated.** A man who, two-to-five years after his own sincere conversion, can deliver the morning-after of *this* meeting is a different portrait of *the convert's disposition* than the profile draws. The profile must either revise its account of his faith, or carry — as a permanent and unresolved tension — the fact that this man's faith and this man's morning are the same man's.
- The profile's "Catrin and Rhian — kept together by the wife/partner split" framing must now also account for **the fact that they were kept together because they would not have survived being separated.** What reads in the profile as architectural elegance is, after this scene, a survival arrangement.
- The profile's "Children — by mother" lists, for Catrin and Rhian, ten children each. **Every one of those twenty children has, as their origin, the morning scene this outline describes.** The saga must decide what each of those children eventually knows, and from whom. The profile can hold the lists. The saga has to hold the question.
- **The profile's chronology of when each woman entered the household must be re-pinned against this scene.** The new canonical state at the time of the meeting: **Theadora and Dakota are wives; Addyson, Aurora, and Madison are partners; all five are already in residence.** **Catrin and Rhian arrive next, non-consensually. Kourtney and Margurie come later.** The profile's "Chronology & ages (framework)" section must be revised to match. **Additionally:** the profile's final settled breakdown (wives Aurora/Addyson/Rhian/Theadora; partners Catrin/Dakota/Madison/Margurie/Kourtney) implies **at least three women change categories between this scene and the profile's settled state** — most notably Dakota (wife → partner), Aurora (partner → wife), and Addyson (partner → wife). The mechanism of each transition is open and important and belongs in a revised profile chronology.
