# Patti Anne and Aleksander Arrive in Saudi Arabia — Scene Outline

## Placement

**When:** The first leg of the three-continent tour.
**Where:** Palmer Peter's compound in Saudi Arabia. A large, interconnected living space at the center of nine houses.
**Context:** Patti Anne and Aleksander have just arrived from Canada. They are beginning their three-continent tour with the most culturally and emotionally demanding stop: their Muslim convert son's plural household. Palmer Peter has five wives, three of whom are currently pregnant, and he has warned them all to be on their "best behavior" for his Catholic mother's arrival.

---

## Characters

- **Patti Anne Key:** The Catholic matriarch. Exhausted from crossing the globe, but running on pure, stubborn maternal love. She has to reconcile the boy she raised in Canada with the patriarch of this massive Middle Eastern compound.
- **Aleksander:** Quiet, watchful, deeply protective. He observes the unspoken rules of the household that others miss.
- **Palmer Peter:** Patti Anne's son, Salena Sarai's twin. A Muslim convert. He is usually fiercely confident, but today he is nervous. He desperately wants his mother to approve of, or at least feel comfortable in, the life he has built.
- **The Five Women (Palmer Peter's Wives):** Arrayed in the living room. They are on their best behavior, eager to please but inherently part of a system Patti Anne struggles to comprehend. Three of them are visibly pregnant.
- **The Grandchildren:** Numerous, playing on the living room floor, oblivious to the cultural tension.

---

## Scene Outline

### 1. The Gates and the Heat
- Patti Anne and Aleksander arrive at the compound. The physical contrast is immediate: the dry, overwhelming heat of Saudi Arabia replacing the breezy Perth sunshine. 
- The compound is massive—nine houses enclosed by a high wall, secure and private.
- Patti Anne takes a deep breath before the doors open. She touches the rosary in her pocket. She is preparing herself to cross not just a threshold, but a universe.

### 2. The Son's Greeting
- Palmer Peter meets them at the entrance. He is dressed in traditional Saudi attire (a thobe). For a split second, the visual disconnect jars Patti Anne, but then he smiles—the exact same smile as his twin sister Salena Sarai—and the disconnect vanishes. He is just her boy.
- He hugs his mother tightly. She feels the tension in his shoulders. He is nervous. The confident patriarch is just a son hoping his mother won't judge him.
- "Welcome, Mum. Welcome, Aleksander," he says, ushering them out of the heat and into the air-conditioned sanctuary of the main house.

### 3. The Living Room Floor
- Palmer Peter leads them into the central living room—the massive hub that connects the houses. 
- The scene inside is overwhelming: children everywhere, toys on the floor, and five women waiting to greet them. 
- The wives are arranged with quiet, respectful posture. They have clearly been prepped. The "best behavior" mandate is palpable in the room. They offer traditional greetings, tea, and dates.
- Aleksander stands near the back, his watchful eyes taking in the room. He sees the subtle hierarchy among the women—who speaks first, who pours the tea, who manages the children on the floor. He notes it but says nothing.

### 4. The Math and the Bellies
- Patti Anne sits down on the plush seating. She looks around the room, smiling warmly at her daughters-in-law. 
- Then, she does the math. She sees one pregnant belly. Then a second. Then a third.
- A momentary pause. The cultural and theological weight of her son having three wives pregnant at the exact same time hits her. It is the very thing she struggles to translate in her heart.
- Palmer Peter is watching her face closely, holding his breath.
- Patti Anne doesn't flinch. She doesn't preach. Internally, both Patti Anne and Aleksander are screaming, *What the hell are you doing?* The plural marriage, the three simultaneous pregnancies—it offends Patti Anne's Catholic sensibilities and Aleksander's protective instincts. But they both know the golden rule of this household: if they open their mouths to judge, they lose access to their grandchildren. So they bite their tongues. Hard. 
- She smiles, her eyes crinkling. "Three babies on the way," she says, her voice full of genuine warmth. "God has blessed this house. Congratulations to all of you."
- The collective exhale in the room is almost audible. Palmer Peter's shoulders drop an inch. The wives smile back, the tension breaking.

### 5. The Message from the Twin
- Later in the evening, as the wives are tending to the children and the meal, Patti Anne gets a quiet moment with Palmer Peter.
- She reaches into her bag and pulls out the token or simply delivers the verbal message she carried across the ocean from Perth.
- "Salena Sarai says she loves you," Patti Anne tells him. "And she told me to tell you to breathe."
- Palmer Peter lets out a short, wet laugh. He runs a hand over his face. Even from thousands of miles away, his twin sister read him perfectly.
- "I was worried," Palmer Peter admits quietly to his mother. "I know this is a lot for you. The wives, the babies. I know it's not what you pictured."
- Patti Anne takes his hand. She doesn't tell him she agrees with it, because she doesn't. But she tells him what she must to keep the peace. "You're my son. I just want to know these babies. Lord help me, I am going to need more yarn if I'm knitting three blankets at once."

### 6. The School Question
- The conversation between mother and son shifts to practicalities. With so many children—and three more on the way—Patti Anne asks the question that any Key family matriarch would ask: "Where are they going to go to school?"
- The Keys value education (Victoria Ballet Academy, Gamaliel's academy, Salena Sarai's university degree). Patti Anne wants to know what the plan is for this massive next generation.
- Palmer Peter explains his vision. He isn't sending them to a standard local school. They are attending institutions like Dar Ad-Dhikr (a dedicated Dar al-Quran) and intensive Tahfeed programs to focus entirely on memorising the Quran, Arabic language, and Islamic sciences.
- He explains that he wants his children to be fully immersed in the faith, prioritizing the Hifdh (Quranic memorization) track over a standard Western-style secular education. 
- Patti Anne nods slowly. It is another stark departure from the Key family's usual path (like Salena Sarai's university or Gamaliel's academy), but she accepts it as part of the total commitment her son has made to his new life. 

### 7. The Nighttime Arrangement
- The evening winds down. The sheer scale of the household means bedtime is a complex logistical operation. 
- Patti Anne and Aleksander are shown to a beautifully appointed guest house within the compound walls. 
- Before they retire, they notice the subtle, unspoken shifting of the adults. Palmer Peter is a patriarch of a plural household, but out of respect (or perhaps sheer awkwardness) in front of his Catholic mother, the usual overt dynamics of the compound are muted. 
- It becomes clear that every night during their visit, Palmer Peter will discreetly take just one woman to his quarters. He cannot—and will not—parade his plural marriage in front of his parents more than he absolutely has to. 
- Patti Anne and Aleksander get ready for bed in their guest room. They close the door. They look at each other. Finally alone, without the grandchildren to protect, they let out the breath they have been holding all day. The silence of the guest house is a stark contrast to the noise of the living room floor.

### 8. The End of the Journey
- The scene pulls back to earlier in the evening, before the guest house. Aleksander is sitting on the floor with a couple of the toddlers, quietly rolling a toy car back and forth.
- Patti Anne is holding court on the sofa, drinking sweet tea, entirely out of her element but entirely at home because her family is here.
- The three-continent journey has begun. Saudi Arabia's overwhelming plural life is just the first stop. Next, they will head to Prague.


---

## Tone & Style Notes

- **The Hidden Judgment:** The scene hinges on the internal vs. external contrast for the grandparents. Inside, they are completely appalled by Palmer Peter's choices. Outside, they are perfectly gracious, because the grandchildren are the hostage in this dynamic. They play the game to get the prize.
- **The Tension of "Best Behavior":** The wives should feel slightly stiff at first, anxious to please the Canadian matriarch. Patti Anne's warmth is what breaks the ice and turns a formal reception into a family gathering.
- **Palmer Peter's Vulnerability:** He is the master of this massive compound, but in front of his mother, he is just a son craving approval. His relief when she congratulates the pregnant wives is the emotional climax of the scene.
- **Aleksander's Watchfulness:** Aleksander doesn't need much dialogue here. His presence is felt in his observation of the living room floor dynamics.

---

## What This Scene Establishes

1. **The Culmination of the Trip:** This is the heaviest and most demanding stop for Patti Anne, proving that her love can bridge any theological or cultural divide.
2. **The Reality of Palmer Peter's Life:** It grounds the abstract idea of "five wives" into a physical, breathing room full of women and children. 
3. **The Twin Bond Validated:** Salena Sarai's message from Perth hitting perfectly in Saudi Arabia shows that the twins remain deeply connected despite the completely divergent paths their lives have taken.
4. **Patti Anne's Defining Trait:** She is the mother who shows up. She doesn't have to agree with her children's choices to fully, fiercely embrace their lives.
