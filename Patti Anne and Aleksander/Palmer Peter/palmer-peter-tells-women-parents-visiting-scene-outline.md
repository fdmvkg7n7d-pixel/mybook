# Palmer Peter Tells His Women That His Parents Are Coming for a Visit — Scene Outline

## Placement

**When:** Before Margurie and Kourtney have entered Palmer Peter's life. He currently has five women: Aurora, Theadora, Addyson, Dakota, and Madison.

**Where:** The shared living room at the center of Palmer Peter's compound in Saudi Arabia — the communal heart that all seven individual houses open onto.

**Context:** Patti Anne and Aleksander have visited before. This is not their first time seeing Palmer Peter's life in Saudi Arabia. The women have met his parents — or at least some of them have. But every visit carries its own weight, its own preparation, its own dynamics. At this point in the timeline, only Dakota and Addyson have children. Aurora, Theadora, and Dakota are currently pregnant. Palmer Peter gathers all five women to tell them his parents are coming — and to warn them to be on their best behavior.

---

## Characters

- **Palmer Peter Key**: Son of Patti Anne and Aleksander. Twin brother of Salena Sarai. Muslim convert. The calm, deliberate, secure man who built a compound with five women around one shared living room. He tells his women the way he does everything — steadily, without performance, trusting that the household he has built can hold this.
- **Aurora**: One of the four legal wives. Currently pregnant.
- **Theadora**: One of the four legal wives. Not pregnant. No children yet.
- **Addyson**: One of the four legal wives. The longest-tenured — the first woman in Palmer Peter's life. Has children. Currently pregnant again. She has the most history with Patti Anne and Aleksander's visits.
- **Dakota**: One of the five partners. Has children. Not currently pregnant.
- **Madison**: One of the five partners. No children yet. Currently pregnant.
- **Catrin**: One of the five partners. Identical twin with Rhian. Welsh. No children yet.
- **Rhian**: One of the four legal wives. Identical twin with Catrin. Welsh. No children yet.

## Scene Outline

### Palmer Peter Gathers the Women

- Evening in the compound. The children are settled — some asleep, some in their mothers' houses with older siblings watching over them. The shared living room is lit warmly. This is the room where the household meets as one — the center of the radial design, the common hearth.
- Palmer Peter calls all five women to the living room. This is not unusual — the living room is where household matters are discussed, where decisions are shared, where the family operates as a unit. But the women can tell from Palmer Peter's tone that this is an announcement, not a conversation.
- They settle in. Five women, one man, one room. The arrangement is natural — each woman has her usual spot, her usual proximity to the others. The dynamics of the household are visible in how they sit: who is next to whom, who leans forward, who settles back.

### The Announcement

- Palmer Peter does not build up to it. He is not a man who creates suspense. He tells them directly:
- "My parents are coming for a visit."
- Patti Anne and Aleksander. From Canada. To Saudi Arabia. To this compound. To this living room.
- The sentence lands differently on each woman. They have been through this before — they know what a visit from Palmer Peter's parents looks like. But knowing does not make it simple.

### The Warning

- Palmer Peter lets the announcement settle. Then his tone shifts — still calm, still steady, but with an edge of seriousness the women recognize. This is not a request. This is Palmer Peter being clear.
- "I need all of you to be on your best behavior in front of my parents."
- He looks around the room. He makes eye contact with each woman individually. He is not singling anyone out — he is including everyone. No exceptions.
- He continues: "My mother is a Catholic woman from Canada who loves me enough to fly halfway around the world to sit in this living room with all of you. My father comes with her every time. They do not have to understand this life to love me, and they do not have to approve of everything they see to keep showing up. But they show up. And I will not have them disrespected, embarrassed, or made uncomfortable in my home."
- The room is quiet. Palmer Peter rarely speaks this directly to the group as a whole. When he does, they listen.
- "Three of you are pregnant." He looks at Aurora, Theadora, and Dakota in turn. "My mother will see that. She will do the math. She will feel however she feels about it. That is her right. Your job is not to manage her feelings. Your job is to be kind, to be respectful, and to be yourselves — your best selves. The same thing I ask of myself every time she walks through that door."
- He pauses. Then, softer: "She loves you. All of you. She may not say it the way you expect, and she may not show it the way you're used to. But she has sat in this room before and she has held your children and she has eaten your food and she has come back. That is love. Do not take it for granted."
- The warning is not harsh. It is not a threat. It is a son who loves his mother and knows what it costs her to come here — and who loves his women and knows what it costs them to be seen. He is standing in the middle, the way he always stands, holding both sides with the calm of a man who has never doubted that love can hold contradictions.

### Palmer Peter Orders the Children Out

- After the warning settles, Palmer Peter looks around the room. Some of the children have drifted in — as children do in a household this size, drawn to the gravity of adults gathered in one place. Addyson's children, small bodies in pajamas and bare feet, hovering at the edges, leaning against their mothers' legs, half-listening to words they don't fully understand.
- Palmer Peter's voice shifts. Still calm. But unmistakably an order — not a suggestion, not a request. The head of the household speaking:
- "Take the children out of the living room."
- The room understands immediately. Whatever comes next is not for the children. The warning was the surface. There is something underneath it — something Palmer Peter will say only to the five women, only with the doors closed and the small ears gone.
- The women move without argument. Addyson stands first — the veteran, always the first to act — and gathers her children quietly. Aurora places a protective hand over her pregnant belly as she stands. The others help, shepherding the small ones out of the living room and back toward their individual houses. Soft protests from a toddler who doesn't want to leave. A quick whisper from a mother. A door closing.
- Within two minutes, the living room holds only Palmer Peter and his five women. No children. No small ears. No witnesses but the people who share this life.
- The room is different now. The warmth is still there — it is always there — but the air has tightened. Palmer Peter ordered the children out *after* the warning, not before. That means the warning was the gentle part. Whatever he does next, he did not want his children to see.

### Palmer Peter Takes Rhian on the Living Room Floor

- The children are gone. The doors are closed. Five women and Palmer Peter in the shared living room.
- Palmer Peter turns to Rhian. He does not speak. He does not explain. He does not ask.
- He takes Rhian on the living room floor.
- In front of all of them. In front of Aurora, Theadora, Addyson, Dakota, and Madison. In the center of the shared living room, the communal heart of the compound, the room where they plan meals and organize visits and discuss household matters. On the floor.
- This is why he ordered the children out. Not for a conversation. Not for a deeper warning. For this.
- The six other women watch. They do not leave. They do not look away. This is part of the household — part of what it means to live in this compound, to share this man, to share this living room. Palmer Peter is the head of this household, and this is how he exercises that authority: openly, in front of all of them, without apology and without privacy.
- Rhian does not resist. She does not hesitate. Whatever passes between them — whatever dynamic they have built, whatever understanding exists in this household about what the living room floor means — she accepts it.
- Catrin watches her identical twin sister on the floor with the man they share. Her face is unreadable. Whatever she feels — and she feels something, because she is Catrin and Rhian is the other half of her — she holds it. This is not new. This is how they live. But it is never not something.
- The other women hold their positions. Addyson, the veteran, watches with the stillness of a woman who has seen this before and knows her place in the order. Madison, the direct one, does not look away — she never looks away from anything. Dakota, the quiet one, looks at her hands. Theadora sits composed, her posture unchanged, as if the room has not shifted at all.
- Palmer Peter takes his time. The living room floor is not a rushed thing. It is deliberate. It is a demonstration — not of cruelty, not of dominance for its own sake, but of the structure of this household. He is the center. The women radiate from him. And sometimes the center makes itself visible.
- When it is over, Palmer Peter stands. Rhian stays on the floor for a moment — then rises. She smooths herself. She does not look at Catrin. She does not look at anyone. She returns to her seat.
- The room recalibrates. The air loosens. The tension that built when the children were sent out — the question of *what comes next* — has been answered.
- Palmer Peter looks around the room at his five women. His voice is calm. The same voice that announced his parents' visit. The same voice that warned them to be on their best behavior.
- "This does not happen while my parents are here. Not in the living room. Not where they can see. We are ourselves — but we are ourselves with respect for the people who raised me."
- The room understands. The demonstration was the rule. The living room floor is theirs — but not while Patti Anne and Aleksander are under this roof. Best behavior means best behavior. And now every woman in the room knows exactly what that means, because he showed them what it looks like when there is no one to behave for.

### The Reactions — Seven Women, Seven Responses

**Addyson — The Veteran:**
- Addyson has been here the longest. She has seen Patti Anne and Aleksander more than anyone else in this room. She was the first — the woman who was already here when the compound was still just a plan, when Palmer Peter's life in Saudi Arabia was still taking shape. She has children already—seven of them, the largest brood in the compound.
- Her reaction is practical and immediate. She starts thinking logistics — rooms, meals, schedules, which children need to be presentable, which parts of the compound need attention. She is not nervous. She is the one who knows how this works. She has done this pregnant before — or close to it. She knows how Patti Anne's eyes land on a belly.
- She nods at Palmer Peter's warning. She does not need to be told. But she respects that he said it to everyone, not just her. She looks at Madison and Aurora — the two who haven't been through this yet, pregnant in front of his mother for the first time — and her look says: *I'll help you through it.*

**Aurora — The Warm One:**
- Aurora's face opens into a genuine smile. She likes Patti Anne. She likes the warmth Patti Anne brings, the way she tries, the way she holds Palmer Peter's face when she arrives. Aurora is the woman in the household who treats Patti Anne's visits as family — not an inspection, not a test, but a mother coming to see her son. She is pregnant — her first — and there is a part of her that wants Patti Anne to see. To know. To be a grandmother to this baby too.
- She asks immediately: "How long are they staying?" She wants to know so she can plan something — a meal, an outing, time together. Aurora does not perform for guests. She simply includes them.
- Palmer Peter's warning lands gently on her. She takes it seriously — she always takes him seriously — but she is not worried. She believes the best of Patti Anne. She puts a hand on her belly and says quietly: "She'll be happy for us. I know she will."

**Theadora — The Composed One:**
- Theadora nods once. Measured. She respects Patti Anne and Aleksander — respects the effort it takes for a Catholic mother from Canada to sit in this living room and love her son without flinching. But Theadora is private. She guards her household, her children, her routines. A visit means adjusting. She will adjust — gracefully, completely — but she registers the cost quietly.
- "When?" The first practical question after Addyson's logistics. Theadora needs a timeline.

**Catrin and Rhian — The Twins:**
- The Welsh twins react in tandem — as they do with most things — but differently.
- Catrin speaks first: "Will Salena Sarai come too?" The twin asking about the other twin. Catrin has a particular affection for Palmer Peter's twin sister — the mirror of her own twinship, the woman who is to Palmer Peter what Rhian is to her. If Salena Sarai is coming, the visit takes on a different dimension for Catrin.
- Rhian is quieter. She glances at Catrin — the silent check-in that identical twins do without thinking — and then at Palmer Peter. Her concern is not about logistics or guests. It is about him. She reads Palmer Peter the way only someone very close can: she is checking whether this visit is welcome or weighted. Whether something has happened in the family. Whether the announcement carries something he has not said yet.
- Palmer Peter meets Rhian's eyes. Whatever passes between them — reassurance, acknowledgment, the private shorthand of their relationship — settles her. She nods.

**Dakota — The Nervous One:**
- Dakota shifts in her seat. She is the quietest woman in the room and the most uncertain about her place in it. She has children. She is a partner, not a wife. She is acutely aware of how Patti Anne's eyes move through the compound — counting, observing, calibrating. She is not pregnant this time, but three of the others are, and she knows what that looks like from the outside — a household multiplying.
- She does not say anything at first. Then, quietly: "Do they know about…" She trails off. She does not finish the sentence. Whatever she is asking — whether Patti Anne and Aleksander know something specific about her, about her situation, about a change — she cannot bring herself to say it in front of the group.
- Palmer Peter looks at her directly. His voice is gentle: "They know. And they love you. You're family, Dakota."
- Dakota's eyes fill. She nods quickly and looks down. Palmer Peter's warning about best behavior — she takes it the hardest. She is already on her best behavior. She is always on her best behavior. It is the only way she knows how to hold her place in a household this large.

**Madison — The Direct One:**
- Madison crosses her arms. Not defensively — it is simply how she sits when she is thinking. She is the most direct woman in the household, the one who says what others think. She is pregnant — her first — and she is showing. There is no hiding it.
- "Are we doing the thing where everyone pretends this is normal?" She says it with a half-smile, one hand resting on her belly. It is not a complaint — it is an observation. The thing: the performance of a plural household presenting itself to a Catholic mother and a Canadian father. Three pregnancies visible at once. The careful choreography of making five women and their children and one man look like a family that functions — which it does, but which still requires a different kind of explanation every time someone from the outside walks through the door.
- A few of the women laugh. The tension in the room eases. Madison's directness is a relief — it names the thing everyone feels without making it heavy.
- Palmer Peter smiles: "We're doing the thing where my parents come and we are ourselves. That's all we've ever done."
- Madison looks at him. "And if your mother counts three pregnant bellies and does the math?"
- Palmer Peter: "Then she does the math. And she loves us anyway. Because that's who she is."
- Madison holds his gaze for a moment, then nods. She accepts it. Not because she is fully convinced, but because she trusts him — and because his warning was fair. Best behavior. She can do that. She'll just do it her way.

### After the Announcement

- The logistics begin. Addyson takes the lead — meal planning, sleeping arrangements, which house the guests will stay in. Aurora offers to cook the first night's dinner. Theadora asks about the itinerary. Madison and Addyson discuss whether the children should all be gathered for the arrival or introduced gradually.
- The room hums with activity. This is what the shared living room is for — the center of a household that functions, that plans, that holds seven lives together around one common purpose.
- Palmer Peter watches them. Five women, talking over each other, organizing his parents' visit the way they organize everything — as a collective, with individual textures, with love underneath the logistics. This is what he built. This is what his parents are coming to see.
- He does not say what he is thinking, which is this: his mother will walk through the door and hold his face and look past him at the five women and the children and the compound and the life he chose — and she will love him. She will love all of it. Not because she understands it. Because she is Patti Anne, and that is what Patti Anne does.

## Tone & Style Notes

- The scene is warm, domestic, and grounded. This is not a crisis — it is a household organizing for a visit. The tension is low-grade and familiar: the nerves of presenting a complex life to parents who love you but come from a different world.
- Each woman's reaction should reveal character. Addyson is the veteran. Aurora is warmth. Theadora is composure. Dakota is vulnerable. Madison is the truth-teller. Five distinct people, one announcement.
- Palmer Peter's calm is the center of the scene. He is not anxious. He is not performing. He trusts his household and he trusts his parents. The security he was born with — the child who never doubted he belonged — is the same security he extends to the women he has built a life with.
- Madison's "Are we doing the thing where everyone pretends this is normal?" is the line that releases the pressure. It should be funny and true and affectionate.
- The closing image — Palmer Peter watching the five women organize — should feel like the culmination of everything his profile establishes: a man who reproduced the Key family's structure on different ground, by different rules, and made it work.

## What This Scene Establishes

1. **The household as a functioning unit.** Five women in one living room, planning together. The compound works. The shared living room is the center.
2. **Individual character textures.** Each woman reacts differently — practical, warm, composed, curious, nervous, direct. They are not interchangeable.
3. **Palmer Peter's warning.** He loves his mother and he loves his women and he stands in the middle. The warning is not harsh — it is a son who knows what it costs Patti Anne to come, and who will not let that courage be taken for granted. Best behavior. No exceptions.
4. **Three pregnancies.** Aurora, Theadora, and Dakota are pregnant. Patti Anne will see three bellies. The math is visible. The scene sets up the tension of the visit without resolving it.
5. **Palmer Peter's calm authority.** He announces, he warns, he reassures. He is the secure child grown up — the still center of a large, complex household.
6. **The family connection.** Patti Anne and Aleksander have visited before. The relationship between the generations is ongoing, not a single event. The door between Canada and Saudi Arabia is open.
7. **Dakota's vulnerability.** The quietest, most uncertain woman in the room — a partner, not a wife, with children — gets the direct reassurance from Palmer Peter: "They know. And they love you. You're family."
8. **Addyson as the anchor among the women.** The veteran who has been through this before — her look to the younger women says *I'll help you through it.*
9. **Timeline marker.** This is before Margurie and Kourtney. The household is five women, not seven or nine. The twins Catrin and Rhian have not yet arrived. Only Addyson has children so far—seven of them. The compound is still growing.
