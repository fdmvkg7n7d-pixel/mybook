## NAME MEANING & THE TWIN PAIR

- **Palmer** — an old occupational surname-as-given-name meaning *pilgrim* (literally one who carried a palm frond back from the Holy Land). The saga can lean into this: a boy named *Pilgrim* who, as an adult, makes the most literal pilgrimage of anyone in the family — leaving Catholic Canada for the Arabian Peninsula, converting, and building a life there. The name was given long before anyone could know. The family notices it only in hindsight.
- **Peter** — *the rock* (Petros). The steady one. Fitting for the twin who, of the two, is the anchor.
- **The twin naming rhythm:** **Palmer Peter** and **Salena Sarai** — each a two-name pairing, balanced against each other the way the twins themselves are. (Distinct from the soft-K naming tradition on Klára's side of the family tree — Klára, Klárka, Káta, Simonka — because Palmer Peter and Salena Sarai are *Key* children by blood, not part of the Drahoslav-line naming echo.)

## BORN INTO CERTAINTY (THE KLÁRA CONTRAST)

Palmer Peter is, with his twin, the saga's portrait of **the child who never had to wonder whether he belonged.** Where Klára folded wrapping paper and kept the ribbons and held her things light because she had learned not to own what she could not carry, Palmer Peter tore through his presents *"with the velocity of children who know there will always be another Christmas."* (See `../Gamaliel Galvin and Klara/gamaliel-galvin-and-klara-key-profile.md`.)

This is his foundational fact and it matters for everything he later becomes:

- He is **Key by blood and has never doubted it.** He goes to bed without fear and *"sleeps the way children sleep who know they will wake up in the same house they fell asleep in."*
- That security is the platform from which he can, as an adult, do something genuinely radical — leave the country, leave the faith, restructure his whole life around an unfamiliar culture — **without it reading as flight.** Klára's divergences (and later Simonka's) are shadowed by old wounds. Palmer Peter's divergence comes from the opposite place: a person so sure of his footing that he can walk very far from it and never feel he is falling. The family that held him made him brave, not careful.

**The thematic rhyme of the cousins' generation:** the saga keeps producing children who walk away from the family's defaults and toward something the family does not understand — Simonka (ballet → psychology → the Collegium), and Palmer Peter (Catholic Canada → Muslim Saudi Arabia → plural marriage). The difference: Simonka hides hers; **Palmer Peter does not hide his.** He is the secure one. He tells them. The drama in his arc is not concealment — it is the family's attempt to keep loving, across an ocean and a faith line, a son who is genuinely happy in a life they would never have chosen.

## VICTORIA BALLET ACADEMY — FOLLOWING GAMALIEL

Palmer Peter trains at **Victoria Ballet Academy**, the same full-time school (academics integrated with professional-level ballet training, Toronto area) that Gamaliel and Klára attended from age five through eighteen.

**The thirteen-year gap.** Gamaliel is grown — and, by the time Palmer Peter is old enough to be a serious student, Gamaliel and Klára have already graduated, moved to Prague, and opened their own academy. So Palmer Peter does **not** train alongside his big brother day to day. He trains in the institution his brother *came out of.* He is "Gamaliel Key's little brother" at a school where Gamaliel is something of a legend — the boy who danced the pas de deux at Mike Key's funeral, who left for Prague to build something of his own.

This shapes Palmer Peter's relationship to ballet:

- **Inheritance vs. ownership.** The profile of Gamaliel and Klára insists their ballet was *theirs* — not Rachel Darcy's, not the family's lineage, but a thing they built. Palmer Peter complicates that cleanly: he is the first person to take up ballet *because of* a family member. His ballet **is** partly inherited — from Gamaliel. He has to find, over the years, what part of it is actually his.
- **The shadow of the legend.** Teachers who taught Gamaliel teach Palmer Peter. They compare, even when they try not to. Whether Palmer Peter is as gifted as his brother, more gifted, or simply *different* — [TBD] — is a real question of his adolescence.
- **Does he go to Prague?** A natural pull: train at the school his own brother and foster-sister built, in the city named (in part) for their dead grandfather (Mike Key Studio). Whether Palmer Peter ever studies or works at Gamaliel and Klára's Prague academy is **open** — and it intersects with his eventual move to Saudi Arabia. *(See open items.)*
- **The brother who converted first — to a different faith.** Palmer Peter is the family's first and only Muslim, and the first Key to set foot in Saudi Arabia — but he is **not** the first of Patti Anne's sons to leave the family's Catholicism. **Gamaliel converted to Judaism years earlier** — following Klára and the faith of her Jewish great-grandmothers, a wanting that began when Gamaliel was twelve (`../Gamaliel Galvin and Klara/gamaliel-galvin-and-klara-key-profile.md`). This reframes Palmer Peter's arc: by the time he chooses Islam at nineteen, the family has already survived a son crossing a faith line and loved him exactly the same. Gamaliel is the proof-of-concept — not for the destination, but for the crossing. The brothers rhyme in shape, not in faith: each converts *toward belonging* — Gamaliel toward Klára and Prague, Palmer Peter toward the life he builds in the Gulf. Two converts, two faiths, thirteen years apart.

**Ballet as the through-line into adulthood.** Crucially, Palmer Peter does **not** abandon ballet when he leaves for Saudi Arabia. Ballet is the *vehicle,* not the casualty: [TBD which — a performing career, a teaching post, the founding of a school, a cultural/expat company in the Gulf]. The image the saga can hold: a Canadian-born, Muslim-convert ballet dancer in Saudi Arabia — every part of that sentence a distance traveled from a toddler tearing open presents at a Toronto Christmas.

## THE TWIN BOND — PALMER PETER & SALENA SARAI

The center of Palmer Peter's emotional life is his twin.

**Who they are together.** Palmer Peter and Salena Sarai are the *"twins who belong by blood and have never doubted it."* They are introduced, always, as a unit — *the twins* — and the saga has already shown them moving as one: opening presents together, competing to hold Willow, fading toward sleep at the cottage on the same New Year's Eve while the older kids stayed up. Salena Sarai is *"the one who never fully naps"* (the restless, vocal one); Palmer Peter is the one who *"fights sleep with the grim determination of a child who believes that if he just stays very still, no one will notice he is about to fall asleep standing up."* Even as toddlers, their temperaments are drawn as complementary: she is the noise, he is the stillness.

**Fraternal, not identical — and differently gendered.** Being a brother–sister twin pair means they are never mistaken for one another, never interchangeable, and yet bonded at the root. This becomes load-bearing in adulthood: when Palmer Peter moves to Saudi Arabia, converts, and marries plurally, **Salena Sarai is the person on the other end of that distance.** The twin bond is the saga's instrument for measuring how far he has gone — because Salena Sarai is the one who feels every kilometre of it.

**The adult tension (the heart of this thread).** Possible shapes for how Salena Sarai holds her twin's choices:

- **The keeper of the thread.** Whatever the rest of the family does, Salena Sarai stays in constant contact — the one who calls, who visits Riyadh or Jeddah, who learns the names of his wives and his children, who refuses to let *the twins* become a past tense. She is his bridge back to the family.
- **The one who grieves it.** Or: Salena Sarai takes it hardest. Not disapproval exactly — *loss.* The other half of her unit chose a life on another continent, inside a faith and a marriage structure she does not share, and the twin who was *always there* is now reachable only by screen and by airfare. Her wound is not moral; it is the specific grief of a twin who has been left.
- **Both, across time.** Most likely: she grieves it *and* keeps the thread — the way real love does both. The saga's recurring family doctrine (*door cracked, porch light on, dog at the door*) finds its twin-scale version here: Salena Sarai keeps the line open across an ocean, not because she understands his life, but because he is her twin and that is older than understanding.

**Echo to the elder pairing.** Palmer Peter and Salena Sarai are, in the next generation, a structural rhyme with **Gamaliel and Klára** — two people bonded since the very beginning who become each other's fixed point. The difference: Gamaliel and Klára's bond turned romantic and built a shared life in one place; Palmer Peter and Salena Sarai's bond is sibling and is tested by *distance* rather than secrecy. One pair collapsed the gap between them; the other has to love across a widening one.

**The opposite-directions image (canon).** Both twins are dancers who grew up in the same Toronto studio — and both leave the continent for it, in opposite directions: **Palmer Peter east to Saudi Arabia, Salena Sarai across the Pacific to a ballet university in Perth, Australia (WAAPA).** Two halves of one unit flung to opposite ends of the earth for love of the same art. Salena Sarai — the natural reader of the people she loves (the three-year-old who said *"Klára loves Gamy"*) — understands her brother's radical choices even when she grieves them, which is precisely why she becomes the keeper of the thread. *(See `../Salena Sarai/salena-sarai-key-profile.md`.)*

## THE SAUDI ARABIA ARC

*Handle with care and accuracy. This is a major adult divergence, treated seriously — not as scandal, not as cautionary tale, but as a real life a beloved family member genuinely chooses and is happy in.*

### The move

As an adult, **Palmer Peter moves to Saudi Arabia.** The mechanism is open [TBD], with ballet as the likely vehicle — a performing contract, a teaching position, or building/joining a dance institution in the Gulf, where there has been real and growing investment in arts and entertainment in recent years. He does not move to flee anything (see "Born Into Certainty"); he moves *toward* something.

### Conversion to Islam — at twenty

In the Saudi context, Palmer Peter **converts to Islam at age twenty** — **two years after arriving** in the country at eighteen. The timing matters: he does not convert *in order* to move, and he does not convert the moment he lands. He moves first (for ballet, at eighteen), lives there for two years, and **then** embraces the faith — a considered decision made from inside the life, not a precondition for entering it. This should be written as a genuine embrace of faith, not a transaction:

- It is, for the family, the most significant line he crosses — the Keys are **practicing Catholics**, and Patti Anne especially carries her faith. A son leaving the Church is a different order of distance than a son moving abroad.
- And yet it rhymes with the family's own history of *choosing*: this is a family that has repeatedly chosen love and belonging over rules and expectations (Mike choosing to raise Patti Anne as his own; Mike protecting Gamaliel and Klára; Terri Anne's twenty-five-million-dollar act of faith). Palmer Peter, the secure child, makes a choice of his own — and asks the family to extend to him the same grace it has always prized in itself.
- **The pilgrim's name** pays off here (see "Name Meaning").

#### How he comes to it — the two years (18 → 20)

The conversion is the *end* of a quiet, gradual process, not a sudden event. The shape of those two years:

1. **Year one — the outsider who pays attention.** Palmer Peter arrives at eighteen as a Canadian dancer, Catholic by upbringing but not especially devout — faith for him is family ritual, not yet personal conviction. He is the *still, watchful one* (his core temperament): he doesn't argue with the new country, he **observes** it. The five daily prayers structuring everyone's day; the call to prayer over the city; Ramadan emptying and then filling the streets; the way an entire society organizes its time around devotion. To a dancer — someone whose whole life is **discipline as a path to grace** — a faith built on daily, bodily, repeated practice is not alien. It is legible. It rhymes with the barre.

2. **The bridge — discipline becomes devotion.** This is the heart of *why him.* Ballet already taught Palmer Peter that repetition is not drudgery but devotion; that you submit your body to a form, daily, and freedom comes *through* the submission, not despite it. Islam's five-times-a-day prayer, its physical postures, its fasting, its rhythm, lands on a person already fluent in exactly that grammar. He doesn't have to be argued into it intellectually first; he **recognizes** it in his body before he assents to it in his mind. The convert's deliberateness and the dancer's discipline are the same disposition (see "Temperament").

3. **The people — a teacher, a friend, a company.** Conversion in this family is never abstract; it comes through *belonging.* [TBD specifics, but the shape:] someone in Palmer Peter's Saudi life — a fellow dancer, a mentor at his company/school, a friend who answers his questions without pressure — is the human door. He is invited, not recruited. He asks; they explain; he keeps coming back. The Keys have always converted to *people* before *principles* (Klára into the family, the whole saga's pattern); Palmer Peter embraces a faith the same way he'd embrace a family — through a person who made room for him.

4. **Gamaliel, the precedent — in shape, not in faith.** Palmer Peter is the family's first Muslim, and no one hands him the door he walks through in Saudi Arabia. But he is **not the first son to leave the family's Catholicism** — his eldest half-brother **Gamaliel converted to Judaism years earlier,** following Klára into the faith of her great-grandmothers (`../Gamaliel Galvin and Klara/gamaliel-galvin-and-klara-key-profile.md`). So Palmer Peter has a precedent *inside his own family* — a brother who crossed a faith line and remained recognizably himself, remained a Key, remained loved. Whether Gamaliel actively counsels him (a convert's phone call across faiths — *I know what it is to choose; I know what it costs and what it gives*) or is simply quiet proof that *this can be done and the family survives it* is open — but Gamaliel's earlier conversion makes Palmer Peter's feel less like a rupture and more like a shape the family already knows.

5. **The decision at twenty — calm, not announced as rebellion.** When he takes the *shahada* (the declaration of faith) at twenty, it is **characteristically undramatic.** Palmer Peter is the boy who tried to win by holding *very still*; he is the man who makes enormous decisions quietly and does not stage them as defiance. He does not convert *at* his family — he converts *toward* something, and then he tells them, plainly, the way he tells them everything. The drama is theirs to feel, not his to perform.

**Tone note:** write the *how* with the same care as the *what* — a real, gradual, sincere coming-to-faith, grounded in his temperament (discipline→devotion) and in human relationship, not in plot convenience or exoticism. Research the actual texture of conversion (the shahada, learning to pray, Ramadan as a new convert, the convert's early community) before any detailed scene.

### "Multiple marriages are very interesting" — plural marriage

Palmer Peter finds **plural marriage** compelling and enters into it. In Saudi Arabia, **polygyny is legal within an Islamic framework — a man may marry up to four wives**, with religiously and culturally significant obligations of equitable treatment and provision. Framing for the saga:

- **Consensual, adult, lawful, and sincere within its context.** This is not depicted as exploitation. Palmer Peter's marriages are real marriages — partnerships, households, in time **children** (Patti Anne's grandchildren; Salena Sarai's nieces and nephews; Gamaliel's and Klára's too).
- **Why it "interests" him.** [TBD — and worth developing with nuance.] Options: a genuine theological conviction; an attraction to the structure of a large, plural household after growing up in a big, interwoven family; the appeal of building exactly the kind of sprawling, many-membered home the Keys themselves are. There is a quiet irony available: the Keys are an enormous, complicated, many-branched family — Palmer Peter simply builds his own enormous, complicated family by different rules, on different ground.
- **The family's challenge.** Not whether to approve — they will not all approve — but whether they can do what this family has always done at its best: **keep the door open** to a member living a life they did not choose for him. Salena Sarai's grief vs. Salena Sarai's loyalty. The wives and children as *new Keys-by-marriage* whom the family must decide whether and how to embrace.

- **Patti Anne loves him anyway (CANON).** His mother's verdict is settled and simple: **she loves Palmer Peter no matter what.** The conversion, the distance, the plural marriage — none of it changes the fact of her love. Patti Anne is devout, and a son leaving the Church is a real grief to her *as a Catholic* — but her love is not conditional on his choices, and it never wavers. This is consistent with everything the saga has built her to be (the mother whose love is *faster than annoyance and always wins* — the Willow/boots scene). The proof is in the calendar: when the family scatters across the planet, **Palmer Peter comes home to Patti Anne and Aleksander for Christmas.**
  - **The first Christmas (age 18) — pre-conversion.** The very first Christmas after he moves out, Palmer Peter is still eighteen and **has not yet converted** (that comes at twenty). So his first homecoming is simply a still-Catholic son coming home for the family's Christmas — no faith line crossed yet.
  - **Later Christmases (after 20) — post-conversion.** Once he is Muslim, the same homecoming gains its quiet grace: a Muslim son returning not for the Mass but for his mother, welcomed exactly the same. *(See `../Patti Anne and Aleksander` → "The Empty Nest — and Christmas.")*

#### Chronology & ages (framework)

Fifty-eight children is only plausible with **heavy overlap between mothers and frequent multiples** — which suits this family (the Keys run on twins). Rather than fix 58 birthdates, the saga uses a **structural** chronology: when each woman joined the household, her childbearing window, and where the multiples fall. Anchor: Palmer Peter converts at **20**, marries/partners from his **early-to-mid 20s**, and the household grows across roughly **two decades**.

**Order the women enter Palmer Peter's life (proposed):**

1. **Addyson — first, and the long spine of the family (14).** The earliest and longest relationship; her fourteen span almost the whole two decades. **Multiples are essential here** — e.g. several sets of twins (and at least one set of triplets) across her childbearing years, which is how fourteen is reachable. Her eldest is the eldest of *all* Palmer Peter's children; her youngest among the last.
2. **Dakota — early, brief childbearing (1).** Joins early; has one child (Babylon Poppy) and no more — a deliberately small motherhood inside a huge household.
3. **Madison — early-to-mid (2).** Two children, possibly twins.
4. **Kourtney — mid (4).** Four children across the middle years.
5. **Catrin & Rhian — the identical twins, joined together, parallel pregnancies (10 each).** They enter around the same time and have children **in parallel** — their pregnancies often overlapping, ten apiece, with **multiples on both sides** (a hallmark: twins bearing twins). ### The mansion — a compound of individual houses around one shared living room (CANON)

Palmer Peter **builds a mansion for his nine wives and partners** — and its architecture is the whole philosophy of his household in one floor plan:

- **Each woman has her own house.** Nine individual residences — private, separate, complete dwellings — so that every wife and partner has her own home, her own space, her own door. This directly answers the Islamic principle of **equitable treatment** (each wife provided for equally and independently) and gives every woman dignity and autonomy rather than rooms in a shared house.
- **One shared living room in the middle.** At the center of the individual houses is a **single communal living room** that they all open onto / share — the common heart of the compound. The design is radial: private houses arranged *around* a shared center.
- **The meaning of the layout (the saga's read):** *separate homes, one shared heart.* It is, architecturally, exactly what Palmer Peter is building emotionally — many private lives held together by a common center. And it is a deliberate echo of the family he came from: **the Keys are a sprawling, many-branched family whose center was always one gathering place** (Mike and Terri Anne's mansion, the cottage, the family Christmas). Palmer Peter — the secure child who never doubted he belonged — **rebuilds that exact shape on different ground and by different rules:** many separate households radiating from one common room, the way the eight Key sisters radiate from one set of parents. He didn't reject the Key model of family; he reproduced its *structure* in a new form.
