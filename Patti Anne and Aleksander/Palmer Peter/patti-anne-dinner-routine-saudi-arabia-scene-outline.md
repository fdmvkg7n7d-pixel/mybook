# Patti Anne and the Dinner Routine in Saudi Arabia — Scene Outline

## Placement

**When:** The first full evening of Patti Anne and Aleksander's visit to Saudi Arabia.
**Where:** The main dining area of Palmer Peter's compound. 
**Context:** Patti Anne has survived the initial shock of the plural household, met Addyson and the seven children in the morning, and had the "school question" conversation with Palmer Peter. Now comes the first major communal meal—a logistical and emotional set piece involving five women, one man, seven children, and two deeply uncomfortable but polite grandparents.

---

## Characters

- **Patti Anne Key:** Holding the line. She is trying to act like a normal grandmother at a normal family dinner, while sitting at a table with her son's five wives/partners. 
- **Aleksander:** Quietly observant. He watches how the women interact with each other and with Palmer Peter.
- **Palmer Peter:** The patriarch. He sits at the head of the table. He is hyper-aware of his parents' hidden judgment, but he must still manage the sheer scale of his household. 
- **Addyson:** The veteran and mother of the seven children. Her youngest three are triplets, and she is still nursing them. She orchestrates the meal while physically managing her massive maternal load.
- **Aurora, Theadora, Dakota:** The three pregnant wives. They navigate the physical and social space of the dinner while pregnant.
- **Madison:** The direct one. She is not pregnant and has no children, but she is sharp and observant. She watches Patti Anne watching them.
- **The Seven Children:** Adding noise, chaos, and the undeniable reality of family to the room.

---

## Scene Outline

### 1. The Call to Dinner
- The evening call to prayer (*Maghrib*) has passed. The compound transitions into dinner mode.
- Patti Anne and Aleksander are led from the living room into the massive communal dining space. It is a room built for a tribe, not a nuclear family. The table is incredibly long. 
- The logistics of setting the table are handled effortlessly by the five women. It is a practiced choreography. Addyson directs the older children to help. 

### 2. The Seating Arrangement
- Palmer Peter takes the head of the table. He seats his mother to his immediate right and his father to his immediate left—the positions of utmost honor.
- The women take their seats. There is an unspoken but obvious hierarchy in how they sit. Addyson sits near the children to manage them. The three pregnant wives (Aurora, Theadora, Dakota) sit where they are most comfortable. Madison sits where she can observe.
- Patti Anne looks down the table. It looks like a banquet, but it is just a Tuesday night dinner for her son. 

### 3. The Meal and The Choreography
- The food is served family-style. Large platters of traditional Saudi food (Kabsa, grilled meats, flatbreads) mixed perhaps with a single Canadian dish Aurora or Addyson tried to make in honor of the guests.
- The dinner is loud. Seven children ensure that. Patti Anne focuses on this noise—she asks the children to pass the bread, she asks them about their day. She uses the grandchildren as a shield against the overwhelming presence of the five women.
- Aleksander watches the women. He notices that they don't compete for Palmer Peter's attention. The system is too established for that. Instead, they support Addyson. Addyson is nursing one (or two) of the triplets at the table; Dakota and Madison seamlessly cut her food and pass dishes to her so she can eat. They operate as a single organism. 

### 4. The Hidden Judgment and The Conversation
- Palmer Peter tries to steer the conversation to safe territory: the weather in Canada, Salena Sarai's ballet in Perth, Gamaliel's academy in Prague.
- The women participate politely. Madison asks a direct, intelligent question about Perth. Theadora asks about the flight. 
- Internally, Patti Anne is struggling. She watches Palmer Peter speak to Dakota, then turn and speak to Aurora. She watches him be a husband to five women simultaneously. It violates every Catholic sacrament she holds dear. She grips her fork tightly beneath the table.
- But externally? She smiles. She answers Madison. She compliments the food. The "Hostage Dynamic" is in full effect—she will not break the peace, because she wants to hold the toddler sitting next to Addyson again tomorrow.

### 5. The Moment of Grace
- Toward the end of the meal, one of the older toddlers (or one of the triplets) spills a large cup of water or juice across the table, right toward Patti Anne.
- Before Palmer Peter can react, Dakota (who is pregnant) and Madison move instantly. Dakota scoops the child up to keep them from crying, while Madison swiftly throws towels over the spill, protecting Patti Anne's lap. Addyson calmly replaces the cup. It happens in three seconds. 
- Patti Anne watches them work together. It is impossible to deny that, whatever she thinks of the structure, these women support each other. They are a team.
- "Thank you, Madison. Thank you, Dakota," Patti Anne says. Her voice softens. The ideological wall cracks just a fraction in the face of simple, domestic care. 

### 6. The Evening Clears
- Dinner ends. The children are excused. 
- Palmer Peter stands up. He thanks the women for the meal. He looks at his mother. "A quiet house tonight, Mum. You should rest."
- Patti Anne agrees. She and Aleksander stand to retreat to the guest house.
- As she leaves the dining room, she looks back. The five women are clearing the massive table together. Five lives, tied to her son, clearing plates. She doesn't understand it, but she can no longer pretend it isn't real.

## Tone & Style Notes

- **The Scale of the Meal:** Emphasize the sheer size of everything—the table, the amount of food, the noise level. This isn't a family dinner; it's the feeding of a small village.
- **The Internal/External Split:** Continue the theme of the "Hidden Judgment." Patti Anne is playing a role. She is the gracious Canadian grandmother on the outside, and a deeply conflicted Catholic on the inside. 
- **The Women's Solidarity:** The spill is the crucial beat. It shows Patti Anne that the women aren't rivals fighting over Palmer Peter; they are a cooperative unit. This challenges Patti Anne's preconceived notions of plural marriage.
- **Aleksander's Silence:** Aleksander speaks very little, but his gaze should track the efficiency of the women. He respects competence, and he is forced to admit that this household is highly competent.

---

## What This Scene Establishes

1. **The Reality of the Household:** It forces Patti Anne to sit at a table and visually confront the fact that her son has five women. There is no hiding from it at dinner.
2. **The Women's Dynamic:** It establishes how Addyson, Aurora, Theadora, Dakota, and Madison interact with each other—not as competitors, but as a well-oiled machine.
3. **The Power of the Grandchildren:** The children act as the buffer. As long as the children are making noise, Patti Anne doesn't have to address the theological elephant in the room. 
4. **A Small Concession:** The moment with the spilled water forces Patti Anne to genuinely thank and appreciate the women, softening her rigid internal judgment just slightly.
