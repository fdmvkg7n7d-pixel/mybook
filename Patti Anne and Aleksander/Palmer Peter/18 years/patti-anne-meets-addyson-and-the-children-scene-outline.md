# Patti Anne Meets Addyson and the Grandchildren — Scene Outline

## Placement

**When:** The morning after Patti Anne and Aleksander arrive in Saudi Arabia. 
**Where:** The central living area/kitchen of the compound.
**Context:** Patti Anne has survived the initial shock of the "math" (the three pregnant wives: Aurora, Theadora, and Dakota). Now, she is up early—because she is always up early—and she encounters the reality of the household's actual children. At this point in the timeline, only Addyson (the longest-tenured partner) has children. She has seven. 

---

## Characters

- **Patti Anne Key:** The Canadian Catholic grandmother. She woke up in a guest house in a massive Saudi compound, missing the simple cold mornings of Ontario. But when she sees grandchildren, her grandmotherly instincts completely override her cultural discomfort.
- **Addyson:** The veteran of the household. The first woman in Palmer Peter's life. She is the mother of all seven living children currently in the compound. She is steady, highly capable, and fiercely protective of her massive brood. She holds the "long spine" of the household.
- **The Seven Children:** Addyson and Palmer Peter's children. Ranging in age from older children down to her youngest—a set of triplets whom she is still nursing. They are the physical embodiment of Palmer Peter's legacy.
- **Aleksander:** (Optional/Background) Quietly drinking coffee, watching his wife do what she does best: bridge a gap with pure love.

---

## Scene Outline

### 1. The Early Morning Quiet
- Patti Anne wakes up early. The Saudi heat hasn't fully set in yet. The compound is quiet, but not silent—a house with seven children is never completely silent.
- She leaves the guest house and walks into the main central living space, following the smell of coffee or tea.
- She expects to be the only one awake. She is wrong.

### 2. The Command Center
- Addyson is in the kitchen. She is already managing the logistics of seven children. 
- The contrast is striking: Addyson isn't frantic. She is moving with the calm, practiced efficiency of a woman who runs a small army. She is packing lunches (or preparing breakfast) for the older children who will head to their Dar al-Quran / Islamic institutes, while simultaneously nursing one of her triplets. It is an image of staggering maternal endurance.
- Patti Anne pauses in the doorway. She knows Addyson. She remembers her from earlier visits when the compound was smaller, when there were fewer wives and fewer children. Addyson is the constant. 

### 3. The Greeting and the Math (Part 2)
- Addyson sees her. She doesn't stiffen the way the newer wives did the night before. "Good morning, Patti Anne. Tea or coffee?"
- Patti Anne accepts the coffee. She sits at the large island or table.
- She looks around at the children. Seven of them. All belonging to the woman standing in front of her. 
- Last night, Patti Anne did the math of the *pregnancies* (Aurora, Theadora, Dakota). This morning, she does the math of the *children*. Palmer Peter already has seven children, all with his first partner, and three more on the way with three other women. 
- The sheer scale of what her son is building hits her again. 

### 4. Grandmother Mode
- One of the other triplets (or an older toddler) wanders over to Patti Anne, staring at the strange woman with the familiar eyes. 
- Patti Anne doesn't hesitate. She puts her coffee down and holds out her arms. "Come here, you."
- The child climbs into her lap. Patti Anne breathes in the scent of her grandchild. The internal judgment, the fear, the Catholic guilt—it all evaporates the moment a grandchild is in her arms. This is the "prize" she and Aleksander are playing the game for.
- Addyson watches this. The tension in Addyson's shoulders drops. She knows Patti Anne struggles with the plural marriage, but seeing the matriarch effortlessly love her children bridges the gap between them.

### 5. The Conversation
- Patti Anne and Addyson share a quiet, honest conversation over the morning noise.
- Patti Anne asks about the children—especially the logistics of managing the triplets, how they are doing in their Islamic schooling (the Ma'had and Dar al-Quran). 
- Addyson answers proudly. She doesn't apologize for the size of her brood or the nature of their education. She is the mother of the eldest children; her position in this house is unshakeable. 
- Patti Anne touches Addyson's hand. "You're a good mother, Addyson. Seven children is no small thing. You hold this house together."
- It is a massive concession from Patti Anne. She is acknowledging Addyson's primary status in the household, effectively validating her as the matriarch of the compound, even if she doesn't use the word "wife."

### 6. The Day Begins
- The rest of the compound begins to wake up. 
- The older children come in, dressed for their Islamic schools. Patti Anne greets them, kissing foreheads, asking them questions she only half-understands the context of, but projecting absolute love.
- Palmer Peter walks in. He stops, taking in the scene: his mother, who he feared would reject his life, sitting at the kitchen table holding his youngest child while his oldest children gather around her, with Addyson calmly pouring coffee.
- He catches Addyson's eye over Patti Anne's head. Addyson gives him a single, small nod. *It's okay. She's okay.*

## Tone & Style Notes

- **The Competence of Addyson:** Addyson should be portrayed as a powerhouse. Raising seven children in a massive, plural compound requires intense logistical and emotional intelligence. She is not a victim; she is the "long spine" of the house.
- **Patti Anne's Pragmatism:** The scene highlights Patti Anne's core trait—her love for her family overrides her ideological discomfort. She might hate the framework, but she loves the people inside it.
- **The Contrast with the Night Before:** Last night was tense, formal, and overwhelmingly plural (three pregnant bellies). This morning is domestic, practical, and focused on the actual children. 

---

## What This Scene Establishes

1. **Addyson's Status:** It firmly establishes Addyson as the mother of the current generation. She has seven children; the other women are pregnant but do not yet have children.
2. **Patti Anne's Strategy:** It shows how Patti Anne navigates the visit. She zeroes in on the children. They are her anchor in an alien world.
3. **The Size of the Family:** Seeing seven children in one kitchen grounds the reality of Palmer Peter's legacy. He isn't just playing at being a patriarch; he is actively raising a small tribe.
