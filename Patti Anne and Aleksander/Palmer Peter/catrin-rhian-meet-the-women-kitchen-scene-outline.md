# CATRIN AND RHIAN MEET THE OTHER WOMEN — THE COMMON KITCHEN

## Scene Outline — Beats, Dialogue Cues, and Thematic Notes

> **Companion to** `palmer-peter-meets-catrin-rhian-scene-outline.md` **and** `palmer-peter-catrin-rhian-morning-after-scene-outline.md`. **Read both first.** This scene happens after Palmer Peter has, in five separate disclosure scenes (still to be written), told each of the five existing women about the twins. Those disclosures have happened, individually, over the preceding two-to-three weeks. **The five women have not, however, all met together about it.** The kitchen scene is the first time the seven women are in one room — and the first time the twins cross out of the guest wing into the household proper.

---

**POV:** **Catrin.** *(Same POV continuity as the previous two scenes. The reader has now spent the entire arc, since the meeting, behind Catrin's eyes. This is the scene in which that POV begins to share air with other women's POVs — Catrin watches each of the five register her; the reader, through Catrin, gets first impressions that will shape how each of the five reads on the page from here on. Future scenes can shift POV to one of the wives or partners; this one keeps Catrin's discipline of watching.)*
**Tone:** Hushed. Charged. Domestic in setting, geological in stakes. **The common kitchen is doing the work of a chapel and a courtroom and a hearth at once,** and the prose has to let the three registers stand on the same floor. Not melodramatic. Not understated either. Render the small concrete things — flour on a counter, a wooden spoon, a child somewhere down a corridor — with full attention; let the meaning of the meeting live *under* them.
**Location:** **The common kitchen** at the center of the compound — part of the central shared living room (`palmer-peter-key-profile.md` → "The mansion"), the "one shared heart" of the radial nine-house design. *Architecturally:* a large kitchen opening directly onto the shared central living room, which in turn opens onto each of the (currently five, soon to be seven) individual residences. The kitchen is the connective tissue. **It is the most important room in the compound — and the room the entire architecture's moral claim rests on.** If the "shared heart" is real, it is real here. If it is not real, it stops being real here.
**Time in saga:** **Approximately three weeks after the morning-after scene.** Palmer Peter has, in the intervening weeks, told each of the five women individually about Catrin and Rhian — in disclosure scenes still to be written. The twins have spent those three weeks in the guest wing: meals brought, the woman from the corridor as their primary household contact, no visitors, weekly calls home to their mother that have begun to find a sentence (Beat 8 of the morning-after outline — the call is still its own unwritten scene). They have not seen Palmer Peter again at length. He has come twice to ask if they need anything; both times Rhian said no for both of them.
**Ages:** Catrin and Rhian are eighteen. The five women span roughly Palmer Peter's age to a few years older [TBD per woman].

> **HOUSEHOLD STATE — REMINDER.** At this scene-time: **wives = Theadora, Dakota; partners = Addyson, Aurora, Madison.** Children already present in the compound include Addyson's eldest several, Aurora's first(s), possibly Dakota's first, possibly Madison's first. Kourtney and Margurie have not yet entered Palmer Peter's life. The two new residences for Catrin and Rhian are nearly finished but not yet occupied — the twins are still in the guest wing tonight.

## CANON ESTABLISHED BY THIS SCENE

*(The architectural claim of the compound is tested here. What this scene establishes governs every later compound scene.)*

- **Palmer Peter is not in the room for the first hour. (CANON.)** He has not arranged the meeting. He does not, at first, know it is happening. The architecture's first real test happens in his absence — the seven women find each other without him as a witness or a mediator. This is canon and is important: whatever the women decide about each other in the first hour, they decide without him.
- **At about the one-hour mark, Palmer Peter comes into the kitchen. (CANON.)** He has returned to the house from wherever he was — a meeting elsewhere in the compound, or out — and walked through the central living room toward Aurora's residence to fetch her, because **tonight is her night in the household's equitable-treatment rotation, and he intends to sleep with her.** He sees, through the kitchen opening, that the seven women are in one room. He does not turn back. He does not pretend the meeting is not happening. He does not greet anyone collectively. **He addresses Aurora alone, and tells her — does not ask her — that she is to come with him to his bedroom now.** **(CANON.)** *(Whether he came knowing the meeting was in progress and chose to assert the rotation anyway, or whether he came not knowing and pressed on out of obliviousness, is — like Catrin's "why did you say yes" — left unresolved. Both readings are true. The saga refuses to settle them. **But the choice to* tell *rather than* ask *is its own decision, and the saga must hold it as such**: tonight, in front of two new women, he has dispensed with the courtesy of the request-shape. Whether that is because the courtesy was always cosmetic — and is now being briefly removed for the twins' instruction — or because some private accumulation of the evening's news has tightened his patience, is open. **Either way: tonight, the courtesy is gone, and the women in the kitchen all register the absence.**)*
- **Aurora goes with him. (CANON.)** Not because she has chosen to in this instant — she has not been given the room to choose in this instant — but because tonight is her night, because she came to him freely *years ago* and that older freedom is what the rotation now draws on, and because refusing him in front of the twins would put them at the centre of an adult confrontation they did not ask to be at the centre of. The going is its own kind of generosity to the twins — and it is a defeat, and tonight, without the cover of the asking-shape, it is more visibly a defeat than it has ever been. The prose must hold all three at once.
- **None of the other four women intervenes. (CANON.)** Not Theadora the wife. Not Dakota the wife. Not Addyson the long spine. Not Madison in the doorway. *Their non-intervention is its own discipline* — the rotation is the rotation; objecting in front of the twins would corrupt the meaning of objection. They watch. The watching is part of what the household is.
- **The scene does not follow Palmer Peter and Aurora out of the kitchen. (CANON.)** The prose stays in the kitchen with the women who remain. What happens behind the closed bedroom door of his residence stays behind that door. The closing image will register only that the door has closed somewhere else in the compound. *Refusing to follow is the scene's most important craft discipline after the choice not to enter Palmer Peter's head.*
- **Catrin and Rhian cross the threshold from the guest wing into the household proper by their own decision. (CANON.)** Not summoned. Not led. They have not been forbidden — Palmer Peter said in the morning-after scene only that they would not leave the house *unaccompanied*; the inside of the house has not, in three weeks, been explicitly fenced — but the geography has, until now, fenced itself. They have not crossed because they have not known the way. Tonight they do.
- **It is Rhian who decides to go. (CANON.)** Continuity with the morning-after: Rhian is the one who moves first. Catrin follows.
- **The five women, by this point, all know about the twins.** Palmer Peter has told each individually. None of them has yet met them. **The five have not yet all spoken together about what has happened.** Tonight, when the twins cross into the kitchen, is also the first time all five of *them* are in one room with the subject at hand.
- **The meeting is not engineered. (CANON.)** Coincidence, in a household this small, is not really coincidence — but the prose must let the meeting *happen* rather than be staged. Two women hear footsteps that are not staff and come; a third was already in the kitchen; a fourth follows a child; the fifth comes last and most deliberately. There is no chairperson. There is no script. *That is the whole point.* The architecture has to hold a meeting it was not designed to convene.
- **Addyson speaks for no one. (CANON.)** The long spine of the household — the first relationship, the mother of his eldest children, the woman whose standing inside the household exceeds her standing on paper — could, in principle, claim the role of senior matriarch in this room. **She refuses it.** What she does instead defines who she will be to the twins from this night on, and the saga must hold that her refusal is its own kind of leadership.
- **Theadora speaks first; Dakota speaks last. (CANON.)** Of the wives, Theadora — the one with the longer marriage contract, or the more formal disposition [TBD] — opens. Dakota, the other wife, says nothing for the entire encounter and speaks only at the very end, when she rises to leave. What she says at the door is the scene's hardest line.
- **No one in the kitchen is cruel to the twins. (CANON — and harder than it sounds to write honestly.)** The cruelty was Huw's, and Palmer Peter's; the five women in this kitchen, whatever they think of the arrival of two more women, are not the agents of what was done. None of them attacks the twins. None of them mocks them. None of them refuses to address them. The saga's craft test in this scene is to render **five women with five different reactions to a household they did not consent to expand, all of which are honourable, and none of which add up to welcome.**
- **No one in the kitchen is uncomplicated to the twins, either. (CANON.)** None of the five women is *unalloyed* in her response. The line between "this is not your fault" and "this is still a problem for me" lives in every face in the room, and the prose must keep all five lines visible at the same time.
- **The twins do not leave the kitchen knowing where they stand with any of the five women. (CANON.)** They leave with first impressions and nothing settled. The scene resolves nothing. *Resolving anything would be a lie.* The kitchen is the first room of a many-roomed answer.
- **A child is in the compound while this happens. (CANON.)** At least one — most plausibly one of Addyson's middle children — wanders into the central living room during the scene, sees, registers what they see, and is gently taken away by their mother or by the staff. The child is the saga's reminder that **there are people in this house who are too young to have any frame for what is happening, and who will, eventually, ask.** The scene includes the child briefly and does not resolve the question of what the child has understood.

## BEFORE THE SCENE — WHAT CATRIN CARRIES INTO THE KITCHEN

*Three weeks have passed. Establish the shape of the time.*

- **She has stopped counting arches in the plaster.** She does not know exactly when she stopped. Sometime in the second week. She has, instead, begun to read — books the woman from the corridor brings (English-language; mostly novels; a few of which Catrin recognises as books she has been told are *good for women*, which is its own small architecture she does not yet have the energy to resent).
- **She has called her mother three times.** Each call has been seven to nine minutes. Each call has been the same sentence — *we are safe, we are together, we cannot say where we are, please do not look for us, we love you* — delivered in a voice she does not recognise as hers. Her mother has wept on every call. Her mother has not asked questions she knows would not be answered. *(The mother is, in Wales, doing the arithmetic of what her daughters are not saying. She has not yet found Palmer Peter's name. She will. The how-she-finds-it is its own future scene.)*
- **She has seen Palmer Peter twice.** Both times briefly. Both times he stood in the doorway of the guest-wing sitting room and asked whether they had what they needed. Both times Rhian said no for them — *we have what we need* — and he nodded and left. He has not pressed. He has not lingered. He has not, in three weeks, tried to manufacture intimacy. *(This is, the prose can let the reader notice, exactly consistent with the morning-after canon: he is not lying. He said he would not touch them. He has not. He said they would have what they needed. They do. The terms are being kept. The keeping does not absolve the terms.)*
- **She has begun to feel her body again.** Sleep has come back, broken but real. Hunger is not shameful anymore. The numbness of the first ten days has, in the third week, given way to something worse: *time*. There is now time in her days. Time is the medium in which she has to live this. She does not yet know what to do with time.
- **She has heard, in the corridors, voices that are not the woman from the corridor.** Children's voices, far off. A woman calling another woman's name once, in a tone that was not English — Arabic, maybe, or a name she does not know. The compound has, audibly, *people* in it. She has known this since the morning-after disclosure. Tonight is the first night she has decided to walk toward the voices.

## WHAT RHIAN CARRIES (briefly — not POV but the engine)

- **She has been mapping the house.** In the second week she began, on the back pages of a notebook the woman from the corridor brought, to draw the compound — what she could see from the guest-wing windows, what she could infer from the angles of sun in the courtyards, what she could hear from where in the corridors. She is the daughter who saw her father's books; she does this. She has not shown the map to Catrin until tonight.
- **She has, three days ago, asked the woman from the corridor a direct question.** *"Where is the central living room?"* The woman, who has so far kept the line of practical kindness exactly where she placed it on the first morning, paused — long enough that the answer was, in itself, the answer — and then named the direction. The woman did not say *you may not go there.* She did not say *you may.* She named the direction. **This is the permission Rhian decides, tonight, to read it as.**
- **She has decided to go this evening because the *adhān* for *Maghrib* will sound shortly,** and she has noticed — in the corridor sounds — that *Maghrib* is the prayer at which the household tends to fall briefly silent. She wants to enter the central space at an hour when the residents are most likely either to be praying or to have just finished. Not to ambush. To *time* the crossing.

## WHAT THE FIVE WOMEN CARRY (briefly — not POV, but each one's first appearance is now structural canon)

*Render each woman in this scene with enough first-impression detail that future scenes can build from her. This is, in effect, **five characters' first onstage moments at once.** Take the inches required.*

- **Theadora — wife.** *[TBD heritage, age, length of marriage.]* The one with the most ceremonial standing tonight. She has known about the twins for nineteen days. She has not slept well in any of them. She will speak first because she has decided, in advance, that *someone has to*, and because she is the one for whom *first* is structurally appropriate. She is composed. Her composure is the visible part of an enormous unseen labour.
- **Dakota — wife.** *[TBD.]* Younger, perhaps, or simply quieter. American, suggested by the name. She has known for fifteen days and has, in those days, said almost nothing to anyone — not to Palmer Peter, not to the other women, not even, fully, to herself. She will sit at the edge of the kitchen and not speak. When she does speak — at the door, leaving — what she says will not be addressed to the twins. It will be addressed to *no one in particular,* and it will be the line of the scene.
- **Addyson — partner, long spine.** *[TBD heritage; profile's children-names suggest Italian heritage.]* The first relationship; mother of his eldest several children; in this household the longest. The one with the most *historical* authority. She is also, in this room, **the one who is most aware that she has no contract to fall back on.** She is in the kitchen when the twins arrive. She is, in fact, the one already there — she had come to make tea before *Maghrib*; she is still here. **She does not stand up when the twins enter.** This is deliberate and important.
- **Aurora — partner.** *[TBD.]* Mother of small children, present in the compound. Of the five, she is the one whose immediate household life — meals, schedule, the use of the central living room — will be most reshaped by two more residents. She has known for seventeen days. She follows a small child into the central room and stops when she sees what is in the kitchen.
- **Madison — partner.** *[TBD.]* The quietest variable. Arrives last of the five — she has heard, from one of the others, that something is happening, and she has come because *not* coming would be a different statement than coming. She stands in the doorway of the kitchen, not across the threshold, for the entire scene. The prose should make sure the reader feels her there throughout — the woman in the doorway is the scene's silent fifth instrument.

## BEAT 1 — THE WALK

*Short. The crossing itself.*

- *Maghrib* has not yet been called. The light in the guest wing is the long gold of late afternoon. Rhian closes the notebook she has been drawing in and says, in the same flat voice she has used since the morning-after, *"Come."* Catrin does not ask where.
- They walk. The corridor they take is the one the woman from the corridor named three days ago. There are turns Catrin does not remember from the staff route; some doors she has not seen open before. The light changes — the corridors here are open to small interior courtyards, and the courtyards are green, more green than she has seen in three weeks. There is a tree she now recognises as olive. There is a smaller tree she does not.
- They pass a closed door from behind which a child is laughing. Catrin's chest closes for a beat. Rhian does not pause. *(The first child voice. The architectural fact made flesh: there are children here.)*
- They arrive at a wide arched opening. Beyond it: a high-ceilinged room, low couches, a carpet, a slow ceiling fan. On the far side, an opening into a *kitchen* — a working kitchen, professional in scale, lit warmer than the rest, smelling of cardamom and bread and something stewing.
- A woman is in the kitchen.

---

## BEAT 2 — ADDYSON, ALREADY THERE

*The first encounter is one-to-one (well — one-to-two). The whole scene's tone is set in how it begins.*

- **Addyson is at the counter.** A teapot in front of her. Loose tea in a small bowl. She is — Catrin sees as they cross the room toward the kitchen opening — measuring it by eye. She is in her mid-thirties [TBD]. She is dark-haired; her hair is loose at home. She is dressed in something Catrin cannot immediately classify — neither Saudi nor Welsh, something her own.
- **She sees them.** Her hand stops on the bowl. Her face does not change for one beat — Catrin will, later, replay this beat many times — and then her hand resumes its measuring and she finishes putting the tea in the pot.
- **She does not stand up.** She does not put down what she is doing. She does not exclaim. She does not perform surprise. She finishes the small task of putting the tea in the pot, and *then* she looks at them properly, and *then* she speaks.

**Dialogue cue (Addyson, the shape — not a script):**

> "You came at *Maghrib*. He won't be here. You should know that."

- That is the first sentence the twins hear from any of the women already in this house. **It is not a welcome. It is not a refusal. It is information** — the same register Palmer Peter used in the morning-after. The household's whole moral atmosphere is set in this one sentence: *we do not lie to each other here. We tell each other what is.*
- **Catrin watches Rhian receive this.** Rhian — who has, for three weeks, been the one who decides — for the first time in three weeks, *waits*. She lets Addyson's sentence sit before she answers.
- Rhian, eventually: *"We thought he might be."* (Half-truth. They both, in fact, planned for him not to be.)
- Addyson, mildly: *"You thought right."*
- Pause. The pause is *information*. Addyson is, in that pause, deciding *what kind of room she is going to make this be*. She has, in the nineteen days since Palmer Peter told her, thought about almost nothing else. She has not made the decision until this minute.
- **She makes it.** She lifts a third cup down from the shelf. Then a fourth. *Without asking.* Tea is going to be made. The twins are going to be offered it. This is the smallest possible act of *hosting* in a kitchen one of them already hosts in, and it is the most freighted gesture in the scene. Addyson will not stand to greet them. She will not embrace them. She will not pretend they are welcome in the sense that word usually means. But she will pour them tea, and the tea is the line she has chosen to draw between herself and the men in this story. **It is what she can do without lying.**

**Internal beat (Catrin):** *She understands, watching the third and fourth cups come down, that the woman in front of her has just done a harder thing than the woman knows. Addyson has, in front of two strangers her partner brought home against their will, refused to be either his accomplice or his judge. She has chosen the smallest available door and she has gone through it. Catrin does not yet know whether to be grateful or to be afraid of what the door costs.*

## BEAT 3 — THE OTHERS COME

*One at a time, on three different motivations. The room fills.*

### 3a — Theadora (the wife who came on purpose)

- **Theadora enters second.** She has heard footsteps that were not staff and she has come to *see.* She is the wife and she has decided that being the wife means *being in the room when the room needs a wife in it.* She crosses the central living room and stops in the kitchen opening behind Catrin and Rhian. They feel her before they hear her.
- They turn. Theadora is composed; she is dressed for the evening; she has, plainly, been preparing herself for the possibility of this encounter for nineteen days, and the preparation is visible in the small ways: the way her hands are exactly still at her sides, the way her face is exactly neutral, the way her eyes do not flick.
- **She speaks. She is the first to address them by name.**

**Dialogue cue (Theadora, the shape):**

> "Catrin. Rhian. I am Theadora. I am his wife."

- Three sentences. Each one a fact, none of them a question. She has rehearsed them. **The third sentence is the one she has rehearsed hardest.** *I am his wife.* She is saying it not to wound the twins — she is saying it to *place* herself in the room, so they know who is speaking. She has decided, in advance, that pretending the marriages do not exist would be a worse cruelty than naming them.
- Catrin — who is the people-reader — sees instantly that Theadora has not come to fight. She has come to *be visible*. To make sure that, whatever the twins decide to think of the household, they do not have to invent a wife from a rumour.
- Rhian: *"Hello."* It is the most ordinary word any of the women have said. It is also the only word she has the breath for.

### 3b — Aurora (the one who followed a child, and then came back)

- **Somewhere down the central living room, a small child appears.** Two years old, maybe three. A girl. She is barefoot. She has run out of a corridor that opens onto Aurora's residence. She does not see the kitchen yet; she sees the carpet and runs toward it because the carpet is for running on.
- **Aurora appears behind her, half a beat later.** She catches the child up — practised, one-handed, without breaking stride — and *then* sees the kitchen. *Then* sees the twins.
- Aurora is younger than Theadora [TBD], holding her child on her hip. The child looks at the twins with the open frank assessment of children who have not yet learned not to. Aurora's free hand goes to the child's back.
- Aurora does not speak immediately. **She is the one of the five whose life is most immediately reshaped by what is happening,** and she has not — in the seventeen days since she was told — figured out how she feels about it. She has figured out only how she feels about *her child being in this room with two strangers whose presence in the house means something her child should not yet have to know*. The hand on the child's back is the most honest movement in the scene.
- She nods to the twins. Once. **She does not introduce herself.** It is not a snub. She has not yet decided who she is to them, and she will not perform an introduction she has not yet earned. She crosses the central living room and disappears back down her corridor to put the child to bed.
- *(The child, over Aurora's shoulder, looks at Catrin until the corridor turns. Catrin does not look away. The child does. The child will, in some later scene, remember this.)*
- **Aurora returns to the kitchen some minutes later, alone.** The child is asleep. The household nanny is with her. Aurora has — in the corridor, in the small time of putting a child to bed — *decided* to come back. She has not yet decided what to say when she does. But she has decided that, of the five women in this house, **she will not be the one absent from the room when the room contains the new arrivals.** *(This decision will matter very much in less than an hour. She does not yet know.)*
- When she re-enters she does, this time, introduce herself. *"I'm Aurora."* That is all. She takes the cup Addyson has lifted down for her. She drinks from it.

### 3c — Madison (the one in the doorway)

- **Madison arrives.** She has heard from one of the staff, or from Aurora as Aurora passed, that *something is happening in the kitchen.* She crosses the central living room and stops in the kitchen doorway. She does not enter. She does not speak. She stands.
- She is the quietest of the five. The least *legible* to Catrin in the first impression: a woman who has decided that her presence in this scene matters but that *she* does not need to. She watches.
- Catrin will, in months to come, understand Madison as **the woman who knows everything and says almost nothing** — the household's silent witness, the one who reads but does not speak. Tonight is the first instalment of that long pattern. The prose should let the reader feel her there at the edge of every subsequent beat, not centring her, not forgetting her.

### 3d — Dakota (the wife who came last and would not sit)

- **Dakota comes last.** It is some minutes into the scene; the tea is poured; Addyson has set a small plate of dates on the counter; Theadora is sitting, finally, on a low stool at the kitchen's edge. Madison is in the doorway. The two children's voices in the corridor have moved further away. The room has, for several minutes, been a kitchen with women in it.
- Dakota appears in the doorway behind Madison. **Madison steps aside without being asked.** Dakota walks past her into the room.
- Dakota is — *[TBD; suggest a woman in her late twenties, perhaps American, perhaps Saudi-raised expatriate, the profile-line "Dakota & Madison's read English/colour-and-nature names" is a cue but not a constraint]* — and her presence changes the room's temperature. Not because she is angry. Because she has not, in fifteen days, decided what she is, and that undecidedness is visible.
- **She does not sit.** Addyson lifts a fifth cup down. Dakota does not take it.
- She looks at the twins. She looks at Theadora. She looks at the floor. She looks back at the twins. *She does not speak.*
- This is the scene's longest silence. **It is the silence in which the architectural claim of the compound is, in real time, being weighed by the woman in the best position to weigh it.** Dakota is a wife. Dakota has a contract. Dakota has, in some sense, the most to lose by accepting the twins' presence as permanent, and the most authority to say so. The other women — even Theadora — wait to see what she will do, because what she does will set the upper limit of how badly this can go.
- She does, eventually, take the cup. She does not drink from it. She holds it. She lets the silence end at her own pace.

## BEAT 4 — A FIRST TURN AROUND THE ROOM

*All five women are now present. The scene's middle is the small exchange of practical sentences that begins to make the household speakable. This is the longest stretch of the scene's hour, and the prose can take its time inside it. The hour matters because of what arrives at its end.*

- **Addyson asks the twins a simple question.** Not about Huw. Not about Wales. Not about Palmer Peter. About **the guest wing.** *"Is there anything you need that the woman has not brought?"* The question is the smallest possible offer of help and the most loaded. It implies the woman from the corridor; it implies the existence, until now, of *not-this-room*; it implies that the women of this compound now know the twins have been in the guest wing for three weeks and are willing to ask whether the wing has been enough. **It is also — under the surface — Addyson's quiet *protest at Palmer Peter on the twins' behalf*.** She is asking what *he* has failed to ask in three weeks.
- Rhian answers honestly: *"We have what we need."* (The same sentence she has said to him twice.) Then, after a beat: *"Thank you."*
- The *thank you* costs Rhian something the prose should mark. It is the first time either twin has thanked anyone in this house for anything. It is offered to Addyson, not to the room.
- **Theadora makes one offer.** Not in the form of an offer — in the form of a statement of practice. *"At Friday lunch the children eat in this room. You are welcome to."* She does not add *if you wish*. She does not add *or to stay in your wing*. She makes the fact of the household available; she does not press it.
- Catrin watches Rhian decide whether to answer this. Rhian does not. The non-answer is allowed by the room. Theadora does not press.
- **Madison, from the doorway, says one word.** Catrin will, later, not remember what it was — only that Madison spoke once and that it was a piece of small ordinary conversation (something about the tea, or the heat, or a sound from the corridor) and that it was timed precisely to break the silence at the moment the silence was about to harden. Madison is the household's pressure-release. **Note her now, for every scene to come.**
- **Aurora, now back from putting the child down, addresses Theadora about a household matter** (the children's evening meal, a question about a delivery). It is, in front of the twins, a small visible demonstration that the household has *ordinary business* — that it is not, all the time, organised around what was done three weeks ago. This is its own kind of gift to the twins. Aurora is not performing it as a gift. She is performing it as Tuesday.
- Dakota has not spoken. The cup is still in her hand. She has not drunk from it.
- **Time passes.** Twenty minutes. Forty. The conversation moves — a question about the city, a question about a book Catrin is reading, a long stretch in which Addyson and Theadora discuss without quite involving the twins (and the twins are, gratefully, allowed to listen rather than answer). The kitchen, by the end of the first hour, is *almost* a room with seven women in it the way any room with seven women in it sometimes is. The "almost" is the whole content of the next two beats.

## BEAT 5 — THE *ADHĀN*

*The call to prayer that Rhian timed the visit around finally sounds. What happens at the call is one of the scene's load-bearing gestures.*

- **The *adhān* for *Maghrib* begins.** It comes through a courtyard window and through the open arches of the central living room. The kitchen, for the duration of the call, falls into a different kind of quiet.
- **Theadora rises.** She inclines her head to the twins — not a bow; an acknowledgement — and leaves the kitchen to pray in her residence. Madison, after a beat, follows in the direction of her own.
- **Addyson does not rise.** She remains at the counter. She is *not* — the twins will, in time, learn — observant in the same form. Her path with the faith [TBD; possibly she is a convert who has retained Italian Catholic habits in private, possibly she is the household's quiet non-praying member, possibly her relationship to Islam is more complicated than the household discusses out loud] is its own thing. The scene establishes that **not all five women pray when the call comes**, and the household has, evidently, accommodated this. This is information the twins file with relief — there is, in this house, room for more than one way of being.
- **Dakota does not rise either.** She remains standing where she has been standing, the cup in her hand. She watches Theadora go. *Her not-praying tonight is, for the twins, the most unreadable thing about her: they cannot yet tell if it is her ordinary practice or tonight's protest.*
- **Aurora does not rise.** She has, the prose can quietly establish, prayed *Maghrib* with her child in her residence before coming back. *(She had decided, on the corridor walk back to the kitchen, that she would pray in advance so that she would not have to leave the room a second time. The household's rotation of prayer-times is the rotation of attention; she has, tonight, chosen to give her attention to the kitchen.)*
- **Catrin and Rhian remain seated.** No one has asked them to do anything; no one has asked them not to. They sit. They drink the tea Addyson poured them. It is the first thing they have consumed in this house that was not brought to them on a tray.
- **The call ends. The kitchen contains, now, Addyson at the counter, Aurora at the table, Dakota standing, the twins seated.** Madison and Theadora are at prayer in their own residences.

## BEAT 6 — DAKOTA AT THE DOOR

*The scene's first hard line. Render with no music.*

- The kitchen contains, after *Maghrib*, Addyson at the counter, Aurora at the table, Dakota still standing, the twins seated. Theadora and Madison are at prayer elsewhere.
- Dakota sets the cup down. Untouched. She has held it for the full length of *Maghrib* without drinking from it. **The setting-down is the gesture.** The cup placed back on the counter is the cup *not accepted*; the offer Addyson made by lifting the fifth cup down is the offer Dakota has, in the end, not taken.
- She walks to the doorway. She stops in it, her back to the kitchen, and stands for one beat.
- She does not turn. She says — to the doorway, to the central living room beyond it, to no one and to everyone:

> "I was here first."

- That is the line.
- She is not threatening anyone. She is not warning anyone. She is naming a *fact*. She is the wife with the contract; she came of her own free will; she has been here longer than the new arrivals have been alive in this room. *I was here first.* The twins are meant to hear it. Addyson is meant to hear it. Aurora — at the table behind her — is meant to hear it. The household is meant to hear it. **Dakota, somewhere in the door, is meant to hear it from her own mouth, because hearing herself say it is part of how she will be able to walk out and continue her life inside this changed compound.**
- She walks away. Her footsteps cross the central living room and turn down a corridor and are gone.
- **The twins do not understand the full weight of the line tonight.** They will, over months — and they will understand it *twice*: once tonight, in the form of *the new wife resents us*, and again in less than ten minutes, in the form of *the new wife was naming the rotation itself*. (See Beat 6b.) The prose plants the seed and leaves it.

**Internal beat (Catrin):** *She understands one thing immediately: the wife who said that sentence is the woman in this house most likely to be the one who eventually leaves. She does not know whether to hope for it or to dread it.*

## BEAT 6B — PALMER PETER IN THE DOORWAY

*The scene's second hard moment. The architectural test of Beats 2–5 has been a slow, accreting test of whether the women can build a room without him. Beat 6B is the test of what happens when he walks back into it.*

- A few minutes after Dakota's footsteps have gone. Theadora has not yet returned from her residence. Madison has not yet returned from hers. The kitchen contains Addyson at the counter, Aurora at the table, the twins seated.
- **A door at the far end of the central living room opens.** Catrin hears it before she sees it. The sound is the front door of the main house — Palmer Peter has come back. His footsteps cross the entrance hall and turn into the central living room. They are unhurried. They are not the footsteps of a man who has been told anything.
- He appears in the wide arched opening between the central living room and the kitchen.
- He does not look surprised to see them. He does not look *unsurprised*, either. **He looks at the room the way he looks at any room — exactly, briefly, as if cataloguing it.** Whether he had been told, on entering the house, that the seven women were in the kitchen, or whether he is registering it for the first time in this instant, **the prose must not decide.** Both readings are true. (Catrin's "why did you say yes" discipline.)
- He does not greet anyone collectively. He does not address the twins. He does not address Addyson. He does not address Dakota's absence — which he must, the prose can let the reader sense, register, because the kitchen contains a cup turned-down on the counter that wasn't there an hour ago.
- **He looks at Aurora.** He says, simply:

> "Aurora."

- That is the first word. Her name, by itself. A summons that does not perform summoning. She has been *named*.
- **The kitchen goes still.** Addyson's hand stops on the teapot. Catrin's body, which has been adjusting to being in a room with women, recognises in one second that the room has changed back into a room with *him* in it. Rhian, beside her, does not move at all.

### 6Ba — The telling

- Aurora looks up. She has been waiting — without knowing she was waiting — for tonight. She knows the rotation. She has known, on the corridor walk back to the kitchen, that tonight was hers. She has not, all evening, told the other women. She has not told herself in words. She has, somewhere below words, known.
- **Palmer Peter does not phrase what he wants as a request. He tells her. In front of everyone. In four words or close to it.**

**Dialogue cue (Palmer Peter, the shape):**

> "Aurora. Come. My room."

- Or close to that. Three short statements. *Aurora.* The naming. *Come.* The summons. *My room.* The destination. There is no *please*. There is no *would you*. There is no *when you have a moment*. There is no *I would like to speak with you* — the small civilised softening he has, the prose can let the reader feel, used on other nights with other women. Tonight that softening is gone.
- **The kitchen registers the absence immediately.** Addyson's hand, on the teapot, does not move for one full beat. *(Addyson knows what an ordinary fetching sounds like in this household. This is not what it sounds like.)* The twins, who have no comparative — who have only ever, this evening, heard Palmer Peter address Aurora in this one register — register only that something has happened that the other women in the room consider notable. **They learn the household's vocabulary backwards: they learn the rule (he asks) from its visible violation (tonight he did not).**
- **There is no ambiguity for the twins to parse.** The morning-after scene's discipline established that Palmer Peter does not lie; tonight it establishes a corollary — *he also does not encode when he could be plain*. **The plainness is the cruelty.** *"My room"* spoken in front of the twins, in front of Addyson, in front of the empty places where Theadora and Madison would be if they had not gone to pray, tells Catrin and Rhian everything they need to know about the rotation without a word of explanation. They will, lying on their backs in the guest wing in an hour, not need to be told what just happened. They were told, in the kitchen, in the form of *Aurora. Come. My room.*
- **Catrin understands.** She does not look at Rhian. She does not look at Aurora. She looks at the floor. She is, in this instant, learning a piece of the household's vocabulary that no one will ever explicitly teach her: *the form in which a man with a household of women summons one of them to bed.* The summons is what she will, from tonight, hear underneath every gentler version of it the household offers her in the months to come.

**Internal beat (Catrin):** *The summons is the cruellest thing he has done in front of her since the meeting* — *not because of the words, which are nearly nothing, but because of the silence around them. Five other women in this house have, over years, agreed to be addressed this way. Tonight, in front of the new arrivals, one of them is being addressed this way without the small dignity that has, evidently, on other nights been extended. **The dignity is the part being briefly removed for her and her sister's instruction.** Whether the removal is deliberate or simply the truth showing through when he has not bothered to maintain the cover, Catrin cannot tell. She files the question.*

### 6Bb — Aurora's going

- **Aurora rises.** She is composed. She has, the prose can let the reader feel, done this several times before — but never quite *this*. The rising itself is practised, but it is the practice of a slightly different motion; she has, on other nights, risen to *an asking*. Tonight she is rising to a *telling*, and the small adjustment her body makes between the two is the most legible thing about her in the room.
- **There is one beat of stillness before she rises.** Not defiance. Not hesitation. The half-second a person takes when they have been spoken to in a register they did not expect and have to choose, very quickly, whether to make the register an issue. Aurora chooses, in the half-second, that she will not — not here, not in front of the twins, not with Addyson watching, not over four words. She files what was said for a later conversation that may or may not happen. She rises.
- **She does not look at the twins.** This is its own kindness — looking at them would be making them complicit in her going. She also does not look at Addyson. She does not look at Dakota's empty doorway. She looks at the floor, then at the kitchen opening where Palmer Peter is standing, and she walks toward him.
- She does not take Palmer Peter's hand. He does not extend it. He inclines his head — a single small acknowledgement, which after the bareness of *Aurora. Come. My room.* reads almost worse than no acknowledgement would have — and steps aside to let her pass into the central living room first. He follows.
- Their footsteps cross the central living room together, and turn — not down Aurora's corridor — down **his.** The corridor to his residence. Catrin counts the steps without meaning to. Twelve. Fifteen. Then a door, somewhere in the house, **closes.**
- It is not loud. It is exactly the sound of a door closing.

**Internal beat (Catrin):** *She understands, at the sound of the door, four things at once. First: that the sentence Aurora said when she came back to the kitchen ("I'm Aurora") was, in some way she will spend a long time understanding, the bravest sentence anyone said in this room tonight, because Aurora knew what tonight was. Second: that Aurora's not refusing him in front of the twins was a kindness to the twins — and that the kindness cost Aurora something the twins were not asked to consent to her paying. Third: that Dakota's "I was here first," which Catrin had heard ten minutes earlier as the new wife's resentment of the new arrivals, was not, in fact, principally about them. It was about the rotation. It was about the door that has just closed. **Fourth, and this is the one she will carry longest: that the absence of the request-shape in Palmer Peter's four words tonight was, in some way she does not yet have language for, performed. The other women in the kitchen, on other nights, in other rooms, are asked. Tonight, in front of her and her sister, one of them was told. The asking, she understands without yet being able to say so, is the courtesy he extends to the household when there is no audience for whom the courtesy needs to be visibly absent. She and her sister are the audience tonight. The courtesy was withheld for them to see.***

### 6Bc — The four left in the kitchen

- **The kitchen contains, now: Addyson at the counter, the twins at the table, and an empty cup, and another empty cup, and the warmth of a teapot that is not, yet, cool.**
- **None of the three women speak for a long time.**
- Theadora does not return. Madison does not return. *(The prose can let the reader feel that they know. The household's listening is total. They have heard Palmer Peter come home. They have heard him ask Aurora. They have heard the door close. They are, in their residences, performing the household's other discipline — the discipline of not coming into a kitchen that has just been changed by what has happened in it. They will return, perhaps, much later, perhaps not until morning.)*
- Eventually, Addyson — without looking at the twins — lifts the teapot and refills the twins' cups. *(She does not refill her own. She did not pour herself one all evening; she has, the twins will later realise, been hosting the room without consuming it.)* The pouring is the first ordinary act after the door. The pouring is the household reasserting that the kitchen is a kitchen.

---

## BEAT 7 — ADDYSON AND THE TWINS, ALONE

*The kitchen is now three women. This is the scene's quiet centre, after the architectural test of Beats 3–6 — and after the harder test of Beat 6B, which the kitchen has just survived without quite knowing it has.*

- Addyson, at the counter, says nothing for several seconds after the pouring. She knows the twins have just had two pieces of household vocabulary delivered to them in fifteen minutes — Dakota's *I was here first*, and the door closing somewhere down Palmer Peter's corridor — and she knows that any sentence she says now will be doing the work of both translations.
- She does not translate. She does not name the rotation. She does not explain Dakota. She does not explain Aurora. She does not, the prose must let the reader feel, *narrate* what the kitchen has just been through.
- She speaks. *Low. Not soft — low.*

**Dialogue cue (Addyson, the shape):**

> "I do not know yet what I am to you. You do not know yet what I am to you. Neither of us has to decide tonight. But you should know: in this kitchen, you will not be lied to. By me, by them, or by him."

- That is what she gives them.
- It is, in light of what they have just seen, an even larger sentence than it was going to be ten minutes ago. *In this kitchen* is no longer abstract. *In this kitchen* now means: not in the corridor down which Aurora has just gone; not behind the door that has just closed; not in the rotation. *In this kitchen* — only here. The smallness of the room she is claiming is the honesty of the claim.
- Rhian, who has not cried in three weeks, does not cry now — but Catrin sees her sister's shoulders, which have been held in a specific architecture for three weeks, lower a quarter-inch. **The lowering is the only visible thing the kitchen has, tonight, given them.**
- Catrin does cry. Quietly. She does not perform it; she does not apologise for it; she lets it happen for the length of one breath and then it stops, because crying is what her body has needed to do for three weeks and Addyson's sentence is the first room in this house in which her body has, by some small accounting, been permitted to. *(This — finally — is the cry the morning-after outline noted she had been waiting for "a moment in which crying would do something." Tonight, in this kitchen, in front of this woman, was such a moment. The prose can render it in two sentences. It does not need more.)*
- Addyson does not comfort her. She does not reach across the counter. She does not say *there, there.* She lets Catrin cry the one breath, and when the breath is finished she pours her more tea.
- **One quieter thing then happens.** Rhian — who has not spoken for some time — looks at Addyson and asks the smallest possible question. Not about Aurora. Not about Dakota. Not about Palmer Peter. About **the rotation**, which she has now understood the existence of and cannot un-understand:

> "Is it always like this?"

- Addyson does not pretend not to know what the question means. She does not answer it in the form Rhian asked. She gives the only honest answer available:

> "It is what it is. Each of us came to it on our own. You did not. That is the difference, and we know it."

- That is the entirety of the household's first explicit acknowledgement to the twins of *the difference between the five and the two*. Addyson has named it. She has not promised to fix it; she has not pretended it is not there; she has not lied. **The kitchen's whole moral atmosphere is in that one sentence: *we know.***
- Rhian receives this. She does not reply.

## BEAT 8 — THE WALK BACK

*Brief. Match Beat 1 in length and register. The corridor Palmer Peter is in tonight is not on their route. The prose must not let the route appear to have been chosen — they go back the way they came in — and yet the absence of his corridor from their route is its own absence the reader registers.*

- Eventually the twins stand. They do not thank Addyson again — once was enough; twice would be too much. They incline their heads in the way the household evidently inclines heads, and they leave.
- The walk back through the corridors is different from the walk in. Some of the doors that were closed are open now; voices come from behind two of them; a man — a member of the staff, not Palmer Peter — passes them and does not look up. The compound is, after three weeks of being a place that contained them, beginning to be a place they are *in*.
- **They pass the opening to one corridor they did not pass on the way in.** They do not turn down it. They do not slow as they pass it. It is, they both know without naming it, his corridor. They walk on.
- They reach the guest wing. They close the door of their room. They sit on the edges of their two beds. They do not speak for a long time.
- **Rhian, eventually, says:** *"Five."* It is the first time either of them has said the number out loud in the three weeks since it was given to them. It is not a count of enemies. It is not a count of allies. It is a count of *people*. The household has begun to be people.
- Catrin: *"Five."*
- After another long silence, Rhian says one more thing. *"Four tonight."* — meaning, *Aurora is somewhere else tonight; the kitchen, at the end, was four of us.* It is the smallest possible acknowledgement of what happened in Beat 6B. Catrin does not answer.
- They lie down. They do not, tonight, hear the *adhān* for *Isha* when it comes.

## BEAT 9 — CLOSING IMAGE

*One short coda. The compound at the end of the hour after the door closed.*

- *Late evening. In the kitchen, Addyson has cleared the cups. She has washed Dakota's untouched one separately, by hand. She has set it upside down on a cloth to dry. She has not, in any sentence she will say aloud, made anything of the fact that she washed it separately. The decision is its own thing. It belongs to her.*
- *In the guest wing, the twins are not yet asleep but are not speaking. They lie on their backs on their two beds. The* adhān *for* Isha *is being called. They do not rise. They are not Muslim. They are not, tonight, doing arithmetic about any of it.*
- *In Aurora's residence, the child sleeps. The nanny sits in a chair by the door, awake, reading. The bed Aurora is not in tonight is made.*
- *In Dakota's residence, a wife is awake. She has not undressed. She is sitting on the edge of her bed with her hands in her lap. She has not, in fifteen days, decided what she is. The decision is one night closer to whatever it will be.*
- *In Theadora's, a wife is reading — a book she has read before, which is what she reads on nights she cannot pay attention to anything new. She is, very precisely, not listening for the sound of his door opening again.*
- *In Madison's, a woman is — as ever — quiet, and watching, and writing down nothing.*
- *And in Palmer Peter's residence, somewhere in the compound, **a door has closed and has not yet reopened**. The prose does not enter that room. The prose does not stand outside it. The prose does not even fully name it. It is the room down a corridor the twins did not turn into, and it contains two people, and the saga's whole moral architecture requires that the door stay closed to the reader for tonight.*
- *The compound holds.*
- *That it holds, tonight, is not a verdict. It is only that it holds. **Tonight more than other nights, that "only" is doing a great deal of work.***

## WHAT PALMER PETER DOES *NOT* DO

*(He is absent for most of the scene and present for the hinge of it. The negatives apply to both halves.)*

**During his absence (the first hour):**
- He has not arranged the meeting.
- He has not warned the women, this evening, that the twins might come.
- He has not warned the twins that the women might come.

**When he arrives (Beat 6B):**
- **He does not greet the room.** He does not say *hello*. He does not say *I see you have met*. He does not perform any of the small social gestures that would acknowledge the kitchen as having been *something* in his absence. He treats the kitchen-with-seven-women as a kitchen — a room he is passing through to fetch the person he came to fetch.
- **He does not address the twins.** Not by name. Not at all. The whole point of his entry, from his perspective, is that the twins are not the matter at hand tonight. *Aurora is the matter at hand tonight.* That the twins are now in the room is, to him, ambient. **The not-addressing is its own kind of cruelty, and the prose must let it sit as such** — refusing to mistake his courtesy to Aurora (not phrasing the ask in front of the twins) for courtesy to the twins themselves (who have been, in his frame, simply *also present*).
- **He does not look at Addyson.** This is its own thing. Addyson is the long spine of the household; she has, all evening, been the room's host; she is the one woman in the kitchen whose face he would most easily read. He chooses not to read it. He does not look at her once.
- **He does not ask anyone in the room for anything.** He does not ask the room for permission. He does not ask Aurora for her consent. He does not soften *Aurora. Come. My room.* into a request-shape. **The household has, on other nights, been treated to that softening; tonight the softening is gone.** Whether its absence tonight is for Aurora, for the twins, for himself, or simply because he has not bothered, the prose refuses to decide. The room is not asked; Aurora is not asked; the courtesy is not extended. He has come for her, and he is taking her, and the verb is *take.*
- **He does not offer Aurora his arm or his hand.** He does not touch her. He does not lead her by the body. The non-touch in this beat is its own architecture: she walks toward him under her own power because the alternative — being taken by the wrist in front of the twins — would be a violation of a different order, and he is not, the prose can let the reader feel, willing to commit *that* one in front of the new arrivals. **The line he draws here is the line he draws.** It is not a kindness. It is the upper limit of what he allows himself to do in front of an audience, and the saga must hold that the limit exists *and* that the limit is appalling.
- **He does not, when leaving with Aurora, look back.** Not at the kitchen. Not at the twins. Not at Addyson. He has done what he came to do; he is doing the next thing.

**After (offstage):**
- He does not, the next morning, ask any of the five women what happened in the kitchen before he arrived. He does not, the next morning, ask the twins what happened either. He does not ask Aurora how the meeting changed by his arrival. **He acts as though all of it is none of his business.** The prose must let the reader sit with this — because his absence from the conversation is *both* respect for the women's agency *and* a refusal to take responsibility for the architecture he built. Both readings are true. The saga must not settle them.

## WHAT THE FIVE WOMEN DO *NOT* DO

*(The discipline of the kitchen is largely a discipline of restraints — and Beat 6B sharpens several of them.)*

- **None of the five attacks the twins.** Not Dakota. Not Aurora. Not any of them.
- **None of the five embraces the twins.** Not Addyson. Not Theadora. Not any of them.
- **None of the five asks the twins about what happened with their father.** Not tonight. The story is theirs to tell or not tell, and the kitchen is not where it is asked for. *(That this restraint is universal across five women who have each been told a version of the story by Palmer Peter is itself a household decision — silent, prior, and shared. The prose should let the reader register that the five women have, in some sense, *agreed* on this without ever having met to agree on it.)*
- **None of the five asks the twins to make any commitment about the future.** No one asks whether they will marry him. No one asks whether they will stay. No one asks whether they will convert. *Asking would be the wrong room for the question.*
- **None of the five performs welcome.** *(See "no one in the kitchen is uncomplicated to the twins" canon.)*
- **None of the five performs grievance, either.** Dakota's *I was here first* is the closest thing in the scene to a grievance, and it is named, not performed. The kitchen permits naming. It does not permit performance.

**During Beat 6B — additional restraints specifically held in the moment of his asking Aurora:**
- **None of the four other women intervenes when Palmer Peter asks for Aurora.** Not Addyson, the long spine, who could have. Not Catrin or Rhian, the twins, who would not have been listened to and who know it. *Theadora and Madison are not in the room.* The intervention not made is the room's hardest restraint, and the prose must hold it as a discipline rather than an absence.
- **Aurora does not refuse him in front of the twins.** She does not say no. She does not say *not tonight, in front of them*. She does not look at the twins to perform a moment of solidarity at his expense. *Refusing in front of the twins would have put them at the centre of a fight that was not theirs.* The prose must let her going be readable as **both** a kindness she chose to extend to them **and** a defeat she did not choose to suffer.
- **Aurora does not look back from the doorway.** Looking back would have offered the twins a small drop of pity that none of the women in this household, at this stage, is willing to give them — because pity, in this kitchen, would be its own kind of lie.
- **Addyson does not speak after Palmer Peter leaves with Aurora until she has poured the tea.** The pouring comes first. The sentence comes second. *The order is the architecture of how this household reasserts itself after an intrusion.*

## TONE GUARDRAILS

- **No exoticism.** Same as the previous two scenes. The kitchen is a kitchen. The tea is tea. The *adhān* is the time the call sounds. None of the texture of the country causes what is happening. The architecture is the architecture; the women in it are the women in it.
- **No glamour.** Five women in a kitchen meeting two more women, in front of dates and tea, after one of them has been brought home against her will. There is no version of this room that is *elegant.* The kitchen has its own kind of beauty — the warmth, the smells, the way a third and fourth cup come down from a shelf — and the prose can hold the beauty. It must not let the beauty stand in for resolution.
- **No interiority for Palmer Peter — even when he is in the room.** Beat 6B brings him onstage for the first time since the morning-after. The reader still does not enter his head. The whole point of the beat is that the kitchen — having spent an hour assembling itself in his absence — has to absorb him without the reader being given any interior cover for what he does. The opacity established in the meeting scene and the morning-after holds.
- **(NEW for this scene) No interiority for Aurora during the going.** The prose stays in Catrin's POV. Whatever Aurora thinks as she rises, walks past Palmer Peter at the kitchen opening, and goes down his corridor with him is **not narrated**. Catrin can see what she sees — the hand that opens once and closes, the small unhurried rising, the not-looking-back — and that is all the reader gets. Aurora's own interior belongs to Aurora's own scenes, which this is not. *Rendering her interior here would be the prose colonising her body in the moment the household has already colonised it; the prose refuses.*
- **(NEW for this scene) The door stays closed.** Whatever happens in Palmer Peter's room tonight is not on the page. Not implied. Not lingered over. Not even, in the closing image, explicitly named. The door has closed and that is the entirety of what the reader is told. *The saga's whole moral architecture requires the door to stay closed to the reader tonight.* (Whether a much later scene, in a different POV, ever opens that door is a question for that later scene, not this one.)
- **First impressions, not portraits.** Each of the five women appears in this scene for the first time. The prose must give the reader enough to *recognise* each of them in future scenes — but it must not pretend to know any of them in full. Their full interiorities belong to their own scenes (the not-yet-written disclosure scenes; their later POVs).
- **No alliances declared.** No one in this scene becomes the twins' friend. No one becomes the twins' enemy. Whatever each of these women is to Catrin and Rhian, she will become over time. **The kitchen is the first night of a long becoming.**
- **The architecture is on trial; the verdict is not in.** The "separate homes, one shared heart" of the compound is, tonight, being weighed. The prose does not give the verdict. *That it can be weighed at all without breaking is the news from this kitchen.* Whether the heart holds is a question for the rest of the saga.

  - **Theadora** — wife; *[heritage TBD]*; mid-to-late twenties; in residence longest of the wives or second-longest.
  - **Dakota** — wife; *[heritage TBD; American is suggested by the name]*; mid-twenties; in residence two or three years.
  - **Addyson** — partner; *[Italian heritage suggested by the children's names in the profile]*; early-to-mid thirties; the first relationship, the longest of any of the five; mother of his eldest several.
  - **Aurora** — partner; *[heritage TBD]*; mid-twenties; mother of a small child (and possibly pregnant — see open).
  - **Madison** — partner; *[heritage TBD]*; late twenties; quietest; possibly no children yet, possibly one small.
- [ ] **What Addyson's faith looks like in the household.** Whether she is a non-praying convert, a practising one with a different schedule, or something else. Whatever it is becomes the reason she stays in the kitchen at *Maghrib* and the others go.
- [ ] **The woman from the corridor — her name, finally; her status in the household.** Is she staff, is she family, is she something between? She is, by this scene, a recurring character; she needs a name.
- [ ] **The specific child who appears in Beat 3b.** Name. Age. Whose. (Suggest one of Addyson's middle children rather than Aurora's small one, for a different texture — but Aurora coming to fetch a child of her own works structurally too. Pick what fits the household.)
- [ ] **Whether Dakota leaves the household.** The line *I was here first* is the seed. Whether it grows into a leaving (with all its consequences for the profile's eventual breakdown) or into a different kind of staying is one of the saga's largest open scenes.
