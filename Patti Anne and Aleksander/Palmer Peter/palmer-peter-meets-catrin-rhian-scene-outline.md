# PALMER PETER MEETS CATRIN AND RHIAN

## Scene Outline — Beats, Dialogue Cues, and Thematic Notes

> **CONTENT & CRAFT WARNING.** This scene depicts the **non-consensual acquisition of two adult women as settlement for their father's debt** — that is, human trafficking, perpetrated by a character the saga has previously rendered sympathetically. The scene must be written as **the moral hinge of the entire Palmer Peter arc.** It is not a clever inversion. It is not transactional cool. It is not "Saudi exoticism." It is the act that the rest of his life — and the rest of the saga's treatment of him — will be measured against. Render it with the seriousness it requires.

> **SAGA-LEVEL IMPACT.** This scene **breaks and supersedes** several lines in `palmer-peter-key-profile.md`. See "PROFILE REVISIONS REQUIRED" at the bottom of this file. Do not draft prose from this outline without first revising — or at minimum flagging — the affected sections of the profile, or the saga will contradict itself.

**POV:** **Catrin.** *(Craft note: Palmer Peter POV would glamorize him; the twins POV is the only register that lets the scene be honest about what is happening.)*
**Tone:** Held. Quiet. Catastrophic. The register of literary fiction that takes violence seriously by **slowing it down and refusing to look away** — closer to Yanagihara, late Atwood, McCarthy at his most controlled than to thriller-prose. **No music. No charm. No dancer's-eye lyricism around what Palmer Peter does.** When the scene needs to be ugly, let it be ugly.
**Location:** Palmer Peter's home in Saudi Arabia — a formal *majlis* (sitting room) used for receiving guests. **The compound is already partially built.** At this point Palmer Peter lives in **an early form of the radial architecture** the profile describes (`palmer-peter-key-profile.md` → "The mansion"): a central house with the *majlis* and the shared central living room, plus **five individual residences already in use** — one each for the two wives and three partners already in his life. *(See "The household the twins are entering," below.)* **Two further residences are under construction** in a part of the compound the twins do not see tonight. Those two doors will be Catrin's and Rhian's — and the construction was already underway *before Huw arrived in the country*. **(This is one of the scene's hardest facts. See canon list.)***
**Time in saga:** Palmer Peter is in his **early-to-mid twenties** — *post-conversion* (he took shahada at twenty; see profile). He is not yet married. **Catrin and Rhian are eighteen. (CANON.)** Newly adult; their first time out of the United Kingdom is this trip. The age gap is real and the saga must not pretend it isn't.
**Faith status of the twins:** **Not yet Muslim.** Welsh, raised culturally Christian or secular [TBD]. *Any later conversion of theirs is now permanently shadowed by this scene; the saga must contend with that, not paper over it.*

### The events of the night

- **Huw** — Welsh investor, in his late fifties or early sixties [TBD] — has lost a great deal of Palmer Peter's money in a fund he was managing. He has come to Saudi Arabia in person to ask Palmer Peter for **a further one hundred million dollars (USD)** to put into **a second investment, which Huw believes will recover the first.** It will not. Huw's judgement is gone; the man across the coffee tray can see it; Huw cannot. **(CANON.)**
- **Palmer Peter calls the marker in. (CANON.)** He refuses the new hundred million, and — in the same breath — calls in the existing debt in full, immediately, in a form Huw cannot meet. The call destroys Huw's firm and, with it, Huw.
- **Huw, in the wreckage, offers Catrin and Rhian to Palmer Peter as settlement. (CANON.)** He does this **in front of them.** He does not consult them. He has not warned them. The offer is the act of a man who has stopped being a father in any sense that matters.
- **Palmer Peter accepts. (CANON.)** Coldly. Without negotiation. Without theatre. He accepts as if he is closing a position. The acceptance is **not** a ruse, not a test, not protective theatre aimed at Huw. He takes them.
- **Catrin and Rhian leave with Palmer Peter that night. (CANON.)** They do not consent. They do not resist — there is, in the room, nothing to resist *to*; the father who would have been the resistance is the one who sold them. They leave because there is nowhere else to go and because their father's signature is on a piece of paper that says they are now Palmer Peter's.
- **Palmer Peter does not touch them sexually that night, or for some time after. (CANON.)** This is established as fact, not as virtue. The saga does **not** render the waiting as restraint, mercy, or kindness — *patient ownership is still ownership.* The prose must hold this line absolutely: he can wait, and he does wait, and the waiting is part of what he is, not a correction of it. (How long he waits, what marks the eventual change, and whether any version of consent is possible inside a household he owns — all open and serious questions for later scenes.)
- **The marker call ruins Huw. (CANON.)** His firm collapses in the following weeks. His reputation in the City of London does not recover. He is, by the end of the year, a man who lives in a small flat in [TBD — Cardiff? Swansea? somewhere that is not the life he had] and who, when his daughters' names come up, cannot meet anyone's eye. He never sees Catrin or Rhian again in the same room as freedom. **(How — and whether — they ever speak to him again is open and important.)**
- **The compound's two unfinished houses are reassigned tonight. (CANON.)** They were under construction before Huw arrived; the architecture pre-existed the victims. The saga must reckon with this every time the compound is rendered. The "separate homes, one shared heart" architecture the profile describes was *already* Palmer Peter's design — but **the sixth and seventh doors will now be Catrin's and Rhian's, and they did not choose to live behind them.** The five women already in the compound chose; these two were chosen *for*. The same architecture houses both kinds of arrival, and the prose must let that horror sit.
- **This is the moral original sin of the Palmer Peter arc** — and the saga must now reckon with the harder version of it: **he was not driven to it by deprivation, loneliness, or a single hour of opportunity.** He had two wives. He had three partners. He had children. He had a household that — by every standard the profile establishes — was already *enough.* The acquisition of Catrin and Rhian is therefore not the act of a man who needed them. It is the act of a man who *could*. The saga's question is no longer *what made him do it* — it is *what kind of man, having all that, still does this.* Every later beat — the further two women (Theadora as wife, Margurie as partner), the fifty-eight children, the Christmases home to Patti Anne, any later "conversion" of Catrin and Rhian, Salena Sarai as keeper of the thread, Gamaliel's "door already walked through," the five women already inside the compound who will learn what their husband and partner has done — must be reread through this scene.

## CATRIN'S AWARENESS — THE THREE-PHASE ARC

*The user asked for "a combination of three" — blindsided, feared, knew. Here is the shape that works structurally, applied to **both** twins across the scene's runtime:*

1. **Phase 1 — Unaware (Beats 1–3).** Both twins arrive believing they are here to support their father through a hard meeting with his creditor. They expect Huw to ask for time. They do not expect him to ask for another hundred million dollars. They have **no conception** of what he is actually about to do.
2. **Phase 2 — Fearing (Beats 4–5).** **Rhian** — the numbers one, the one who reads creditors' hands — feels the room change first. Half a beat before Huw says it, she understands that her father is not asking for time; he is asking for **more**, and the man across the table is not going to give it to him, and Huw has no other plan. She does not yet know what the *no other plan* contains. She fears it without being able to name it. **Catrin** sees Rhian's face change and does not understand what she has seen.
3. **Phase 3 — Knowing (Beats 6–7).** When Huw says the words — *take them instead* — both twins know forever. The knowledge is total and arrives in the same second. *Knowing* is then the rest of their lives.

**Why this matters for the prose:** the POV is Catrin's, so the reader gets *unaware → bewildered (Rhian's face) → knowing*. Catrin's late realisation — coming *through her sister* before it comes through her father — is the saga's first rendering of the bond that will hold the two of them together through everything that comes next: **Rhian sees it first; Catrin learns it from Rhian; they will live the rest of their lives in this order.**

## BEFORE THE SCENE — WHAT CATRIN CARRIES INTO THE ROOM

- **She is here for her father.** She does not like the man they are about to meet — she has never met him — but she has come because Huw is her father and Huw is afraid, and a daughter goes where her father is afraid.
- **She believes the meeting is about time.** Huw has told them, on the plane, that he owes "a substantial sum" to a Saudi investor — he has used the word *investor*, not *client*, not *backer*, a small dignity-preserving inversion — and that he has come to ask for an extension. She has not seen the books. Rhian has. Rhian has not told her what she saw.
- **She does not know about the second investment.** Huw has not mentioned the hundred million. She thinks they are here so he can ask for *forbearance on what he already owes*. She has prepared a face for that meeting. The face she has prepared will not survive the meeting that actually happens.
- **She does not know her father is desperate.** She knows he is anxious. *Desperate* is a register she has never seen him in, and she does not know its shape, and she will not recognise it until she sees it.
- **She has prepared one thing — herself.** Modest dress chosen with care. Some Arabic learned on the plane. A small leather notebook in her bag in case her father needs anything written down. She is **prepared to be useful**. The word will return to her, later, in a way she cannot bear.

## WHAT RHIAN CARRIES (briefly — not POV but load-bearing)

- **She has seen the books.** She knows the firm cannot survive a marker call. She knows Huw's only path is forbearance. She knows the *odds* of forbearance are not good. She does not yet know that Huw has formed a second plan.
- **She has not told Catrin.** Not to protect her — Rhian does not patronise her sister — but because Catrin reads people and Rhian reads paper, and Rhian's paper-read is not yet Catrin's to carry. She intended to tell her on the flight home.
- **She trusts her father less than Catrin does.** Not by much. By a sister's-margin. Enough that, when the room turns, Rhian turns half a beat before her sister.

## WHAT PALMER PETER CARRIES (briefly — seen from outside, throughout)

*The scene shows him only from Catrin's POV. The reader never enters his head. What the reader sees:*

- A young man — younger than they expected. *(This is its own small horror: the man who is about to do this is their age.)*
- Calm. Not warm. *Exact.* The calm of someone who has already decided most of what is about to happen and is waiting to see only the last variable.
- A host who pours coffee correctly. Who removes his guests' coats without ceremony. Who answers Huw in fluent Arabic, then in fluent English, without flourish.
- A man whose face does not change. Not when Huw asks for the hundred million. Not when Huw says the other thing. **Especially** not when Huw says the other thing.
- **A husband and partner of five women, with children already, with a household already, with two new houses being built in his own compound at the time of this meeting.** *None of which is visible in the* majlis*.* The twins do not know. Huw does not, in the moment, think of it. The reader knows — and the prose has to carry that knowledge as a low ache under every other line. The man across the coffee tray is not a lonely bachelor in the desert. He is the head of a household whose architecture he has been building for years, who has just decided, before he answered Huw, that the household has room for two more.

*The previous version of this outline described Palmer Peter as "the still one," "secure, therefore brave," "devout by temperament." All of those words are still true in this scene. **This is what those words look like when the soul behind them has decided to take** — having already, by every measure available, more than enough.*

## BEAT 1 — THE HOUSE BEFORE THEY ARRIVE

*Briefly. The reader is in Catrin's POV; she is not in the house yet. Use this beat for the **car** — the three of them in the car from the hotel, the last fifteen minutes of the world that ends in Beat 6.*

- The three of them in the back of a car in Riyadh [or Jeddah — TBD]. Catrin by the window. Huw in the middle. Rhian on the far side. Catrin notes that her father's hands are folded too still on his knees. She does not yet know what too-still looks like in their father; she only knows it doesn't look right.
- Huw says, twice, *"It will be a short meeting."* The repetition is the tell. Catrin files it without yet understanding what she has filed.
- Rhian looks out her window the whole way. She does not speak. Catrin tries to catch her eye once. Rhian does not turn.
- The car stops. The driver opens the door. The evening air is hot and smells of something Catrin cannot name. Huw steps out first. *He does not offer his daughters his hand.* He has never not offered his daughters his hand. Catrin notices, and does not yet know that she will, for the rest of her life, date the end of her father from this small absence.

## BEAT 2 — THE ARRIVAL

- The door. The shoes. Catrin removes hers carefully, places them where she has read they should go. Rhian, behind her, does the same. Huw fumbles his and does not bend to set them right; a young man — *staff*, Catrin assumes — does it for him without comment.
- Inside: low lighting, low cushions, the smell of cardamom, a tray with a tall-necked pot and small handle-less cups. A young man steps forward to greet them.
- **He is their age.** This is the first wrongness. The man their father owes — the man their father is afraid of — is twenty-something. Catrin had built, from Huw's tone in the car, the image of an older man, a heavy man, a man with the kind of presence that earns fear. Palmer Peter has none of that presence and is, somehow, more frightening for not needing it.
- He greets Huw in Arabic — *Ahlan wa sahlan, Huw.* First name. Easy. Catrin reads it, in the moment, as warmth, and is briefly, foolishly relieved. *(She will, ten minutes from now, reread it as the courtesy a man extends to a man he is about to ruin.)*
- He greets her and her sister in English. He does not address them as *ladies*. He says, *"Welcome. Please — come in."* He uses each of their names once, accurately, before he has any way of knowing which of them is which. Catrin finds this strange. She will, later, understand it: **he was watching them. He was sorting them. He sorted them in the time it took Huw to remove his shoes.**
- They sit. **Huw on the central cushion. Catrin on his left. Rhian on his right.** Catrin does not know they are being flanked until the geometry settles and her father is between them, and she sees — does not put words on, but sees — that they have made him into a man with two walls.

---

## BEAT 3 — THE COFFEE

*Short. Use it to slow the scene. The slowness is the violence's runway.*

- Palmer Peter pours four cups. Right-handed, *dallah* in the left. Huw first. Then Catrin. Then Rhian. Then himself. The order is the order of a host. Catrin watches his hands because she does not yet have anywhere else to put her attention. His hands are steady. Hers, holding the cup, are not. She notices the difference. She files it.
- Dates are offered. Huw takes one and does not eat it. Rhian declines. Catrin takes one and eats it because *refusing twice would be rude* and she has come prepared to be useful.
- A minute of small conversation — the flight, the heat, the city. Palmer Peter speaks the way he poured: deliberately, with no excess. He does not ask after their hotel. He does not ask whether they have been to Saudi Arabia before. He is **not making them feel welcome**, Catrin realises; he is **making them present**. There is a difference. She files that, too.
- The conversation lapses. Palmer Peter does not fill the silence. He looks at Huw. Huw understands that the meeting has begun.

---

## BEAT 4 — THE ASK

*This is where the room begins to die. Take your time.*

- Huw sets his cup down. He puts both hands on his knees. He begins to speak and, for the first sentence, Catrin thinks she is hearing the speech she expected — the explanation, the request for time. She *wants* to be hearing it. She edits what she is hearing to match what she wants to hear.
- By the third sentence she cannot edit anymore. **Her father is not asking for time. He is asking for more.**
- The amount: **one hundred million dollars.** Huw says it cleanly, without flourish, the way a man says a number he has rehearsed in front of a mirror. He says it because saying it slowly would be worse. He explains the second investment — [TBD specifics, but the texture: a thing he has been told about by someone who is not telling him the truth; a thing he believes in because he needs to believe in something; a thing that, said out loud in a *majlis* in Riyadh to a twenty-something who has never given Huw a reason to lie to him, **sounds exactly as foolish as it is**.] Huw does not hear the foolishness as he is speaking. **Catrin does.** She hears it the way you hear a glass slip from a hand a half-second before it lands.

**Across the small space between her and Rhian, over Huw's lap, Catrin meets her sister's eye.** It is the first time their eyes have met since the hotel lobby. Rhian's face is the wrong colour. Catrin understands — without knowing what she is understanding — that her sister has seen something she has not.

- Palmer Peter listens without moving. He does not nod. He does not write. He does not interrupt. When Huw is finished, Palmer Peter does not pause. **He answers immediately.** This is the first cruelty: a pause would have meant *I am considering*. The absence of pause means *there is nothing to consider*.

**Dialogue cue (Palmer Peter — the shape):**
> "No, Huw. And I am calling the existing position in. In full. Tonight."

That is the sentence. Render it close to that. No more words than that. **Cold is the wrong word for it — *cold* implies temperature, and what is in his voice has no temperature at all.** It is the voice of a man reading aloud the time.

## BEAT 5 — THE COLLAPSE

*The interval between the call and the offer. The hardest beat to write — because the reader has to feel Huw becoming a man capable of what he is about to do, in real time, without the prose either rescuing him or rushing him.*

- **Huw does not speak for what feels to Catrin like a very long time.** Probably it is six seconds. Probably it is ten.
- His face does not register shock. His face registers **search** — the look of a man going through every drawer in a house, opening every one, finding every one empty, and going back to the first drawer to check it again.
- He looks at Palmer Peter. He looks at the tray. He looks at the door. He does not look at his daughters. *He does not look at his daughters.* This is the second tell, and Catrin sees it, and her stomach goes.
- Rhian, beside her, very slightly shakes her head. *No.* It is not a *no* to Catrin. It is a *no* to the room. To whatever is coming. To her father, whom she has just understood is going to do it.
- Catrin still does not know what *it* is. She knows only that her sister, who reads paper, has read something that has made her shake her head at the air. **In the half-second before Huw speaks again, Catrin's mind offers her every option for what he might say, and the option her mind does not offer is the option he says.**

**The thing Huw says.** Render it short. Render it terribly. He looks up at Palmer Peter — finally, for the first time since the answer landed — and says something with the structural shape of:

> "Take them instead."

Three words, or close to it. He does not look at his daughters as he says it. He cannot. **The reason he cannot is that he has just understood, in the same second the words leave his mouth, that he is going to live the rest of his life as the man who said them.**

- Catrin's coffee cup is still in her hand. She is aware of this fact with surgical clarity. She is aware of nothing else.
- Rhian, on Huw's other side, makes a sound. Not a word. A small involuntary intake — the sound a body makes when it has been struck without warning. It is the only sound any of them will make for the next several seconds.

## BEAT 6 — THE ACCEPTANCE

*This is the moral floor of the saga. Write it without flinching, without ornament, and — critically — **without giving Palmer Peter any interiority the reader can use to forgive him.** He is opaque. He is a closed door. What he does is what he does.*

- Palmer Peter does not pause. (The second time in five minutes the absence of pause is the cruelty.)
- He does not look at the daughters. He looks at Huw. He says, simply:

> "Yes."

- He says it the way a man at an auction says *yes*. He does not say *agreed*. He does not say *I accept*. He says *yes* because *yes* is the smallest word in the language that closes a deal, and he is closing a deal.
- He then — and this is the part the prose must render precisely, because it is what tells the reader what kind of man has just been revealed — **draws the conversation back to the paperwork of the first debt.** As if the second matter is already settled. As if Catrin and Rhian, sitting on either side of their father, are now an item on a closing checklist that has been moved from the *open* column to the *closed* column. He does not look at them. He does not need to. **They have been re-categorised.**
- He says, still to Huw, that **his lawyer will be at Huw's hotel in the morning** with the papers for the marker. He says that **a separate set of papers** will be drawn up to document the second matter. He uses no word that names what the second matter is. He uses the word *matter*. He says it twice. The word lands in Catrin's chest each time like a small flat stone.
- **At no point does he speak to Catrin or Rhian.** Not once. Not to acknowledge them. Not to threaten them. Not to reassure them. They are, for the duration of this beat, **objects in the room**, and the prose must let the reader feel that the *absence* of address is worse than any address could be.

**Internal beat (Catrin):** *She is waiting for someone in the room to refuse this. She is waiting for her father to take it back. She is waiting for Palmer Peter to laugh, or to look horrified, or to stand up and order them all out of his house. She is waiting for the world to right itself. She is, she will later understand, waiting for an adult — and there are no adults in this room. There is a man who has sold her, and a man who has bought her, and the man who has bought her is her own age.*

- Rhian, beside Huw, has begun to cry without sound. Catrin sees the wet on her sister's face and **cannot reach her** — Huw is between them, and Huw is no longer a person who can be reached across.

## BEAT 7 — THE LEAVING

*Brief. Functional. Render the small terrible logistics of two women being moved.*

- Palmer Peter stands. The meeting is done. He does not extend his hand to Huw. **He does not throw Huw out either.** He simply allows the staff to bring Huw's coat, and Huw — who has not yet stood — finds himself being helped up by hands that are not unkind but are not his daughters'.
- **Huw, at the door, tries to speak to Catrin.** He says her name. Once. Her name is the only word he gets out. She does not turn her head. She does not refuse to turn her head as a *gesture*; she simply cannot make her head move in the direction of his voice. *(This is, in a sense, the first thing Palmer Peter has done **to** her — though he has not touched her. He has placed her in a room from which she cannot turn toward her father. The architecture of harm is already being built around her, in the first ten seconds of her being his.)*
- Huw is taken to the car. **The car leaves without Catrin and Rhian.** This is the moment the saga must let land: *the car leaves without them.* They watch the rear lights through a window. Rhian, finally, makes it across the room and takes her sister's wrist. They stand at the window.
- Palmer Peter — who has been speaking quietly to a member of staff — turns to them. He addresses them for the first time in the scene.

**Dialogue cue (Palmer Peter, to the twins, the *shape*):**
> "You will stay here tonight. A room has been prepared. We will speak in the morning."

- He uses neither of their names. He does not say *please*. He does not say *I am sorry*. He does not say *you are safe*. **He does not say anything that would let either of them go to sleep believing this is a kind of help.** He has not lied. He will not lie. *He has bought them and he is housing them, and that is the entirety of what he is willing to put into words tonight.*
- He leaves the room. They are shown to a room — one room, two beds, a door that closes from the inside but not the outside [TBD — render the specifics of the un-freedom precisely; do not invent restraint that wasn't there, do not invent freedom that wasn't either]. **The route the staff member takes to the room is deliberately the long way round.** The corridors chosen do not pass within hearing of the five women's residences. The twins see only doors that look like service doors; they do not yet understand that two of those doors open onto courtyards onto which other women's houses open. **The household has been quietly walled off from this arrival, tonight, on Palmer Peter's instruction.** (The five women will learn tomorrow. The twins will learn tomorrow. Tonight there are only the two of them in a single room, and the rest of the household exists in another part of a building they cannot yet see the shape of.)

## BEAT 8 — THE NIGHT

*One short coda. The two of them in the room they have been given. The first hour of the rest of their lives.*

- They do not speak for a long time. Rhian sits on the edge of one bed. Catrin stands at the window, then sits on the edge of the other.
- Eventually Rhian says — quietly, and not as accusation but as fact, the way she states a number from a ledger — *"He was always going to."* Catrin understands her sister means Huw. She does not yet know whether she agrees.
- Catrin says, after another long time, *"He didn't even look at us."* She means Palmer Peter. She means it as the worst sentence she has ever said about another human being.
- They do not cry together. They will, eventually, but not tonight. Tonight they are too newly **his** to know how to do anything together that is not also for him.
- At some point in the night the *adhān* for *Fajr* sounds across the city. Catrin, who has not slept, hears it. She does not know what it is. She knows only that somewhere in this house a man she has been given to is washing, and standing, and praying — that the same hands that closed her in this room are, in this minute, raised in worship. **This is the saga's first rendering of the fact that the Palmer Peter who prays and the Palmer Peter who took them are the same man.** The saga must never let the reader forget it. The two cannot be split. They are one man, in one house, and the prayer does not absolve the room.

**Closing image:** First light, very thin, at the bottom of the window. Two women on two beds. A door that does not need to be locked because there is nowhere, in this country, that they could go. *Somewhere in the same building — they do not know it yet — five other women are also waking, in five other rooms, in five other houses around a central courtyard the twins have not seen, into a morning none of them yet knows is about to be the morning of a new arrival.*

## WHAT PALMER PETER DOES *NOT* DO

*(The previous outline's "does not" list had to be entirely rewritten. The discipline of the scene is now different — and the negatives are different, too.)*

- **He does not threaten anyone.** He does not need to.
- **He does not raise his voice.** He does not need to.
- **He does not touch either daughter.** Not in this scene. Not to lead them. Not to comfort them. *(The horror of the scene is partly that he does not need to touch them to take them.)*
- **He does not lie about what is happening.** He does not pretend it is something else. He does not call it a marriage, an arrangement, an opportunity, a gift. He calls it nothing. He just does it.
- **He does not show emotion.** The reader is given no opening to read fear, attraction, hesitation, or even satisfaction in his face. He is opaque. He stays opaque. *(This is the cruelest craft choice of the scene, and the necessary one.)*
- **He does not let Huw recant.** When Huw, at the door, says his daughter's name, Palmer Peter does not turn. He does not give Huw the chance to take it back. The transaction is closed, and a closed transaction is closed.
- **He does not, in the after-room, allow himself to be merciful in any way the prose can let the reader hold onto.** He says they will stay the night. He says they will speak in the morning. He gives them nothing else. **Any later kindness from him must be earned by the saga — never inherited from this scene.**

## TONE GUARDRAILS (REWRITTEN FOR THIS VERSION)

- **No exoticism.** This is even more important now than before. The *majlis*, the *qahwa*, the *adhān* — these are not the *cause* of what happens in this room. What happens in this room is **a man with money taking two women he has been offered.** It would happen, identically, in a Manhattan office, a London club, a Moscow dacha. The setting is Saudi Arabia because that is where Palmer Peter lives. The setting is *not the alibi for the act.* Render Riyadh / Jeddah as a real city full of real people, most of whom would, if they knew what had happened in this *majlis*, be horrified. Islam does not authorise this. Saudi law does not authorise this. *Palmer Peter authorises this, alone, in his own house, by his own hand.*
- **No glamour.** Cold-competent acquisition is the aesthetic of bad thrillers. Refuse it. Palmer Peter's calm is **not** stylish. It is the calm of a person who has just done something that should not be possible inside one human evening.
- **No interiority for Palmer Peter.** Catrin POV is total. The reader never enters his head in this scene, and ideally — for some time — not in any subsequent scene either. Earned interiority can come back later, if the saga decides it can; it must not be a gift this scene gives him.
- **No music around the violence.** The previous outline's lyricism ("two notes of the same chord," "perfume like cut grass after rain") was for a different scene. This scene has no such ornaments. The prose is plain. The plainness is the respect.
- **No false comforts.** Do not let Catrin or Rhian — or the prose around them — discover, by the end of the scene, a *thing they can hold onto*. They have not found anything to hold onto. They have a room and a door and a sister. That is what they have. The reader has to sit with that.
- **No transactional shadow** — *previous version had this as a guardrail; that guardrail is now void.* This scene **is** the transactional shadow. Every later scene with Catrin and Rhian inherits it. The saga's responsibility is now to handle that shadow with honesty: it cannot be deleted, only carried.

## PROFILE REVISIONS REQUIRED

*This scene supersedes the following passages in `palmer-peter-key-profile.md`. The profile must be revised before — or no later than alongside — the drafted prose, or the saga will contradict itself.*

1. **"The wives and partners — nine women" section.** The claim that the marriages/partnerships are *"Consensual, adult, lawful, and sincere within its context. Palmer Peter's marriages are real marriages — partnerships, households"* is **broken for Catrin and Rhian.** It must be rewritten to acknowledge that **two of his nine were not consensual at entry**, and to leave open — as a real question of the saga — whether and how anything resembling consent ever develops, and what it means if it does.

2. **"Tone guardrails (for any scene in this arc)."** The current language — *"Keep Palmer Peter's agency and happiness real. The arc's tension belongs to the family's adjustment, not to a presumption that his life is a mistake"* — is **no longer the saga's stance.** It must be rewritten. The arc's tension now belongs to the **moral weight of what he did and to whether the people who love him can keep loving him after they know.**

3. **"Born Into Certainty (The Klára Contrast)."** The reading of Palmer Peter as *"the secure child, therefore brave"* still holds — but the saga must now hold the harder reading alongside it: *the secure child, therefore unaccountable to himself.* Security as the soil that grew, in this man, the absence of any internal check on what he allowed himself to do. The Klára contrast becomes sharper and crueller: Klára was given a place at a table and spent her life trying to deserve it; Palmer Peter was given a place at a table and grew up believing the table was his to dispose of.

4. **"Patti Anne loves him anyway (CANON)."** This line **survives**, but its meaning is now devastating. *Loving him anyway* is no longer "loving a son who converted and moved abroad" — it is "loving a son who did *this* and what does that do to a Catholic mother who carries her faith." **Patti Anne's discovery scene is now one of the saga's most important unwritten scenes.**

5. **"Catrin & Rhian — identical twins from Wales, kept together by the wife/partner split."** The profile's framing — that the wife/partner split was a *clever solution* to keep two sisters together — must be reread. **It is no longer a charming workaround.** It is the architecture by which a man kept two trafficked sisters in the same compound under the same legal cover. The profile must say so. The Welshness, the names, the parallel pregnancies — all still canon, all now grief.

6. **"The mansion."** The reading of the compound as *"separate homes, one shared heart… he didn't reject the Key model of family; he reproduced its structure in a new form"* survives **only as Palmer Peter's own self-narration.** The saga must hold, alongside it, the truer reading: **the compound is the architecture of his ownership.** Nine separate doors with one shared center is what a man builds when he has acquired nine people and needs them not to leave. **Five of the eventual nine doors were already in use, with women who entered consensually, when Catrin and Rhian arrived.** The horror of the architecture is precisely that *it houses both kinds of arrival identically.* The wives and partners who came freely live behind the same door-design as the two who were brought. Whether the five who came freely can continue to live there once they know — and whether the compound's "separate homes, one shared heart" can survive what arrived in it that night — is now a permanent open question of the saga.

8. **"Chronology & ages (framework)."** The current entry-order — *Addyson first → Dakota → Madison → Kourtney → Catrin & Rhian → Aurora & Theadora → Margurie* — is **superseded** by this scene. The new canonical state at the time of the meeting: **Theadora and Dakota are wives; Addyson, Aurora, and Madison are partners** — all five already in residence. **Catrin and Rhian arrive next, non-consensually.** **Kourtney and Margurie come later.** The profile's chronology section must be revised to reflect this.

9. **"Wives vs. partners — the categorical assignments."** The current profile's settled assignment (*wives: Aurora, Addyson, Rhian, Theadora; partners: Catrin, Dakota, Madison, Margurie, Kourtney*) is **the eventual / final state, not the state at the time of this scene.** At scene-time: **wives = Theadora, Dakota; partners = Addyson, Aurora, Madison.** Between this scene and the profile's settled state, **at least three women change categories**: Theadora and Dakota cease to be wives (divorce, death, dissolution — TBD), and Aurora and Addyson become wives (formal marriage after a period as partners). The mechanism of each transition is open and important. *(This also means Rhian's eventual move from acquired-captive to legal wife is structurally one of several category-change arcs across the household — not a singular event.)*

7. **"Children — by mother."** The fifty-eight children list survives as fact. **What each child knows, eventually, about how their mother came to be in this house** is now one of the saga's largest open arcs — and applies most acutely to Catrin's ten and Rhian's ten, who, the moment they are old enough to ask, will ask.
