# Aleksander Visits Holic's Mansion — Wednesday Scene Outline

## Placement

**When:** Wednesday — the day after Patti Anne's visit to Holic's mansion. The day after she told Gamaliel, Klára, and Aleksander everything she saw.

**Where:** Holic's mansion in Prague.

**Continuity Lead-in:**
- Tuesday: Patti Anne visited Holic's mansion alone. She saw the run, the marking, the three hours, the blindfold removal, Simonka Káta's tears. She said goodbye at the ten-foot fence. She came home and told Gamaliel, Klára, and Aleksander everything.
- Tuesday night: Aleksander said "We'll always go." He said "You're not going there alone. If we go, we go together. But not tonight." He cried quietly because his wife went alone and his granddaughter is behind a ten-foot fence.
- Wednesday morning: Tonight is over. Aleksander goes.

---

## Characters

- **Aleksander**: Patti Anne's partner. Father of Palmer Peter and Salena Sarai. Step-grandfather to Simonka Káta and Klárka Pavlînka. He goes alone. He did not go yesterday — Patti Anne went alone, and that will not happen again. But today he does not bring Patti Anne, does not bring Gamaliel, does not bring Klára. He goes himself. This is deliberate. He is not here to repeat what Patti Anne did. He is here to do what Aleksander does.
- **Holic**: Owner of the mansion. The man who served Patti Anne lunch in front of the run. The man who said "He is marking his territory." The man who apologized for letting Patti Anne watch — but not for anything else. Now he receives the second visitor from this family in two days.
- **Simonka Káta / Good Girl (GG)**: Patti Anne's granddaughter. Aleksander's step-granddaughter. Yesterday she cried when she saw her grandmother. She said she was not a good granddaughter. Today she is in the kitchen eating dog food when her step-grandfather walks in.

---

## Scene Outline

### Aleksander Arrives at the Mansion

- Aleksander arrives at Holic's mansion on Wednesday morning. He comes alone. No phone call ahead. No announcement. He simply arrives at the door.
- He is dressed simply — the way he always dresses. Nothing performed. Nothing intended to intimidate through appearance. The intimidation, if it comes, will come from who he is and what he says.
- Holic opens the door — or a staff member does and fetches Holic. Either way, Holic sees the second member of this Canadian family standing on his doorstep in two days. Yesterday it was the grandmother. Today it is the grandfather.
- Holic welcomes him in. The same graciousness. The same composure. The same mansion. Holic is a man who receives visitors the same way regardless of what happened the day before. The patio has been cleared. The lunch table is gone. The run is somewhere behind the house, the same as always.

### Holic Shows Aleksander to the Kitchen

- Holic does not take Aleksander to the patio. He does not take him to the run. He leads him through the mansion to the kitchen.
- The kitchen is large, well-appointed — the kind of kitchen that belongs in a mansion with staff. But what Aleksander sees when he walks through the doorway stops him.
- Simonka Káta — Good Girl — is on the floor of the kitchen. She is eating dog food. From a bowl. On the floor. The way a pet eats. The way a dog eats.
- Aleksander stands in the doorway. Holic stands beside him, or slightly behind — the gracious host, showing his guest to the room, the way he showed Patti Anne to the patio yesterday. The same gesture. The same calm. *This is my house. This is how we live here. Let me show you.*
- Aleksander looks at his step-granddaughter on the kitchen floor. This is not the run. This is not the blindfold, the earplugs, the three male pets. This is breakfast. This is a Tuesday morning — no, Wednesday morning — and a twenty-three-year-old woman is eating dog food from a bowl on the floor of a kitchen, and the man who owns her brought her step-grandfather to see it.

### Aleksander's First Response — Calm and Deliberate

- Aleksander does not react the way Patti Anne reacted. There is no tear on his cheek. There is no "what the fuck." He has had twenty-four hours that Patti Anne did not have. He has heard everything. He has processed the rage, the grief, the helplessness — all of it, in the kitchen last night, with his hands over his wife's. He did not come here unprepared.
- He looks at Simonka Káta on the floor. He looks at her for a long time. His face betrays nothing.
- Then he looks at Holic. His voice is even. Measured. The kind of calm that is more unsettling than shouting.
- "Is this how she eats every morning?"
- Holic answers. Whatever he says — yes, this is the routine; this is part of the structure; GG eats as a pet eats — Aleksander listens. He does not interrupt. He nods once. He files it.

### Aleksander Addresses Simonka Káta

- Aleksander walks into the kitchen. He does not kneel. He does not crouch down to the floor. He stands — his full height — and speaks to Simonka Káta the way he would speak to her at any family table.
- "Simonka Káta."
- She looks up. Dog food on her lips. Her step-grandfather is standing over her in Holic's kitchen. Yesterday it was her grandmother at the fence. Today it is Aleksander in the kitchen. The family keeps coming.
- The embarrassment from yesterday returns — but different. Yesterday was shock. Today is something quieter. She knew, after yesterday, that they might come. She did not know it would be him. She did not know he would see her like this — not in the run, not blindfolded, not with three pets. Just eating. Just eating dog food. On the floor. And somehow this is worse, because this is not the extreme. This is the ordinary. This is Tuesday morning. This is what her life looks like when no one is watching.
- Aleksander speaks. His voice is steady, warm, and carries no judgment — the same voice that said "We'll always go" at the kitchen table last night:
- "Your grandmother told you yesterday that she loves you no matter what. I am here to tell you the same thing. I love you. This family loves you. That has not changed. It will not change."
- He pauses. Then:
- "You are eating dog food on the floor of a kitchen. And I love you. Both of those things are true right now, and only one of them is permanent."
- Simonka Káta's eyes fill. She does not cry — not the way she cried yesterday. But her chin trembles. She looks back down at the bowl.

### Aleksander Turns to Holic — The Anger

- Aleksander turns from Simonka Káta to Holic. The warmth in his voice disappears. What replaces it is cold, precise, and absolute.
- "My wife sat on your patio yesterday. You served her lunch in front of the run where our granddaughter was blindfolded with three men. You recommended the cheese."
- The words land the same way they landed at the kitchen table last night — measured, surgical, devastating.
- "She watched a man urinate on our granddaughter and you told her he was marking his territory. She stayed for three hours. She said goodbye through a ten-foot fence. And then she came home and told us everything."
- Aleksander steps closer to Holic. Not aggressive. Not threatening. Just close. Close enough that Holic cannot look away.
- "You told her it was Simonka Káta's choice. Maybe it is. I am not here to argue that today. I am here to tell you three things."
- He holds up one finger: "First. If you ever sit a member of this family in front of that run again without telling them exactly what they are about to see, I will make it my life's work to dismantle everything you have built here. Not with my hands. With lawyers, with regulators, with every authority in this country. You will lose this mansion. You will lose the club. You will lose the run and the kennel and every pet in it. I am a patient man, and I have resources, and I do not make promises I cannot keep."
- Second finger: "Second. Simonka Káta eats at a table. She eats human food. She eats with dignity. What I just saw in this kitchen is not a choice — it is a condition. And I will not pretend otherwise."
- Third finger: "Third. She is welcome home. Any day. Any hour. No questions. No shame. The door is open, the light is on, and it will stay that way until she walks through it. You will not prevent that. You will not discourage that. You will not make her feel that leaving here means losing something she cannot get back. If I learn that you have, I will come back. And next time I will not come alone."
- The kitchen is silent. Simonka Káta is still on the floor. The bowl of dog food is in front of her. Holic stands in his own kitchen, in his own mansion, receiving the second ultimatum from this family in two days.

### Holic's Response

- Holic is quiet for a moment. The graciousness has not left his face — but something behind it has shifted. Yesterday it was a grandmother with tears and profanity. Today it is a grandfather with lawyers and regulators. The temperature in the room is different.
- Holic listens to the three points. He lets Aleksander finish. He does not interrupt. And then he responds.
- On the first point — the run, the ambush — he says nothing. He neither agrees nor refuses. He lets it hang.
- On the third point — the open door, Simonka Káta welcome home — he does not challenge it. He does not say he would prevent it. He leaves it alone.
- But on the second point — the food, the floor, the dignity — Holic does not yield. Not an inch.
- His voice is calm. The same calm. Always the same calm. But this time there is no apology in it, no careful softening, no concession. This is the line where Holic does not move:
  **"She is a pet. She will continue eating this type of food off the floor without any single doubt."**
- The words are absolute. No qualification. No "for now." No "unless she decides otherwise." She is a pet. Pets eat pet food. Off the floor. This is not negotiable. This is not a condition Aleksander can change with lawyers or regulators or threats. This is what Holic's world is, and Simonka Káta is in it, and she eats off the floor.
- Aleksander hears it. The sentence lands in the kitchen like a door closing. Not slammed — placed. Locked. Holic is telling him: *You can threaten the mansion. You can threaten the club. You can promise to come back with an army. But she is a pet, and she eats like a pet, and I will not pretend otherwise for your comfort.*
- Aleksander does not flinch. He does not argue. He does not repeat his ultimatum. He looks at Holic — a long, steady look — and what passes between them is not a negotiation. It is two men who understand that they have reached the place where words stop working. Aleksander has drawn his line. Holic has drawn his. The lines do not meet.
- The kitchen is silent. Simonka Káta is still on the floor. The bowl of dog food is in front of her. Her step-grandfather and her owner have just spoken over her head about what she eats, how she eats it, and what she is. She has not been asked.

### Aleksander's Goodbye to Simonka Káta

- Aleksander turns back to Simonka Káta. She is still on the floor. She has not moved. She has not eaten since he spoke.
- He does not ask her to stand. He does not ask her to come with him. He does not ask her to explain.
- "I meant what I said. Every word. You call us when you're ready. Or don't call — just come. The door doesn't lock from our side."
- He looks at her one more time — his step-granddaughter, on the floor of a kitchen in Prague, with a bowl of dog food in front of her — and he sees two things at once: Good Girl, and Simonka Káta. The pet, and the person. He speaks to the person.
- "We love you, Simonka."
- He walks out of the kitchen. He walks through the mansion. He walks out the front door. His back is straight — the same way Patti Anne's was yesterday.
- Behind him, in the kitchen, Simonka Káta pushes the bowl away. She does not eat the rest.

### After Aleksander Leaves — Holic and GG Alone

- The front door closes. The mansion is quiet again. Two visitors in two days — a grandmother and a grandfather — and now the house belongs to Holic and GG once more.
- Simonka Káta is still in the kitchen. The bowl is pushed to the side. She has not moved from the floor.
- Holic comes back to the kitchen. He stands over her — the same way he always does after something happens, the same calm, the same presence. He does not mention Aleksander. He does not revisit the three-point ultimatum or the threat of lawyers. Aleksander's words are still in the room, but Holic moves past them the way the mansion moves past everything — by continuing.
- He tells GG that he wants to try something new with her.
- A breeding stand.
- The kind of equipment farmers use to hold female animals in position for breeding. A frame — metal or wood — that restrains the animal so she cannot move during the process. Legs held, body braced, head fixed or free depending on the design. It is functional, agricultural, stripped of any pretense. It is not a piece of furniture. It is not a prop. It is a tool designed for one purpose: to hold an animal still while another animal mounts her.
- Holic tells her that another owner is bringing a pet over. This is another test.
- The breeding stand. A new pet. A test. The morning after her step-grandfather stood in this kitchen and told her he loved her and told Holic she should eat at a table with dignity. The morning after Holic told Aleksander — without any single doubt — that she is a pet and she eats off the floor. Now the next step: she will be held in a breeding stand while a stranger's pet is brought to her.
- Simonka Káta hears it. The scene does not record her reaction — whether she nods, whether she agrees, whether something in her hesitates. The breeding stand is coming. The other owner's pet is coming. The test is coming.
- Two days ago, her grandmother said goodbye through a ten-foot fence. Yesterday, her step-grandfather said he loved her in this kitchen. Today, Holic is introducing a breeding stand.
- The family keeps coming. And the mansion keeps going.

## Tone & Style Notes

- Aleksander's visit is the counterpart to Patti Anne's — but fundamentally different. Patti Anne came unprepared and was overwhelmed by what she saw. Aleksander comes prepared, having heard everything, and is deliberate in every word. Patti Anne's power was emotional and maternal. Aleksander's power is strategic, precise, and backed by the implicit threat of institutional force.
- The dog food scene is crucial — it is not the extreme (the run, the marking, the tie), but the ordinary. Aleksander seeing GG eating dog food on the kitchen floor is devastating precisely because it is mundane. This is not a session. This is breakfast. This is what "choice" looks like on a Wednesday morning.
- Aleksander's three-point ultimatum should feel rehearsed — not spontaneous. He has been composing it since last night. Every word is chosen. The contrast with Patti Anne's raw, unscripted outburst ("What the fuck are you doing to my lovely granddaughter?") is the point.
- Aleksander's love for Simonka Káta should feel different from Patti Anne's — equally real, but expressed through action and structure rather than pure emotion. He is the man who builds frameworks. His love shows up as: *I will dismantle his world if he hurts you, and I will keep the door open until you walk through it.*
- The final image — Simonka Káta pushing the bowl away — should be ambiguous. Is it the beginning of something? Is it just a moment? The scene does not answer.

## What This Scene Establishes

1. **Aleksander as a force.** He is not a background character. He is not Patti Anne's quiet partner. He is a man with his own authority, his own love, and his own way of protecting his family.
2. **The ordinary horror.** The dog food on the kitchen floor is not the run, not the marking, not the tie. It is worse in its way — because it is breakfast. It is every day. It is the texture of Simonka Káta's daily life, not the exception.
3. **The three-point ultimatum.** Aleksander draws lines: no ambush viewings of family, Simonka Káta eats with dignity, and she is welcome home with no barriers. These are not requests. They are conditions.
4. **Holic does not move.** Patti Anne brought tears and fury — Holic apologized for the witness. Aleksander brings structure, strategy, and the promise of institutional consequences — and Holic refuses. "She is a pet. She will continue eating this type of food off the floor without any single doubt." For the first time, the family hits a wall that will not bend. Holic concedes nothing about the daily reality of GG's life. The lines do not meet.
5. **Simonka Káta pushes the bowl away.** Small. Ambiguous. But real.
6. **The family keeps coming — and the mansion keeps going.** After Aleksander leaves, Holic introduces a breeding stand and tells GG another owner is bringing a pet over for a test. The family's love pulls in one direction; the mansion's machinery pulls in the other. Simonka Káta is in the middle.
7. **The breeding stand.** A new escalation — agricultural equipment, another owner's pet, another test. The contrast with Aleksander's "she eats at a table with dignity" could not be sharper.
6. **The family keeps coming.** Tuesday: the grandmother. Wednesday: the grandfather. The message is clear — this family does not stop.
