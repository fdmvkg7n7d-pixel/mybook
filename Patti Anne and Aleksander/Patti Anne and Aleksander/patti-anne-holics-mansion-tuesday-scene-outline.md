# Patti Anne Visits Holic's Mansion to See Her Granddaughter Simonka Káta — Tuesday Scene Outline

## Characters
- **Patti Anne Key**: Gamaliel's biological mother, Klára's foster mother, and grandmother to Simonka Káta (and her older sister Klárka Pavlînka). On Tuesday she travels to Holic's mansion specifically to see her granddaughter Simonka Káta. Drawing on her history of fiercely protecting Klára from her abusive father, Patti Anne approaches the situation with a mix of maternal concern, strength, and quiet determination. She stays outwardly calm for a time but the visual breaks her composure, leading to a raw outburst.
- **Klárka Pavlînka**: Older sister of Simonka Káta (by fifteen months). She is Patti Anne's granddaughter. She was called over on Sunday to meet the Canadian visitors and cried in Patti Anne's arms. She is not the focus of the Tuesday visit.
- **Holic**: Owner of the mansion. Central figure in the club and the intense pet-play dynamics involving Simonka Káta ("Good Girl"/GG in some contexts) and others. He welcomes Patti Anne into his mansion, tells her that Simonka Káta is busy with a group of three man pets, informs her that Simonka Káta is blindfolded with plugs in her ears, shows her the run, and tells her that it is Simonka Káta's choice to become his pet.
- **Simonka Káta**: The granddaughter Patti Anne is going to see. Younger sister of Klárka Pavlînka. Deeply involved in the Holic household and the pet-play arc. She is in the run with three man pets, blindfolded and with plugs in her ears.
- **The three man pets**: Male human "pets" currently active with Simonka Káta in the run as part of the club's dynamics.

## Setting
- **Time**: Tuesday, during the one-week July visit (the intensive summer camp with ~200 campers is ongoing; camp ends in August).
- **Location**: Holic's mansion in Prague — specifically the enclosed run (the play/kennel area used for these activities).
- **Context**: After the emotional Sunday family visit (including the barbecue wish that Simonka could join) and the Monday academy tour (where they watched classes and met campers and teachers), Patti Anne has come to Holic's mansion to check on her granddaughter Simonka Káta. Holic has already welcomed her, told her Simonka is "busy," and given details about the blindfold and earplugs. Now he shows her the reality in the run, and responds to her outburst by claiming it is Simonka Káta's choice to become his pet.

## Scene Outline

### Arrival, Welcome, and Initial Exchange
- Patti Anne arrives at the mansion.
- Holic welcomes her in.
- He tells her Simonka Káta is busy with a group of three man pets.
- Patti Anne stays calm and composed at first: "I don't care. Simonka Káta is one of my lovely granddaughters. I can wait until she's not busy."
- Internally: She thinks what a little boy Holic must be to use her granddaughter in the degrading way.
- Holic adds that Simonka Káta is blindfolded and has plugs in her ears.

### Lunch on the Mansion Grounds — Patti Anne's Wait
- **The Invitation:** Holic, ever the gracious host, suggests they have lunch while they wait for Simonka Káta to finish. He gestures toward the rear of the mansion. Patti Anne accepts — not because she's hungry, but because she's not leaving without seeing her granddaughter.
- **The Setting:** They sit outside on a stone patio directly in front of the enclosed run where Simonka Káta is busy. A table under a pergola draped with climbing vines. The garden beyond is immaculate — hedgerows, flower beds, old trees. A staff member brings out a spread: cold meats, bread, salads, cheese, fruit, sparkling water, wine. It's beautiful, almost absurdly civilized — and the run is right there. Patti Anne can see it. She knows her granddaughter is inside it, blindfolded, with plugs in her ears, with three men. They are eating lunch feet away from it.
- **Holic the Host:** Holic is charming, attentive, and unhurried. He pours her water before she can reach for it. He recommends the local cheese, tells her which bread is baked on-site. He compliments her travel — "It's a long way from Canada. You must be very devoted to your family." He speaks about Prague with pride, about the mansion's history, about the gardens. Every gesture says: *I am a reasonable, cultured man. This is a civilized place.*
- **Patti Anne Observes:** Patti Anne takes it all in — the grounds, the staff, the run just steps away. She eats politely but not much. Her eyes keep drifting to the run. She can hear sounds from inside — muffled, indistinct — or maybe she imagines she can. She notices how well-maintained everything is, how smoothly the household runs, how casually Holic eats lunch in front of the place where her granddaughter is being used. She files every detail away. The grandeur doesn't impress her. It sickens her. She thinks about what kind of man sits a grandmother down to eat cheese and bread within sight of the run and acts as if this is normal.
- **Surface Conversation — Prague, the Mansion, the Garden:** Holic talks about the property — how long he's owned it, the renovations, the gardens. Patti Anne listens, asks a few polite questions. She compliments the flowers. Her voice is warm, steady, betraying nothing. She is patient. She has always been patient.
- **The Conversation Shifts — Family:** Patti Anne steers the conversation toward family. She talks about Gamaliel and Klára, about the trip, about how much the family has looked forward to seeing the girls. She mentions Klárka Pavlînka crying in her arms on Sunday. She says it simply, without accusation — just a grandmother sharing a moment. She watches Holic's face for any reaction.
- **Patti Anne Probes — Simonka Káta:** Patti Anne asks Holic how Simonka Káta is doing. Not about the "activities." About *her*. Is she eating well? Is she sleeping? Does she seem happy? Is she keeping up with her studies? The questions are gentle, maternal, entirely reasonable — but each one is a quiet probe, testing what Holic knows about her granddaughter as a person versus what he knows about her as a pet. Holic answers smoothly. He speaks about Simonka Káta with familiarity — her routines, her preferences, her temperament. Patti Anne listens carefully. She notes that he knows a great deal about her granddaughter's daily life.
- **Holic Probes Back:** Holic asks Patti Anne about her own life — about Aleksander, about the family in Canada, about how she raised Gamaliel. There is a subtle edge beneath his politeness, as if he is measuring her the same way she is measuring him. Patti Anne answers openly and without defensiveness. She talks about her children with pride and tenderness. She does not perform. She is simply who she is — a grandmother who crossed an ocean to see her granddaughter.
- **The Undercurrent:** Throughout the entire lunch, neither of them says the real thing. Patti Anne does not say: *I think what you're doing is monstrous.* Holic does not say: *Your granddaughter chose this.* It is all beneath the surface — in the pauses, in the eye contact, in the careful word choices. They are two people who understand power, sitting across from each other at a beautiful table in a beautiful garden, eating beautiful food — with the run right in front of them. Not hidden. Not across the property. Right there. Holic chose this patio deliberately. Patti Anne knows it. He is showing her that this is his world, and in his world, this is where you eat lunch.
- **Patti Anne's Internal State:** She is composed. She sips her water. She dabs her mouth with a napkin. But inside, the wait is agony. The run is right there — she could stand up and walk to it in seconds. Every minute that passes is another minute her granddaughter is blindfolded, with plugs in her ears, with three men, while her grandmother sits eating lunch and pretending to be civilized about it. The proximity makes it worse. Not knowing what's happening behind those walls makes it worse. Knowing makes it worse. The longer they sit, the more the rage builds — quiet, controlled, and absolute. She does not let it show. Not yet.
- **The Lunch Ends:** The plates are cleared. Holic stands and gestures toward the run — steps away from where they've been sitting the entire time. Patti Anne sets her napkin on the table, stands, and says simply: "Yes. I'd like to see my granddaughter now." Her voice is perfectly even.

### Holic Shows Patti Anne Inside the Run
- Holic walks her the few steps to the enclosed run — the same structure she has been staring at throughout the entire meal.
- Simonka Káta is there with the three man pets.
- She is blindfolded, plugs in her ears, visibly engaged in the group activity.
- Patti Anne sees her granddaughter in this state for the first time.
- A single tear rolls down her right cheek. She does not wipe it away.

### What Patti Anne Sees — The Male Pet Marking Simonka Káta
- As Patti Anne stands inside the run, taking in the sight of her blindfolded, ear-plugged granddaughter with the three man pets, one of the male pets moves over to Simonka Káta and urinates on her body.
- Patti Anne's breath stops. She stares. Her granddaughter — her lovely granddaughter — is being urinated on by a man, and she cannot see it happening, cannot hear what is being said around her.
- Patti Anne turns to Holic. Her face is rigid. She asks what is happening.
- Holic explains, calmly and matter-of-factly, as if describing something perfectly ordinary: "He is marking his territory."
- The words land on Patti Anne like a blow. *Marking his territory.* On her granddaughter. As if Simonka Káta is the territory. As if she belongs to him.

### The Breaking Point – Patti Anne's Outburst
- The composed, patient grandmother facade cracks completely.
- Patti Anne turns to Holic. The single tear is still on her right cheek. Her voice is no longer calm. It is raw, furious, and absolute:
  **"Holic, what the fuck are you doing to my lovely granddaughter?"**
- Before Holic can respond, she continues — voice shaking with controlled rage:
  **"You are disgusting to let my lovely granddaughter do this thing."**
- This is the moment her earlier calm ("I can wait"), the civilized lunch on the patio, the polite conversation, the patient waiting — all of it is gone. The grandmother who sat through an entire meal feet from the run, who watched Holic pour her water and recommend cheese while her granddaughter was blindfolded inside, has reached the end of what she can contain.

### Holic's Response
- Holic tells Patti Anne that it is Simonka Káta's choice to become his pet.
- This is presented calmly or matter-of-factly, framing the situation as consensual from his (and presumably Simonka's) perspective.
- The response directly contrasts with Patti Anne's view of it as degrading and non-consensual control.

### Three Hours — Patti Anne Watches and Waits
- Despite her outburst, Patti Anne does not leave. She stays. For three hours.
- She and Holic remain at the run — sometimes talking, sometimes in silence. The conversation moves through waves: tense exchanges, Holic explaining the dynamics and philosophy of the club, Patti Anne pushing back, asking questions, challenging, listening. She does not agree with any of it. But she stays because her granddaughter is in there.
- For three hours, Patti Anne watches Simonka Káta with the group of male pets. She watches everything. The blindfold stays on. The earplugs stay in. Simonka Káta does not know her grandmother is standing right there, watching.
- Patti Anne sees how the pets interact with her granddaughter. She sees the dynamics — the hierarchy, the rituals, the physical reality of it. She absorbs every detail. Her face betrays less and less as the hours pass. The rage has not faded — it has gone deeper, colder, quieter.
- Holic continues to explain, to justify, to frame. He speaks about consent, about structure, about Simonka Káta's agency. Patti Anne listens. She does not interrupt as much as before. She is gathering information now — cataloguing everything she sees and hears.
- The three hours feel like a lifetime. Patti Anne does not sit down. She does not eat. She does not look away.

### The Pets Go Into the Kennel — Simonka Káta Waits Outside
- After three hours, the session ends. The three male pets move from the run into the kennel. They file in one by one.
- Simonka Káta does not go with them. She stays outside in the run. Alone. Still blindfolded. Still with the plugs in her ears. She waits — obedient, motionless, not knowing what comes next.
- Patti Anne watches her granddaughter kneeling alone in the run, separated from the group, waiting in silence and darkness for someone to come for her. The image burns itself into Patti Anne's memory.

### Holic Removes the Blindfold and Earplugs
- Holic walks into the run. He approaches Simonka Káta calmly, the way he always does.
- He removes the blindfold first. Simonka Káta blinks — adjusting to the light.
- He removes the earplugs. Sound rushes back in.
- Simonka Káta looks up. She sees Holic. And then — standing just beyond him — she sees her grandmother.

### Simonka Káta's Reaction
- The moment she recognizes Patti Anne, Simonka Káta's expression shifts. The realization hits her — her grandmother is here. Her grandmother has been here. Her grandmother saw everything.
- A flush of embarrassment rises in her face. She looks down. She cannot meet Patti Anne's eyes. The shame is immediate and involuntary — not shame about who she is, but the raw, exposed feeling of being seen like this by someone who loves her, someone from her family, someone who remembers her as a little girl.
- She tries to hold it together. Her jaw tightens. Her hands press against the ground. But it doesn't hold.
- Simonka Káta begins to cry. Quietly at first — her shoulders trembling, her head still down. Then harder. The tears come without control. She is kneeling in the run, still marked, still exposed, and her grandmother is watching her fall apart.
- She does not try to explain. She does not try to justify. She just cries — the way a granddaughter cries when the person she loves most has seen the thing she never wanted them to see.

### Patti Anne Walks to the Fence
- Patti Anne does not hesitate. She walks toward the run's ten-foot fence. She does not run. She does not rush. She walks with the same steady, deliberate strength she has carried all day — through the lunch, through the three hours, through everything she has witnessed.
- She stops at the fence. Ten feet of chain link between her and her granddaughter. Simonka Káta is on the other side — kneeling, crying, unable to look up.
- Patti Anne wraps her hands around the fence. She looks through it at her granddaughter. Her voice is clear, steady, and full of love. No anger now. No profanity. No judgment. Just a grandmother speaking to her grandchild:
  **"Simonka Káta, I still love you no matter what you are doing here."**
- The words carry across the run. Simple. Unconditional. Final.
- Simonka Káta hears them. Her crying does not stop — but something in it changes. The shame is still there, the embarrassment is still there, but now something else is there too. Her grandmother's love. Not withdrawn. Not conditional. Not waiting for an explanation. Just there — on the other side of a ten-foot fence, as solid and unmovable as the woman standing behind it.
- Patti Anne holds the fence a moment longer. Then she speaks again — still calm, still steady, still looking through the chain link at her granddaughter:
  **"Call me any time if you would like to."**
- A pause. Patti Anne's hands release the fence. She takes one last look at Simonka Káta — her granddaughter, kneeling in the run, tears on her face, still marked, still exposed — and says softly:
  **"Good-bye, my lovely granddaughter."**
- Patti Anne turns and walks away from the fence. She does not look back. Her steps are steady. Her back is straight. She walks past Holic without a word, through the mansion, and out the front door.
- Behind her, in the run, Simonka Káta is still crying. But she heard every word.

- Minutes ago, her grandmother told her she loved her. Minutes ago, she was crying. Now she is on the floor of the kennel being taken by the man who urinated on her body, and her grandmother is walking away through Prague with her back straight, not knowing this is happening.

- Simonka Káta does not resist. She does not call out. She does not use a safeword. Whether this is because she has chosen it, or because she no longer knows the difference between choosing and surrendering, is the question the scene leaves unanswered.

### Tied Together — One Hour on the Kennel Floor

- Simonka Káta lies on the kennel floor, tied to the pet who marked her and then claimed her. Neither of them moves. The tie holds them in place — two bodies joined, waiting for the release that biology will decide, not them.

Holic observes from the doorway or from inside the kennel — watching the dynamics play out. This is what the hierarchy looks like when a pet has marked and claimed. The day has broken over her in waves — the session in the run, the marking, the blindfold coming off, her grandmother's face, the shame, the crying, the kennel floor, the tie, the hour of being guarded and claimed. Her body is shaking. Her breathing is uneven. She is not crying anymore — she is past crying. She is somewhere quieter and more fragile than tears.

### Simonka Káta Speaks — and Holic Apologizes

The Good Girl surface has settled back into place — but underneath it, something is moving. Something that is not Good Girl at all. Something that is Simonka Káta.

It is about *her grandmother.* It is about the woman who crossed an ocean, who sat through a lunch she couldn't eat, who watched for three hours, who walked to a ten-foot fence and said *I still love you.* Simonka Káta is not questioning what she does here. She is questioning what she has done to the person who loves her.

Somewhere in Prague, Patti Anne is sitting at a kitchen table in her jacket, telling Gamaliel and Klára and Aleksander what she saw. And here, in the mansion, a granddaughter and the man who owns her sit in the silence after an apology that is real and not enough.
