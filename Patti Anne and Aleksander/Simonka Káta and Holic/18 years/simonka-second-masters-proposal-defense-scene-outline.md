# SIMONKA'S SECOND MASTER'S THESIS PROPOSAL DEFENSE

## Scene Outline — Beats, Dialogue Cues, and Thematic Notes

> **Companion to** `simonka-kata-profile.md`, `holic-club-rules-and-bylaws.md`, `holic-club-monthly-meeting-scene-outline.md`, **and** `holic-asks-simonka-about-safewords-scene-outline.md`. **Read the profile and the bylaws first.** This scene depends on the canon already established about (a) Simonka's psychology studies at Charles University, (b) her secret life as GG inside the Collegium, and (c) the Collegium's absolute silence rule. The scene's whole tension is that **Simonka is proposing to do academic research into the human-pet community she is a member of, without disclosing that she is a member.**

> **CONTENT & CRAFT NOTE.** This scene is the moment Simonka's two lives — the daughter studying psychology at the oldest university in Europe, and GG inside the Collegium — are brought into the same room without anyone in the room (except her) knowing it. The prose must hold the doubleness without making it *clever*. The scene is, in its register, closer to an oral examination than a thriller. The committee deliberates; the proposal passes 3–2; Simonka walks out with two academic enemies on the record and a topic she now has to spend two years working on inside a world she is not allowed to admit she lives in. That is a great deal of moral and dramatic weight, and the prose is asked to carry it inside the small concrete grammar of an academic defense — wooden chairs, water in a glass jug, a paper agenda, the small precise courtesies of Czech university procedure.

**POV:** **Simonka.** The first scene in this thread written from her interiority at length. *(Most of the existing Simonka/Holic files are observed from outside her — Klárka's POV, Klára's POV, Gamaliel's POV, Holic's POV at the club. This is her room. The prose stays inside her head for the duration.)*
**Tone:** Formal. Procedural. Low ceilings of feeling, very high ceilings of consequence. The register is **Czech academic** — precise, slightly old-fashioned, courteous in a way that does not soften disagreement. The prose should resist the temptation to make the committee theatrical. Real master's defenses are quiet rooms. The quietness is what the scene has to be inside.
**Location:** **A small committee room at Charles University, Faculty of Arts (or Faculty of Social Sciences — TBD per current Czech departmental structure for psychology master's), Prague.** A long table; five committee chairs on one side; one chair for the candidate opposite; a water carafe; a small lectern Simonka does not use. A window onto a courtyard. The room is **not** the dramatic hall the family would imagine. It is a working room. Many committees have sat here before. Many will after.
**Time in saga:** **Simonka is twenty-three** [or twenty-four — TBD; she met Holic at twenty-three per profile, so this can be **shortly before**, **during**, **or shortly after** her first months as GG. The scene gains different weight in each placement; recommend **eight to ten months into her time as GG**, which gives the proposal real lived data to be derived from, and gives Holic's silence-rule risk maximum stakes]. The scene takes place on a weekday morning in autumn. Simonka has slept badly for the last week. The committee has had the proposal on their desks for three weeks.
**Stage:** **Second master's thesis proposal.** Her **first** was withdrawn six months ago — a study of attachment patterns in long-term foster placements, on which she had done preliminary work and which her advisor had approved without enthusiasm. She withdrew it not because it was failing but because she could not, in the end, write a proposal she did not believe was her own question. The new proposal is the question she actually wants to answer. (See "Canon" below.)

## CANON ESTABLISHED BY THIS SCENE

*(So later writing can rely on it.)*

### The first proposal (withdrawn) — for context

- **Simonka's first master's thesis proposal was on attachment patterns in long-term foster placements**, with field interviews proposed in the Czech foster system. It was submitted, accepted in principle, and worked on for some months. **She withdrew it.** **(CANON.)** Her stated reason to her advisor was that she could not get sufficient access to a clean enough cohort; her real reason — never disclosed to anyone, including the advisor — was that **the topic was her mother's life, and she was not willing to study her mother in that frame.** Withdrawing cost her six months and a portion of the department's patience. *(The family knows she changed topics. The family does not know to what.)*

### The second proposal (the subject of this defense)

- **Simonka's second master's thesis proposal is on the human-pet community — *Collegium*-style consensual adult human-pet relationships — as a site of negotiated consent, ritualised submission, and care-architecture. (CANON.)** Framed academically:
  - *Working title (TBD; render close to this):* **"Per Vincula, Cura: Negotiated Consent and Care-Architecture in Adult Human-Pet Relationships."**
    *(The motto is the Collegium's: see* `holic-club-rules-and-bylaws.md`*. **Whether the title openly uses the motto is one of the most important craft questions of the scene.** Using it is a tell only one person in the world — Holic — could read; not using it leaves the proposal slightly thinner. **Recommend it is used,** because Simonka has decided — secretly and against Holic's advice — that the motto belongs to no single community and that her use of it in scholarship hides in plain sight.)*
  - *Discipline:* Psychology, with methodological scaffolding borrowed from sociology / qualitative ethnography. Charles University psychology master's permits this with strong methodological justification.
  - *Methods:* **Anonymous semi-structured interviews with adult participants in consensual human-pet relationships**, recruited through gatekeepers in adjacent BDSM and kink communities, with full ethics-board approval, full anonymisation, opt-out at any stage, and a clear "researcher does not participate" boundary on paper. *(That the researcher does in fact participate, as GG, is the unspoken centre of the entire scene.)*
  - *Theoretical frame:* Negotiated-consent theory; care-ethics; attachment under conditions of asymmetric authority. Specifically positions the human-pet relationship as a **structured, ritualised, and care-intensive form of voluntary asymmetry**, not as pathology. This framing is what the No voters will most strongly object to.
  - *What the proposal does NOT do:* It does not name any club. It does not name the Collegium. It does not reference any individual practitioner. It does not propose participation by the researcher. It does not propose photographic or video documentation. It is, on its face, the most defensible possible version of this research.

- **Her professor/advisor thinks this could be a very excellent thesis. (CANON.)** While leaving Holic's mansion after directly observing GG's role-state, the squeeze-chute scene, the four-hour duration, the safeword/pleasing-Holic complication, the mansion tour, and Bruno in the run, he concludes that Simonka has access to an unusually rich and ethically complex field site. His support is serious but conditional: the thesis can be excellent only if the methodology and ethics are strong enough.

- **Her professor/advisor has written observation notes for the committee. (CANON.)** After showing up at Holic's property on the day GG was placed in the squeeze-chute with six pets, he wrote everything down for the committee meeting: the apparatus, the safety procedure, the safeword complication, Holic's supervisory role, and GG's function as the stillness-anchor. These notes inform his committee position.

- **Her advisor likes the new proposal. (CANON.)** *(See Beat 1.)* Her advisor — call her **Doc. Lukešová** [TBD; senior female academic, perhaps mid-fifties, the woman who supervised the withdrawal of the first proposal and the construction of the second; methodologically rigorous; not a champion of fringe topics but a champion of *Simonka*] — has read three drafts. She has pushed back on each. The third draft she approved. Her approval is real and is the reason the defense is happening. **Her support in the room is not theatrical; she does not advocate from the bench. She has done her advocacy already, in the writing.**

- **The committee is five members. (CANON.)** Advisor + four others. Composition:
  1. **Doc. Lukešová** — *Simonka's advisor.* **Vote: yes.**
  2. **Prof. Havelka** — *senior psychology, methods-focused, mid-sixties, the department's longest-serving methodologist.* **Vote: no.** *His objection is methodological: the consent of subjects who are, by the proposal's own definition, in asymmetric power relationships cannot be cleanly evidenced; the research design, however careful, cannot resolve the consent paradox at the heart of the population.* He delivers this objection at length, with respect, with citations, and without ever suggesting the topic is unworthy. **He is correct on the methodology and he knows it. He is wrong only in his assumption that the irresolvability of the paradox is a reason not to study it.** *(He will become the most consequential of the two No voters because he is right enough to keep being right for the next two years.)*
  3. **Doc. Procházková** — *clinical psychology, late forties, the department's ethics-board representative.* **Vote: no.** *Her objection is ethical-reputational: the study brings the department into proximity with a population whose practices, however consensual, will be reported in the popular press in ways the department cannot control. She is not moralising. She is administrating.* She articulates the objection in three sentences and does not push beyond them.
  4. **Doc. Bartoš** — *social psychology, early forties, the department's most recent hire and the one with the most exposure to current international research.* **Vote: yes.** *He has read recent English-language literature on kink studies, knows the field has matured, and votes in favour partly out of academic conviction and partly out of generational alignment.*
  5. **Prof. Veselá** — *committee chair; developmental psychology; late fifties; the chair because of seniority, not because of topical engagement with the proposal.* **Vote: yes.** *Her yes is the closest call in the room. She has worried at the proposal for three weeks. She votes yes because she has decided that the candidate has done the work, that the methodology — pending Havelka's objections, which she takes seriously — is salvageable, and that the department's job is to permit serious questions to be asked. **Her yes is the scene's quietest piece of integrity.** She will not say so to Simonka.*

- **The vote is 3–2 in Simonka's favour. (CANON.)** Yes: Lukešová, Bartoš, Veselá. No: Havelka, Procházková. The proposal passes. **It passes with conditions.** *(See Beat 6.)*

- **The conditions on the passage. (CANON.)** Prof. Havelka, before Veselá calls the vote, secures the committee's agreement that **any approval is conditional on three things:**
  1. **Quarterly methodology check-ins** between Simonka and Havelka (yes, *Havelka* — the No voter — is the additional methodological reader; he requests this himself, and Lukešová accepts on Simonka's behalf without consulting her). This means **the most rigorous opponent of Simonka's topic becomes its co-supervisor for two years**, with the right to demand redesigns at any quarterly review. *(This is the scene's hardest gift. Havelka has, in losing the vote, won the project's actual oversight.)*
  2. **No participant-observer element.** The boundary that the researcher will not participate must be **formally signed** by Simonka, with the ethics board, and any breach is grounds for the master's being withdrawn. **(This is the condition that, if Simonka is honest with herself, she has already broken.)**
  3. **No public presentation of preliminary findings** until the methodology has been signed off by Havelka, in writing. (The Collegium's silence rule and the university's gag are, by accident, in alignment on this point.)

- **Simonka accepts the conditions. (CANON.)** She accepts them at the moment they are offered. She does not negotiate. She does not visibly hesitate. The acceptance is its own information — to her, more than to the committee.

- **No one in the room suspects the researcher is herself a participant. (CANON.)** Not Lukešová, who has known her for three years. Not Havelka, who has read the proposal more carefully than any other committee member. Not Procházková, whose ethics board would, if it knew, reject the proposal in fifteen minutes. **The doubleness is the scene's entire weight.** Simonka watches them deliberate about her without their knowing it. The prose must hold the watching without ever letting Simonka relieve herself, in interior monologue, of the disclosure she is not making. The interior is the same as the exterior. *Watching is what she does.*

- **Holic knows about the proposal. (CANON.)** Simonka has, over months, told him about her academic work. She has, against the Collegium's silence rule's *spirit* if not its precise letter, shown him drafts of the proposal. He has read them. **He has not encouraged her. He has not discouraged her.** His non-intervention is its own information. *(Whether his non-intervention is trust, calculation, indifference, or some private theory of his own about what risk to the Collegium her research actually constitutes, is open and important. See open items.)*

- **Simonka does not call Holic from the defense day. (CANON.)** The scene closes with her not yet having told him she has passed. She does not call until evening. The walk from the committee room to wherever she goes next is **the longest walk of her academic life so far**, and she takes it alone.

## BEFORE THE SCENE — WHAT SIMONKA CARRIES INTO THE ROOM

*Establish, briefly, what the proposal costs her in fear, separate from what it costs her in pride.*

- **She has not slept seven hours together in a week.** She has run the defense in her head — questions, answers, page references, the methodological cross-examination she expects from Havelka — every night and most days. She is, in the way of academic candidates the world over, **over-prepared and consequently slightly stupid with it**: she has, by morning, memorised so much that small simple questions risk feeling like trick questions, and small simple answers like the wrong ones.
- **She is alone in this in a way she has never been alone before.** Her mother, Klára, knows she has a defense today; Klára does not know the topic. Her father, Gamaliel, knows she has a defense today; same. Her sister Klárka has been told a defense is happening and has, mercifully, not asked for details. *Holic knows the topic, has read the proposal, and is, by his own choice, not in Prague today.* He is **at the farm**, where the kennel yard is. He has, in their phone call last night, said exactly three things: that she will do well; that he will see her tomorrow; that she will know what she has to know when she has to know it. The third sentence is the one she has been repeating to herself on the tram in.
- **She is wearing a thin silver chain around her neck under her blouse.** It is not a collar; she would not be so foolish as to wear a collar to a defense at Charles University. It is a chain. It belongs to her. She bought it for herself, in a jewellery shop two streets from the faculty, the day after the third draft was approved. **Wearing it is the only piece of GG she has allowed into this room.** *(She will, in the closing image, touch it once.)*
- **The first proposal is in her bag.** She did not need to bring it. She has brought it anyway — the hard copy, marked with Lukešová's pencil annotations, withdrawn six months ago. It is in the bag at her feet under the candidate's chair. **It is in the bag as a small private superstition.** It is what she would have defended had she been a different person. She is not that person. The bag is heavy with the proof.

## WHAT THE COMMITTEE CARRIES (briefly — five short interiorities, seen by Simonka)

*The reader meets each committee member through Simonka's POV. She is the people-reader. She has, in the way of candidates, been over-reading them for weeks.*

- **Lukešová** — *yes vote, advisor.* Has read every word of the proposal six times. Is, this morning, wearing a darker jacket than usual — which Simonka reads, correctly, as her *defense day* jacket. Her face is exactly neutral. Her neutrality is the largest gift she can give her student in this room. She will not advocate from the bench; she has done the advocacy in the writing.
- **Havelka** — *no vote, methodologist.* In his mid-sixties. The proposal sits in front of him heavily annotated — in pencil, in a hand that has annotated five thousand proposals in his career and is annotating this one with the same attention as any of them. He is not Simonka's enemy. He is the proposal's most rigorous reader, and he has decided, over three weeks, that he cannot in conscience approve it. *He will say so. He will say so well.* Simonka has read enough of him to know that being told no by Havelka, *in his register*, is a kind of being taken seriously that most candidates do not get.
- **Procházková** — *no vote, ethics.* Late forties; minimal eye contact; the proposal is in front of her with three small flags on three pages. She has read it once, attentively, and will not need to read it again. Her objection is administrative and is not about Simonka. **Simonka, watching her, understands that she has been categorised — by Procházková — as a candidate who has written a competent proposal on a topic the department should not be associating itself with, and that the categorisation is the entirety of what Procházková is going to bring into the room.** It is, in its small way, more chilling than Havelka's engaged opposition.
- **Bartoš** — *yes vote, social psychology.* Early forties; the most informally dressed of the five (which is to say, his tie is one shade less serious than the others' ties). He is reading something on his phone as Simonka enters; he puts it away as the chair sits. He has — Simonka can tell, from the small notes on his copy of the proposal — read the proposal *with interest*. The interest is its own kindness. He is the only committee member whose interest she has noticed at all.
- **Veselá** — *chair; yes vote.* Late fifties; will say the least until the deliberation. She is the one Simonka has the least read on. Her face, over three weeks of imagined defense, has stayed exactly blank in Simonka's mental rehearsal. **It will stay exactly blank for the duration of the actual scene, too, until the moment she calls the vote.**

## BEAT 1 — THE CORRIDOR

*Brief. The candidate's first beat is the wait outside the room.*

- Simonka arrives at the faculty building forty minutes early. She walks the courtyard once. She does not go in. She goes to a small café two streets over and orders a black coffee she does not drink.
- She returns. She climbs the stairs to the second floor. The corridor outside the committee room is empty. There is a wooden bench. She sits on it.
- **Lukešová appears.** Her advisor walks down the corridor, sees her, stops. They have agreed in advance: no last-minute conversation. Lukešová nods. Simonka stands. Lukešová puts one hand on her arm — once, briefly — and goes into the room without speaking.
- The hand on her arm is the only physical contact Simonka will have with anyone before her body becomes the object of a vote.
- The other four committee members arrive separately over the next ten minutes. Bartoš nods to her. Havelka does not — not coldly, just absently, his mind on the proposal. Procházková does not look at her at all. Veselá smiles small, automatic, and goes in.
- Simonka is left alone in the corridor for four minutes by her watch. She does not rehearse. She has rehearsed enough. She watches a fly on the windowsill at the far end of the corridor.

## BEAT 2 — THE ROOM

*She is called in. The room is what the room is.*

- The door opens. A research secretary — not committee — says her name. Simonka stands. She walks into the room.
- The committee is seated. Veselá is in the centre. Lukešová is on Veselá's right. Havelka is on Veselá's left. Bartoš is at the far right; Procházková at the far left. The candidate's chair is opposite Veselá. There is a water carafe between them.
- Simonka sits. She does not pour herself water. She has decided — weeks ago — that she will not touch the carafe until at least one member of the committee has touched theirs; she will not be the first to need a glass of water in front of these people.
- Veselá opens. The opening is procedural and standardised — Simonka has heard the form of it from other candidates. Her name. The faculty. The degree. The proposal title. *"Per Vincula, Cura: Negotiated Consent and Care-Architecture in Adult Human-Pet Relationships."*
- The title is read aloud. Lukešová's face does not change. Havelka's face does not change. Bartoš's mouth, very slightly, moves at one corner — Simonka catches it; he has, perhaps, heard the title for the first time on a tongue and is registering its rhythm. Procházková is looking down at her copy. Veselá finishes the formula.
- Simonka is invited to speak.

## BEAT 3 — THE OPENING STATEMENT

*Render in summary; do not draft the full statement here. The point is the room receiving it.*

- Simonka stands. She has been told she may sit; she stands. *(She has rehearsed this small choice. Standing makes her marginally more vulnerable and marginally more serious. She has decided that the serious half of the calculation is worth the vulnerable half.)*
- She speaks for **twelve minutes.** She has timed it. She does not look at notes; the notes are in front of her, but she has memorised the statement to the level where the notes are insurance, not script.
- The structure of the statement, briefly:
  1. **The gap in the literature.** Adult consensual human-pet relationships have been described in popular and journalistic registers, and in adjacent BDSM-studies literature, but rarely investigated as a distinct site of *care* — as opposed to a site of *kink*. The proposal's claim is that they constitute a particular form of care-architecture worth investigating in its own terms.
  2. **The methodology.** Anonymous semi-structured interviews. Gatekeeper recruitment. Full ethics-board scaffolding. Strict no-participation boundary on the researcher's part.
  3. **The framing.** Negotiated-consent theory. Care-ethics. Attachment theory under conditions of asymmetric authority — *which*, she is careful to argue, *exists in many ordinary domains, including therapy, medicine, parenting, and pedagogy, and is not, by itself, pathological*. The proposal locates human-pet relationships within this larger family of voluntary asymmetries, rather than outside the bounds of social-scientific seriousness.
  4. **The contribution.** What the field will know, in two years, that it does not know now: *the negotiated grammar of care inside ritualised submission, in the words of its practitioners.*
- She finishes within fifteen seconds of the twelve-minute mark. She sits down. She does not touch the water. Her heart, she will later think, did not race once during the statement — it raced before, and it will race afterwards, but during, the same calm that descends on her in the kennel yard at the farm descends on her here. *(She files this fact for herself; she will not, in the next two years, tell anyone in the room about it.)*

## BEAT 4 — THE QUESTIONS

*The longest stretch of the scene. Each committee member's question reveals their vote without the vote being named.*

### 4a — Bartoš (yes, social psychology)

- He goes first. *(Convention permits any member to go first; Veselá has nodded him forward, knowing his question will warm the room.)*
- His question is **technical and friendly**. He asks about the gatekeeper-recruitment strategy and whether Simonka has identified specific gatekeepers in the Prague-region BDSM community. He is, in effect, asking whether the study is *feasible* — not whether it is *correct*.
- Simonka answers honestly. She has identified three gatekeepers in principle, two of whom have provisionally agreed to refer participants. She does not name them. **(The third gatekeeper is, in fact, a peripheral Collegium contact she cannot name even in principle without breaking the club's silence rule. She has built her recruitment plan around the assumption that this third gatekeeper exists and that the other two will, in practice, cover the gap. The contingency is professionally risky and personally honest.)**
- Bartoš nods. He thanks her. He has, in effect, voted before the vote.

### 4b — Procházková (no, ethics)

- She does not preamble. She asks her three sentences. The shape:

> "Doctor Veselá, with respect — I will keep this brief. Candidate, your study proposes anonymous interviews with adults in a population whose practices, when reported, will not stay anonymous in the press cycle this faculty would attract. What is your plan for the faculty's exposure, separate from your participants'?"

- *That is the question.* It is not a question about ethics in the academic sense; it is a question about *the institution's exposure*, dressed as ethics.
- Simonka has prepared for this. She has prepared a sentence about anonymisation protocols and a sentence about gatekeeper-mediated press handling and a sentence about her advisor's role in any public-facing communication. She delivers the three sentences. They are competent. They are not, she knows, what Procházková wanted to hear. **Procházková wanted to hear that the topic would not be pursued.** She has not heard it. She marks something on her copy and does not ask a follow-up.

### 4c — Havelka (no, methods)

- He is the longest. He takes nine minutes.
- He begins by **thanking her for the proposal's quality.** He says — for the record — that the proposal is among the better-written second-master's documents he has read this year. *He means it.* Simonka, watching his face, understands: **he is going to vote against her, and he is going to do it from a position of having said, on the record, that her work was good.** This is what intellectual honesty in his register looks like. She has the strange experience of being honoured and dismantled in the same breath.
- His methodological objection comes in three parts:
  1. **The consent paradox.** *"Your population is defined by asymmetric authority. Your method assumes their consent to participate in research can be treated as separable from the asymmetric authority that defines them. Tell me how."*
  2. **The participant-observer line.** *"Your design draws a bright line at researcher non-participation. I accept the line as drawn on paper. I do not accept that the line, once research begins, is operationally maintainable. Tell me what you will do when a participant or a gatekeeper invites or expects you to participate, and you have to either decline (compromising rapport) or comply (compromising the design)."*
  3. **The framing risk.** *"You position these relationships as care-architecture rather than pathology. The framing is defensible academically and may be correct. It is also* — *and you know this* — *the framing the community itself produces about itself. Tell me how your study will not become the community's apologetics with a methodology bolted on."*
- These are the three best questions anyone has ever asked Simonka about anything.
- **She answers them, one at a time, in order, for a total of seven minutes.** She does not flinch. She does not lose her line. She does not over-claim. On the consent paradox she invokes the *negotiated-consent* literature and acknowledges the paradox is not fully resolvable. On the participant-observer line she names the formal protocols and acknowledges that the operational risk is real and will require continuous reflective practice. On the framing risk she names the literature on *insider/outsider* tension in qualitative research and acknowledges that her framing will need to be argued, in the dissertation itself, against the strongest available critique.
- **Each of her three answers is an honest acknowledgement of the limit of the answer.** This is, she has learned in three years at Charles University, the only register Havelka respects. *He respects it now.* When she finishes, he says — quietly, without warmth, without coldness — *"Thank you."* The thank-you is the closest thing in the scene to a compliment.
- **He is still going to vote no.** Simonka knows. Lukešová, on Veselá's right, knows. Havelka, even as he says thank-you, knows.

### 4d — Lukešová (yes, advisor)

- She asks one question. The smallest possible question. *"In your judgement, candidate, what is the minimum number of completed interviews at which the methodology becomes informative?"*
- It is the question of an advisor who has decided to ask, in the room, the question that anchors the *feasibility floor* of the study. **She is asking it for Veselá's benefit** — Veselá has not made up her mind, and Lukešová is giving her a small piece of practical reassurance to factor into her vote.
- Simonka names a number. *Twelve.* She defends it briefly.
- Lukešová nods. She does not ask a follow-up.

### 4e — Veselá (yes, chair)

- She asks last. Her question is the most surprising of the five.
- *"Candidate, when you withdrew your first proposal, you told Doc. Lukešová that you could not get sufficient access to a clean cohort. With this new proposal, what is the relationship between the question you wanted to ask in the first proposal and the question you are asking in this one?"*
- It is, **structurally,** a question about Simonka herself — *what changed in you between proposal one and proposal two* — and it is the closest the scene gets to the truth.
- Simonka has not prepared for this question.
- **She answers honestly, within the limit of what she can say in this room.** The shape:
  > "I withdrew the first proposal because I could not write a question I believed was mine. The new proposal is a question I believe is mine. The relationship between them, in the language of the discipline, is that both proposals are about *how care is structured inside relationships defined by asymmetry*. The first asked about the foster system. The second asks about a community that has built its own answer to the same question. I came to the second by recognising that I was already asking it."
- That is the answer.
- It is true. Every word of it is true. It is also, if anyone in the room had ears for the doubleness, **the closest she will ever come to telling them what she has not told them.**
- Veselá looks at her for one long beat. Then she nods.
- *(Simonka will, for years, wonder whether Veselá knew. The honest answer is: probably not. Possibly slightly. Veselá heard the word "I" in the answer and registered that the candidate had said something more personal than was structurally required. She did not, the saga must hold, draw the conclusion. She voted yes partly on the basis of the answer's evident sincerity, which is a different thing from voting yes on the basis of what the answer was actually about.)*

## BEAT 5 — THE EXIT

*Brief. The candidate is asked to leave the room while the committee deliberates.*

- Veselá thanks the candidate. She says the committee will deliberate. She asks her to wait in the corridor.
- Simonka stands. She does not look at Lukešová. She walks out. The door closes behind her.
- In the corridor she does not sit on the bench. She walks to the far end of the corridor and stands at the window — the one above the fly from the morning — and looks down into the courtyard. There are students crossing it; the day has begun for the rest of the faculty while she has been in the room. She does not hear what is being said in the room. The door is solid. The wall is thick. **She does not, by ear or by inference, know which way the vote is going.**
- She is in the corridor for eleven minutes.
- She does not, in the eleven minutes, touch the chain at her throat. She is too aware of the cameras she suspects but cannot confirm are in the corridor ceiling. The chain stays under the blouse.
- She thinks, instead, of Holic at the farm. She thinks of the way he said *you will know what you have to know when you have to know it.* She decides, in the corridor, that the sentence was him giving her the answer she would need *whichever way the vote went*. Yes and no are both *things she has to know*. The sentence covers both. She does not know whether to be comforted or frightened by the architecture of his foresight.

## BEAT 6 — THE CALLING BACK

*The vote is read.*

- The secretary opens the door. Simonka returns to the room. The committee is, very slightly, more relaxed than when she left — chairs shifted, papers gathered. Bartoš has poured himself water at last. Havelka is screwing the cap onto a pen.
- Veselá speaks.

**Dialogue cue (Veselá, the shape — render close to this, in the formal academic register):**

> "Candidate. The committee has deliberated. The proposal, with conditions, is approved by a vote of three to two. The conditions, agreed by the committee, are as follows."

- She reads the three conditions in the order they were agreed:
  1. Quarterly methodology check-ins between the candidate and Prof. Havelka, who has agreed to serve as additional methodological reader for the duration of the project.
  2. The candidate will sign, with the ethics board, a formal acknowledgement of the no-participant-observer boundary, breach of which is grounds for withdrawal of the master's.
  3. No public presentation of preliminary findings until the methodology has been signed off, in writing, by Prof. Havelka.
- Simonka receives the conditions. She does not flinch on condition 1 (Havelka as her co-supervisor for two years). She does not flinch on condition 2 (the boundary she has already broken). She does not flinch on condition 3 (the gag).
- Veselá asks, formally: *"Does the candidate accept the conditions?"*
- Simonka says: *"Yes."*
- That is the entire word.
- Veselá nods. The proposal is approved. Veselá thanks the committee. The committee thanks the candidate. *(All of this is procedural. The room is closing.)*

**Internal beat (Simonka):** *She has won. She has also, in the same minute, agreed to two years of methodological supervision by the only person in the room who could destroy the project, agreed to sign a document she has already in spirit broken, and agreed to a gag she would have agreed to without their asking. She has, the prose can let the reader feel, in winning, lost something she did not know was on the table.*

---

## BEAT 7 — THE TWO NO VOTERS, AFTER

*Two small beats as the room breaks up. Each No voter does one small thing on the way out.*

### 7a — Havelka

- Havelka stands. He gathers his papers. He walks past Simonka's chair on the way to the door. He stops. He does not put a hand on her shoulder; he does not condescend. He says, quietly:

> "Candidate. I will see you in three months. Bring me your interview protocols and your reflective journal. We will speak about the third question I asked you today."

- That is the entire interaction.
- It is the most important sentence anyone says in the room after the vote. **Havelka is going to hold her to the framing-risk objection for the next two years.** *That is what condition 1 means in practice.* She nods. She says, *"Thank you, Professor."* He nods back and leaves.

### 7b — Procházková

- Procházková gathers her papers, stands, and on her way out does not stop at Simonka's chair. She does, however — and this is the small thing — meet Simonka's eye for one beat at the door. **The eye contact is not warm. It is not hostile.** It is *acknowledgement*: *I voted no, you know I voted no, we are now colleagues of a kind for the duration of this project, and we both know what I know.* Procházková leaves without speaking.
- This is, Simonka understands, the most generous thing Procházková could have done. It is also, in its way, **the most chilling**, because it is the closest anyone in the room has come to telling her she has been *seen*.

## BEAT 8 — LUKEŠOVÁ AND SIMONKA, ALONE

*The room empties. Simonka and her advisor remain.*

- Bartoš leaves last of the others, with a small genuine smile to Simonka that costs him nothing and is, in the moment, worth more to her than the entire committee's procedural courtesy.
- Lukešová has not stood. Simonka, opposite her, has not stood either.
- They look at each other across the table.

**Dialogue cue (Lukešová, the shape):**

> "Three to two is a passing vote. Havelka and Procházková are not your enemies. They are now your audience, which is harder. You have two years. Begin."

- That is what she says.
- *Begin.* Not *well done*. Not *I am proud of you*. Not *we have work to do*. Just *begin*.
- It is the single word Simonka most needed.
- Simonka, finally, allows her shoulders — which have been held in a specific architecture for three weeks — to lower a quarter-inch. *(The same lowering Catrin and Rhian's would, in another scene in another country, make in Addyson's kitchen. The saga produces this lowering in its women when, and only when, an older woman gives them the smallest possible permission to be tired.)*
- She says: *"Yes, Doctor."*
- Lukešová stands. Simonka stands. They leave the committee room together. In the corridor Lukešová turns left toward her office and Simonka turns right toward the stairs, and they part without ceremony.

## BEAT 9 — THE WALK OUT

*Brief. The candidate, alone, leaves the building. She will not call Holic until evening.*

- Simonka takes the stairs slowly. On the landing between the second and first floors she stops and pours herself a glass of water from the corridor cooler. *(The carafe in the room sat untouched on her side of the table for ninety minutes. Her body knows.)*
- She goes outside. The courtyard is in autumn sun. There are leaves on the cobbles. She walks across the courtyard and out through the archway onto the street.
- She does not call Holic. She does not text him. She has the phone in her hand. She does not use it.
- She walks toward the river. She does not know, when she sets out, where she is going. She walks for forty minutes. She arrives at a bench by the Vltava that she has not sat on since her undergraduate days. She sits.
- She touches the chain at her throat. Once. She does not take it out from under her blouse. She leaves it where she has left it all day.
- She thinks, sitting on the bench:
  - *I have won.*
  - *I have signed a paper that says I will not do the thing I am already doing.*
  - *Professor Havelka is right and I have agreed, in the next two years, to be told so quarterly.*
  - *Holic was right last night. I know what I have to know.*
  - *I have not yet decided whether what I have to know is good news or bad.*
- She sits for an hour. She watches the river. The afternoon goes gold, then early-evening blue.
- Eventually, she takes the phone out of her pocket. She does not call Holic. She calls **her mother**, Klára. The call is short. *"Mama. I passed. Three to two." "I'm proud of you, miláčku. What was the topic?" "I'll tell you when I see you."* That is the entire phone call. Klára does not press. *(This is canon — Klára never presses Simonka. The saga has established this. The non-pressing is one of the few mercies inside which Simonka has built her whole double life.)*
- She hangs up. She puts the phone back in her pocket.
- **Then she calls Holic.** The call is shorter. *"I passed." "Good." "With conditions." "Tell me when you come." "Tomorrow." "Good."* That is the entire phone call.
- She stands. She walks home.

## BEAT 10 — CLOSING IMAGE

*One short coda. Optional but recommended.*

- *Late evening. In her apartment in Prague, Simonka takes off her blouse. She takes off the silver chain. She puts the chain in a small wooden box on the dresser — the box she keeps for things she wears in only one of her two lives. She closes the box.*
- *She showers. She eats — bread, cheese, an apple. She does not open the laptop. She does not look at the proposal. She does not read.*
- *She goes to bed at nine. She sleeps, for the first time in a week, for nine straight hours.*
- *Somewhere, in another country, in a faculty building she has not been to, her great-grandfather Mike Key is long dead; her grandfather is long dead; her aunt Patti Anne is asleep in a house in Canada; her cousins are alive in nine cities; her brother is alive; her mother and father are alive in Prague, two tram stops away, asleep next to each other in the bed they have shared since they were nineteen.*

## TONE GUARDRAILS

- **No academic exoticism.** Charles University is a working university with working committees and working rooms. Render it as such. Do not over-decorate it with ancient-Europe atmosphere. The Faculty of Arts has the same fluorescent lighting and the same scuffed parquet as any social-sciences faculty in any city in 2020s Europe. The seriousness of the room is in the people, not the room.
- **No melodrama around the vote.** A 3–2 vote is, in academic practice, **a passing vote with two named dissents**. It is not a near-miss. It is not a humiliation. It is not a vindication. It is **a working result**. The prose must render it that way. *Simonka's interior may register the closeness as larger than it formally is; the prose's narration must not.*
- **No interior monologue in which Simonka relieves herself of the secret.** The discipline is total: she does not, inside her head, narrate "I am GG and they do not know." She watches. She files. She agrees to conditions. The doubleness lives in the *gap between what she says and what she knows*, not in any sentence in which she names the doubleness to herself. **The reader has to do the work of holding the doubleness; the prose must not do it for them.**
- **No villainising of the No voters.** Havelka and Procházková are honourable academics doing their jobs. Havelka is *correct on methodology*. Procházková is *correct on institutional exposure*. The fact that Simonka's proposal is the right one to pursue does not make their objections wrong. The saga has to hold all four facts at once. *This is harder than it sounds. The prose must resist the candidate-vs.-the-establishment narrative the reader will reach for.*
- **No heroic chair.** Veselá's yes vote is the scene's quietest piece of integrity. The prose must render it without elevating Veselá. She is doing her job. She does it well. The doing is enough.
- **No closure with Holic.** The evening phone call is six words on his side. The prose must let the brevity be the relationship, not a withholding from the reader.
- **No mention of GG.** The name does not appear in the scene. Not in Simonka's interior. Not in dialogue. Not in narration. *The scene is, in this respect, the saga's strictest single discipline.* She is, today, a candidate. GG is at the farm. The two have not yet learned to be in the same room.
