# SCENE OUTLINE: KLÁRA HELPS SIMONKA AND HER PARTNER — THE MOVE SHE CAN'T DO (FIVE WEEKS BEFORE THE RECITAL)

**Part of the Prague Academy / Key family saga.**
**Placement:** A weekday evening in the Mike Key Studio, **five weeks before the Annual End-of-Year Recital** the June Simonka is seventeen. Companion/prequel to `simonka-painted-piece-recital-scene-outline.md` (the recital night itself). The piece is a **painted duet**; this is the scene where its one partnered move nearly breaks Simonka.

**Characters in the scene:** Simonka Káta (seventeen, the non-dancer attempting the one piece she will ever perform), **[partner — name & surname TBD]** (a senior academy student, Simonka's partner in the duet; has a teasing habit of tickling her), Klára Key (mother, artistic director, brought in to coach the move).

**Characters referenced but not present:** Klárka Pavlînka (sister, who will help paint on the night), Gamaliel (father, elsewhere in the building), the senior dancers who make this look easy, the front-row family seat that will be empty on the night.

**Setting:** The Mike Key Studio after hours — the first studio of the academy, the one named for Mike Key. High windows going blue with evening. The barre. The piano, lid down. The marley floor scuffed pale where a thousand turns have landed. A single work-light instead of the full rig. Two water bottles, a rolled towel, and Simonka's notebook open on the bench — diagrams of the piece in her own handwriting, more lab notes than choreography.

**Tone:** Warm, funny, then suddenly tender. A comedy of failure that turns into something real. The joke — *the watcher finally gets onstage and is undone by being tickled* — is genuinely funny, and the family would laugh. But underneath it is the actual fear: Simonka has spent seventeen years controlling exactly how much of herself other people get to read, and this move requires her to surrender that completely — to be held, turned, and *seen* without defending herself. The tickling is just the body's honest confession that she cannot yet let go. Klára, who reads bodies for a living, sees the real problem under the funny one and coaches *that*.

## BEFORE THE SCENE

- The piece has been in rehearsal for a few weeks. Everything else works — the blank-slate opening, the pedestrian gestures, the slow walk toward legibility. Simonka, to her own surprise, can do the plain human stuff; it is not virtuosic, and that is the point.
- **The one thing that does not work is the partnered move** — the supported lean/lift at the heart of the piece, the moment the two figures finally touch and her partner turns her slowly to the light to be "read." It requires Simonka to give him her **full weight**, go completely still, keep her face blank, and trust him to hold and turn her. On the night, both of them will be painted; this rehearsal is in practice clothes.
- **Why she keeps failing it:** the instant his hands settle at her ribs and waist to take her weight, she **flinches and laughs.** She is ticklish there, and her partner — easy, good-natured, a little bit of a class clown — has discovered this and cannot entirely resist exploiting it, which makes it worse. Every attempt collapses into giggling and a dropped line. Five weeks out, she still has not held the move once.
- **What it has done to her:** Simonka, who is excellent at everything she chooses to do because she only ever chooses things she's already thought through, is **failing in public at the one physical thing she ever agreed to attempt** — in her parents' building, on her great-grandfather's floor, surrounded by people who have danced their whole lives. She is mortified. She has started talking about pulling the piece. The word she uses, to herself, is *failure* — and for the watcher who built her identity on being the competent observer, that word is unusually loud.
- **How Klára gets involved:** Simonka's partner, out of his depth and worried she'll quit, quietly asks Klára to come look. Or Klára, who misses nothing in her own building, has already noticed her daughter leaving rehearsals tight-faced and invites herself. Either way, she comes to the studio after hours, when it's just the two kids and the work-light. (Pick one — leaning toward the partner asking for help, which says something good about him.)

## THE SCENE

### The Failure, On Display

- Klára arrives quietly and does what she always does first: she watches. She asks them to run the move so she can see it, and says nothing else.
- They set up. Her partner steps in, hands find Simonka's ribs to take her weight — and she flinches, laughs, breaks it. They reset. Same thing. The partner, mortified now that the director is watching, tries to be more careful, which makes him tentative, which makes the lift genuinely unsafe — and *that* makes Simonka brace, which makes her even more ticklish. A doom loop.
- Simonka, furious at herself, not at him: *See. This is why I don't do this. I had one job. Stand still and let him pick me up. A six-year-old in the junior class can do this.* She grabs her notebook like she's going to cross the whole piece out. *I should pull it. I'll do front of house. It's fine. It's what I'm good at.*
- The partner, gently, taking the blame: *It's me. I keep — I shouldn't tease. I'll stop, I swear.* (Establish him as kind and self-aware, not a pest. The tickling is affection that hasn't found its boundary yet, and he knows it.)

### Klára Reads the Real Problem

- Klára does not rush to reassure. She is the artistic director; she has fixed ten thousand moves. But she's also a woman who was a frightened, guarded child once herself, fostered into safety, who learned the hard way what it means to let someone hold you. She sees instantly that this is not a tickling problem.
- Klára: *You think you're failing because you laugh.* A beat. *You're laughing because you won't give him your weight. The laugh is your body saying no. It's the most honest thing in the room.*
- Simonka, the psychology student, goes still — because her mother has just diagnosed her more cleanly than she can diagnose herself. *...That's annoyingly good.*
- Klára: *I've watched bodies longer than you've been alive.* (Echo of Simonka's own gift, handed down the maternal line — Klára reads bodies the way Simonka is learning to. The trait didn't come from nowhere.)
- Klára names it plainly: the whole piece is *about* being read without defending yourself. The move is not a technical obstacle in front of the meaning — it *is* the meaning. *You built a nine-minute piece about letting people read you, and you can't let one boy hold your ribs. The move isn't fighting the piece. The move is the piece. Until you can do it, you don't believe your own idea.*

### The Coaching

- Klára coaches it as a *trust* problem, not a *tickle* problem — and the fix is concrete, the kind of practical wisdom a lifelong teacher has:
  - **Take the tickling off the table by facing it, not avoiding it.** Klára has the partner put his hands flat at her ribs and *just leave them there* — no lift, no movement — while Simonka breathes and lets the flinch rise and pass. You cannot be tickled by a hand you've stopped treating as a threat. The body habituates. (Make her partner part of the solution: he has to be steady and unteasing here, and he is — this is where he stops being the kid who tickles and becomes the partner who holds.)
  - **Give the weight on purpose, all at once, not in nervous halves.** Half-given weight is what makes a lift unsafe and a body ticklish-tense. Klára teaches the counterintuitive thing every dancer knows: you are *safer* when you fully commit. *Trust is heavier than fear. Give him all of it.*
  - **A focal point and the breath out.** Klára gives Simonka somewhere to send her eyes and tells her to exhale into the lift — the body cannot brace and exhale at the same time.
  - **Reframe "still" as active, not passive.** Klára: *You think being lifted is doing nothing. It's the hardest thing in dance. Stillness is a muscle. You're not letting him move you. You're holding still* with *him.* (This reframes the move into something the analytical non-dancer can respect and attack — stillness as discipline, not surrender.)

### The First Clean Rep

- They try again, slow. His hands settle. The flinch comes — and this time she breathes through it instead of laughing it away. It passes. He takes her weight; she gives it, all of it; she finds her focal point and exhales; he turns her to the work-light.
- She holds. Blank-faced, still, *read* — and she doesn't flinch.
- A silence. Then Simonka, upside-down or mid-turn, completely deadpan: *Don't say anything. If you say anything I'll laugh and we'll have to start over.* They lower her. The two kids are grinning like they got away with something. It worked.
- Klára, dry, gathering her things, not making a big production of it because that would wreck it: *Five weeks. Do it a hundred more times until your body forgets it was ever afraid.* At the door she lets one real thing out: *You're not failing, Simonka. You're learning the only thing the dancers learn that you never had to. How to be held.* And she goes, leaving them to run it again in the blue light.

### The Tag (optional, short)

- Left alone, the partner — chastened, sincere — promises he really will stop tickling her. Simonka, who has just learned she can survive being touched at the ribs, surprises herself: *No. Keep doing it.* Off his look: *If I can hold the move while you're trying to make me laugh, I can hold it in front of three hundred people.* She has turned the thing that broke her into her own training tool — pure Simonka. (And, if the relationship is meant to be quietly romantic, this is the beat where it tips: the tease stops being a problem and becomes *theirs.*)
