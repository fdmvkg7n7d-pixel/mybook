# SCENE OUTLINE: THE SISTERS DANCE TOGETHER — ONCE, IN PRIVATE (KLÁRKA GRADE 12 / SIMONKA GRADE 11)

**Part of the Prague Academy / Key family saga.**
**Placement:** Two years *before* the painted-piece recital. The single school year in which the sisters overlap as students — **Klárka in Grade 12 (her final year), Simonka in Grade 11.** A late evening in the Mike Key Studio, near the end of that year, after the building has emptied. Prequel/echo to `simonka-painted-piece-rehearsal-five-weeks-out-scene-outline.md` and `simonka-painted-piece-recital-scene-outline.md`.

**Characters in the scene:** Klárka Pavlînka (eighteen, Grade 12, the dancer, about to graduate), Simonka Káta (sixteen/seventeen, Grade 11, the one who watches).

**Characters referenced but not present:** Klára and Gamaliel (parents, gone home; the dogs with them), the academy's other students, the future none of them can see yet — Klárka's teaching career, Simonka's Charles University years, the painted piece two recitals from now.

**Setting:** The Mike Key Studio after hours — the academy's first room, named for their great-grandfather. One work-light. The barre. The piano with its lid down. The marley floor cooling. The high windows dark. The particular quiet of a dance studio when everyone who is supposed to be in it has gone.

**Tone:** Hushed, warm, a little secret. This is not a performance and it is not a rehearsal. It is two sisters, fifteen months apart, alone in the room they grew up in, doing the one thing they never do together — because in a few weeks Klárka graduates and the shape of them changes forever. No audience. No costume. No marks on the body. Just the two of them and the floor. It is the private, unrepeatable counterpart to the public painted piece that comes later: the time Simonka danced for *love of her sister*, versus the time she danced to *say something*.

## BEFORE THE SCENE

- **The fact, established:** for one year only, the sisters are students at the academy at the same time — Klárka finishing Grade 12, Simonka in Grade 11. After this year Klárka graduates (and goes on to teach), and the window where they could ever dance *together as students* closes for good. (See `klarka-pavlinka-profile.md`, `simonka-kata-profile.md`, and the recital/rehearsal scene outlines.)
- **Simonka does not dance** — this is canon and it stays canon. She has spent her whole life in these studios as the watcher: front-of-house at recitals, the analytical eye, the sister who studies bodies instead of training one. She has never performed, and she does not intend to. (This scene does **not** make her a dancer; it is a single private exception, years before she chooses one public exception of her own — the painted piece.)
- **Why it happens:** It is late. Klárka has stayed behind to run a variation one more time before her final assessments; Simonka has stayed because she always stays — she's reading on the bench, watching, the way she has watched her sister her whole life. Klárka finishes, breathing hard, and instead of packing up she does something she has wanted to do for years and never asked: she puts her hand out and asks her little sister to dance with her. Just once. Just here. No one watching. *Before I graduate. While we still can.*
- **The stakes (small and enormous):** For Klárka, this is a goodbye she can't say in words — to childhood, to the studio as *hers*, to being a daughter of the school before she becomes a teacher in it. For Simonka, it is terrifying in a way her sister doesn't fully grasp: dancing means being *un*-able to hide, being read instead of reading, doing the thing she has spent her life choosing not to do. She almost says no. She says yes — because it is Klárka, and because some part of her knows this year does not come back.

## THE SCENE

### The Ask

- Klárka finishes her variation. The studio rings with the after-silence of hard work. She is eighteen, sweat at her hairline, the most herself she ever is — in motion, in this room.
- She doesn't pack her bag. She walks over to where Simonka sits on the bench with a book she stopped reading ten minutes ago, and holds out her hand.
- Klárka: *Dance with me.*
- Simonka, reflexive: *I don't dance.* (Her whole identity in three words.)
- Klárka: *I know. That's not what I asked.* A beat — the sister, not the dancer, talking now. *I'm gone in a few weeks. We've been in this building together our whole lives and we've never once been on the floor at the same time. I don't want a performance. I don't want you to be good. I just want to dance with my sister once before I'm not a student here anymore.*
- Simonka looks at the door (no one), the windows (dark), the floor (waiting). The watcher calculates the exits and finds she doesn't want one. She puts the book down. *If you tell anyone I will deny it until I die.*
- Klárka, grinning: *Deal.*

### Learning the Hard Way That She Can't Hide

- Klárka keeps it absurdly simple — not a variation, not steps Simonka couldn't do, just *moving together*: a walk, a turn, hands joined, weight shared. The vocabulary of a five-year-old's first class, which is exactly the point.
- And immediately Simonka discovers the thing she has spent her life avoiding: on the floor, she cannot watch. She can only be *in* it. Klárka can read her completely — every hesitation, every braced muscle, every place she's holding herself apart — because that is what dancers do, they read bodies, and Klárka has been reading Simonka's since before either of them could talk.
- Klárka, gently, correcting without correcting: *Stop watching yourself. You're doing the thing you do to everyone else — standing outside it, taking notes. You can't take notes and dance. Pick one.*
- Simonka, caught: *...That's annoyingly accurate.* (Seed of the exact lesson her mother will name two years later in the rehearsal scene — that she defends herself instead of giving her weight. Klárka finds it first, by accident, here.)
- A few false starts. A collision. Both of them laughing — the specific helpless laughter of sisters doing something undignified in an empty room. (If the tickling/partner motif is meant to echo forward: Klárka *knows* exactly where Simonka is ticklish and absolutely uses it once to break her sister's death-grip seriousness — and the laugh, for one second, makes Simonka let go and actually move.)

### The Thing That Actually Happens

- And then, for a few bars, it works. Simonka stops narrating, stops bracing, and just moves with her sister — not well, not trained, but *true*. Two girls who share a face and a childhood and a floor, turning in a single work-light in the room named for the great-grandfather who protected the family that made them both.
- It is not impressive. It is not a performance. It is the most unguarded Simonka has been in years, and Klárka — who has spent eighteen years being the one watched while her sister watched — gets, for thirty seconds, to be watched *with* instead of watched *by*. The roles don't reverse. They dissolve.
- Neither says anything big. Klárka, quiet, still moving: *See. You can do it. You just never wanted to.* Simonka: *I still don't.* Klárka: *I know. But you can.* (The whole sister-dynamic in four lines: Klárka the pioneer who does everything first; Simonka who could follow and chooses not to — except, just this once, she did.)

### After

- They stop. Out of breath, a little embarrassed, entirely happy. Klárka finally packs her bag. They turn off the work-light together and leave the dark studio.
- No grand declaration. The scene lands on smallness: two sisters walking out of the building into the Prague night, the thing that just happened already becoming a private memory only the two of them will ever hold.
- Optional closing image, looking forward: Simonka glances back at the dark studio once. The watcher, filing it away. She doesn't know yet that in two years she'll stand in that same room, painted, and do her own one-time-only thing on a stage — but the seed is here. Klárka taught her, in an empty room with no one watching, that she *could* let herself be seen. The painted piece is where she decides to.
