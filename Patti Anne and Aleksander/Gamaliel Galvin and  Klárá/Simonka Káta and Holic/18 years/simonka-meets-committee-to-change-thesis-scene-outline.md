# SIMONKA WITHDRAWS — THE COMMITTEE MEETING

## Scene Outline — Beats, Dialogue Cues, and Thematic Notes

> **Placement:** Six months before the thesis defense. Simonka has been working on her first master's thesis proposal — a study of attachment patterns in long-term foster placements in the Czech Republic — for ten months. She has completed preliminary fieldwork, gathered literature, and submitted an initial proposal that her advisor, **Doc. Lukešová**, approved without enthusiasm. Simonka has now decided to withdraw it entirely.
>
> **This scene is the first time the saga's central question appears in academic form.** The reader (if this were a novel) does not yet know about Holic, GG, the Collegium, or anything that will come. The scene works as a self-contained academic confrontation: a student asks to abandon approved work and pursue something the committee will find unrecognizable.
>
> **Companion to** `simonka-kata-profile.md`, `simonka-second-masters-proposal-defense-scene-outline.md`, and the existing Simonka/Holic outlines. This scene is the *origin point* — everything the later defense defends begins in this room, with this conversation.
>
> **The full five-member committee is present.** This is not an informal consultation between Simonka and her advisor. Lukešová has called the full committee because a withdrawal after ten months of approved work requires formal review. The room holds five academics. Simonka is the sixth person.

> **Content & craft note.** This scene is about an academic decision, not a personal disclosure. Simonka does **not** tell the committee about GG, the Collegium, Holic, the farm, the brand, or anything from her secret life. What she tells them — what she *can* tell them — is that she has found a different question. The question she proposes is, on its surface, entirely academic: a study of consensual adult human-pet roleplay communities as sites of care-architecture. She is not disclosing her own access to the field. She is proposing to study it from the outside, as any researcher would study any subculture. **The committee does not know that the researcher is, in fact, a member.** The scene must hold this gap with precision.

---

**POV:** **Simonka Káta.** She has been preparing for this meeting for weeks. She has written the new proposal — or at least a draft of it, rough enough that she has not shown it to Lukešová yet, but complete enough that she can articulate its core question. She has rehearsed what she will say. She knows she is going to lose ten months of work and a portion of the department's patience. She is willing to pay that cost.

**Tone:** Academic, restrained, firm. The register is Czech-university formal — courteous, precise, unadorned. Simonka does not apologize for the withdrawal. She does not defend the old proposal. She does not oversell the new one. She presents the facts: the old question is not hers; the new question is. The committee will decide.

**Location:** **A seminar room at the Faculty of Arts (Filozofická fakulta), Charles University, Prague.** The same room — or one very like it — where the later defense will happen. Long table; six chairs; a window onto a courtyard; a whiteboard with the residue of a morning lecture still faintly visible. The room is unremarkable. Its unremarkableness is part of the scene.

**Time in saga:** Approximately six months before the thesis defense. Simonka is twenty-three or twenty-four. She has been at the university for roughly a year in her master's program. She met Holic eight to ten months ago (the timeline is deliberately elastic here — the saga can pin the exact meeting date in a future pass). **She is not yet GG.** Or she is GG only in the earliest, most private sense: the collar has been offered, perhaps accepted, perhaps not yet worn outside her apartment. The brand does not yet exist. The Kennel does not yet know her name. **She is, in this room, entirely Simonka — a student with a question she cannot explain without revealing more than she is willing to reveal.**

## THE FIVE COMMITTEE MEMBERS

*(The same five who will later vote on the defense — here, in their first collective appearance. Their positions at this meeting prefigure their votes.)*

### 1. Doc. PhDr. Lukešová, CSc. — The Advisor (yes)
- Mid-fifties. Simonka's supervisor. Approved the first proposal without enthusiasm; now faces a student who wants to abandon it entirely.
- **Her position at this meeting:** Cautious but open. She has called the full committee because the withdrawal is procedurally significant, not because she opposes it. She wants to hear the new proposal before committing either way.
- **Her concern:** Not the topic's risk — she has backed unconventional work before. The concern is *timing*. Ten months in, with preliminary fieldwork done. A withdrawal now costs the department time and Simonka time. Lukešová needs to know the new topic is worth the cost.
- **She does not know** about the Collegium. She will not know until much later, and even then, only in fragments.

### 2. Prof. PhDr. Veselá, DrSc. — The Chair (yes)
- Late sixties. The department's senior figure. Procedurally meticulous, temperamentally calm. She runs meetings the way a conductor runs an orchestra: with economy, with authority, with an ear for when to let silence do the work.
- **Her position at this meeting:** Neutral on the substance. Her role is procedural — to ensure the committee follows the rules for a mid-stream proposal change. She will not advocate for or against the withdrawal. She will, however, *listen very carefully* to what Simonka says, because Veselá has an instinct for when a student's change of topic is panic and when it is clarity. **She suspects this is clarity. She is not yet sure.**
- **She does not know** about the Collegium. She will not connect it to anything until the defense, and even then, only slightly.

### 3. Doc. PhDr. Havelka, CSc. — Methods (no)
- Fifties. Quantitative-methods specialist, social psychology. The committee's hardest grader on procedure. He does not care whether a topic is interesting; he cares whether it can be studied rigorously.
- **His position at this meeting:** Already skeptical — not of the withdrawal itself, but of what Simonka is replacing it with. He will ask the question that no one else asks: *"How will you recruit participants?"* — and when Simonka answers, he will identify the gap she cannot fill without naming the unnamed.
- **He does not know** about the Collegium. He will not need to. His objection will be purely methodological, and it will be correct.
- *(The saga will later reveal this as his defining trait — he watches, he records, he decides only when the data forces a decision. It is the same trait that will lead him, at the defense, to vote no from a position of having read the proposal carefully.)*

### 4. Dr. Procházková — Ethics (no)
- Forties. Ethics and institutional-risk specialist. She has spent her career navigating the boundary between academic freedom and institutional liability. She is not hostile to unconventional topics; she is hostile to topics that put the faculty at risk.
- **Her position at this meeting:** Concerned. Human-pet roleplay is a topic that, in the wrong press cycle, could generate headlines the faculty would rather not see. She will ask the question no one else wants to ask: *"What is your plan for institutional exposure, separate from your participants'?"*
- **She does not know** about the Collegium. She will not need to. Her objection will be institutional, and it will be correct in its own register.
- *(At this meeting, Procházková is the most visibly uneasy member. She is not rude. She is not dismissive. She is doing her job — the job of asking the question that protects the institution from itself.)*

### 5. Dr. Bartoš — Social Psychology (yes)
- Thirties. The youngest committee member. Recently promoted, quantitatively trained, but with a quiet intellectual curiosity about subcultures and non-mainstream populations. He is the one member who is *personally interested* in the topic — not pruriently, but academically.
- **His position at this meeting:** The warmest reception. He will ask technical questions — about the literature, about the framing, about whether Simonka has considered the adjacent BDSM-studies research — but his questions are *friendly*. He wants the proposal to work.
- **He does not know** about the Collegium. He would, if he knew, be the most fascinated member — and also the most conflicted, because his interest could be seen as a reason to recuse himself. (He will not know, so the conflict does not arise here.)

## SEATING

The room is arranged as it will be at the defense, though this meeting is less formal:

- **Veselá** is in the centre, at the head of the long table.
- **Lukešová** is on Veselá's right — the advisor's traditional position.
- **Havelka** is on Veselá's left — the methodologist's anchor.
- **Bartoš** is at the far right — the junior member, seated where the view of the whole room is best.
- **Procházková** is at the far left — the ethics specialist, positioned where she can watch both the student and the advisor simultaneously.

Simonka's chair is opposite Veselá. There is a water carafe between them. **She will not touch it.** (This is the first instance of the small ritual — the untouched carafe — that will recur at the defense.)

## THE BEATS

### Beat 1 — THE SUMMONING

*Lukešová called the meeting, but Veselá chairs it. That distinction matters.*

- Simonka arrives on time. She is always on time. She carries two things: a folder containing the withdrawn foster-care proposal (marked with Lukešová's pencil annotations, the margins full of notes from ten months of work) and a single sheet of paper — the new proposal, in outline form, still in draft. **Lukešová already has the proposal. It has been on the committee's desks for review.**
- All five committee members are present. This surprises Simonka slightly — she expected an informal meeting with Lukešová, perhaps one other member. The full committee signals that the withdrawal is being treated as a formal procedural event, not a student's whim. **She is relieved by this. Formality means the committee will evaluate her on procedure, not on personal loyalty to the old topic.**
- Simonka sits. She places the folder on the table. She does not open it.
- Veselá opens. The opening is procedural and standardised: the faculty, the degree, the candidate, the existing proposal. *We are here to consider a request for withdrawal and substitution.* **Veselá does not express an opinion. She states the situation and invites the candidate to speak.**

**Dialogue cue (Veselá, the shape):**
> "Candidate Dimitrov. You have requested the withdrawal of your approved master's thesis proposal and the substitution of a new topic. The committee has reviewed your request. We will hear your reasons. We will evaluate the new proposal. We will decide."

Simonka: *"Thank you, Professor."*

Veselá: *"Please proceed."*

---

### Beat 2 — THE WITHDRAWAL

*Simonka explains. She does not tell the whole truth, but she does not lie. The distinction matters.*

Simonka's explanation, in outline:

1. **The old question is not mine.** She says this directly. *"I have been working on this proposal for ten months. I have done preliminary fieldwork. I have read the literature. I have written three draft chapters. And I cannot, in the end, write a thesis on this topic."* She does not say why — does not mention her mother, does not mention the foster system's personal proximity. She says only that the question is not hers.
2. **I found another question.** She slides the single sheet of paper across the table. **It passes through five pairs of hands.** Lukešová reads it first (she already knows the topic from Simonka's email). Then Veselá. Then Havelka. Then Bartoš. Then Procházková. Each reads it in silence. The room is quiet for the time it takes five academics to read one page.
3. **The new proposal.** Simonka describes it — briefly, in academic language, the way she will later describe it to the full committee at the defense. *"I am proposing a study of consensual adult human-pet roleplay communities — specifically, the construction of care and identity within ritualized power-exchange relationships."* She names the framing: negotiated-consent theory, care-ethics, attachment theory under conditions of asymmetric authority. She does not name the Collegium. She does not name Holic. She presents the topic as a subculture she has identified through published literature and preliminary contact with community gatekeepers.
4. **The cost.** Simonka acknowledges what the withdrawal costs: ten months of work, the department's patience, the timeline for graduation. *"I know this is late. I know I am asking for a reset. I am willing to take the extra semester if that is what it requires."*

---

### Beat 3 — THE COMMITTEE'S FIRST REACTIONS

*Each member responds in turn. The order reveals their position.*

### 3a — Bartoš (yes, social psychology)

- He goes first. Not because he is the chair, but because he is the one most *interested*. His questions are technical and friendly. He asks about the literature — whether Simonka has read the key works on consensual non-consent in BDSM studies, whether she has considered the anthropological literature on ritual and roleplay.
- Simonka has read the literature. She names three or four key texts. Bartoš is impressed — not by the topic, but by the rigor of her preliminary reading. **He has, in effect, voted yes before the vote.**
- He asks one follow-up: *"Have you considered presenting this at a student conference before the defense? It would help you test the framework against peer feedback."*
- Simonka: *"I have. I plan to submit an abstract to the Prague Graduate Symposium in the spring."*
- Bartoš nods. He is satisfied. He will not ask again.

### 3b — Havelka (no, methods)

- He speaks second. He has read the single sheet of paper carefully. He has not looked up while reading. He places it face-down on the table and begins.
- His first question: *"How will you recruit participants?"*
- Simonka answers. She names three gatekeeper channels, snowball methodology, full informed consent, IRB protocol. The answer is technically competent.
- Havelka's second question: *"You have named three channels. One of those channels — the one your sample most depends on — you have not named. Why?"*
- Simonka: *"Because the gatekeeper has not given me permission to name them. They have agreed, in principle, to refer participants, but the agreement is conditional on anonymity."*
- Havelka: *"And if the gatekeeper withdraws that agreement before your fieldwork begins?"*
- Simonka: *"Then I have two other channels. Both are viable. The sample size will be smaller, but still sufficient for the methodology."*
- Havelka: *"How much smaller."*
- Simonka: *"Approximately forty percent."*
- Havelka: *"And will the methodology survive a forty-percent reduction in sample size."*
- Simonka: *"Yes. But the generalizability of the findings will be reduced accordingly."*
- Havelka nods. He writes something in his notebook. He does not say whether the answer satisfies him. **He has not decided. He will not decide for a long time — months, possibly years. This is who Havelka is: a man who asks the right question, records the answer, and waits for the data to prove whether the answer was correct.**
- He does not ask again. He does not need to. **The recruitment question is the one that will lead him, at the defense, to vote no. It is already present here, in this room, six months early.**

### 3c — Procházková (no, ethics)

- She speaks third. She has read the single sheet of paper with a different eye than Havelka's — not the methodologist's eye but the ethicist's. She is looking for risk.
- Her question: *"Candidate, your proposed topic concerns a population whose practices, when reported in any public forum, will attract media attention disproportionate to the academic merit of the work. What is your plan for the faculty's institutional exposure, separate from your participants' privacy?"*
- It is not a hostile question. It is a procedural one. Procházková has seen unconventional topics generate headlines, and headlines generate inquiries, and inquiries generate investigations. She is asking Simonka to account for the chain of consequences.
- Simonka has prepared for this. She speaks about anonymisation protocols, about gatekeeper-mediated participant contact, about the IRB process, about Lukešová's role in any public-facing communication. Her answer is competent. It is also, she knows, an answer that assumes the community will cooperate with anonymisation — and the community she is actually studying has, in its own rules, a silence requirement that makes anonymisation not just desirable but mandatory. **Simonka is, in this answer, describing the Collegium's own rules as if they were standard research ethics. The committee hears standard research ethics. The reader — later — will hear the Collegium.**
- Procházková marks something on her copy. She does not ask a follow-up. **She has not decided. But the seed of the no vote is planted: the topic carries institutional risk that the candidate has not fully accounted for.**

### 3d — Veselá (yes, chair)

- She speaks fourth. As chair, she has been listening, not questioning. Now she asks one question — the smallest possible question, but the one that matters most.
- *"Candidate. You withdrew your first proposal ten months after approval. You are now asking us to approve a second. What is the relationship between the question you were asking in the first proposal and the question you are asking in this one?"*
- It is a question about *continuity* — about whether Simonka is running *from* something or *toward* something. The distinction matters to Veselá. Students who run from a topic out of panic do not write good theses. Students who run toward a topic out of conviction sometimes do.
- Simonka answers. The answer is the same shape as the answer she will give at the defense, but less polished — this is the first time she has articulated it aloud:
  > *"Both proposals are about how care is structured inside relationships defined by asymmetry. The first asked about the foster system. The second asks about a community that has built its own answer to the same question. I came to the second by recognising that I was already asking it."*
- Veselá looks at her for a long beat. **She hears the word "I" in the answer, and she registers that the candidate has said something more personal than was procedurally required. She does not draw the conclusion. She files the observation.**
- Veselá: *"Thank you."* She does not say yes or no. She returns to the chair's neutrality.

### 3e — Lukešová (yes, advisor)

- She speaks last. She is the advisor — she has already been through this conversation with Simonka in private, or something like it. But this is a committee meeting, and her role as advisor requires her to state her position on the record.
- Lukešová: *"I have read three drafts of the new proposal's outline. The methodology is not yet airtight, but the question is real. I will support the withdrawal. The committee will review the new proposal and the withdrawal request, and we will get back to you in two weeks."*
- She turns to Simonka. *"We will review your request. The committee will get back to you in two weeks. Until then, the withdrawal stands but is not final. You will wait for our decision."*
- Simonka: *"Two weeks."*

---

### Beat 4 — THE COMMITTEE'S DELIBERATION (brief, in Simonka's absence)

*Simonka is asked to step out while the committee confers. This is standard procedure — the candidate leaves, the committee discusses, the candidate is called back in.*

- Simonka stands. She does not look at the water carafe. She walks out. The door closes behind her.
- In the corridor, she stands at the window — the same window she will stand at during the defense's deliberation six months later — and looks down into the courtyard. The day is [season TBD]. There are students crossing below. The world continues while the committee decides.
- Inside the room, the committee confers for approximately eight minutes.
  - **Bartoš** supports the withdrawal. The topic is unconventional but the preliminary reading is solid.
  - **Havelka** is noncommittal. The recruitment question is unresolved. He will not commit to yes or no until he sees the full proposal.
  - **Procházková** is cautious. The institutional risk is real. She will not commit until she sees the full methodology and the IRB protocol.
  - **Veselá** is neutral. She sees the candidate's conviction, but conviction is not enough. The full proposal will determine the outcome.
  - **Lukešová** supports the withdrawal conditionally. She has staked her supervisory reputation on Simonka's ability to produce a rigorous proposal in two weeks.
- The committee's informal consensus: **the withdrawal request is noted. The committee will review both proposals and get back to Simonka in two weeks.** No vote is taken. No formal decision is recorded. This is a consultation, not a defense. The formal decision will come in two weeks.

---

### Beat 5 — THE CALLING BACK

*The secretary — a graduate student who is not on the committee — opens the door. Simonka returns to the room.*

- Veselá speaks: *"Candidate. The committee has conferred. We have heard your reasons for withdrawing the first proposal. We have read the outline for the new topic. Doc. Lukešová has already reviewed your proposal and voted in favor. The full committee will deliberate on the withdrawal and the new proposal, and we will get back to you in two weeks."*
- Simonka: *"I understand. Thank you."*
- Veselá: *"You may go."*
- The meeting is over. It has lasted approximately fifty minutes.

---

### Beat 6 — THE CLOSING AND THE IMMEDIATE RETURN

*Simonka gathers her things. The old proposal folder goes into her bag. The single sheet of paper stays on the table. She leaves the university and returns immediately to Holic's mansion.*

- Lukešová does not walk Simonka out. The relationship has shifted — not because of warmth or coldness, but because the terms have changed. Simonka is no longer a student with an approved proposal. She is a student waiting for a committee's decision that will come in two weeks.
- Havelka does not look up. He is reading his journal article again. He has already asked the question that matters to him. He will ask it again when the full proposal arrives.
- Procházková gathers her notes. She does not look at Simonka. She has already identified the institutional risk. She will wait for the full proposal to determine whether the risk is manageable.
- Bartoš offers a small, genuine smile. It costs him nothing and is, in the moment, worth something to Simonka. She does not smile back — she is too focused on what comes next — but she nods.
- Veselá watches Simonka leave. She does not know it yet, but she is watching a student who will, in six months, split this committee 3–2 on a topic that no one in this room fully understands. **Veselá's instinct — that this is clarity, not panic — is correct. But correct instincts do not make the work easier.**

---

### Beat 7 — THE IMMEDIATE RETURN TO HOLIC'S MANSION

*Simonka leaves the university. She does not go to her apartment. She returns immediately to Holic's mansion.*

- She does not call ahead. She does not text. She does not delay. The committee has given her two weeks to wait, and the waiting begins now. She walks from the Faculty of Arts directly to Holic's mansion.
- The walk is long. She does not take the tram. She walks the entire way. The walk gives her time to fold the academic world away — the committee, the proposal, the two weeks of waiting — and prepare to enter the household world.
- She does not stop. She does not look in at the café where she has, twice in the past, sat with Holic. She does not sit on the bench by the river where she once sat in GG's collar for an hour. She walks.
- When she arrives at the mansion, the gates are open. She walks through the front door. The foyer is quiet.
- She does not find Holic. She does not announce herself. She simply begins to undress — right there in the foyer, immediately upon entering. Blouse, skirt, cardigan, shoes. The academic clothes come off. The university stays behind the door.
- She sits in the pet pose. On the foyer floor. Naked. Waiting. No command. No instruction. She knows what the pose is. She knows where the floor is. She knows what comes next.
- Bruno comes out of the living room.
- He does not circle. He does not hesitate. He takes her immediately on the foyer floor.
- The committee's two weeks have not even begun. Simonka's university clothes are in a pile by the door. The brand is under nothing now. The waiting is over before it started.

### Beat 8 — THE KENNEL DOOR

*The squeeze chute did not pan out. The ten pets were not brought in. The apparatus sits empty. The waiting begins differently.*

- After Bruno takes her on the foyer floor, Simonka does not get up immediately. She lies there. The academic clothes are still in a pile by the door. The committee's two weeks are still ahead of her.
- Then she shifts. Not to standing. To crawling. She is GG now, and GG crawls.
- Bruno shifts beside her. He does not leave. He follows.
- Together — GG and Bruno — they crawl from the foyer, through the house, out the back door, across the yard, to the kennel.
- They arrive at the kennel. They stop. They settle. No door. The kennel is open — always has been, always will be.
- GG inside. Bruno beside her. Both waiting. Neither commanded. Neither told what comes next.
- The kennel is open. The yard is quiet. The two weeks of waiting begin here — not in steel and pressure, but in the open, at the kennel, together.
- The squeeze chute did not pan out. The committee's decision is still two weeks away. But GG and Bruno are at the kennel, and that is enough for now.
### Beat 9 -- KLARKA PAVLINKA ARRIVES (THE MORNING AFTER)

*The day after the committee meeting. Morning. The kennel. The yard is quiet except for the sound of breathing.*

- Nine hours. Simonka and Bruno have been together in the kennel for nine hours. Not in the chute -- in the kennel itself, on the floor, in the open space with no door. They arrived together after Bruno took her on the foyer floor, and they have not separated since. The committee's two-week waiting period has barely begun, and already it is filled with everything that cannot be spoken about at university.
- They are both exhausted. Both sleeping. Not in positions of dignity or ceremony -- collapsed, tangled, heavy with the kind of fatigue that comes from sustained physical and psychological surrender. The kennel does not judge. It simply holds them.
- Holic is in the house. He has not checked on them. He does not need to. He knows what the kennel holds, and he knows that checking would break the thing that is holding it together.
- Then: a knock. Morning. The front door.
- Holic opens it. **Klarka Pavlinka is standing on the threshold.**

She has not called ahead. She has not texted. She has come here because she knows something has changed -- not the specifics, not the thesis or the committee or the proposal, but the shape of her sister's absence. Simonka was supposed to come home after the meeting. She did not. Klarka checked the apartment. Empty. She called Simonka's phone. No answer. She knew, with the certainty of a fifteen-month-older sister who shares the same blood and the same family architecture, that the only place Simonka could be was here.

- Holic opens the door and welcomes her in. *"Klarka. Good morning. Come inside."*
- He does not apologize. He does not explain. He invites her in as he would invite any guest -- because that is what she is, for now. A guest. A sister. A visitor to a world she does not yet understand.

**Klarka's anger:**
- She is very angry with him. Not confused. Not curious. *Angry.* She has seen Simonka enough times since the thesis change, enough late arrivals, enough absences, enough unexplained silences, to know what this house is. She does not know the details -- the committee, the two weeks, the proposal, the squeeze chute that did not pan out -- but she knows enough.
- She knows that Simonka has been turned into a human pet.
- She knows that Holic is the man who did it.
- She does not come here to ask questions. She comes here to confront the man who has taken her sister and remade her into something that is no longer a student, no longer a daughter, no longer a person with a future in psychology at Charles University.

- Holic does not argue. He does not defend himself. He simply leads her to the back door, opens it, and shows her the kennel.

*The kennel. The yard. The open space with no door.*

- Simonka and Bruno are still sleeping. Exhausted. Nine hours. The morning light falls across the kennel floor and illuminates the collapse -- not the dramatic, theatrical collapse the press would write about, but the quiet, heavy, animal exhaustion of two bodies that have been together for too long to separate and too tired to move.
- Klarka sees them.
- Her anger does not disappear. It crystallizes. But it does not harden into silence. It becomes a demand.
- Klarka: *"Simonka. Wake up. We need to talk."*
- Simonka stirs. Nine hours of exhaustion does not break in an instant. But Bruno stirs first.
- Bruno wakes. He is immediate. Instinct takes over. He moves toward GG, driven by the familiar rhythm of the kennel.
- Simonka's eyes snap open. She is not GG in that moment. She is Simonka Kata.
- Simonka: *"Red."*
- Bruno stops immediately. The safeword cuts through the instinct. He backs away, settles, waits.
- Simonka stands. She is exhausted. She is naked. But she is upright. She turns to Klarka.
- Simonka: *"What the hell are you doing here?"*
- Holic stands beside her. He does not intervene. He does not soften the exchange. He watches. He lets the sisters speak.
- The committee's two weeks are still ahead. The kennel is open. Bruno is waiting. But Klarka is here. And she came for an answer.

## WHAT SIMONKA CARRIES OUT OF THE ROOM

- **The old proposal is withdrawn but not yet finalized.** She has not yet signed the withdrawal form. The committee will decide in two weeks. But the decision is made. Ten months of work — field contacts, literature reviews, draft chapters — will be filed and forgotten. The cost is real, and she has accepted it.
- **The new proposal exists in her head and on one sheet of paper.** She has two weeks to wait for the committee's decision. She has, in fact, been working on it for months — the single sheet of paper is the tip of an iceberg she has been building in secret, in the margins of the foster-care work, in the hours between midnight and 3 AM when she could not sleep because the question would not leave her alone. She does not write during these two weeks. She waits. The squeeze chute did not pan out. She is at the kennel door with Bruno. The two weeks are the waiting.
- **She has not told anyone.** Not Klárka. Not her parents. Not Holic (if Holic and she are already in contact at this point — the timeline allows for it). **The proposal is hers alone.** This is the first time in the saga that Simonka holds something entirely by herself, without sharing it with anyone who knows her other life.
- **She is afraid.** Not of the work — she is not afraid of work. She is afraid of what the work will reveal. Not to the committee. To herself. The thesis is, she knows even now, a way of answering a question she has not yet fully articulated. If she writes it, she will find out what the question is. **She does not yet know whether she wants to know.**

## WHAT EACH COMMITTEE MEMBER CARRIES (after the meeting)

- **Lukešová:** Curious, cautious, committed to rigor. She will read the full proposal when it arrives in two weeks. She will evaluate it methodologically, not emotionally. She will likely approve it — but the approval will come with conditions, and the conditions will shape the thesis as much as the topic itself. **She does not know about the Collegium. She will not know until much later, and even then, only in fragments.**
- **Veselá:** Neutral on the substance, observant on the student. She has registered the candidate's conviction and the word "I" in the answer to the continuity question. She does not yet know what to make of either. **She will not know the full shape of this thesis until the defense, six months later, when she will cast the deciding yes vote.**
- **Havelka:** Methodologically alert. He has already identified the recruitment problem. He will, if the proposal reaches the full committee, vote no — not because the topic is wrong, but because the methodology is insufficient. **His eventual no vote is honest. It is also, Simonka will come to understand, the most valuable thing anyone can give a researcher: a clear statement of what must be fixed before the work begins.**

### The Question Behind the Question
