# THE RECORDS — THE FINDING

The rain over Josefov was a thin, grey needlework that blurred the sharp edges of the Old-New Synagogue. Inside Rabbi Eliáš Löwy’s study, the air smelled of old paper, cold tea, and the specific, dry scent of the archives.

Klára sat on the edge of the velvet chair, her back as straight as it had been at the barre an hour before. She was twenty-seven years old, a mother now herself, and the weight of the daughter sleeping in the pram back at the apartment—Klárka Pavlínka—had changed the gravity of her own life. She could no longer be a girl with a missing map.

Gamaliel sat beside her, silent. He had driven her here, found the parking, held the umbrella, and then stepped back. This was the Thursday hour he usually spent in study, but today the books were closed.

On the desk between them lay a single manila folder. It was not thick, but it was dense.

Rabbi Löwy looked at Klára over the top of his spectacles. He did not smile, but his eyes were steady. "I told you years ago, Klára, that names want to be found. They wait. But they do not push." He rested a hand on the folder. "I will tell you what is here. And then the paper is yours."

Klára nodded. Her throat felt tight, a familiar ghost of the foster-care years, but she pushed through it. "Tell me."

"Your mother’s line," Löwy began, his voice dropping into the cadence of a storyteller who knows the ending is heavy. "The foster file you brought me had one copy of a birth certificate—your own. But the archives of the *Matriky* and the Federation records have more. We followed the maternal line back from your mother, Jana."

He opened the folder.

"Your grandmother was **Hana Ginzová**. She was born in Prague, 1922. She survived the war in hiding, in a cellar in the Malá Strana. We found her marriage record from 1946. And her mother—your great-grandmother—was **Růžena Steinerová**."

Klára whispered the name. "Růžena."

"Růžena Steinerová," Löwy repeated. "She was born in 1898 in the town of Kolín. She moved to Prague in 1919. She lived at Maiselova 12."

Klára’s breath hitched. Maiselova 12. "That’s... that’s three doors from the restaurant where we go for Sunday lunch. I’ve walked past that door a hundred times."

"You have been walking her street," Löwy said softly. "But there is more. The maternal line is unbroken, Klára. Růžena to Hana, Hana to Jana, Jana to you. Under the Law, you were never outside. You were born a daughter of Israel. You have been carrying a citizenship you simply did not know how to read."

Gamaliel reached out, his hand finding Klára’s. He didn't say anything. He was the convert, the one who had spent years earning his place at this table. Klára was the one who had been at the table all along, sitting in the dark.

"And Růžena?" Klára asked, her voice trembling. "What happened to her?"

Löwy sighed, a sound like dry leaves. He slid a photocopy across the desk. It was a transport list, the columns of names typed with a cold, mechanical precision.

"Transport Au 1, September 1942," Löwy said. "From Prague to Theresienstadt. Růžena Steinerová is number 412. She did not return. She died in the camp in the winter of 1943."

The silence in the room was absolute. Klára looked at the name. Number 412. A woman who had lived on Maiselova Street, who had given birth to Hana, who had passed down a line that had eventually reached a foster child in Carlisle, Ontario.

"There is a date," Löwy said, pointing to a notation in the margin of a different document. "The death was recorded by the camp administration. The 14th of Kislev. In the secular calendar that year, it was the 12th of December."

Klára took the folder. She felt the texture of the paper, the weight of the ink. She thought of Klárka Pavlínka, sleeping in her crib, and the way her tiny hands gripped her mother’s fingers. 

"She has a name now," Klára said, looking at Gamaliel. "My daughter. She isn't just named for me. She’s named for a line."

"We will go to the Pinkas Synagogue," Gamaliel said, his voice low and certain. "Now. Her name is on the wall."

Klára stood up. She felt taller, as if the missing pieces of her spine had finally been slotted into place. She turned to Rabbi Löwy. "Thank you, Eliáš."

"Do not thank me for giving you what was always yours," the Rabbi said. "But do one thing. The 14th of Kislev. Keep the day. Light a candle. Say the words. That is how the name stays found."

***

They walked the short distance to the Pinkas Synagogue in silence. Inside, the walls were covered from floor to ceiling in the names of the 77,297 Bohemian and Moravian Jews who had been murdered. The names were written in red and black, a tidal wave of lost lives.

Klára found the section for Kolín and Prague. She ran her finger down the columns, her heart hammering against her ribs.

*Steinerová Růžena 14. 2. 1898 – 12. 12. 1943*

She pressed her finger against the red ink of the name. It was cold, the stone of the wall unyielding. But for the first time in her life, Klára didn't feel like a ghost. She felt like a daughter.

"I'm here," she whispered to the wall. "And your great-great-granddaughter is here too. We came back."

Gamaliel stood behind her, his hands on her shoulders. 

Klára took a deep breath. "I want to write to Rabbi Loewy in Vaughan. I want him to know. I want him to see that the boy he taught and the girl he saved... we’re whole now."

"He'll come for the wedding," Gamaliel said. "I know he will."

Klára nodded, still looking at Růžena's name. The calendar of her life had just acquired a new, permanent date. The 14th of Kislev. A day for a candle. A day for a name. A day for home.
