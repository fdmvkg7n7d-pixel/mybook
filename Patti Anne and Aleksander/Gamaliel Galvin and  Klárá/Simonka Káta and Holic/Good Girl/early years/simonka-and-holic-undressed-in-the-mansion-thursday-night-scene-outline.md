# Simonka and Holic — Undressed in the Mansion — Thursday Night Scene Outline

## Placement

**When:** Late Thursday night, immediately after Simonka Káta tells Holic that she confronted Patti Anne and Aleksander.

**Where:** Holic Bartunek's mansion, Prague. A non-pet space: kitchen, study, or Wolfdog room.

**Continuity Lead-in:**

- Thursday evening: Simonka confronted Patti Anne and Aleksander. At the door, Aleksander almost asked her to call or text when she got there, but stopped himself. Simonka is going to Holic's mansion. She will be with Holic. The new boundary means her family does not ask for a check-in.
- Later Thursday night: Simonka returns to Holic's mansion and tells him what she did. She makes clear that her boundary with Patti Anne and Aleksander is not Holic's victory over her family. The family door remains open.
- This scene begins after that conversation, with Simonka in Holic's mansion in person. She does **not** call Holic, and she does **not** call her family. She is already where she chose to be.
- **Canon correction:** the **dog collar stays on permanently**. It is already on her neck in this scene and is not removed. The question is not whether the collar goes on; the question is whether Holic and Simonka treat the permanent collar as a reason to erase Simonka's voice.

---

## Scene Purpose

This scene turns the aftermath away from phone contact and into physical presence.

Simonka is with Holic in his mansion. She has told him what she did. Now she needs to transition out of the clothes and posture of the family confrontation without having the next GG step assumed for her.

By the end of the scene:

1. Simonka gets undressed by her own choice.
2. The dog collar stays on permanently throughout the scene.
3. Holic does not treat undressing, or the permanent collar, as automatic consent to silence or full GG-form.
4. Simonka chooses the next step herself: she goes into GG-register and crawls to her bowls to eat.
5. The family door remains open offstage, but no one calls and no one is called.

---

## Emotional Core

Simonka has just done two difficult things in one night:

- She confronted Patti Anne and Aleksander.
- She told Holic about it without letting him turn her boundary into his victory.

Now she wants to remove the clothes she wore for that confrontation.

The undressing is not a sexual display and not a surrender into GG. It is a transition. The clothes carry the family house, the porch light, Patti Anne's careful apology, Aleksander stopping himself at the door, the dogs watching her leave. Taking them off is not rejecting those things. It is setting them down.

The collar does **not** come off. That is now part of the permanence of Holic's claim and Simonka's chosen GG life. But permanence does not mean muteness in every room. The scene's central rule becomes:

**Collared is not the same as voiceless. Undressed is not the same as gone. Crawling to the bowls must still be chosen, not assumed.**

---

## Tone

Quiet, intimate, non-explicit. The focus is agency, pacing, and the difference between vulnerability, permanent marking, and disappearance.

---

## Scene Outline

### Beat 1 — The Room After the Telling

Simonka has finished telling Holic about confronting Patti Anne and Aleksander.

The room is still.

The dog collar is on her neck. It has been on her neck. It is not on the table, not waiting in Holic's hand, not a threshold to be crossed later. It is already part of the scene.

There is no call to make. No phone-centered beat. Simonka is already with Holic. Her family knows she is going back to the mansion, and part of the new boundary is that they do not require her to prove she arrived.

Holic does not move first.

This is important. If he treats the permanent collar as a command to resume GG-space immediately, he turns the confrontation into something to erase. The strongest choice is that he waits.

---

### Beat 2 — Simonka Names What She Wants

Simonka stands or looks down at her clothes.

She says, simply:

> I want to take these off.

Holic hears the sentence but does not make it larger than it is.

He asks:

> Do you want privacy?

Simonka may answer:

> No.

Or:

> No. Just don't make it mean more than it means.

This is the key condition. She is choosing to undress. She is not choosing every possible thing that might follow. The collar being permanent does not change that.

---

### Beat 3 — Holic Accepts the Limit

Holic responds quietly.

Possible line:

> Then it means what you say it means.

Or, simpler:

> I understand.

He should not praise her yet. Praise would tilt the moment toward GG. He should not command her. He should not touch her unless she asks.

The point is restraint.

---

### Beat 4 — Simonka Gets Undressed

Simonka undresses herself.

Keep the description non-graphic and focused on action and meaning:

- coat off;
- shoes off;
- blouse, skirt, dress, or whatever she wore to the family house removed;
- clothes folded over the back of a chair or placed neatly on a bench.

The folding matters. She is not discarding the family confrontation. She is putting it down carefully.

The collar remains on her neck the entire time.

She may think of Patti Anne's face. Aleksander's hand stopping before the door. The way the dogs stayed behind her when she left.

She remains upright. She remains Simonka.

Possible line:

> I can take my clothes off and still not disappear.

A beat, because the collar is visible and permanent.

> I can wear this and still be the one talking.

Holic answers:

> Yes.

That answer should be quiet and exact. He must understand the rule she is setting: her body entering vulnerability, and the collar staying on, are not the same as her voice leaving the room.

---

### Beat 5 — The Clothes Are Set Aside

The clothes are now folded.

The visual should be clear:

- Simonka undressed;
- clothes folded;
- dog collar still on.

This is the new triangle of the scene. The clothes are the family house. The collar is Holic/GG. Simonka is between them, not erased by either.

Holic may look at the clothes, then at her, then at the collar. He does not touch the clothes. He does not touch the collar.

Possible Holic line:

> Do you want them put away?

Simonka:

> No. Leave them there.

Leaving the clothes visible matters. She is not hiding where she has been.

---

### Beat 6 — The Permanent Collar Is Named

Because the collar stays on permanently, the scene needs to name what that means now.

Holic may ask, carefully:

> Do you want to be GG tonight?

But the question is no longer about putting on the collar. It is about posture, speech, rules, and how far into pet-space she wants to go while the collar remains on.

Simonka looks down — not because she can remove it, but because she can feel it.

She may answer first with the thing she does **not** want:

> I don't want to disappear into it tonight.

Then, after a beat, she names the thing she **does** want:

> But I want my bowls.

This is the pivot. She is not refusing GG. She is refusing to let GG erase the conversation she just had. She wants the household's pet routine — the bowls, the floor, the meal — but she wants it as a choice she names while still fully aware of what happened with Patti Anne and Aleksander.

Holic hears the distinction. He must not punish it or rush past it.

---

### Beat 7 — Holic Accepts the Choice

Holic's response defines the scene.

Possible line:

> Then go to them, Good Girl.

Simple. No argument.

The collar remains on. That is not in question. But the shift into GG-register happens because Simonka named what she wanted and Holic accepted it. He does not put words in her mouth. He does not treat the permanent collar as sufficient by itself. He gives the transition its proper address.

The pet name returns. Simonka lets it land.

She is still carrying the family confrontation. She is also GG.

Both are true.

---

### Beat 8 — She Crawls to Her Bowls to Eat

Simonka lowers herself to the floor.

This is the first full physical shift of the scene. The undressing was one choice. Going to the bowls is another.

She crawls to the place where her bowls belong — the mat near the kitchen wall, the low place in Holic's mansion that is hers in GG-register. Holic either has already set the bowls there or sets them down as she moves:

- water bowl;
- food bowl;
- the familiar dog food that belongs to GG's household routine.

Keep the prose non-graphic and practical. This is not spectacle. It is dinner, in the form this household uses for GG.

Holic may say only:

> Eat.

Or:

> Good Girl. Eat.

GG eats from her bowl. She drinks from her water bowl. The clothes she wore to confront Patti Anne and Aleksander remain folded nearby. The collar remains on her neck. No one from the family calls.

This image is the point of the scene: she has not been dragged from family speech into pet silence. She has spoken, undressed, chosen the bowls, and crawled to them herself.

---

### Beat 9 — No One Calls

The absence of a call should be felt but not over-explained.

No one from the family calls Simonka.

Simonka does not call them.

Holic does not ask whether she wants to call, because the point tonight is that she is already here and she has already spoken. If she wanted the family, she could reach for them. She does not.

This is not abandonment. It is the first night of the new boundary holding on both sides.

Possible internal beat for Simonka:

She knows they are not calling. She knows what it costs them not to call. For a moment, that knowledge is almost harder than if they had broken the rule.

She eats anyway.

---

### Beat 10 — Closing Image

Simonka is in GG-register now, but not because Holic rushed her there.

Her clothes are folded nearby. The collar is on her neck. GG is at her bowls. Holic stands or sits nearby, present but not pressing.

The final image mirrors the family-side scene:

- At Gamaliel and Klára's house, Patti Anne and Aleksander do not call.
- At Holic's mansion, GG eats from her bowls.

Both sides practice restraint, in completely different languages.

Final possible line/image:

The clothes stay folded. The collar stays on. GG eats from the bowl she chose to crawl to. No one calls.

## What This Scene Establishes

1. **The dog collar stays on permanently.** It is already on Simonka's neck and is not removed. This scene should not imply the collar is optional or waiting on a table.
2. **There is no call because Simonka is with Holic in his mansion.** The family has not been asked for a check-in, and Holic is physically present.
3. **Simonka gets undressed without disappearing into GG.** Undressing becomes its own discrete choice: she removes the clothes from the family confrontation, but the collar remains on.
4. **Simonka chooses the bowls.** She does enter GG-register, but only after naming what she wants: she wants her bowls. Crawling to eat becomes her chosen transition, not Holic rushing her out of Simonka's voice.
5. **Holic passes an important test, if he accepts the sequence.** He tolerates the undressing, hears the distinction, and lets her crawl to her bowls because she asked for that form.
6. **The family boundary holds offstage.** The absence of a call is part of the new agreement, not a sign that no one cares.
7. **Permanent collar does not mean constant silence.** Simonka can be vulnerable and collared in the mansion, speak first, and then choose pet routine.
