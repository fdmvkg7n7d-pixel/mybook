# Bruno's Owner Visits the Club President — Scene Outline

## Placement

**When:** The second Monday, late at night (around midnight). The monthly meeting is the next day — the first Tuesday.  
**Where:** The private offices of the Collegium, accessed through a side entrance. The President's office, the same one Holic stormed into hours earlier.  
**Continuity:** Takes place after Holic's late-night confrontation with the Club President (`holic-meets-club-president-scene-outline.md`). The President has already promised to convene the disciplinary committee at the monthly meeting. Bruno's owner has been temporarily banned from club facilities. He comes to the President privately, through a back entrance, after hours on the second Monday — the night before the first Tuesday meeting.

---

## Characters

- **Bruno's Owner**: The man who gagged Good Girl, chained her in the box truck, and delivered her to the other owner's mansion. Desperate, defensive, but not yet panicked. He has been temporarily banned from the club. He has heard about Holic's visit.
- **The Club President**: A powerful, older man who oversees the Collegium's rules and discipline. Elegant, composed, used to handling crises behind closed doors. He has already heard Holic's version of events.
- **Secret relationship:** The Club President and Bruno's owner are second cousins. The members of the Collegium do not know this. It is a family connection they have kept hidden, maintained with care and occasional private meetings.

---

## Scene Outline

### Beat 1: The Side Entrance

Bruno's owner arrives at the Collegium through a side entrance — not the main doors, not the member's entrance, but a service corridor that leads directly to the private offices. He has a key. Not a member's key. A family key.

The building is mostly dark. The late-night staff have gone. The secretary has left. Only the President's office is still lit, the door unlocked, the light spilling into the corridor like an invitation.

Bruno's owner does not announce himself. He does not knock. He walks in.

The President is at his desk, still reviewing the notes from Holic's visit — the gag, the box truck, the twenty pets, the withheld medications, the broken bones. He looks up when the door opens.

He is not surprised.

> "I knew you would come."

Bruno's owner closes the door behind him. He does not sit.

> "Did he tell you everything?"

The President sets down his pen.

> "He told me enough."

### Beat 2: The Family Connection

The President stands. He walks to the window, pulls the shade slightly — not fully closed, just enough that the street below cannot see into the office. This is a gesture born of habit, not paranoia. He has always been careful about what the Collegium sees.

Then he turns back, and for the first time in the scene, his posture changes. The formal stiffness of the Club President drops. He speaks in a different register — not the measured, institutional voice he used with Holic, but something closer, older, more familiar.

> "You should not have gagged her, cousin."

He says *cousin* without looking up. Without emphasis. As if it is the most natural word in the world.

Bruno's owner exhales. The tension that has been holding his shoulders rigid since the ban finally breaks — not into panic, but into something more dangerous. Relief. The relief of a man who has been carrying a secret and has just been handed permission to set it down.

> "I didn't think—"

> "You never do."

The President moves to a side cabinet, opens it, and pours two glasses of something — brandy, or whiskey, or whatever the President keeps for family. He hands one to Bruno's owner. They do not clink glasses. They do not toast. They drink.

This is the first time in the scene that the reader understands: they are not just owner and President. They are not just club members who happen to know each other. They are blood. Second cousins. The family that shares a name the Collegium has never heard and would never connect to the President's public identity.

### Beat 3: What the Members Don't Know

The President sits. Bruno's owner finally does too — not in the visitor's chair, but in the chair beside the desk, the one no one else is permitted to use. The chair that has always been there, but always been empty when anyone else was in the room.

The President speaks, and his words are careful, precise, the way a man speaks when he is weighing both family loyalty and institutional survival:

> "The monthly meeting is tomorrow. The first Tuesday. Holic will be there. He will be on the Circle. He wants both of you expelled — you and the other owner. He wants your names struck from every record."

Bruno's owner's grip tightens on his glass.

> "I didn't break any rules. The gag—"

> "She couldn't safeword."

> "She didn't need to. She was willing."

The President looks at him. Not with judgment. With the exhausted patience of a man who has had this conversation with his cousin before — not about the Collegium, but about the things his cousin does when he thinks no one is watching.

> "It does not matter if she was willing. The rule is absolute. No gag during an interaction. No restraint that prevents a pet from speaking. You know this. I know this. Holic knows this."

Bruno's owner is silent. The brandy sits untouched.

The President continues:

> "The other owner withheld her medications. Broke her wrist. Broke her feet. Twenty pets. You delivered her to all of it. You put the gag on her. You chained her in a box truck."

He pauses.

> "This is not a rule violation, cousin. This is a catastrophe."

### Beat 4: The Defense

Bruno's owner leans forward. His voice is lower now, less defensive, more strategic. He is not a stupid man — he has survived in the Collegium for years by reading rooms, by knowing when to push and when to yield. He knows the President is his only ally in this building. The only one who has a reason to protect him that is not institutional.

> "The other owner wanted her. Not me. He proposed the arrangement. I facilitated it. That is not the same as doing it."

The President considers this. He does not agree or disagree. He files it.

> "Holic does not care about the distinction. He wants you both out."

> "Then we give him something else."

> "Like what?"

Bruno's owner sets his glass down. He has been thinking about this since the ban was announced. Since the moment Holic stormed out of the President's office and the word spread through the Collegium's late-night channels that something had happened.

> "We give him a reason not to push for expulsion. We make it about procedure, not punishment. We say the gag was a mistake — regrettable, yes, but not malicious. We say the other owner exceeded the scope of the arrangement. We say we are sorry. We say we will comply with whatever disciplinary measure the Circle imposes — a warning, a suspension, a fine. But not expulsion."

The President listens. He does not interrupt. He has heard this tone before — from his cousin, from other members, from men who have made mistakes and are now trying to negotiate the consequences.

> "And if Holic does not accept that?"

> "Then we make it about the club's reputation. About what happens to the Collegium if this goes public. About the Concursus. About the bylaws. About procedure."

The President nods slowly. He is not convinced. But he is considering.

> "You are asking me to choose between the rules and my family."

Bruno's owner does not answer. He does not need to.

### Beat 5: The President's Position

The President stands. He walks back to the window, pulls the shade a little further. He does this not because he is hiding, but because he is thinking — and thinking requires privacy, even from the walls of his own office.

He speaks without turning around:

> "The monthly meeting is tomorrow. The first Tuesday. I will convene the disciplinary committee as promised. I will hear both sides. I will vote with the Circle."

Bruno's owner's expression tightens.

> "You're not going to help me."

> "I am helping you. I am keeping the ban temporary instead of permanent. I am making sure you get a hearing instead of an expulsion. I am keeping your name out of the minutes until the committee decides."

He turns back.

> "What I will not do is break the rules for you. Not in public. Not in the Circle. Not where the other owners can see."

Bruno's owner is quiet. He knows his cousin. He knows that when the President says *not in public*, he means *not where it can be traced back to us*. He does not take this as a rejection. He takes it as the best his cousin can offer within the constraints of the institution.

> "And privately?"

The President looks at him. There is no warmth in the look — but there is no coldness either. It is the look of a man who has carried the weight of family loyalty for a long time and has learned to balance it against everything else.

> "Privately, I will do what I can. But you must understand: Holic is on the Circle. Dr. Past is against you. The Steward will follow procedure even when it hurts you. Liesl and Bohdana are swing votes. You do not have a majority."

Bruno's owner absorbs this. He knows the Circle's composition. He knows the math. He knows he is fighting an uphill battle.

> "Then what do I do?"

The President sits back down. He folds his hands. He speaks in the voice of a man who has spent his entire life navigating institutions, reading power, and protecting the people he cares about — even when they do not make it easy.

> "You attend the first Tuesday meeting. You apologize. You accept whatever penalty the Circle imposes. You do not argue. You do not defend yourself in the meeting. You let the Steward run the procedure. You let the Circle vote. And you trust me to make sure the penalty does not include expulsion."

> "And if they vote for expulsion anyway?"

> "Then I will use my authority as President to suspend the ruling pending review. It will buy you time. But it will not save you forever."

Bruno's owner nods. He understands. He is not happy. But he understands.

### Beat 6: The Other Owner

There is a question that has been sitting in the room since the beginning, unspoken but heavy:

> "What about the other owner?"

Bruno's owner is not asking out of concern. He is asking out of strategy. If the other owner is expelled and Bruno's owner is not, the disparity will be visible. It will create a fracture. It will make the President's position harder.

The President considers this. He knows the other owner — not personally, but by reputation. A man who takes his pets too far. A man who has been warned before. A man who is, by any reasonable standard, more culpable than Bruno's owner.

> "The other owner withheld her medications. Broke her bones. Used twenty pets on her over two nights. His culpability is greater. The Circle will see that. Holic knows that."

He pauses.

> "The other owner is your problem, not mine. You facilitated. He executed. The Circle will distinguish between the two."

Bruno's owner absorbs this. It is not the answer he wanted. But it is the answer he will work with.

### Beat 7: The Departure

Bruno's owner finishes his brandy. He sets the glass down. He stands.

> "I'll be at the meeting."

> "You will be at the first Tuesday meeting. You will apologize. You will accept the penalty. You will not argue."

> "I know."

The President does not stand. He does not walk his cousin to the door. This is not a rejection — it is a boundary. In this office, they are President and member. In the side corridor, they are second cousins. In the Circle meeting tomorrow, they will be President and accused. The boundaries matter.

Bruno's owner opens the door. He pauses.

> "Thank you. For keeping the ban temporary."

The President does not look up from his notes.

> "Do not make me regret it."

The door closes. The office is quiet again. The President sits alone at his desk, the notes from Holic's visit spread before him, the weight of the first Tuesday meeting on his shoulders, and the knowledge that tomorrow he will have to choose — in public, in front of the Circle, in front of Holic — between the rules of the Collegium and the blood of his family.

He does not yet know which he will choose.

## Emotional Core

- **The hidden bond**: The President and Bruno's owner are second cousins. The Collegium does not know. This is a secret that shapes every interaction in the scene — the side entrance, the private chair, the brandy, the use of the word *cousin*.
- **Institutional survival vs. family loyalty**: The President is torn between his duty to the Collegium and his obligation to his family. He does not resolve this tension in the scene — he manages it.
- **Bruno's owner's desperation**: He is not panicking. He is calculating. He knows he made a mistake, and he knows the Collegium's rules are real. But he also knows his cousin is the President, and he is counting on that connection to soften the blow.
- **The President's authority**: He is not cruel. He is not corrupt. He is a man who has spent years building a careful balance between family and institution, and he will not let one destroy the other — not easily, not without a fight.
- **The ticking clock**: The monthly meeting is tomorrow — the first Tuesday. There is no delay, no postponement. The confrontation is imminent.
