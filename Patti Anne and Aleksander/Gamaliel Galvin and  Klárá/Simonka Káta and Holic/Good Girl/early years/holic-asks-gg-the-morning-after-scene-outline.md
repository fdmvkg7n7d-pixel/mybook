# HOLIC ASKS GG THE MORNING AFTER

## Scene Outline — Beats, Dialogue Cues, and Thematic Notes

> **Companion to** `holic-invites-prostitute-gg-watches-scene-outline.md`. **Read that outline first.** This scene is the morning immediately following it.

> **This outline supersedes** the prostitute-scene's canon that *"Holic does not, in the morning, refer to the evening in conversation with GG."* He does. **He asks.** The supersession is itself a piece of who he is becoming — the previous outline left him as the owner who took the test as passed and did not look back; the morning makes him more troubled than that, and the saga should let the addition stand as a complication, not a correction. *He did not ask in aftercare last night. He did, after sleeping, decide that he had to ask in the morning. The interval is the man.*

> **Content & craft warning.** Same as the prostitute scene. Two consenting adults in a long-standing human-pet arrangement, the morning after a hard piece of training. Holic asks the two questions the household has not previously required him to ask. Simonka answers — truthfully, partially, and without ever fully telling him what she does not yet have the language to tell. The scene's content is the **gap between what Holic now knows enough to ask and what Simonka cannot yet say.** No erotic register. No glamour. No nudge-wink.

---

**POV:** **Simonka Káta.** Continuity from the prostitute scene.
**Tone:** Morning. Quieter than last night. Less held; more open. The platform-discipline has receded; the household's daytime register has resumed — closer to *Simonka in Holic's house in the morning* than to *GG on the platform in the dark place of the room*. The collar is still on her, but the position is not. She is in his bed, under his arm, in his robe (oversized). The prose can let the body be a body now, not an architecture.
**Location:** **Holic's bedroom.** The same room as the prostitute scene. The platform is still at the foot of the bed where it was last night. Outside, a Prague morning — early light through the windows, the small city sounds of a tram going past two streets over, a church bell at the half-hour from a parish she does not know the name of.
**Time in saga:** Hours after the prostitute scene's closing image. Pre-thesis (Simonka has not yet conceived the second thesis topic). The scene takes about thirty-five minutes in story-time, all of it in or near the bed.

---

## CANON ESTABLISHED BY THIS SCENE

*(So later writing can rely on it.)*

- **Holic asks. (CANON.)** The morning after the prostitute scene, Holic asks Simonka two specific questions: **(1) why she did not use her safewords, and (2) why she did not ask to go to her doghouse.** The asking is its own canonical fact about Holic. It places him, for the saga, in a different moral position than the previous outline's "he has decided he knows the answer" closing left him in. **He had decided he knew. He has, after sleeping, undecided.** That is the man this scene shows.
- **The doghouse-as-option is canon. (CANON — established here, retroactive to the GG arrangement.)** On any night Simonka is in GG-form at the mansion, **she has the standing option to ask to be moved to her outdoor doghouse.** The option is part of the household's negotiated apparatus, set at the beginning of GG (alongside the safewords, the bowls, the pet name, the platform, the pill). The doghouse is, in this architecture, *not* a punishment-cell; it is, in addition to being her sleeping-place on doghouse-weekends, **a permanently available exit from any specific scene she does not wish to be in.** Asking to go there is not safewording the relationship; it is safewording the room. **Holic established this option openly when GG began. He has, until last night, never had cause to refer to it in the middle of a scene.**
- **Simonka knew the option existed. (CANON.)** She did not, on the platform, forget it. She did not, on the platform, decide against it consciously. She held position. The held position was its own decision — but not, in the moment, a decision she experienced as a decision. *That she had options she did not take is a fact she discovers, in this scene, with Holic.*
- **Simonka does not lie to Holic, this morning or ever. (CANON — reinforces the prostitute scene's standing rule.)** She does, in this scene, withhold. The withholding is not lying. The line between the two is one of the saga's permanent moral questions, and the line is walked, in this scene, by Simonka in real time.
- **Simonka does not tell Holic about the doorway-look. (CANON for this scene.)** The prostitute's look, which was the prostitute scene's hinge, remains in Simonka's possession alone. She has decided — in some chamber of her mind she does not consult in words this morning — that the look is hers to carry. *(Whether she ever tells him is open. Suggested: she never does. The look is the first thing in her GG life that becomes wholly her own, and the not-telling is what makes it so.)*
- **The pill makes its first onstage appearance in this outline. (CANON — established by the prostitute outline's pill-regime canon; rendered for the first time in prose-shape here.)** Holic gives Simonka the morning pill from his own hand, with a glass of water, in the bed, near the end of the scene. **The gesture is identical to every other morning's pill in the household.** This morning — because of last night, because of the questions, because of the answers and the withholdings — the identical gesture lands differently. Neither of them remarks on the difference. The prose holds it.
- **Holic does not learn what the prostitute's look meant.** He continues to misunderstand the *"She looked at me"* sentence from the previous scene's bath, even as he asks better questions this morning than he asked last night. The misunderstanding is intact at the end of this scene. **He is asking the questions that surround the misunderstanding; he is not asking the question that would resolve it. He does not know there is a question that would resolve it.** Simonka, who does know, does not point him at it.

## BEFORE THE SCENE — WHAT SIMONKA CARRIES INTO THE MORNING

*She has slept some. The body has done what the body does with the residue of a held position. The mind has done less.*

- **She slept for about four hours.** Broken sleep — the kind one has the first night in any room one has not slept in before, even though she has slept in this room many times. *Tonight the room was a different room.*
- **The collar is still on her neck — and it will stay there.** This is now permanent-collar canon. She has woken with it; she has not taken it off; he has not taken it off; and that permanence is its own information. The unfinished quality of the morning is no longer measured by whether the collar comes off. It is measured by whether Holic releases her by name, posture, and routine. Last night's aftercare bath did not, in fact, complete — he praised her, he washed her, he put her in his robe, he put her in his bed, and *he left the conversation unfinished*. She has woken inside an aftercare extension that has not yet been formally closed. This is the household's first piece of information for her this morning, before any word has been spoken.
- **She does not yet know that he will ask.** She has prepared, on waking, for several possible mornings. The morning in which he asks the two questions he is about to ask is not one of the mornings she has prepared for.
- **She is hungry.** This will become, briefly, a fact later. She is also thirsty. She is also aware that she has not, this morning, eaten or drunk anything, and that the apparatus of the household includes specific provisions for both, and that the apparatus is suspended until the scene is formally closed.

---

## WHAT HOLIC CARRIES (briefly — seen from outside, throughout)

*The reader does not enter his head. What the reader sees this morning is a man who has woken and decided something. What it is, the prose will not name.*

- He has slept the easy sleep he sleeps after a piece of training he is satisfied with. He has woken between four-thirty and five. He has lain in the dark for some time before *Fajr* — *not Fajr, there is no Fajr in Prague, but the equivalent dark hour before the city's true morning* — and in that dark hour he has thought.
- What he has thought, the saga does not give the reader. The result of the thinking is the scene the reader is about to be in.
- **The collar he has not removed is not a temporary decision anymore; it is permanent.** Aftercare can resume in the morning; aftercare can include a conversation; the conversation happens while she remains collared. He has not, by leaving it on, denied her completion of aftercare; the collar now belongs to the ongoing architecture. The extension is in the questions he has not yet asked, not in a collar waiting to come off.

---

## BEAT 0 — WAKING

*Very short. Establish the room, the bodies, the light, the collar.*

- She opens her eyes. The light through the windows is the early-spring Prague light — soft, cool, the particular blue-grey of the city's morning before the sun fully clears the rooftops.
- His arm is across her shoulder. His breathing is the breathing of a man who has been awake for some time before she woke.
- She becomes aware, in a small cascade, of: the collar; her own thirst; the unfamiliar weight of the robe she fell asleep in; the absence of the position-discipline in her body, which is still slightly tender across her shoulders from the platform; the small fact that the morning is happening at all.
- He does not greet her. He does not move his arm. He waits for her to know that he is awake.
- She knows.

---

## BEAT 1 — THE COLLAR

*Brief. Before he asks anything, he clarifies the room's status.*

- He says, low, in Czech: *"I have left it on."* He means the collar. *(Render in Czech if you wish — *Nechal jsem ho na tobě* — but the English will carry it.)*
- She does not move. She says, in the small voice she uses with him at the close of a scene: *"I know."*
- He says: *"We are not finished. Is that all right."* It is half a question, half a permission-request. *He has, by phrasing it this way, given her the first opportunity to end the scene she has had since the prostitute walked in last night.*
- She says: *"Yes."* She means it. She is not sure why she means it. The not-sure-why is one of the small honest things in her this morning, and she does not, in answering, perform certainty she does not feel.
- He accepts the answer. He does not move his arm. The room settles.

---

## BEAT 2 — THE FIRST QUESTION

*The safeword question.*

- He waits a full minute. Then he says — still low, still not moving:

> "GG. You did not use a safeword last night. I want to ask you about that."

- It is not a reproach. It is not an apology. It is what it sounds like — *a question framed as a question*. **He uses her pet name, not her real name.** The collar is on; the scene is on; he is asking GG, not Simonka. *That he is asking GG is its own piece of information — he is asking the question inside the framework, not from outside it.* Whether this is appropriate or evasive is one of the saga's open readings, and the prose refuses to settle it.
- She does not answer for a beat.
- Then, into the small space under his arm: *"I did not want to."*
- She watches her own answer. *Is that true.* She is, in the half-second after she says it, the woman who finds out for the first time, from her own mouth, what she did not want to do. **The answer surprises her by being true.** She did not want to safeword. The not-wanting was the platform's whole content.
- He waits. He does not press. He has, evidently, decided in the dark hour that he will let her find her own sentences this morning.
- She finds the next one. *"I wanted you to know I could hold it."*
- This too is true. She is, as she says it, aware that *I wanted to please you* would be the same sentence in another household's vocabulary, and that the difference between *I wanted you to know I could hold it* and *I wanted to please you* is the difference between *a pet who has been trained well* and *a woman who is in love with the man training her*. **The difference is not as large as she wishes it were.** She does not say so.
- He absorbs the answer. He says, after his own beat: *"Holding it is not the only thing. Holding it is one of the things. The others are also things. Yellow is also a thing. Red is also a thing."*
- *(This is one of the most important sentences he has ever said to her in their relationship. It is the kind of sentence a good owner would say. It is also the kind of sentence a good owner would have said before the test, not after. The prose must let both stand. He is, in this morning, becoming a slightly better owner than he was last night — and the becoming, the saga must hold, does not retroactively change last night.)*
- She does not answer immediately. She turns her face slightly toward his collarbone. The robe shifts against her shoulder. The collar moves a quarter-inch against her throat.
- She says, eventually: *"I know."* And then, in a smaller voice: *"I will try to remember that."*
- It is the most honest sentence she has said to him in a week. *She will try. She is not certain she will succeed. She is not promising. She is naming the effort.*

---

## BEAT 3 — THE SECOND QUESTION

*The doghouse question. Harder.*

- He gives her a longer pause this time. Outside, a tram passes two streets over. The room takes the sound in and lets it go.
- Then:

> "GG. You also did not ask to go to your doghouse."

- This sentence is structured differently from the first. It is not a question. It is a *statement of a fact she did not, until this morning, fully know was a fact about the night.*
- She lies still. She has, until this moment, not consciously held in her mind that the doghouse was an option last night.
- Now she does.
- *(Internal beat: the doghouse. The doghouse exists. The doghouse has existed since the beginning of GG. The doghouse is, on her doghouse-weekends, her bedroom; in the household's larger architecture, it is the structure she may ask to be moved to **at any time during any scene**. She knows this. He told her this. She agreed to it. She has, until this morning, never had occasion to think about asking for it. **That she did not, last night, even consciously consider the option is the thing she will think about for a long time after this morning.**)*
- She finds, after a long beat, her sentence.

> "I did not think of it."

- It is true. *She did not think of it.* The doghouse, available, did not occur to her on the platform. The not-thinking-of-it is its own fact, and the fact is — she understands as she says it — *more uncomfortable to her than the not-using of the safewords*. The safewords she at least had in her mind for a moment. The doghouse she did not have in her mind at all.
- He absorbs this. He does not immediately answer.
- She continues — and this is the place in the scene where she comes closest to telling him the truth about the look. *She does not tell him.* She comes close. She skirts.

> "I think — I think I did not think I was allowed to leave. I know I was allowed to leave. I knew it. But on the platform — I did not have access to *knowing it.* I had access to the position. The position did not include leaving."

- She is, as she says this, doing for him in real time the kind of work she will, in two years, do in formal Czech academic prose for Marešová. **She does not know that.** *The reader, who has read the thesis-defense outline, knows.*
- He receives the answer. He says, after some time: *"Thank you for telling me that."*
- He does not say *good girl.* The not-saying-good-girl is itself a piece of information. He is, in this beat, holding her as a person who has just done a difficult piece of describing, not as a pet who has performed correctly. **The collar is on. The address is not.** The contradiction is one of the saga's most useful textures: *he has not closed the scene, and he has, for one sentence, addressed her outside the scene*. Both things at once.

---

## BEAT 4 — THE THING SHE NEARLY SAYS

*The doorway look, which the previous scene's outline established as Simonka's alone. She has the chance, in this beat, to give it to him. She does not.*

- He has thanked her. He has not asked the third question — the question that would, if asked, give her the opening to say what the prostitute did at the doorway. He does not ask it because he does not know there is a third question to ask. *He does not know the doorway happened in the form it happened in.*
- She, lying under his arm, considers — briefly, in a part of her mind she does not narrate — telling him.
- *She does not.* The reasons, as much as she can name them in the moment:
  - **Telling would require explaining**, and she does not yet have language adequate to the explaining.
  - **Telling would burden Holic with a fact he cannot resolve.** He cannot un-arrange last night; he cannot find the woman and apologise to her; the most he could do is feel bad. *Feeling-bad is a use of Holic she does not, this morning, wish to put him to.*
  - **Telling would give the look away.** The look is hers. It is the first thing in her GG life that is hers in the way a person's private things are hers. She is, this morning, learning that she has private things, and she does not, having just learned it, want to relinquish the first one.
  - **Telling would, on some level, be a small infidelity to the woman herself.** The woman gave her the look in professional silence and in personal silence; *to repeat the look to Holic would be to make of it something other than what it was given as.*
- All four reasons are real. None of them is the whole reason. *Simonka does not, this morning, know the whole reason. The saga will, perhaps, know it later.*
- She does not tell him.

---

## BEAT 5 — THE PILL

*The morning's smallest gesture and its largest weight. The pill makes its first onstage appearance.*

- He shifts, eventually. He sits up. The arm comes off her shoulder. He reaches to the bedside table — a glass of water already there, a small enamel dish with two pills in it, the morning's standard household provision.
- *(The two pills: her contraceptive, his unrelated medication — vitamin, supplement, whatever; render unspecific. The point is that the dish has two pills in it every morning, hers and his, and that the dish is the household's smallest piece of daily routine.)*
- He hands her her pill. He hands her the glass. **The gesture is identical to every other morning's pill in the household.**
- She takes the pill from his hand. She drinks the water.
- **Neither of them remarks on the gesture.** They do not need to. Both of them know what last night was; both of them know what the pill is; both of them know that the daily gesture of the pill is the gesture by which Holic, every morning of GG's life, has *been* the manager of her body in a way the saga will, eventually, have to interrogate.
- **This morning, after the questions and the answers and the not-tellings, the identical gesture lands differently.** It lands as: *I am still the manager of your body. You are still in this. We are still in this together. The pill is the proof.*
- *(For Simonka, the second reading — the one she does not articulate but feels — is harder: the gesture is also the small confirmation that **the architecture is intact**. Last night did not break it. This morning has not broken it. She is here, in his bed, in his collar, taking the pill from his hand — and the apparatus is still the apparatus, and she has not, in any beat of the scene, walked out of it. **That she has not walked out is not, this morning, something she feels regret about. It is something she feels at all** — and the feeling, whatever name it eventually gets, is information about who she has become.)*
- The pill goes down. The water goes down. He sets the glass back on the table.

---

## BEAT 6 — THE SCENE CLOSES; THE COLLAR STAYS ON

*Aftercare closes by name, posture, and routine. The dog collar does not come off. It stays on permanently.*

- He turns back to her. He says, quietly: *"You are done. Thank you, Good Girl."* He uses the pet name one last time — the household's formal closing of a scene.
- He does **not** unfasten the collar. The collar remains on her neck. The permanence is now part of the household's grammar.
- He looks at her. He says, without ceremony, in the ordinary register of two people in a bed in the morning: *"Simonka."*
- It is the first time he has said her name in eighteen hours.
- She says: *"Yes."*
- He says: *"Come and have breakfast."*
- It is, this morning, an enormous sentence — though it would not be enormous in any other house in any other relationship. **Coming to have breakfast is what people who live together do.** GG does not have breakfast with Holic. GG eats from a bowl on the floor, on the days she is in full GG-form. **Simonka can have breakfast with Holic while still collared.** That is the new architecture: the collar no longer decides alone who she is allowed to be. *That he is moving her, in the same sentence and in the same morning, from pet-register to person-register without removing the collar is the household's way of saying that the scene is over and the day is beginning.*
- She says: *"Yes."*

## BEAT 7 — THE ROOM, LEAVING IT

> **SUPERSEDED.** At this point in the morning, Simonka does not walk to the breakfast room. She asks to go to her doghouse instead. The scene continues in **`gg-walks-out-to-the-doghouse-scene-outline.md`**, whose Beats 1–10 supersede Beats 7–8 of the present outline. Under permanent-collar canon, the true ending of the morning-after conversation is not a collar being refastened; the collar is already on and stays on. The true ending is Simonka choosing the doghouse and Holic shifting her from person-register back into GG-register by name, posture, and permission. The breakfast-room beats below are kept for the saga's record of the morning that was planned and did not happen.

*Very brief. The scene's coda is the small ordinariness of getting up.*

- She gets out of bed. The robe, his, hangs on her. Her legs are unfamiliar to themselves; they have spent more of the last twelve hours in held position than in walking.
- She walks past the platform at the foot of the bed. She does not look at it. She does not avoid looking at it. *(The platform is, this morning, simply there. It is, the prose can let the reader notice, *not* a charged object now. It is a piece of furniture in a room she sleeps in.* **That this is true is its own piece of information about how the household will absorb last night.** The platform will, on subsequent nights, hold less and more. This morning it holds nothing.)*
- They walk together, in robes, through the corridor to the small breakfast room Holic uses when he is not in formal household mode. The kitchen staff has set out the standard things. He pours her coffee. She accepts it. They sit.
- Outside the breakfast-room window, the spring morning continues. A tram passes. A church bell, somewhere not too far, strikes seven.

## BEAT 8 — CLOSING IMAGE

> **SUPERSEDED.** See the note at the head of Beat 7. The closing image below describes the breakfast-room scene that did not occur; it is preserved for the saga's record of what Holic believed, as he kept the collar on permanently and said *Simonka. Come and have breakfast.*, was about to happen. The breakfast he prepared to have with her — across the table, paper, coffee — occurred, but he ate it alone, and Simonka was, at the corresponding hour, eating from her bowls in the yard. The morning's true closing image is in **`gg-walks-out-to-the-doghouse-scene-outline.md`** Beat 10.

*One short coda.*

- *In the breakfast room, two people sit at a table. He reads, briefly, the paper. She drinks her coffee. Neither of them refers, in any way the kitchen staff could overhear if the kitchen staff were here, to the night before. The morning is the morning. The breakfast is the breakfast. The pill, swallowed an hour ago in the bed, is already doing the small chemical work it has done every morning of her life with him.*
- *In the bedroom, behind them, the bed is unmade. The platform is at the foot of it. The robe she fell asleep in is on the chair. The collar is not on the bedside table; it is on Simonka. None of these things is, this morning, hidden; none of them is, this morning, displayed; they are simply where they are.*
- *Somewhere in Prague, in an apartment she has never been in, **the woman in the dark dress is asleep**. She slept for several hours after the agency car returned her. She will wake at noon. She does not, on waking, think first about last night. She thinks about the small administrative tasks of her morning. **The look she gave at the doorway will come back to her, at some point in the day, the way small unfinished things do. She will not know what to do with the returning. She will continue her day.***
- *Simonka, in the breakfast room, does not think of the prostitute. **She has put the look in the place she now knows she has — the private chamber she discovered, this morning, she possesses.** The look is in it. It will, in two years, become an entire thesis. This morning it is only a thing she has, on a shelf in herself, and the shelf is hers.*
- *Holic, across the table, does not know about the shelf. **He has, this morning, asked the two best questions he has ever asked her. He has received the two best partial answers she has ever given him.** The exchange has, by both their measurements, gone well. **It has also been, by the saga's measurement, the first morning in which the relationship has, in a place neither of them can yet see, begun to be unequal in a new way** — not the established household-inequality of owner and pet, but the new inequality of *one person who has private things and one person who has been told he has private things and does not, on this side of the breakfast table, have anything in particular he is keeping from her.* Whether he ever develops something to keep is a question for a much later scene. **Tonight, by going to bed with the man across the table from her, Simonka will already be the one with the larger interior. She does not know yet that this is true. She will, in time.***
- *The morning continues. He turns a page of the paper. She takes another sip of coffee. The day begins.*

## WHAT SIMONKA DOES *NOT* DO

- She does not refuse the morning aftercare-extension. She does not ask for the collar to come off, because by this point it is no longer a temporary scene object. The dog collar stays on permanently.
- She does not lie when answering either of his questions.
- She does not, however, give him the whole truth of either answer.
- **She does not tell him about the doorway look.** (Whether she ever does is open; see canon.)
- She does not, when he closes the scene with her real name, ask him to put her back in pet-form. The scene closes; she lets it close.
- She does not, at breakfast, refer to the conversation in the bed.
- She does not, leaving the mansion later that morning, refer to it either.
- **She does not say to him: *thank you for asking.*** *(She thinks of saying it. She does not say it. The thanking would feel like rewarding him for a question he should not have needed to be moved to ask. She withholds the thanks without quite naming, even to herself, that the withholding is itself a small evaluation of him.)*

---

## WHAT HOLIC DOES *NOT* DO

- He does not apologise. *Not for last night. Not for the test. Not for the absence of refreshed safewords. Not for the platform.* He has asked questions; he has not retracted the night. **The not-apologising is exactly consistent with who he is, and the prose must hold the consistency without making it into either a vindication or a charge.**
- He does not ask a third question. The two are the two he had decided in the dark hour to ask. He does not, in the moment, generate a third.
- **He does not press for fuller answers.** When Simonka gives him *"I did not think of it,"* he accepts it; he does not say *but you knew, why did you not have access to your knowing.* He has decided — and this is among the small better things he does this morning — that she will tell him in her own time or not at all.
- He does not refer to the prostitute by any term other than the indirect *"last night."* He does not name her profession. He does not name the agency. He does not, in the breakfast room or after, mention that there will be subsequent visits. *(He has decided. He does not need to say.)*
- He does not remove the collar. The dog collar stays on permanently. The household's grammar no longer sentimentalises removal because removal is no longer the mechanism by which Simonka returns to herself.
- He does not, ever, learn what the doorway look meant. *The misunderstanding from the previous scene's bath ("She looked at me" / "It was a kind room tonight") survives this morning intact. He has asked better questions than he asked last night. He has not asked the question that would, if asked, dissolve the misunderstanding. He does not know there is such a question. The saga must hold that **asking better questions is not the same as asking the right question.***

---

## TONE GUARDRAILS

- **No exoticism.** Prague morning sounds; a tram; a church bell. The mansion is a mansion in a Czech city. The household is a household.
- **No erotic register.** This is the morning. They are in a bed. The bed is, this morning, a bed. The prose holds the body in its ordinary morning shape — the small tenderness across the shoulders, the after-collar at the throat, the slight unfamiliarity of legs that have not walked in twelve hours.
- **No glamour around Holic's asking.** He is, this morning, a slightly better owner than he was last night. *Slightly better is not redeemed.* The prose must not let the asking absolve the test. The asking is the asking; the test was the test; the relationship contains both.
- **No psychologising of Simonka's withholdings.** The four reasons she does not tell him about the look are named in Beat 4 because the prose must let them stand visible — *but the prose must not declare which is the truest reason*. None of them is the whole reason. The not-declaring is the moral content.
- **No interiority for Holic.** Same discipline as the prostitute scene. The reader sees him only as Simonka sees him. The dark-hour thinking he did before she woke is named in the *What Holic Carries* section as having happened; its content is not given.
- **No interiority for the prostitute, except the one sealed beat.** The closing image gives one sentence: *the look will come back to her at some point in the day; she will not know what to do with the returning.* That is the maximum interiority the prose permits her this morning. Her recurrence across the saga is canon (per the prostitute outline); how the saga handles her interior in subsequent visits is for those scenes.
- **The pill is rendered without commentary.** Beat 5's interior reading of the pill — *the gesture is the small confirmation that the architecture is intact* — is for the prose to suggest without stating. The reader must feel the gesture's new weight without being told it has weight. *Telling would betray it.*
- **The shelf is the scene's quiet horizon.** Beat 8's closing image plants the shelf — *the private chamber she discovered, this morning, she possesses.* The prose must place the shelf in the reader's hand without making it the scene's overt point. The shelf is what the morning gives her; the morning does not advertise the giving.
