# The Collegium's Monthly Meeting — GG's First

## Scene Outline

> *The club is the **Collegium** (full: *Collegium Fidelium*, "The Society of the Faithful"); its annual show is the **Concursus**; its motto is *Per vincula, cura* — "through the bond, care." See `holic-club-rules-and-bylaws.md` §0. Throughout this outline "the club" = the Collegium.*

## Placement

**When:** **The Tuesday evening immediately after the weekend Simonka makes her choice** — the same day as the family's crisis. The club's monthly meeting falls on its fixed calendar, and this month it lands on the pivot Tuesday. GG attends as a full pet for the first time, only days after the weekend of her return, her branding, and the Spark violation.

This places the meeting in deliberate parallel with the established Tuesday scenes — an intercut day:

- **Tuesday daytime:** Klárka tells Gamaliel and Klára what she and Raphael found (`klarka-tells-gamaliel-and-klara-about-simonka-apartment-tuesday-scene-outline.md`); Gamaliel goes to Charles University and confronts Simonka (`gamaliel-visits-charles-university-to-see-simonka-tuesday-scene-outline.md`).

The intercut is the point. While her family keeps the porch light on and decides *not* to chase her, Simonka spends that same night being collared into GG at the club. **Her father reached for her by day; she answers by going deeper that night.** Her appearance at the university that afternoon — tired, guarded, saying *I chose this* — is only hours old when she walks into the meeting, and the wrong lesson the meeting teaches (pleasing Holic quiets the part of her that is shaking) is *amplified* because she is fleeing the unbearable daytime conversation with her father. She does not go to the meeting calm. She goes raw.

**Where:** The club's regular venue — a **working farm / barn complex** in the same affluent district as Holic's mansion, a short drive away (per `holic-club-rules-and-bylaws.md` §20). Repurposed agricultural buildings: a converted main barn / farmhouse as the meeting hall, a den/kennel wing, and an outdoor **engineered kennel yard** — the large fenced area, dual-use with the annual show, where pets are held during the owners' meeting (§21). The conditions in the yard are **deliberately harsh**, framed by the club as endurance/conditioning. Not Holic's mansion. This is *the club's* space, which matters: at the meeting, Holic is one owner among peers, and GG is one pet among pets.

The annual show is the club's public face; the **monthly meeting is its private machinery** — governance, training, hierarchy, and the place where the club either is or is not what it claims to be.

## Scene Purpose

This scene does three things at once, by design:

1. **Worldbuild** — establish how the club actually runs: its governance, hierarchy, rituals, the structure of a typical monthly meeting, and the gap between its stated ethics (consent, safewords, aftercare) and its lived power dynamics.
2. **Drive conflict** — the Spark violation comes to a head here. The club's consent culture is put on trial, literally: a member is brought before the membership. The meeting tests whether the rules are real or decorative.
3. **Advance Simonka's arc** — GG navigates her first meeting as a pet: her standing, her hunger to prove herself to Holic, her dawning understanding of what she has actually joined, and the first real crack between *Simonka* (Charles University, psychology, a mind trained to see people clearly) and *GG* (the role she chose).

The throughline: **GG entered this world believing Holic's "control is care, if the one holding it knows the difference." The monthly meeting forces her to find out whether the club, as a whole, knows the difference — and whether she does.**

## Tone & Boundaries

- **Frank and adult.** The scene does not euphemize what the club is: a consensual adult kink community organized around human-pet dynamics, with collars, kennels, branding, bowls, leads, pet-space rules, and sex between adults.
- **Non-graphic on sex acts.** Consistent with every existing Simonka file, sexual acts themselves are not described explicitly — the scene fades out or cuts away at the threshold. The intensity is psychological, social, and ethical, not pornographic.
- **Consent is the spine, not the decoration.** Every adult here is an adult and a volunteer. The horror, where there is horror, is precisely about the *failure* of consent culture under pressure — which only works as a theme if consent is taken seriously by the text.
- **Simonka is 23 and an adult.** Her agency is real even when the scene shows her using it against her own interests (refusing safewords to please Holic). The text should never collapse her choice into pure victimhood, and never launder a genuine violation as "just kink."

## The Club — Worldbuilding Established Here

### Governance

- The club is **member-run**, not Holic's. Governance runs through two offices (full detail in `holic-club-rules-and-bylaws.md` §1A–§1B):
  - **The Steward** (the *Magister*) — **Augustyn Reinholt**, courtly, procedural, a true *believer* in *control-is-care*; the face of the club's dignity. He chairs.
  - **The Circle** (the *Consilium*) — a council of **seven** senior owners (the Steward chairs and votes) holding the real power (discipline, admission, expulsion). It carries the club's central fight inside it:
    - **Enforce / principled:** **Holic** (disclosure hardliner — *but must recuse on Spark*, see below), **Dr. Emerich Past** (physician; treats the dosing as a drugging, not a kink question).
    - **Minimize / protect the owner:** **Wenzeslaus "Wenzel" Auerhahn** (eldest, harsh, leads), **Rostislav Kmain** (his ally and echo).
    - **Swing / institutional:** **Liesl Vondráček** (reputational calculus), **Bohdana Selnar** (traditionalist proceduralist), and the **Steward Augustyn Reinholt** (conscience says enforce, procedure may say otherwise).
    - **Recusal (canon):** an owner must recuse when their own pet is the injured party, so **Holic is barred from the Spark ruling** — removing the principled faction's most motivated vote and throwing the outcome to the swing. (Rules §1B.6.)
- Membership is **owners**, not pets. Pets are present and central but are not voting members; their owners speak for them in formal club business. *(This is itself a quiet horror the scene can sit with: a consent-obsessed culture in which the people whose bodies are at stake have no vote.)*
- Entry is by **sponsorship and vetting** — references, interviews, a probationary period. This explains how Holic could bring GG in, and establishes that the club *thinks of itself* as careful.

### The Rulebook (stated ethics)

- **Consent rules** — negotiated limits, ongoing consent, the right to withdraw.
- **The silence rule** — **a pet in pet space does not speak.** While collared, a pet communicates through posture, position, and trained behavior, not words. The **only** permitted speech is a safeword. This is the club's signature discipline: a pet's voice is reserved exclusively for *yellow* and *red*. The intended logic is protective — the safeword can never be lost in chatter, so it always carries weight. The lived consequence is darker: a pet has no voice for anything *except* stopping, which means the only sanctioned thing a pet can say is "less" or "no" — never "yes, but," never "wait, what is happening to me," never testimony, never nuance.
- **Safewords** — universal **yellow** (slow/check in) and **red** (full stop), honored by all members, not just one's own owner. Because of the silence rule, the safeword is *the* exception that proves it: the single circumstance under which a collared pet is allowed to use words.
- **Medical-disclosure rules** — no substances, conditions, or medications hidden in shared scenes; full disclosure before any pet interacts with another owner's pet.
- **Owner-responsibility standards** — an owner is accountable for their pet's safety, aftercare, and limits, and for their *own* conduct toward other pets.
- **Branding tradition** — the hot iron, owner's initials, right buttock; treated as permanent, ceremonial, and weighty within the hierarchy.
- **Collar conventions** — for most pets, collar on = pet space and collar off = the person returns. **GG is the exception:** Holic's dog collar stays on permanently once Simonka has chosen the permanent GG life, so her shift between Simonka and GG is marked by name, posture, place, commands, and consent/safeword structure rather than by removing the collar. Electric collars are permitted within agreed limits; the club regards Holic's use as *restrained* relative to many members.
- **Aftercare mandate** — scenes end in care: warmth, water, rest, debrief.

### The Gap (lived reality)

- The rules are real on paper and **unevenly enforced in practice**. Status protects bad actors. Senior owners are deferred to. The desire of a new pet to "earn her place" is a pressure the rules do not account for.
- The Spark violation is the proof: the system's safeguards (disclosure, safewords) **failed precisely because everyone trusted them to work.** GG didn't use yellow during the dosed scene because she wanted to impress Holic; Holic didn't catch it because disclosure was assumed; the rules existed and still nothing stopped it until afterward.
- **The silence rule made it worse.** The dosed male pet could not say *I don't know what's in me* — in pet space he was permitted only *yellow* or *red,* and he had no reason to say either, because he didn't know he'd been drugged. The one thing the silence rule guarantees a pet can always do (stop) is useless against a violation the pet can't perceive. A pet silenced down to two words cannot report a harm he hasn't been told is happening.

### Where the pets are: the fenced area

- **While the club meets, the pets are kept outside in a large fenced area** — the same kind of fenced enclosure used to hold pets together at the annual show. The formal meeting is **owners only.** *(See `holic-club-rules-and-bylaws.md` §14.)*
- The structural point the scene should land: **the people who are the entire subject of the meeting are not in the room.** Owners deliberate, vote, and rule indoors; pets wait collared on the grass behind a fence. The silence rule and the no-vote rule reach their literal endpoint here — a pet has no words *and* is not even present for the words spoken about them.
- **Sequence:** pets are collared at the threshold, then taken out to the fenced area for the owners' business. Pets are brought in (or owners go out to the area) only for the parts that require them — presentation of new pets, the training/demonstration block, and the later social/scene portion. **Pure governance and discipline happen while the pets are outside.**

### A Typical Monthly Meeting — Structure

1. **Arrival & entry into pet space** — owners arrive; most pets are collared at the threshold, names assumed, then **taken out to the fenced area.** GG arrives already wearing Holic's permanent dog collar; she becomes GG at the door by name, posture, and Holic's handling rather than by the collar being put on.
2. **The Steward opens (owners only, pets outside)** — housekeeping, dues, calendar (the annual show looms over everything), welcomes.
3. **Presentation of new pets** — pets brought in (or owners go out to the area); GG is formally presented to the membership for the first time. Posture, stillness, the brand acknowledged. A ritual of belonging that is also a ritual of ownership. The pet then returns to the area.
4. **Training & demonstration block** — skill-sharing among owners; positions, handling, show-prep for the annual event; the fenced-area group routines (including the non-private toilet-training reality already established).
5. **Club business / disciplinary matters (owners only, pets outside)** — the formal reckoning, handled by the Circle while the pets remain in the fenced area. This month, the Spark violation is raised. *(Per the saga's pacing, the full reckoning/ruling is a later thread; see Continuity Notes.)*
6. **Social / scene time** — the unstructured portion; this is where adult activity happens. **(Non-graphic; the scene cuts away.)**
7. **Aftercare & return from pet space** — pets are brought back out of pet space; most are decollared; debriefs happen; owners are accountable for their pets leaving safe and cared-for. GG's dog collar stays on permanently even when Simonka returns to person-register.

## Scene Structure

## PART ONE — ARRIVAL: GG AT THE THRESHOLD

### Beat 1 — Two People Get Out of the Car

- Holic and Simonka arrive at the **Collegium** — the farm, a short drive from Holic's mansion. In the car she is **Simonka** — and the outline should hold, for one beat, the Charles University woman: the psychology student, the mind trained to read people, sitting in a car on the way to be a pet. *(A grace note available here: the refined Latin name vs. the working farm it actually is — the gentleman's-club self-image meeting the kennel yard.)*
- **The day is still on her.** This is the same Tuesday her father found her in the courtyard that afternoon. She carries the conversation in with her — *you are not in trouble, you can come home* — and she is, instead, entering pet space at the Collegium. The contrast is the engine of the beat: a few hours ago she had a door held open; now she is choosing the permanent collar's world over the door. Holic may notice she is *off,* the way her parents noticed at the Friday meal; like them, he is told *nothing.* (Echo of *nic není* — she does it to Holic too, just in a different language.)
- At the threshold, Holic does not put the collar on; **it is already on and stays on permanently.** Instead he shifts her into GG by name, posture, handling, and the public rules of the Collegium. **Simonka becomes GG.** The transformation lands differently because it is public — she is about to be GG *in front of strangers*, not just Holic — and because she is using pet-space as an escape hatch from a day she can't hold. Becoming GG means she no longer has to be the daughter who walked away from her father in a courtyard. GG has only two words and none of them are *Dad.*
- **Key image:** the last look between them as equals before the collar — Holic asking, without quite asking, *are you ready;* GG answering by lowering herself into the role, and the courtyard going quiet in her at last.

### Beat 2 — The House and the Hierarchy (in the brief window before the pets go out)

- In the short interval between arrival and being taken outside, GG reads the room the way Simonka can't stop reading rooms. She clocks the hierarchy fast: which owners defer to whom, which pets are senior, who the Steward is, who the **Circle** is.
- Worldbuild through her eyes: the converted barn / meeting hall, the den/kennel wing, the **engineered kennel yard out back** where she is about to be sent. The farm setting registers on her — a place built to house and manage animals, now housing and managing people. The other pets — varied, individuated, not a uniform mass. Some clearly thriving and adored; at least one whose situation makes GG (and the reader) uneasy.
- The **two pets from the Spark weekend** are present: the male pet (25) who was dosed, and his owner — the violator. GG sees them. The air changes.
- **The brand, in this window.** New as she is, GG is marked — **H.B.**, the brand **only days old, still healing** from the weekend, the literal timestamp of how recent her choice is. She is the newest pet present and wears the most recent claim: a novice with a permanent mark. She is proud of it and unsettled by it at once — Simonka-the-psychologist notes her own pride and is disturbed by how good it feels to be *Holic's.* The same body her father looked at across a courtyard this afternoon, wondering if she was safe, now bears initials that are not his and not hers. *(Formal acknowledgment of the brand comes later, at her presentation — Beat 5.)*

### Beat 3 — GG Is Taken Out to the Fenced Area

- Before the owners' meeting begins, **the pets are taken outside into the large fenced area.** This is canon procedure: the formal meeting is owners only. *(See `holic-club-rules-and-bylaws.md` §14.)*
- The reversal lands on GG immediately: she dressed carefully, braced herself, walked in to be *seen* — and is promptly put **outside,** on the grass, behind a fence, with the other pets, while the people who own them go in to talk about them.
- **Simonka-the-psychologist cannot not notice it:** the entire meeting is *about* pets — their training, their conduct, their dynamics, their violations — and not one pet is in the room. Owners deliberate indoors; pets wait on the grass. The silence rule had already taken her words; the fence now takes her presence. She is the subject of a conversation she is not allowed to attend.
- The kennel yard as its own small society: the pets among themselves, collared and (mostly) silent, a pecking order of their own, the cold of a Prague Tuesday night. The conditions are **deliberately harsh** — exposure framed by the club as endurance/conditioning for the annual show, not comfort (per §21.3). The contradiction lands on GG directly: this is the same club whose whole creed is *control is care,* and "care," tonight, is a cold engineered yard. The dosed male pet (25) is out here too — close enough to look at, impossible to speak to.

- **The handlers.** The yard is watched by **handlers** — non-owner Collegium staff who manage the pets while the owners meet (rules §21.5). They keep order, lead pets to and from the hall, and — crucially — **stand as the safeword authority while the owners are inside.** A handler is not GG's owner; he has custody, not ownership.
- **GG can always safeword to a handler — and chooses not to.** This is the precise shape of her arc, made literal in the yard. The safeguard is fully intact: at any moment GG could say *yellow* or *red* to a handler and be checked on or pulled out (rules §21.5.1, §4.3). **She doesn't.** Not because she's trapped — because she wants to please Holic. To be found, when the owners come out, to have endured, to have been *good* in the yard. The mechanism works; her *will to use it* is what's compromised (§19.2, §21.5.2). Simonka-the-psychologist can name exactly what she's doing and does it anyway. The horror here is not a missing exit; it's a woman walking past the exit to be praised.
- **GG is very busy with the other pets — men and women.** Left in the yard, GG does not sit at the edge and endure; she throws herself into the other pets, both male and female, with an intensity that is half hunger and half flight:
  - **It is escape.** Hours ago her father held a door open in a courtyard. In the yard there is no father, no diary, no *you can come home* — only the body and the present tense. Throwing herself at the others is how she makes the courtyard go quiet. (Echo of Beat 1: *GG has only two words and none of them are Dad.*)
  - **It is proving.** A new pet earning her place. The yard is an audience of peers, and GG — branded days ago, the newest of them — wants to be seen as belonging, not as the nervous novice. Being *good at this,* even on the grass, scratches the same itch as Holic's "good girl." (Echo: her mother Klára, the foster child who learned to be the one no one had to ask twice.)
  - **It is wordless.** The silence rule (§3) means none of it is spoken — pets have only *yellow* and *red.* The society of the yard runs on bodies: posture, approach, consent and refusal expressed in movement, with the handlers as the only outlet for words. The dancer's daughter, raised to say everything without words, is fluent in exactly this. Her body knew this language before she did. *(Frank but non-graphic; the scene registers her activity and fades — no act-level depiction, consistent with every Simonka file.)*
  - **No special bond — yet.** GG does **not** form a particular attachment to any one pet tonight. It is plural, restless, indiscriminate — many partners, no fixation. This matters: she is not seeking connection in the yard, she is seeking *to be good at the yard.* (A specific bond with a specific pet is left open for a later beat/scene, not this one.)
  - **The dosed male pet (25) is present but not singled out.** He is out here, harmed the same weekend she was, the two of them unable to say anything about it — but GG does not make him her focus. The shared-wound beat is held in reserve, available later; tonight he is one of many.
  - **What it costs her later.** The more *busy,* the more *good,* the more she belongs in the yard, the harder the indoor reckoning (Beats 7–9) will cut — because while she was proving she belonged, the owners inside were deciding what was done to her, and she will come in from the grass flushed, having spent the whole deliberation pleasing strangers instead of being heard. She had a handler an arm's length away the entire time and never once used the only words she was allowed.
- **Key image:** GG on the grass, brand still healing, looking back at the lit windows of the great hall where her fate and everyone's is being discussed without her — the porch-light image inverted. (Across the city, a light is on for her at a door she could walk through. Here, the lit windows are a room she is locked *out* of.)

## PART TWO — THE MEETING OPENS

### Beat 4 — The Steward (Augustyn Reinholt) Opens the Meeting (pets outside)

- **POV note:** GG experiences this beat *from the fenced area* — through the lit windows, as muffled sound, as waiting. The scene can intercut: the owners' procedural calm indoors vs. the pets on the grass outside. The reader sees the meeting; GG only sees the windows.
- Formal tone, almost civic — minutes, dues, the annual show calendar. The banality is the point: a human-pet club run with the procedural dryness of a strata council.
- The show looms. Show-prep is the club's organizing purpose; everything bends toward presentation, endurance, the fenced-area routines.

### Beat 5 — GG Is Presented (brought in from the fenced area)

- For her presentation, **GG is brought in from the fenced area** — one of the few agenda items that requires the pets. The threshold reverses: outside-to-inside, grass-to-hall, the subject finally allowed into the room for exactly as long as she is needed.
- GG's formal presentation to the membership. Posture, stillness, the brand acknowledged, the name spoken: **Good Girl. GG.**
- Holic speaks for her, as owners speak for pets — and as the silence rule requires, since GG cannot speak for herself while collared. **Simonka, inside GG, registers that she has no voice in this room** — no vote, and now, literally, no words but two. First real crack: the gap between *I chose this* and *I have no say here* — and the dawning understanding that the only sentence she is allowed to speak is the one that means *stop.*
- The reward she's been trained to crave: Holic's quiet **"Good girl."** It works on her exactly as designed. She hates a little how much she wants it. She wants it anyway.
- After the presentation, the procedure would ordinarily **send her back out to the fenced area** — though the training block (Beat 6) keeps the pets in / works them in the area, so the flow can run presentation → training without an extra trip outside, at the writer's discretion.

### Beat 6 — The Training Block

- Owners demonstrate and skill-share: positions, handling, show endurance, the fenced-area group routines (the non-private bodily reality already established — handled with the same matter-of-fact frankness as canon, not for shock).
- GG performs. She is good — disciplined, a dancer's daughter even if not a dancer, a body raised in studios. **She is praised. She glows.** The arc beat: she is *succeeding* at this, and success feels like belonging, and belonging is the thing the foster-built family taught her to chase. - Counter-beat: she watches a senior owner correct a pet harder than the rules should allow, and no one stops it. The gap between rulebook and room, shown not told.

## PART THREE — THE RECKONING (CONFLICT)

### Beat 7 — Club Business: The Spark Violation Is Raised (pets outside)

- **Pacing note (saga decision):** the full reckoning/ruling is a **later thread**, not resolved at GG's first meeting. At this meeting the violation is **raised** — the fuse is lit — but the Circle does not necessarily conclude it tonight. Beats 8–11 below are written so they can either (a) play out now in compressed form, or (b) be *set up* now and discharged in a later dedicated scene. Choose per the arc.
- **The fence is now load-bearing.** Disciplinary business happens **owners only, pets outside (§14).** So the entire reckoning about what was done to two pets occurs while **both of those pets are out on the grass behind the fence,** unable to hear it. To involve a pet at all — to ask, to confirm, to hear — the club must make a deliberate exception and **bring a pet in.** That act is no longer routine; it is a statement.
- The Steward (Augustyn Reinholt) moves to disciplinary matters. The Spark weekend is named formally. His proceduralism is the hinge: he will follow the bylaws precisely, which can either protect the violator (no provision compels a pet's testimony) or doom him (disclosure is the keystone rule).
- **The recusal turn (canon, rules §1B.6):** because GG was the injured party, **Holic must recuse** from the ruling — the principled faction's most motivated vote, removed at the worst moment. Open choice for the scene: does Holic submit quietly (the believer obeying the order he reveres) or fight it (the man refusing to be silenced about his own pet)? Can he speak as a *witness* even while barred as a *judge*?
- With Holic sidelined, **Dr. Emerich Past** becomes the lead voice to enforce — arguing it as a *drugging,* a medical violation, not "enthusiasm." **Wenzel Auerhahn** and **Rostislav Kmain** move to minimize. The decision falls to the swing: **Liesl Vondráček** (what protects the Collegium's name?), **Bohdana Selnar** (do the bylaws even empower us — and at what precedent?), and the Steward's conscience-vs-procedure bind.
- The charge, in club terms: the member secretly administered a performance drug to his pet before a **shared scene** — violating consent rules, medical-disclosure rules, owner-responsibility standards, and the trust required between owners who share pets. GG and Holic were the unwitting other party.
- The room divides. This is the worldbuild-meets-conflict core: **is the club its rules, or is it its hierarchy?**
  - **The principled faction** (some of the Circle, Holic among them) treats it as a grave breach — the kind that, unaddressed, makes every safeword in the club meaningless.
  - **The minimizing faction** (the violator's allies, the status-protectors) reframes it: no one was *hurt,* it was *enthusiasm,* the pet "didn't complain," pets exist to please. This faction is the actual danger of the club, and the scene should let the reader feel it.

### Beat 8 — The Pet Has No Voice — Out on the Grass, and Silent Even If Brought In

- **The dosed male pet (25) is not even in the room** — he is in the fenced area with the other pets while the owners discuss what was done to him. To hear him, the club would first have to **bring him in from outside** *and then* decollar him. Two thresholds, not one.
- And even brought in, the **silence rule is absolute:** collared, he may say only *yellow* or *red.* He has no words for an account of what was done to him. His owner speaks "for" him — naturally minimizing.
- This is the bind the scene must land: **the person most harmed is the one person who is, by rule, neither present nor permitted to describe the harm.** The fence took his presence; the collar took his words.
- The turn — and the fight over the club's soul: someone in the Circle moves to **bring the pet in from the fenced area and decollar him** — the only mechanism that frees a pet's voice. Take the collar off and the pet is no longer a pet; he is a person, and a person may speak. A faction resists (fetching a pet from the area and uncollaring him "in session," before the membership, is a double breach of the club's order); the principled faction insists that a club that won't even bring a victim in from the grass to hear him has already chosen the violator.
- **The collar comes off** (if this branch is taken). The moment is heavy precisely because it is the inverse of GG's threshold ritual: where a collar makes a pet, removing it restores a person — and the club has to watch a man become a person again in order to be allowed to say what happened to him.
- Decollared, he describes it plainly: not knowing what was in his body, not being able to tell what was him and what was the drug — the precise erasure of consent that a safeword can't fix, because **you can't safeword a choice you didn't know you were being denied,** and you can't even *report* it while the rules have reduced your entire vocabulary to two words.

### Beat 9 — GG Is Asked What Happened — But GG Is Outside, and Cannot Speak

- The hardest beat for Simonka — and now the fence sits on top of the silence rule. As the other party to the shared scene, GG's account matters. **But GG is out in the fenced area,** and even if brought in, **a pet in pet-space may say only *yellow* or *red.*** To get her account the club would have to make an exception: **bring her in from the grass and release her from pet-space** — return Simonka, while the permanent dog collar remains on — so she can speak.

4. **The silence rule** — a pet in pet-space may speak only its safewords (*yellow*/*red*); all other communication is posture and behavior. Framed as protection (the safeword is never lost in chatter), it functions as control (a pet can say *stop* but never *explain*), and it is the structural reason the dosed pet — and GG — cannot testify without being released from pet-space. For most pets that means decollaring; for GG, whose dog collar stays on permanently, it means being explicitly addressed and treated as Simonka while still collared.

Governance and discipline happen owners-only; pets are brought in only for the parts that require them. 7. **Holic as the best of a bad standard** — genuinely principled within a frame that is itself the problem; the text holds the discomfort instead of resolving it.
8. **The central question posed, not answered** — *what does it mean to belong to someone?* The club's collar (and fence) vs. the family's open door, in counterpoint: the same night, a porch light is on for her at a door she can walk through, while here the lit windows are a room she is locked out of.
