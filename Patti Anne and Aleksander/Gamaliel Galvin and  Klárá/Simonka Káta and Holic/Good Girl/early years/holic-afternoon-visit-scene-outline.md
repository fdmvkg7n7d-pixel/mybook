# Holic's Afternoon Visit — Scene Outline

## Placement

**When:** The afternoon of the same day, hours after the 9:00 AM visit.  
**Where:** The Collegium Club's private medical facility — the cage where Good Girl is being kept, and the recovery run attached to it.  
**Continuity:** Follows `holic-visits-gg-at-nine-am-scene-outline.md`. Holic left the medical facility in the morning, telling Good Girl he would be back *this afternoon*. He kept his word. The assistant doctor has been monitoring Good Girl in his absence.

---

## Characters

- **Holic Bartunek**: Good Girl's primary owner and steward. Furious about the new medical findings, but careful to keep his fury contained so as not to distress her. He brings a homemade soft cookie treat.
- **Good Girl (Simonka Káta / GG)**: Recovering in her cage. Bruised, bandaged, IV line in her arm, a supportive wrap now immobilizing her torso. She waited for him all morning. When he returns, the structure finally breaks.
- **The Assistant Doctor**: Present in the background. She has the X-ray results and the rib diagnosis. She delivers the news to Holic quietly, away from Good Girl.

---

## Scene Outline

### Beat 1: The Afternoon Returns

The door to the medical facility opens.

Good Girl is in her cage — not near the bars this time, not standing, but lying on the padded mat the staff placed for her. The other male pet is somewhere in the run. She is alone in the enclosure. Her bandaged feet are tucked beneath her. Her bruised body is curled slightly on its side. The IV line is still in her arm, the bag lower now, the drip slower. A supportive wrap now encircles her torso, immobilizing the cracked ribs.

The door opens. She does not move at first. But her head lifts.

Holic walks through the door.

She knows his footsteps. She knows the weight of his presence before she even sees his face. Her ears go forward. Her body uncurls. She pushes herself up onto her knees — slow, careful, the bruises on her torso shifting against the wrap as she moves — and then, before the assistant doctor can stop her, before Holic can tell her to stay still, before her own body can remind her of the pain in her feet, she lunges to the bars.

She jumps at him.

Not a full jump — she cannot, not with her bandaged feet, not with the crack in her ribs, not with the weight of everything that has been done to her. But she throws herself forward anyway, her forelegs hitting the bars, her body pressing against the metal as far as it can go, her whole being reaching for the person who said he would come back.

Holic is at the bars in one step. He does not hesitate. He does not tell her to be careful. He reaches through and pulls her against him, one arm around her shoulders, the other hand at the back of her head, holding her the way you hold something you thought you might have lost.

> "I am back."

She makes a sound — not a word, not a bark, not a cry. Something in between. Something that has no name. Something that means *you kept your word.*

### Beat 2: The Treat

Holic does not come empty-handed. He reaches into his coat pocket and pulls out a small wax-paper parcel. Inside: one of her favorite treats — a homemade soft cookie, baked the way she likes them, the way someone in his household bakes them for her when she is being good. Oatmeal and peanut butter, soft enough that even a pet with bruised gums can chew it. The kind of treat that is not medical, not clinical, not part of any recovery protocol. It is simply a gift.

He unwraps it. He holds it to the bars.

Good Girl takes it. She does not eat it immediately. She holds it in her mouth, just for a moment, the way an animal holds something precious before deciding whether to consume it or save it. Then she eats it — slowly, deliberately, savoring the taste, the texture, the recognition that this is not dog food. This is not medicine. This is a cookie. This is something made with hands, not with a chart.

Holic watches her eat it. His expression does not change. But something in his chest loosens.

### Beat 3: The Doctor's Report

The assistant doctor approaches. Holic steps back from the bars — not away, just far enough that the doctor can speak to him privately, without Good Girl overhearing every word. She is in the cage now, chewing the last of the cookie, her eyes fixed on Holic's back.

The assistant doctor has news.

> "We did a full examination after you left. The X-rays showed something we missed this morning."

Holic's face changes. The doctor continues.

> "A hairline crack in her ribs. On the left side. Lower. It's not a break — not a full fracture — but it's a crack. It could have been caused by pressure. By weight. By being pressed down during—"

She does not finish the sentence. She does not need to.

Holic's expression goes flat. Cold. The kind of cold that is not the absence of emotion but the concentration of it.

> "This could have killed her."

It is not a question. It is a statement.

The assistant doctor nods.

> "If the crack had been deeper, if it had punctured a lung, if the bruising had been more severe — yes. It could have been fatal. She is lucky it was only a hairline fracture. She is lucky she is here."

Holic does not respond immediately. He stands at the bars, looking at Good Girl, who is watching him with the quiet intensity of an animal who does not understand the words but understands the tone. She knows something is wrong. She does not know what. She only knows that the person holding the bar is not the person who was holding it a minute ago.

> "Who did it?"

The assistant doctor hesitates.

> "The other owner. We think it happened during the second night. When he—"

> "When he used twenty pets on her."

The assistant doctor does not correct him. She does not agree with him. She simply nods.

> "We have immobilized her torso with a supportive wrap. She will need to avoid pressure on the left side for at least three weeks. No weight on her ribs. No mounting. No—"

> "I know what she needs."

The assistant doctor steps back. Holic turns to the bars.

### Beat 4: Holic's Fury

Holic is furious.

Not the kind of fury that explodes — the kind that implodes. The kind that settles into a person's bones and sits there, heavy and permanent, changing the shape of the room.

He grips the bars. His knuckles go white. His jaw locks. His eyes do not leave Good Girl's face.

> "Twenty pets."

The words are quiet. They are also lethal.

> "They gave her to twenty pets. They withheld her medications. They broke her wrist. They broke her feet. They cracked her ribs. They gagged her so she couldn't speak. They chained her in a box truck."

He pauses. His voice drops lower.

> "They could have killed her."

Good Girl watches him. She does not understand every word. But she understands the anger. She understands the pain in his voice. She understands that this is about her — about what was done to her, about what was almost done, about what might still happen if the right people are not held accountable.

She presses herself against the bars again. Not jumping this time. Just pressing. Just being close.

Holic reaches through and touches her. Gently. On the shoulder. Above the bruise. Above the crack. Above the damage. His hand is steady. His voice is not.

> "No one is going to do that to you again."

It is not a promise to Good Girl. It is a promise to himself.

### Beat 5: She Is Very Emotional

Good Girl's body begins to shake.

Not from pain. Not from fear. From something deeper — something that has been held back since the morning, since the cold water, since the feeding, since the cage, since the waiting. The structure has held. The routine has held. The promise has held.

But now he is here. Now he is touching her. Now he has brought her a cookie. And he is speaking softly to her, the way she has never been spoken to before.

And the dam breaks.

She does not cry — animals do not cry the way humans cry. But her body shudders. Her breathing becomes uneven. Her ears flatten. Her tail tucks. She lowers her head against the bars and makes a sound — small, broken, entirely involuntary — the sound of an animal who has been holding herself together for two days and has finally, finally, been given permission to fall apart.

Holic does not try to stop it. He does not tell her to be quiet. He does not look away. He stands at the bars and holds her shoulder and lets her shake.

> He speaks softly, in a voice he has never used before — the kind of voice reserved for something broken that is learning how to be whole again.
>
> "You are a very magnificent female pet," he says quietly, the same words he used at 9:00 AM, repeated now in a different key.
>
> He pauses. His voice drops, softer than before.
>
> "You are okay. You are okay. No one will hurt you again."

Good Girl's breathing slows. The shaking subsides. She does not stop pressing against the bars. She does not stop looking at him. But she settles. She lets the structure hold her again. She lets the routine resume. She lets the promise be enough.

For now.

### Beat 6: The Walk

After her breathing has slowed, after the shaking has subsided, Holic steps back from the bars. He does not look away. He never looks away. But he shifts his weight, and the change is enough to signal that the moment has moved.

He turns to the assistant doctor, who has been standing nearby, watching but not intervening, giving Good Girl the space to fall apart and the space to put herself back together.

> "Open the run."

The assistant doctor nods. She unlocks the cage door. She steps inside. She does not touch Good Girl — she does not need to. She simply opens the door wider and steps aside, leaving the threshold clear.

Good Girl does not move immediately. She stays where she is for a long moment, her body still pressed against the bars, her eyes still on Holic. Then, slowly, she rises. Her bandaged feet make uneven sounds on the padded mat. Her torso shifts inside the supportive wrap. Her IV line tugs slightly as she moves, but the assistant doctor has already adjusted the bag to a portable stand, so it travels with her.

She steps through the door.

Holic is already inside the run, waiting for her. He does not speak. He does not reach for her. He simply kneels, takes a short leash from the assistant doctor, and clips it to her collar — the way he has done a hundred times before, the way an owner handles a pet outside her enclosure. The leash is not tight. It is not controlling. It is simply present. A connection between his hand and her collar, a signal that she is with him now, that she is his to lead and his to protect.

He stands. He gives the leash a gentle slack — enough that she can move freely, not enough that she can wander. He turns and walks toward the open space of the run, his pace slow and deliberate, matching hers. She follows. Not because she is pulled. Because she chooses to.

The run is the same one she was in this morning — the same concrete floor, the same high fence, the same open air, the same space where she waited through the cold water and the bathroom and the feeding and the medicine. But it feels different now. Because she is not in a cage. Because she is moving. Because he is beside her. Because the leash in his hand says: *you are mine, and I am here.*

### Beat 7: Number One

Good Girl walks slowly. She does not run — she cannot, not with her bandaged feet and her cracked ribs and the IV line swinging from its stand. But she walks. And walking is enough. The leash in Holic's hand moves with her, slack, never taut.

She moves to the far corner of the run, away from the other male pet, away from the fence, away from the door. Holic follows, unhurried, giving her the length of the leash she needs. She stops. She lifts. She squats slightly, her body braced against the discomfort of the wrap, against the weight of the bruises, against the simple exhaustion of a creature who has been held together by structure and routine and promises for two days and has finally, finally, been given permission to move.

She relieves herself. Number one.

It is not dramatic. It is not humiliating. It is simply a bodily function, the kind of ordinary thing an animal does when it is given space to be an animal. The assistant doctor — standing just outside the run's fence, observing but not intervening — notices, makes a brief note — *urine output normal, color clear, no blood* — and continues her rounds. This is a clinical observation. It confirms that Good Girl's kidneys are functioning, that her body is still processing, still working, still alive beneath the bruises and bandages and IV line.

Holic watches from inside the run, the leash loose in his hand. He does not look away. He does not show disgust or pity. He simply registers it as what it is: a body doing what bodies do. A body that is healing. A body that is still here.

Good Girl finishes. She stands. She shakes once — a full-body shake, the kind an animal does to reset itself after a bodily function — and then she turns back toward Holic. She does not crawl. She does not run. She walks. Slowly. Deliberately. One bandaged foot after the other. The leash sways between them, slack, a thread connecting her to the person who brought her out here.

She stops in front of him. She looks up. She does not ask for praise. She does not need to. He gives it anyway.

> "Good girl."

She closes her eyes. She leans into his hand when he reaches down to touch her. And the walk continues.


### Beat 8: The Cooked Fish

Good Girl finishes the walk. She stands in front of Holic, looking up at him with the quiet satisfaction of an animal who has been outside her cage, who has moved, who has relieved herself, who has received praise. But she is not done.

She turns away from him. Not to leave — never to leave — but to lead.

She walks slowly, her bandaged feet still uneven on the concrete, her supportive wrap still tight around her ribs, her IV line still swinging from its portable stand. But she walks with purpose. She knows where she is going.

Holic is in the run with her. He holds her leash — not tight, not controlling, just present. A short length of leather or nylon connecting his hand to her collar, the way a pet is handled when she is outside her enclosure. He does not pull. He does not guide. He lets her lead. He walks beside her, matching her pace, giving her the space to move at her own speed. He knows that when Good Girl walks with intention, it is not a whim. It is a signal.

She leads him to the far side of the recovery run — past the corner where she relieved herself, past the spot where the other male pet has been resting, to a place near the back wall where a dog bowl sits on the concrete floor.

It is not the bowl she was fed from this morning. That one was inside the clinic, used for the feeding protocol. This one is different. It was placed here at lunchtime — by a nurse, by the assistant doctor, by someone who knew what Good Girl needed when Holic was not in the room.

Inside the bowl: the remnants of cooked fish. Not raw. Not kibble. Cooked — flaked white fish, a few small pieces left, the rest already eaten. The bowl is mostly empty. Good Girl had this at lunch, while Holic was gone, while she was waiting. She did not eat it all. She left a little. Enough for someone to see what it was. Enough for someone to know.

Good Girl stops in front of the bowl. The leash goes slack. She does not eat the leftovers — she already ate, she is not hungry for this. Instead, she does something else. She nudges the bowl with her nose. Then she looks up at Holic. Then back down at the bowl. Then up at him again.

She is showing him.

Holic does not understand at first. He kneels beside her in the run, the leash still in his hand, and looks down at the bowl. He sees the fish. He sees the small amount remaining. He looks back at Good Girl.

> "You had this at lunch."

Good Girl's ears go forward. Her body shifts — a small, deliberate movement, the way an animal communicates when it wants something understood. She nudges the bowl again. She looks at Holic. She looks back at the bowl. She does not eat. She *demonstrates*.

She wants him to know that she loves this. That she ate it. That someone brought it to her while he was gone. That it matters to her — not as sustenance, not as medicine, but as something good, something she loves, something she wants *him* to know about.

Holic watches her. He reads the behavior. He understands.

The bowl was placed at lunch. She ate most of it. She left a little — not because she was full, but because she was saving it, the way an animal saves something precious. She left it there so that when Holic came back, she could show him. She could bring him to it. She could say, without words: *Look. This is what I love. This is what I had while you were gone. I want you to know.*

He reaches down and touches the edge of the bowl. Then he touches her head. The leash is still in his hand. He does not pull it. He does not tighten it. He simply holds it, the way he holds everything else in this room: with steadiness, with presence, with the quiet certainty of a man who knows that a leash is not a restraint — it is a connection.

> "Good girl."

She closes her eyes. She leans into his hand. And the moment is over.


### Beat 9: Morning — Taking Her Home

The next morning arrives. Holic is at the medical facility before 9:00 AM — earlier than his visit yesterday. He has already spoken with the main doctor on the phone. Good Girl has been cleared for discharge. Her vitals are stable. Her ribs are immobilized. Her medications are resumed. She is ready to go home.

He walks through the door of the recovery area. The assistant doctor is already there, leash in hand — one of the clinic's leashes, kept on a hook near the cages. The facility has its own leads for transporting pets. This one is clean, clinical, well-used.

Good Girl is in her cage. She has been there all night — sleeping, resting, waiting. When she hears his footsteps, her head lifts. Her ears go forward. Her body uncurls. She pushes herself up onto her knees.

She does not jump at the bars this time. Not because she doesn't want to — because her body is too tired, too bruised, too sore from the night. But her eyes say everything: *you came back.*

The assistant doctor opens the cage door. She steps inside and hands the leash to Holic. He clips it to Good Girl's collar — the same collar she has worn since she arrived at the facility. The leash is the clinic's, not his. It does not matter. The connection is the same.

Good Girl steps through the door. Her bandaged feet make uneven sounds on the concrete. Her IV line is gone — removed overnight, the site clean and covered with a small adhesive bandage. Her supportive wrap is still in place around her ribs. She walks slowly, deliberately, with the careful, measured gait of an animal whose body is healing but whose spirit is intact.

Holic does not rush her. He does not pull the leash. He walks beside her, matching her pace, giving her the space to move at her own speed.

At the exit of the medical facility, the assistant doctor steps forward one last time. She hands Holic a small paper bag — Good Girl's medications: Fosamax Plus D, Actonel with Calcium, antibiotic, the pill, and pain management. Written on the side in the doctor's handwriting: *dosing schedule — do not skip.*

Holic takes the bag. He nods. He does not say thank you — not because he is ungrateful, but because gratitude in this moment would feel inadequate. What the doctor has done, what the facility has done, what the staff have done — it is beyond thanks.

He turns to Good Girl. She is sitting at his feet, looking up at him, the leash slack between them. Her body is tired. Her eyes are not.

> "Let's go home."

He says it plainly. He does not soften it. He does not say *we're going home now, everything is fine* — because not everything is fine. Her ribs are cracked. Her body is bruised. The other owner is still out there. Bruno's owner is still out there. The first Tuesday meeting is coming. None of that is fine.

But this is: he is taking her home. And that matters.

Good Girl stands. She does not need to be pulled. She walks toward the door, the leash moving between them, her bandaged feet carrying her one step at a time, past the recovery run, past the cages, past the concrete floor where she waited through the cold water and the feeding and the medicine and the cooked fish and the good-bye.

She is not waiting anymore.

She is going home.

Holic opens the door. He holds it for her. She walks through. He follows. He closes the door behind them.

The medical facility is behind them now. The afternoon visit is behind them now. The cage is behind them now.

They walk to the parking lot together — Holic with the leash in his hand and the medication bag in his pocket, Good Girl with her bandaged feet and her cracked ribs and her eyes fixed on the path ahead.

She does not look back.

She does not need to.

- **The Promise Kept**: Holic said *this afternoon*. He came back. Good Girl waited. The structure held.
- **The Treat**: A homemade soft cookie — not medical, not clinical, not part of any protocol. A gift. Something made with hands. Something that says *you matter beyond your function.*

- **Holic's Fury**: Quiet, imploding, permanent. He grips the bars. His knuckles go white. He does not shout. He does not need to.

But when Holic returns, when he touches her, when he speaks to her, the dam breaks. She shakes. She makes a sound. She lets herself fall apart — because he is here, and because he will hold her while she does.
- **The Walk**: Holic is inside the run with Good Girl, leash in hand. She is not free, but she is not in the cage either. The walk is a transition — a small step back toward normalcy. The leash is slack, not controlling. She follows him because she chooses to, not because she is pulled. She knows where she belongs.
- **Number One**: Good Girl relieves herself in the run. It is not dramatic. It is not humiliating. It is a sign of life. The assistant doctor notes it clinically: *urine output normal, color clear, no blood.* Good Girl's body is still working.
- **The Cooked Fish**: Good Girl had cooked fish at lunch — while Holic was gone. She ate most of it but left a small amount in the bowl. She does not eat it in front of him. She *shows* him the bowl, nudging it with her nose, leading him to it, demonstrating without words: *I had this. I loved it. I want you to know.* This is not sustenance. This is communication. This is an animal sharing something good with the person she trusts.


- **The Departure**: Holic tells Good Girl he has to leave. He does not soften it. He does not say *I'll be back* — he has already made that promise today. He unclips the leash, folds it, puts it away. He touches her head one last time. *Be a good girl. Wait for me.* She steps into the cage on her own. She does not beg. She does not whine. She simply lies down and watches the door. She waits with certainty, not hope — because he has already proven he keeps his word.
