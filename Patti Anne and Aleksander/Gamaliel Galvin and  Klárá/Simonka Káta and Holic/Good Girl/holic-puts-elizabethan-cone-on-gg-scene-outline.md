## The Device

The **Elizabethan cone** — also called an **E-collar**, a **veterinary cone**, or colloquially the **"cone of shame"** — is a plastic or soft cone-shaped collar designed to fit around an animal's neck and extend outward beyond the muzzle, preventing the animal from licking, scratching, or biting at wounds or injuries. It is a standard veterinary medical device, functional, clinical, and deeply associated with animal vulnerability, post-procedure restriction, and the reduction of an animal to a body under management.

Holic's cone is not improvised. It is a **human-sized veterinary cone**, selected for size, transparency, and weight. It is wide enough to restrict GG's peripheral vision, long enough to prevent her from turning her head fully, and shaped so that when she tries to look down at her own body, she sees the interior of the cone instead. The material is clear or translucent — Holic does not blindfold her, but he **narrows her world**. She can see forward, but not sideways. She can hear, but she cannot locate sounds by turning. She can feel, but she cannot see what is touching her unless it is directly in front of her.

The cone is a **medical device for animals**, and that is precisely the point. Holic is not putting a mask on her. He is not putting a hood on her. He is putting a **veterinary restraint** on her — the same device a vet puts on a dog after surgery. The humiliation is not in the design; it is in the **origin**. She is not a person wearing a costume. She is a pet wearing a medical device designed for pets.

## Scene Outline

### Holic Prepares the Cone

- Holic retrieves the cone from a storage area, cabinet, or closet. He has already fitted it to her size — measured her neck circumference, tested the length, made sure it is snug but not dangerous. The preparation is quiet, deliberate, unhurried. He is not performing for her. He is preparing the tool.
- GG is in position — kneeling, sitting, or lying — depending on where in the training arc this falls. She is already in pet space. She is already GG. She watches him handle the cone. She does not yet know what it is for. She may recognize it from a vet visit with a dog. She may not. The recognition, when it comes, is part of the effect.
- Holic speaks to her. His voice is calm, instructive, the same register he uses for all training: "Good Girl. We're going to try something new today. This is a cone. It helps pets stay still. It helps them focus. You're going to wear it for me."
- The word **"pets"** lands. Not "people." Not "women." Pets. He is telling her, before she even wears it, that the cone belongs to the category of pet things, not person things. She already knows this, but hearing it spoken while he holds the device makes it present and unavoidable.

### Placing the Cone

- Holic approaches GG. He may lift her chin gently, or he may simply lower the cone over her head from above. The motion is smooth and controlled. He does not force it. He does not need to. She is already in a posture of compliance.
- The cone settles around her neck. The fit is snug. The weight is noticeable but not crushing. The plastic rim may rest against her collarbone or upper chest. The cone extends outward, beyond her shoulders, creating a rounded barrier between her head and the world.
- Immediately, her field of vision changes. She can see Holic if he is in front of her. She cannot see him if he steps to the side. She cannot see her own hands unless she raises them directly in front of her face. She cannot look down at her body without seeing the interior of the cone — a pale, curved surface that blocks her view of her own torso, her own legs, her own posture. She is **disconnected from her own body by sight**.
- Holic steps back. He watches her reaction. He does not speak immediately. He lets her feel it: the restriction, the narrowing, the sudden dependence on sound and touch to understand where she is.

### GG's First Moments in the Cone

- GG tries to turn her head. The cone prevents it. The motion is small, instinctive, and she feels the plastic catch, the rim press against her neck, the frustration of a body that no longer moves the way she expects. She is not in pain. She is in **restriction**.
- She tries to look down. She sees the cone. She tries to look to the side. She sees the cone. She tries to locate Holic by sound, and she can — but she cannot turn to confirm his position. She is **stationary in her head while the world moves around her**.
- Holic speaks from her left side. She hears him, but she cannot turn to see him. She must hold still and listen. He speaks from her right side. Same effect. He speaks from behind her. She cannot see him at all. She must rely entirely on his voice, his footsteps, the sound of his breathing. He is teaching her, without saying it, that **the cone makes her his** in a new way — not through pain, not through fear, but through the simple physical fact that she cannot orient herself without him.
- Holic may touch her — a hand on her shoulder, her back, her thigh — while she is unable to see the touch coming. She feels it before she can prepare for it. The surprise is part of the training. She is learning to trust his touch when she cannot see its source.

### Holic's Commands and GG's Responses

- Holic begins simple commands. "Sit." "Stay." "Come." The commands are not new — she has learned them. But the cone makes them harder. When she tries to crawl, the cone may bump against the floor, may shift her balance, may make her aware of her own awkwardness in a way that crawling without the cone does not. She is not graceful. She is a pet in a medical device, moving clumsily, dependent on his direction to avoid running into furniture or walls.
- Holic may guide her with his voice, with a leash, or with taps on the cone itself — the sound of his fingers on the plastic becoming a directional signal. She learns to follow the sound of his taps the way a dog learns to follow a clicker. The cone is not just restriction; it is **re-education**. She must learn to navigate the world differently. She must become a different kind of pet.
- If she performs well, he praises her: "Good Girl. Good Girl." The words are the same, but they feel different when she is wearing the cone. She cannot see his face when he says them. She must trust that he means them. She must trust that he is smiling, that he is pleased, that she is doing well. The praise becomes **blind** in a way that mirrors her restricted sight.
- If she performs poorly — if she bumps into something, if she hesitates too long, if she tries to remove the cone with her hands — Holic corrects her. The correction may be verbal ("No. Stay.") or physical (a gentle but firm redirection of her body, or a tap on the cone). The cone makes correction easier for him and more disorienting for her. She cannot anticipate his movements. She cannot see his hand rise. She is always, in the cone, **a moment behind**.

### The Duration

- The cone is worn for a **significant period**. This is not a brief moment. It is an hour, two hours, an afternoon — long enough for GG to stop thinking of the cone as a device and start thinking of it as **the condition of her existence**. She eats in the cone (Holic may place her bowls directly in front of her, or he may hand-feed her through the opening, or he may require her to find the bowls by sound and smell). She rests in the cone (lying down is possible but awkward; the cone may press against the floor, forcing her to sleep in a specific posture, or Holic may place her in the doghouse with the cone on, where the heater warms her but the walls of the doghouse press against the plastic). She is toilet-trained in the cone (if the scene requires it — the cone restricts her ability to see what she is doing, making her even more dependent on Holic's guidance and approval).
- The duration is what makes the cone effective. It is not the shock of wearing it. It is the **acclimation** to wearing it. By the end of the session, GG should feel strange without it. The cone should become, temporarily, part of her normal. That is the goal: to make the abnormal so sustained that it becomes the frame through which she experiences everything else.

### Holic's Possible Monologue or Instruction

- Holic may speak to her during the session, explaining the cone's purpose in his calm, instructional voice. Not as a justification, but as a **statement of fact**:
  - "This is a veterinary cone. It is used for animals who cannot be trusted to leave their wounds alone. You do not have a wound. But you do have a habit of thinking too much. The cone stops that. You cannot see enough to think. You can only feel, and listen, and obey."
  - "The cone is not punishment. It is management. You are a pet. Pets are managed. This is how management looks."
  - "You cannot look at yourself in the cone. You cannot check your posture, your appearance, your dignity. Those are not pet concerns. In the cone, you are only what I see. And I see Good Girl."
- These lines are not apologetic. They are **definitive**. Holic does not ask whether she likes the cone. He tells her what the cone is for. He tells her what she is while wearing it. The instruction is part of the conditioning.

### Aftercare and Removal

- When Holic removes the cone, the effect is as important as the wearing. The sudden return of peripheral vision, the ability to turn her head, the sight of her own body — these are not merely relief. They are **reorientation**. She must come back to herself, and the contrast between the cone and the open air makes the cone's absence feel like a gift he has given her.
- Holic removes the cone carefully. He may lift it straight up, or he may unfasten it at the neck. He may touch her face, her neck, her hair — the places where the plastic pressed. He may check for red marks, for discomfort, for any sign that the device was too tight or too long. This is the **controlled-care** side of Holic: he manages her, but he also manages her safety.
- The aftercare is physical and verbal. Water. Warmth. Rest. His voice, low and soft: "Good Girl. You did well. You were a very good pet in the cone." He may hold her. He may let her curl against him. He may place her in the doghouse without the cone, where the heater warms her and the walls do not press against plastic, and she can sleep in a posture that is entirely her own — or as entirely her own as any posture in his house can be.
- The debriefing comes later, when she is Simonka again — or as Simonka as she can be. He asks what she felt. What she feared. What she wants to repeat. What she wants to refuse. The cone is not permanent. But its effects — the dependence, the narrowed world, the relearning of her body as a managed pet body — are meant to linger.

## EMOTIONAL BEATS

1. **The preparation:** Holic's calm, the cone's clinical presence, the sense that this is not improvised but planned. The tool is real. It is not a sex toy. It is a veterinary device. That reality is the source of its power.
2. **The placement:** The physical shift — the weight, the restriction, the immediate narrowing of the world. GG's first instinctive attempts to turn, to look down, to see her own body. The frustration is small but real. It is not pain. It is **limitation**.
3. **The disorientation:** Holic moving around her, speaking from different angles, touching her without warning. She must trust sounds she cannot verify with sight. She must trust touches she cannot see coming. The cone is teaching her that trust is not a choice but a necessity.
4. **The acclimation:** The duration. The eating, the resting, the moving, the obeying — all within the cone. She stops fighting it. She starts using it. She learns to navigate by sound, by touch, by his voice. The cone becomes, temporarily, her normal.
5. **The instruction:** Holic's words. The calm, clinical explanation of what the cone is and why she wears it. The removal of her personhood not through violence but through **category** — she is a pet, and pets wear veterinary devices. That is the logic. That is the world.
6. **The removal:** The return of sight, of movement, of self-awareness. The relief is not just physical. It is the relief of being seen again — by herself, by him. The contrast makes the open air feel like a gift.
7. **The aftercare:** The checking of her neck, the water, the warmth, the praise. He is not cruel. He is controlled. The cone is part of the care, not the opposite of it. This is the paradox that defines their dynamic.
8. **The debriefing:** Simonka's voice, returning. The questions about what she felt. The boundary-setting. The safeword discussion. The cone is over, but the data it gathered — about her tolerance, her dependence, her trust — remains.

## TONE & STYLE NOTES

- The tone should be **clinical and controlled**, not theatrical or cartoonish. The cone is a real veterinary device. The scene should feel like a medical procedure repurposed for psychological control. The more clinical it is, the more disturbing it is.
- Holic's voice should remain the same register he uses for all training: calm, instructional, unhurried. He does not raise his voice. He does not need to. The cone does the work.
- GG's experience should be **physical and sensory** rather than purely emotional. Focus on the sound of the plastic, the weight on her neck, the bump of the rim against the floor, the way her own breathing echoes inside the cone, the sudden disappearance of her hands from her field of vision, the inability to see Holic's face when he speaks from behind her.
- The humiliation should be **implicit**, not explicit. She is not mocked. She is managed. The cone is not a joke. It is a tool. The fact that it is a tool for animals is the source of the degradation — not because anyone says it is degrading, but because the category itself is unspoken and unavoidable.
- The aftercare should feel **genuine** — not a performative kindness, but the other side of Holic's control. He restricts her because he manages her. He manages her because he cares for her. The care is real. The restriction is real. Both are true.

## WHAT THIS SCENE ESTABLISHES

1. **The expansion of Holic's toolkit:** The collar, the doghouse, the bowls, and now the cone. Each device adds a layer. The cone is not an escalation into pain; it is an escalation into **dependence and sensory narrowing**. It shows that Holic's control is architectural — he builds the world she lives in, piece by piece.
2. **The veterinary framing:** The cone is not a BDSM hood. It is not a blindfold. It is a medical device for animals. This reframes the entire dynamic not as sexual roleplay but as **management and care of a pet body**. The clinical origin of the device is what makes it land with psychological weight.
3. **GG's deepening immersion:** She has already accepted the collar, the name, the bowls, the doghouse. The cone is the next step. It is harder because it is not about obedience but about **perception**. She must learn to be a pet not only in what she does but in **how she experiences the world**.
4. **The trust dynamic:** The cone makes her dependent on him in a new way. She cannot orient herself. She cannot verify. She must trust his voice, his touch, his direction. The cone is a **trust exercise** disguised as a restraint device.
5. **The aftercare pattern:** The cone, then the removal, then the checking, then the warmth, then the debriefing. This pattern repeats across Holic's training. It establishes that every restriction is followed by a return — not to full personhood, but to a gentler, warmer version of pet space. The cycle is the architecture of their relationship.
