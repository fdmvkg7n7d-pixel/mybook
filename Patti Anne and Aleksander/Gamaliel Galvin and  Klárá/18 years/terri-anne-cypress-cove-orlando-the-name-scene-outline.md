# SCENE OUTLINE: ORLANDO — TERRI ANNE AT CYPRESS COVE — THE NAME

## SCENE INFORMATION

- **Characters:** Gamaliel Galvin Key (19), Klára (18), Terri Anne
- **Location:** **Cypress Cove, Orlando-area, Florida** — the community Terri Anne has moved to after Mike's death: **a naturist (clothing-optional) resort community** on a lake [Kissimmee area; texture TBD: her own villa among the cypresses; a screened lanai where the visiting happens]. She has kept the Vaughan mansion in the family [status TBD], but she lives here now — somewhere with no staircase Mike never climbed, no chair Mike never sat in, and **no armor at all**: the widow of the wealthiest man in the world living where nobody wears their status, sometimes literally. Her neighbors know her as Terri. Most of them have no idea.
- **When:** 2062 — the last stop in North America. Vaughan farewells done; Prague next. They fly Toronto → Orlando with two dog crates and one question.
- **Length (CANON): eight days.** Not just the visit — a real last vacation: **they want to do Discovery Cove and Walt Disney World** before the ocean is between them and Florida. The eight days breathe: Cypress Cove as home base, park days in the middle, the question answered somewhere in the first days, the goodbye at the end.
- **Status:** Outline
- **Canon established by this scene:** Terri Anne lives at Cypress Cove, Orlando. (Resolves the open item on how the surviving matriarch carries on — she did not stay in the house.) The Orlando thread also seeds the family's future there: years later, Sabrina Bryna and William buy the Orlando mansion for Carlota and Keith's university years. **Terri Anne was the first Key in Orlando.**

## SCENE PURPOSE

One question. They have crossed a continent to ask it in person, because it is not a phone-call question:

**They want to name the ballet academy for Mike Key.** The whole school. Not just the first studio (*Mike Key Studio* — vowed over the melted banana split): the institution itself. *Mike Key Academy of Ballet.* They have carried the idea for three years and it has never once felt wrong to them — the school exists because of him; it should carry him.

**And Terri Anne talks them out of it.**

## SCENE ARC

### 1. Arrival at Cypress Cove

- Florida heat after a Toronto spring. Terri Anne on the lanai — older, settled, herself, and **dressed in exactly nothing but a sun hat and sunscreen, because this is Cypress Cove and that is how Cypress Cove works.** She does not make it a moment. She hands them iced tea and tells them where their room is.
- The dogs lose their minds with joy; Willow remembers her; Kleopatra (who never knew Mike, never knew the mansion) decides on sight that this woman is family. That detail does quiet work: the new dog loves her without history. So will the academy's students.
- The visit is unhurried — **eight days**, Cypress Cove as home base. Cypress Cove rhythms: early dinners, the lake, the pool, neighbors who know her as Terri, not as the widow of the wealthiest man in the world.

### 1b. Klára and the Clothes — Slowly (HANDLE WITH CARE)

- **Klára is very nervous about taking her clothes off at Cypress Cove. She does — but slowly,** and the scene must run entirely on her clock, the way the Germany–Poland–Czech night ran on her clock. Same grammar, different room: nobody pressures, nobody persuades, nobody makes it a milestone she owes anyone.
- What the nervousness is made of [for the writing]: a body that learned early to be guarded; a girl who spent childhood keeping things — including herself — packed and protected; the dancer's paradox (a body displayed nightly on stage, but always *in costume, in role, in armor*). Cypress Cove asks for the one thing the stage never asked: herself, with nothing designed about it.
- **How it goes:** Terri Anne sets the terms on arrival without being asked — *"Wear whatever you want. Half my neighbors do, half don't. Nobody here counts."* Day one, Klára stays dressed; nobody comments. Day two, the swimsuit at the pool. Then, late on the second day — the lake at the quiet end of the afternoon, Terri Anne reading nearby, Gamaliel in the water — **Klára, slowly, on her own decision, lets the last of it go and walks in.** No ceremony. Terri Anne does not look up from her book [she absolutely registers it; she will never say so]. Gamaliel does what he has done at every threshold of her life: stays exactly where he is, lets her come to him.
- What it means — and what it must not mean: this is **not** a scene about being seen by anyone else. It is the Vancouver arc completed: Kent was the old fear faced; Cypress Cove is the body, for one afternoon in warm water, *unguarded on purpose, by choice, and nothing bad happens.* The system never owned her; her father never owned her; the costume doesn't own her either. One afternoon of proof, witnessed only by the two people on earth she trusts completely — one of whom pretends to be reading.

### 2. How She Lives Now

- A beat for the saga's record: why Cypress Cove. Candidates [TBD, can combine]: the winters; a place with no rooms shaped like grief; the long-standing Key instinct for warm-weather refuge. She is not hiding — she is *resting*, the way Patti Anne rests in her empty nest. The matriarchs of this family know how to be alone well.

### 3. The Question — and Terri Anne's No (CANON)

- Evening, probably. The lanai, probably. [Keep the staging simple — two chairs, one widow, the lake.]
- Gamaliel — the business director, the one who handles names and papers — lays it out: *Mike Key Academy of Ballet.* The school itself, named for him. They have rehearsed this; it is the most certain they have been about anything except each other.
- **Terri Anne says no — and she is right, and she is the only person on earth who could say it to them.**
- Her reasoning, delivered with the matter-of-fact love of a woman who built a fortune alongside her husband and knows what names do: **a ballet academy needs a hip name.** They are opening a school in Prague for children who have not been born yet. The name on the door has to belong to *those kids* — something young dancers want on a hoodie, something that sounds like the future, something Czech parents can say and Prague teenagers can love. *"Mike Key"* is a Canadian businessman's name on a Canadian gravestone. *"You are not building him a monument,"* she tells them. *"You are building them a school. Name the school for them."*
- And then, before the hurt can even form, she gives them the better place for him — **the theater.** Every real academy eventually has one: the performance hall, the stage, the place where the work becomes art in front of an audience. **"Name the theater for Mike,"** she says. **The Mike Key Theatre.** Because a theater is where people *gather* — and that was Mike: the man rooms gathered around. The students will train in the academy with the hip name; and then, on the nights that matter most, they will dance *in Mike Key's house.* Mike gets every opening night, every recital, every season — every moment a family sits in the dark and watches a child they love do something extraordinary. *"That's him,"* she says. *"That was always him."*
- They see it immediately. Klára cries; Gamaliel writes it down [he doesn't need to; he does anyway — that is who he is].
- **Resolution (CANON):** the first storefront studio remains **Mike Key Studio** (the banana-split vow, kept). The eventual academy's performance hall is the **Mike Key Theatre**. And the academy itself becomes — as established in the opening-era files — **Pražská Baletní Akademie**: the Prague Ballet Academy, in Czech. Exactly what Terri Anne ordered: a name that belongs to the city and the students, not the gravestone — Czech-first, modern, a name Prague parents can say and Prague kids can wear. ### 3b. The Parks — Discovery Cove and Walt Disney World (CANON)

- The middle of the eight days belongs to the parks. Two nineteen- and eighteen-year-olds who skipped childhood's usual frivolity — barre at five, secrets at fifteen, a funeral at sixteen, a prison at eighteen — get, on the eve of becoming founders, a handful of days of pure unearned joy.
- **Discovery Cove:** the all-inclusive day — the lazy river, the aviary, swimming with the dolphins. For Klára it rhymes quietly with the lake at Cypress Cove: warm water as the place her body learns, again, that it is safe. [She is in a swimsuit and completely at ease — the contrast with the Cypress Cove nerves is the point: *costume vs. choice.* Both were hers.]
- **Walt Disney World:** [how many days, which parks — TBD; at minimum the Magic Kingdom]. Gamaliel — the institution-builder — cannot turn it off: he watches Disney's crowd flow, signage, cast members, the machinery of a place built so children feel only the magic, and takes actual notes for the academy. Klára lets him for exactly one hour, then confiscates the phone. [Light comedy; the saga earns it.]
- **Terri Anne comes along** [proposal — at least one park day: the widow on the teacups; Mike would have *hated* the teacups, which is precisely why she rides them].
- **Saga seed:** this is the family's first touch of Walt Disney World — decades before Carlota Delicia and Keith build their careers there (UCF, Imagineering). When Carlota's Orlando era comes, the family already has a memory in those parks: her older cousins, before Prague, before everything.
- **The decision born in the parks (CANON): the grade-eight Disneyland Paris trip.** Somewhere in the middle of a park day — queue, teacups, or the fireworks [staging TBD] — one of them says it out loud: *our students should get this.* The shape of the thought: they are two dancers who went straight from barre to vow to founding, and this week is the first purely childish joy either has been handed in years — and they are being handed it *right before* they become the adults in hundreds of children's lives. So they decide, on the spot, what kind of school they are building: **every grade-eight class of their academy — every year, as a fixed annual tradition — gets a trip to Disneyland Paris.** Grade eight on purpose — the age they were when everything in their own lives turned heavy (the rescue was at eight; by fourteen the secret had begun): the year their students get, institutionally, a few days where nothing is asked of their bodies but joy. Ballet school children live disciplined lives by definition; Pražská Baletní Akademie writes *play* into the curriculum — and writes it as a *rite*: same trip, every class, every year, so that any two alumni decades apart share the same teacups. [Candidate line, Klára, watching the fireworks: *"Nobody ever took me anywhere just because I was a child."* Gamaliel: *"Our kids go to Paris."*]
- **And the companion decision [same conversation or the Cypress Cove dinner after — staging TBD]: the grade-twelve class chooses its own city.** The mirror of the fixed trip: joy *given* at thirteen, choice *earned* at eighteen — the graduating class debates, campaigns, votes, and goes where it decided to go. The institution's last lesson is choosing together. (Decades later: Klárka's class chooses Paris, Simonka's chooses Rome.)
- Terri Anne's reaction [proposal]: she hears the plan at dinner back at Cypress Cove and quietly decides to endow it — the trip should never depend on a family's ability to pay, and no child should stay home. [Whether the endowment is hers or simply folded into the academy's budget — TBD. Either way, CANON: **no student misses the trip for money.**]

### 4. The Matcha

- She makes matcha. Three bowls — the third set down, full, at the empty place. Nobody comments. The ritual from the funeral night, transplanted to Florida, complete with the joke: somewhere, Mike is still calling it lawn water.
- Over the bowls, practicalities: she is the donor; the name on a school is a legal object; she will sign what needs signing [her lawyers and the business manager will handle the paperwork — first canonical task contact between Terri Anne's side and the academy's future business manager, TBD how that threads].

- The last North American goodbye. She walks them and the dogs to the car in the Florida evening.
- Candidate closing line, hers: *"Go on. Build it. I'll come see it when there's a door with my name on it to walk through."* — which doubles as a promise: **Terri Anne will visit Prague.** [Future scene seed: Terri Anne at the academy opening — and note the existing file `academy-opening-prep-grandparents-stay.md` for reconciliation.]

## SAGA CONNECTIONS

- **The name closes the circle from the banana-split night:** *"A studio, yes. The school you can name what you want."* They came to name it for Mike anyway — and his widow, of all people, is the one who redirects them: the studio keeps the vow, the theater gets his name, and the school gets its future. Grief that has finished its work sounds exactly like this.
- **Cypress Cove and the body:** the saga's long thread of bodies — burned, separated, deaf, blind, trained, harmed, healed — gets one of its gentlest beats: Klára, at eighteen, choosing for one afternoon to be unguarded, slowly, at no one's pace but her own.
- **Orlando seeds:** Terri Anne is the first Key in Orlando — years before Carlota and Keith's university mansion. [TBD: whether Terri Anne is still at Cypress Cove when Carlota arrives — the great-grandmother across town would be a warm thread.]
- **The matcha bowl count:** three bowls again — but now the empty place is set on purpose. The grief has become a ritual with a place at the table.
