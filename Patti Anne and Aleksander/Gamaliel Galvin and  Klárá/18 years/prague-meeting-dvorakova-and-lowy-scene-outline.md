# SCENE OUTLINE: PRAGUE — MEETING DR. DVOŘÁKOVÁ AND RABBI LÖWY

## SCENE INFORMATION

- **Characters:** Klára (19), Gamaliel Galvin Key (19–20), **Dr. Tereza Dvořáková**, **Rabbi Eliáš Löwy**
- **Location:** Prague — Dr. Dvořáková's practice [neighborhood TBD; proposal: Vinohrady, a converted apartment office with high ceilings — calm, residential, nothing clinical about the street]; Rabbi Löwy's study in the Jewish community, **Josefov**
- **When:** **Only after they are settled** (CANON): home real, storefront real, ground steady — months after landing. Klára has carried both letters since Vaughan, in the drawer where she keeps the things she does not talk about. Then, one ordinary week, she takes them out.
- **Status:** Outline — a diptych: two meetings, one week
- **Canon:** **Both Gamaliel and Klára meet both of them — together, in the room, at both first meetings.** They came to every important door of their childhood together; they are not going to start arriving separately now. (Ongoing therapy afterward is Klára's own; the *introductions* belong to them both.)

## THE HINGE — THE DRAWER (opening beat, shared by both panels)

- An ordinary Tuesday [or so]. The storefront has students now; the apartment has furniture that wasn't bought in a hurry; the dogs have a vet, a groomer, a favorite corner of Letná park. The life is real.
- Klára opens the drawer — the one where she keeps the things she does not talk about — and takes out both envelopes. She puts them on the kitchen table and looks at them through a whole cup of coffee.
- Gamaliel, across the table, does not ask. [He has been not-asking for months, perfectly.]
- She slides Hartwell's letter toward herself and Loewy's letter toward him, and says: **"This week."**
- That is the whole beat. Two envelopes, one word. The doors open together once they open.

# PANEL ONE — DR. TEREZA DVOŘÁKOVÁ

## THE CONTACT — THE CZECH LETTER (CANON-leaning proposal)

- Klára writes rather than calls — **and she writes in Czech.** The first time in her life her mother tongue is the language of *asking for help* instead of the language the harm happened in. She drafts it more than once. The wastebasket knows things.
- What the letter says [shape]: who she is; that Dr. Hartwell of Toronto is her referring clinician; that she has been in Prague for some months; that she was not ready before and is ready now. It is short. It is exact. It is the most adult document she has ever produced that wasn't a lease.
- Dvořáková's reply comes in two days, also short, also exact, with three possible appointment times and one sentence that decides everything: *"Dr. Hartwellová mi o vás napsala — jen tolik, kolik jste dovolila."* (*"Dr. Hartwell wrote to me about you — only as much as you permitted."*) Boundaries, demonstrated before the first hello. Klára reads the sentence twice and books the earliest time.

## THE MEETING

### 1. The street and the stairs
- Vinohrady [proposal]: plane trees, a courtyard building, a brass plate with her name small and unbothered. Nothing about the street says *clinic*. Klára notices her own body cataloguing exits on the stairwell — old habit, noted without judgment; she can hear Hartwell's voice naming it.

### 2. Both of them go in (CANON)
- **The first meeting is an introduction, not a session.** Klára wants Dvořáková to know, from the first hour, the whole architecture of her life — and Gamaliel is its central beam.
- Dvořáková meets the boy from Hartwell's letter in person: the one who has waited outside doors since he was eight. *"He waits well,"* Hartwell had written — and Dvořáková watches him sit beside Klára, exactly as close as asked, and understands the file.
- Texture of Dvořáková [TBD, proposal]: late forties; unhurried; a Moravian flatness of delivery that reads as cold for ten minutes and then, permanently, as *safe* — she does not perform warmth, she practices reliability. Tea offered, not pushed. A clock positioned so the client can see it and the therapist doesn't look at it.

### 3. What gets said
- Not the history — that is for the work, later, alone. The introduction covers the *terms*: what Klára wants from this room now, as an adult, in this language.
- Klára says a version of the thing she has never said in Czech: that some of what happened to her happened in this language, and she wants — eventually, not today — to be able to stand in it. Dvořáková nods like a builder being shown a load-bearing wall: *"Pak budeme pracovat pomalu. Czech is not a door we kick in. It is a room we light one lamp at a time."* [Working line.]
- Gamaliel speaks exactly once, when asked what he is to her [candidate]: *"Whatever the day needs me to be. Mostly the person outside the door."* Dvořáková writes one word in her notebook. [What word — never revealed. The saga keeps one secret here.]

### 4. The arrangement
- Going forward: sessions are Klára's own, weekly [day TBD — not Thursday; Thursdays have an owner]. Gamaliel back in the waiting room, the arrangement of a decade rebuilt in a new country.
- Records from Toronto: Klára signs the release herself — her own signature, the first time, noticed by all three of them and commented on by none of them.

# PANEL TWO — RABBI ELIÁŠ LÖWY

## THE CONTACT — IN PERSON

- Days later, same week. No letter ahead, no call: **they go to Josefov in person** — some letters should be handed over, not phoned ahead. [Practical note: the Jewish community office can direct them; or Loewy gave practical instructions on the back of the envelope — very Loewy, TBD.]

## THE MEETING

### 1. Josefov, on foot
- They walk in — past the Old-New Synagogue, the cemetery wall, the streets Klára named at the banana-split night. The quarter is no longer a dream city; it is a neighborhood with a bakery and scaffolding and tourists. That ordinariness is the point: holiness that has groceries.

### 2. The study, and the letter read standing up
- **Rabbi Eliáš Löwy** [texture TBD, proposal]: sixties, broader and louder than his Toronto cousin — the branch that stayed has a different weather. Books in four languages; a desk that has lost the war with paper.
- He takes his cousin's letter, **reads it standing up, in front of them, unhurried.** Somewhere in the middle, **he laughs once** — out loud, alone, without explaining. [What Loewy wrote that is funny: TBD and worth gold. Candidate: a dry line about Gamaliel's Hebrew accent being "Toronto's fault, not the boy's."]
- He folds the letter, looks at them both, and says [working line]: *"My cousin writes to me twice in thirty years. Once when his mother died. And once for you two. So. Sit."*

### 3. For Klára — the line with an address
- Löwy already knows, from the letter, whose great-granddaughter is sitting in his study. He says the thing Loewy said on the church steps years ago — that her line did not expire — **but here it has an address:** these streets, these synagogues, registries with names in them.
- [MAJOR FUTURE SCENE — flagged, not spent here:] whether Löwy can actually **trace the great-grandmothers in the Bohemian records.** In this first meeting he says only: *"Names can be found. When you want them found, come back."* — the offer made, the pace left to her. (The drawer-keeper is given a door, not a deadline. He read his cousin's letter carefully.)

### 4. For Gamaliel — six years received
- Löwy tests the student his cousin sent — gently, two questions of substance [TBD: one textual, one personal; the personal one is *"Why here? You could be Jewish anywhere."* — and Gamaliel's answer involves the woman beside him and the city around them and does not need to be long].
- Then the practical path: continued study in this community; the calendar lived in Czech now; and in time — **the beit din and the mikveh.** *(CANON: the conversion completes under Löwy; Gamaliel is likely twenty by completion.)*
- Löwy's closing of that thread [working line]: *"My cousin did six years of the work and gives me the harvest. Typical. Come Thursday."* — **the Thursday hour survives the ocean.** [Whether Löwy knows about the Toronto Thursdays from the letter and chooses the same day on purpose: yes. He never says so.]

### 5. After — the Old-New Synagogue
- They walk out into Josefov in the evening and stop in front of the **Old-New Synagogue** — the building Gamaliel looked up the night Klára first said *Prague*, seven hundred years old, finally not on a screen.
- They do not say much. [Candidate: Klára takes his arm. The closing image of the whole leaving arc — Kent, Vancouver, the farewells, Cypress Cove, the drawer, the two doors — lands here: two nineteen-year-olds in front of seven hundred years, both rooms rebuilt, the life begun.]

## SAGA CONNECTIONS

- **The two rooms, rebuilt (CANON complete):** tools (Dvořáková) and ground (Löwy), name for name, exactly as Vaughan built them — but entered now by an adult, on her own schedule, with her partner beside her or at the door, as each room calls for.
- **The waiting-room constant — and its one exception:** Gamaliel outside Hartwell's door at twelve; outside the Kent interview room at nineteen; outside Dvořáková's door for every ordinary session after the first. But the *first* meetings — the introductions — they do together, in the room. The posture of his whole love: *as close as allowed, never closer than asked* — and this time, he was asked.
- **Thursday survives the ocean:** Loewy's hour becomes Löwy's hour. Same day, different continent, the family line holding the schedule.
- **The wedding seed [TBD]:** two rabbis, distant cousins, reconnected by these two — if Gamaliel and Klára marry in Prague, the officiating question answers itself, twice.
