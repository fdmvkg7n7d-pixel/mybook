# Gamaliel Galvin and Klára — The Conversation About Simonka's Choice

## Tuesday Night Scene Outline

## Placement

**When:** Tuesday night, after Gamaliel Galvin returns from Charles University, where he saw Simonka Káta but could not bring her home. The same day Klárka told her parents what she and Raphael found in Simonka's apartment.

**Where:** The kitchen, and then the back step, of Gamaliel and Klára's house in Prague. The academy is dark across the courtyard. Klárka has gone to bed, or is pretending to. The dogs are home. For the first time all day, Gamaliel and Klára are alone.

**Continuity Lead-in:**

- Monday night: Klárka and Raphael found Simonka's apartment empty and the diary with the note **Přečti mě** / **Read me**.
- Tuesday morning: Klárka told both parents in Gamaliel's office; Klára cried softly throughout.
- Tuesday afternoon: Gamaliel went to Charles University, saw Simonka, led with love, asked if she was safe, did not demand she come home, did not bring the diary.
- Simonka said *I chose this.* She did not come home. She knows home is open.
- Tuesday night: Gamaliel comes back. Now the two parents are finally alone with what their daughter chose.

---

## Scene Purpose

This scene is the private grief that the office scene and the university scene did not have room for. All day, one of them has had to hold the room — for Klárka, for the academy, for Simonka herself. Now there is no room left to hold. There is only each other.

By the end of this scene:

1. Klára names her deepest wound — not *what* Simonka chose, but that Simonka used Klára's own language of bodily autonomy to lock the family out, and said *nic není* at the Friday table, the survival-word Klára used as a child.
2. Gamaliel admits he wanted to make Holic into Klára's father — and consciously rejects the comparison, because Klára had no choice and Simonka claims she did.
3. **Through their dogs — the goldens descended from Willow — they find the only frame that fits: you love something completely, you raise it, and one day it walks where you cannot follow, and love does not get a vote.**
4. They agree on the family's posture: door cracked, porch light on, dog at the door, and wait.
5. No resolution. Contact, not rescue.

---

## Core Emotional Conflict

They are not only grieving Simonka's choice. They are grieving the limit of love itself — the discovery that having loved a person perfectly, having built the whole life right, does not buy you the right to steer them. The dogs are the cruelest and gentlest version of this truth in the house. They are proof of devotion that *worked* — and proof that devotion was never control.

---

## Characters in the Scene

### Gamaliel Galvin

- Father. Just back from the university, still in his coat — the coat is how Klára knows before he speaks.
- Carries the Charles University encounter: Simonka *herself,* smiling at a classmate, saying *I chose this* like it was reasonable.
- Fights the Kent Institution instinct to make Holic into Klára's father, and rejects it out loud.
- Is the one who can give Klára back what she fears she destroyed.

### Klára

- Mother. Has been crying quietly all day in front of others; here, finally, cries fully.
- Her wound is the autonomy she taught Simonka being used as the lock on the door.
- Her foster history is the lens for everything: the difference between *somebody could come for me* and *there is no door to open here.*
- The one who finally voices the dog comparison, because the dogs are hers first — Willow was hers.

### The Dogs — Living Presence

- The household goldens, **descended from Willow** (Willow → Kleopatra → the present dogs). Willow herself is gone by now; her line is not.
- Not symbolic furniture — they physically anchor the scene: leaning on shins, wedging between chairs, lying across both parents' feet on the back step.
- They are the thread back to the snowy-boots New Year's, to a foster child who finally kept something she couldn't carry in a bag, to the whole family doctrine of *someone at the door.*

### Simonka Káta — Absent Presence

- Not present. Present in the empty chair, the Friday *nic není,* the diary in the office across the courtyard.

## Scene Structure

## Beat 1 — He Comes Home in His Coat

- Klára hears the gate, then the young golden going to the door — the breed's whole religion: someone is coming back.
- Klára does not go to the door. She has learned the worst news arrives quietest.
- Gamaliel enters the kitchen still in his coat and does not take it off. That is how she knows.
- **Key image:** the dog at the door; Klára at the counter with cold tea she has not drunk, the posture Patti Anne taught her without meaning to.

## Beat 2 — "You Saw Her."

- Klára: *You saw her.* Gamaliel: *I saw her.*
- He reports the impossible part first: Simonka is *herself.* A notebook. A smile at a classmate. Tired, ordinary, their daughter — saying *I chose this* like the most reasonable sentence in the world.
- Klára makes him sit. He says he can't. She makes him anyway. The coat finally comes off.

## Beat 3 — He Tells It in Order

- The courtyard, the stone, Simonka's separate adult world.
- He texted instead of calling out — held Klára's instruction like a barre.
- *You found it.* He led with love, did not say *how could you,* did not say *stupid,* did not curse Holic's name — and names what each silence cost him.
- He asked if she was safe. *Yes* — too fast. He asked if she could leave if she wanted. She looked insulted, as if he'd questioned whether her own choice was hers.

## Beat 4 — Klára Names Why That Look Hurts

- Klára: that is what it feels like when you've decided a terrible thing is *yours* — you defend the cage, because the alternative is admitting someone built it for you.
- *I know that. I know that, Gamalieli.* Her foster history speaking.

## Beat 5 — Kent, and the Refusal

- He admits: standing in that courtyard, his whole body wanted to make Holic into her father. Make it simple. A thing the law already has walls for. (The Kent Institution visit, age 18/19.)
- He rejects it out loud: *You were a child. You had no choice. This is not that.*
- Klára, devastatingly: *It's worse, in a way. Because this one she picked.* When it was Klára, somebody could come — Mike, the OPP, an eight-year-old boy too sick with a secret to keep it down. There was a thing to do, a door to open, a child to carry out. **Here there is no door to open. She locked it from the inside and the key is in her own pocket and she is twenty-three and she is allowed.**

## Beat 6 — "And I Taught Her That."

- Klára's central wound: the whole school, the whole point of it — *your body is yours, no one decides for you but you* — she said it to Simonka her whole life. *And now she's wearing another man's initials and I taught her the sentence she's using to keep me out.*
- The Friday table: she asked four times what was wrong; Simonka looked her in the face — the face that taught her how to read a face — and said **nic není.** *I survived my whole childhood on that word. She handed it back to me at my own table and I didn't catch it.*

## Beat 7 — Gamaliel Begins to Give It Back

- He refuses the easy collapse: *You taught her she gets to choose. You did not teach her* that. *Those are not the same sentence.*
- The argument: a child raised to believe her body belonged to the family / the school / a husband would have walked into something like this and *never told a soul and never come back out.* Simonka left a diary with a note that says *read me.* That is not a goodbye. That is a girl who can't say it with her mouth saying it the only other way she knows. **She wants to be found. That is the thing Klára gave her.**

## Beat 8 — THE DOGS — The Comparison That Finally Fits

*(The emotional center of the scene. The dogs are physically present — one leaning on Gamaliel, one underfoot.)*

- Klára looks at the goldens — **Willow's line, Willow's great-grand-pups, the dogs that come from the puppy she found a name for in the stars.** Willow is gone now. The line is not.
- The realization arrives through them, because they are the one form of love in this house that *worked* and is still here:
  - She raised Willow from a box with air holes Gamaliel punched badly. She fed her, walked her, kept her off the bed and let her on the bed. She did everything right. And Willow was never *hers* the way an object is hers. Willow was a life that chose to come to the door.
  - **You can love something completely. You can raise it inside the warmest house you ever built. And it is still its own creature. It still walks toward what it walks toward. Love was never the same as control — it only ever felt like it when the creature happened to walk your way.**
- Gamaliel takes it further (or Klára does — keep it shared): every golden in this family has been proof of the same thing twice over. Proof that devotion is real *and* proof that devotion does not get a vote. They open the door every morning trusting the dog comes back. They have never once been able to *make* her.
- The hard turn: *We raised our daughter the way we raised the dogs — open door, every morning, trusting she'd come home at the end of it. And she always did. Until she didn't.* The dogs always came back because the house was the best thing they knew. **The fear underneath everything: what does it mean that, for one week, Holic's world was the better-smelling door?**
- Klára's counter, the thing the dogs also teach: a golden that runs off and comes home is not a golden that stopped loving you. She comes back *because* the door was always open, not despite it. **A closed door does not make a creature stay. It only makes the leaving permanent.**
- This is where Klára stops drowning. The dogs give her a shape for it: you do not chain the thing you love to keep it. You keep the door open and you bear the unbearable arithmetic that the open door is the only door anything ever comes *back* through.
- **Key image:** Klára's hand in the gold fur of Willow's descendant while she says the thing she could not say about Simonka — a creature she raised and loved completely, who walks where she cannot follow, and love does not get a vote.

## Beat 9 — The Banana Split, the Whole Distance Traveled

- Klára: she keeps thinking about the banana split. Sixteen years old, Mike's kitchen, dance clothes, *two years then Prague,* a melted ice cream and a man who wasn't there. Terri Anne's twenty-five million because she wanted him to have done it for them. The whole life built from that.
- *I thought I'd gotten all the way out. A whole generation and an ocean. And the dark found a different door.*
- Gamaliel, carefully: this is not her father reaching across forty years. This is Simonka — a different person, a different kind of dark *if it's dark at all,* and he doesn't even get to decide that, because she didn't ask him.

## Beat 10 — The Back Step

- The kitchen is too small for the size of it; they go out to the back step. Prague orange and quiet beyond the wall. A tram somewhere. **The dog comes too and lies across both their feet** — the same posture, the same anchor, three generations on from a box under a Christmas tree.
- Klára, emptied to the real question: *What do we do.*

## Beat 11 — The Posture They Agree On

- Gamaliel: *I don't know* — the truest thing he's said all night — *Your way.* Keep the door open. Don't go to Holic. Don't shame her. Text that they love her and she's not in trouble and mean it on the nights they don't feel it. Read only enough of the diary to know she's safe and not one page more, because it's her voice, not evidence. Let her come back on her own feet.
- *The only thing worse than a daughter who walked into this would be a daughter too ashamed to ever walk out of it.*
- Klára: *And if she doesn't come back.*
- Gamaliel: then they spend every day she's gone making sure that if she ever turns around, the light is still on. The doctrine, stated plainly and tied back to the dogs: **this family does not carry people out. We keep the door cracked and the porch light on and the dog at the door, and we wait — the way every golden in this house has been allowed to come home, the way Klára herself walked the last part out of childhood on her own feet because Mike kept a place to come to.**

## Beat 12 — Closing Image

- *She's going to call* — Klára says it not because she believes it but because saying it is the last door she has to open tonight. Gamaliel: *She's going to call.*
- They leave the back-step light on when they go in. They will leave it on every night after, without discussing it — a small electric act of faith, in a family once handed twenty-five million dollars' worth of the same thing.
- The dog settles by the door out of habit older than she is.
- **Final line/image:** *The door, cracked. The porch light, on. The dog, at the door.* It is the only thing this family has ever known how to do. It was enough once for a girl in snowy boots with a name she found in the stars. They go to bed praying it is enough again.

## What This Scene Establishes

1. **The private grief lands.** First moment all day neither parent has to hold the room.
2. **Klára's true wound is named:** her own teaching of autonomy became Simonka's lock, sealed with the survival-word *nic není.*
3. **Gamaliel refuses the Kent / Klára's-father collapse** while honoring why the instinct exists.
4. **The dogs become the governing metaphor:** descended from Willow, they are love that *worked* and is still in the house — proof that you can raise a creature completely and it still walks where you cannot follow; that an open door, not a closed one, is the only thing anything comes back through.
5. **The family doctrine is articulated and tied to the dogs:** door cracked, porch light on, dog at the door — echoing Mike's protection, Terri Anne's $25M, and the recurring "someone at the door" motif.
6. **No resolution.** Contact, not rescue. The next beats (Simonka/Klára call, family meeting, eventual Holic question) remain open.
