# GAMALIEL GALVIN — CONVERSION TO JUDAISM

## Arc Document

- **Character:** Gamaliel Galvin Key
- **Faith of origin:** Catholic (the Key family faith)
- **Faith chosen:** Judaism — **following Klára**, into the faith of her Jewish great-grandmothers
- **Status:** Canon arc; scene outlines to develop

---

## THE ONE-SENTENCE TRUTH

Gamaliel does not convert to a religion he found. He converts to a religion he *watched* — in the girl at the next barre, in the foster sister down the hall, in the woman he marries — until the watching became wanting, and the wanting became his.

## THE SHAPE OF THE ARC

### Age 5–8 — Before knowing

Gamaliel and Klára are classmates at Victoria Ballet Academy from age five. He knows her port de bras before he knows her story. There is nothing religious in this era — only the long foundation: *she was always there.*

### Age 8 — Klára comes home

After the rescue, Klára joins the household. She arrives with almost nothing she owns — and one thing nobody placed in a garbage bag or left behind at a foster home: **her great-grandmothers.** The thread of Jewish memory in her bloodline. In a loudly Catholic family — grace before dinner, Mass, the whole warm machinery of it — Klára's heritage is quiet, portable, hers. **And the family honors it in practice from the very beginning: when Klára becomes part of the family, Patti Anne or Aleksander starts taking her to a temple in Vaughan** — delivering her to her great-grandmothers' faith with the same fidelity they bring to their own Mass. **And Gamaliel goes too — by his own wish, from the first visit.** Eight years old, he asks to come along **because he wants to support Klára as she becomes a member of his family**: she has to walk into one more unfamiliar room full of strangers, and he has decided she will not do it alone. (He is, already, the boy from the rescue — the one who decided once and permanently that Klára does not face things alone.) So the pews are shared from the start: week after week, the Czech girl and the boy beside her. The supporting comes four years before the wanting — and is made of the same material.

And the temple is not only heritage — it is **healing**: alongside Klára's psychologist (the clinical room), **Rabbi Loewy becomes one of the two adults outside the family who help her get through the pain of what her father did.** The psychologist gives her tools; Loewy gives her ground — and because Klára is a minor, it is **Patti Anne who gives the permission, and she gives it, for the two of them to talk to each other about her**: clinician and clergy coordinating around one child, neither room ever blindsiding the other. What Gamaliel watches at twelve — the steadiness her heritage gives her — is therefore not an abstraction: he is watching the temple *work*, week after week, on the person he loves.

### Age 8–12 — The watching

Gamaliel grows up beside her and notices, the way children notice everything: that Klára carries something the rest of them inherited differently. The Keys' faith fills rooms. Klára's fills *her*. It survived her father. It survived the placements. It is — like her ballet — a thing no one could take, because she carried it in her body and her memory, not in a suitcase.

### Age 12 — The wanting; Age 13 — the asking (CANON)

**At twelve, Gamaliel realizes he wants to be Jewish.** He carries it for about a year — through Patti Anne's pregnancy with the twins — and **asks at thirteen**, once the babies are born and the house has found its feet.

This is the same season of his life in which he is beginning — without yet having the words for it — to love Klára. The saga should keep these two things distinct but rooted together: he does not want Judaism *in order to* love her, and he does not love her *because* she is Jewish. Both desires grow from the same root: **Klára, and everything she is.** He sees how her heritage affects her — how it steadies her, how it gives the girl who owns nothing an inheritance — and he begins loving her, and the two recognitions arrive in him in the same year, indistinguishable at first the way everything is at twelve.

How it surfaces (RESOLVED — see `gamaliel-asks-patti-anne-and-aleksander-to-convert-scene-outline.md`): **he asks — with Klára in the room and Patti Anne nursing the infant Salena Sarai.** The wanting takes root at twelve (~2055); he carries it deliberately through Patti Anne's pregnancy, refusing to add to her load, and asks at **thirteen** (late 2056, the twins months old), at the kitchen table after dinner, the whole family present: *"Can I convert? I want to be Jewish."* He does not sneak, announce, or rebel — he brings it to the right adults at the right table, exactly as he brought Klára's secret to Mike — and he deliberately does not wait for Klára to leave, because what he wants is woven from what she carries. **Patti Anne and Aleksander support his decision, that night, at that table:** *"Then we'll help you do it properly."* The formal threshold (study, beit din, mikveh) remains an adult's door years away, but the family's answer is yes from the start — he can learn now, the family takes it seriously now, and the same dinner becomes the first night Klára's inheritance is asked about, welcomed, and made room for out loud.

### Age 12–18 — The long patience

The wanting now lives in the open — asked and answered at the family table, supported from the first night. **Within months (early 2057), the family takes him to meet Rabbi Avram Loewy** — the rabbi of the Vaughan temple **they already bring Klára to** — the whole unit, Klára included — and the long learning begins: the turning-away done and met, the terms set (no conversion for a minor; real study instead), the weekly hour in the rabbi's study that runs alongside the barre for the rest of his adolescence (see `gamaliel-meets-the-rabbi-scene-outline.md`). It reads (openly now; a shelf in his room; loaned books going back and forth). It asks Klára questions — carefully, because her heritage is braided into her worst memories as well as her best — and after the kitchen-table dinner, the household itself makes room: her great-grandmothers can be spoken of at this table. The wanting sits tenderly, no longer oddly, inside a Catholic household that loves them both. The relationship turns romantic and secret at fifteen; Mike catches them and protects them; Mike dies when they are sixteen; the vow is made — *two years, then Prague.*

**Mike's death (late 2059) lands inside the learning.** Three years of Thursdays in, the man who took his turn driving Klára to temple is gone. **Rabbi Loewy comes to the Catholic funeral Mass** — back row, kippah — and on the church steps gives the two sixteen-year-olds *"May his memory be a blessing,"* then converts Gamaliel's Thursday hour into a grief hour: *"Come and don't study. Just come."* That night, over the melted banana split drunk from Mike's juice glasses, Gamaliel says ***zikhrono livrakha*** as his own for the first time, and Klára answers it — the first Hebrew that is *theirs*. It is the week the conversion stops being about study and becomes about a tradition that knows what to do with death. *(See the funeral and banana-split outlines in `../../Mike and Terri Anne/`.)*

**The Germany–Poland–Czech trip** (see `gamaliel-galvin-and-klara-trip-to-germany-poland-czech.md`) is the crucible of this era: walking Auschwitz-Birkenau beside the great-granddaughter of Jewish women, Gamaliel understands that the faith he wants is not an aesthetic or an atmosphere — it is a people, a grief, an endurance. He comes home from that trip with the wanting annealed into commitment.

### Age 16–19 — the last Toronto years and the formal path

There is no university. The *"two years, then Prague"* of the funeral-night vow are the **last two years of Victoria Ballet Academy** — and by then the learning is already old: Gamaliel has been studying with **Rabbi Avram Loewy of the Vaughan temple since thirteen** (the family walked him to that door within months of the asking — see `gamaliel-meets-the-rabbi-scene-outline.md`). Six years of the weekly hour, the Hebrew, the calendar lived rather than memorized. In these last Toronto years the question turns procedural: when he comes of age, the rabbi's *no conversion now* expires — and what remains is timing.

Then the leaving: graduation at eighteen; Klára's visit to her father at Kent Institution; the three days in Vancouver; and the permanent move to Prague — Gamaliel **nineteen**, Klára **eighteen**.

**The handoff (CANON):** at the leaving, **Rabbi Loewy writes a letter to Rabbi Eliáš Löwy of Prague — his distant cousin — and gives it to Klára to carry.** One family name, two spellings: the branch that emigrated west (Loewy) and the branch that stayed in Bohemia (Löwy). The letter carries six years of Thursdays in it — a referral and a homecoming at once, the Toronto rabbi sending his student down his own family line, back to the city the name came from. (Dr. Hartwell does the same for Klára — a letter to her conference colleague **Dr. Tereza Dvořáková** in Prague.) **Klára keeps both letters and contacts no one until she and Gamaliel are settled** — home real, storefront real, ground steady. Then she reaches out to Dvořáková and Löwy, on her own feet, on her own schedule. So Gamaliel's path resumes without being rushed: begun in Vaughan, paused for the landing, completed in Prague. **The conversion completes in Prague's Jewish community, under Rabbi Löwy** — the nineteen-year-old (by completion, likely twenty) convert finishing in Josefov itself, in the community whose streets become his family's neighborhood. ### Prague — the life inside the faith

They move to Prague together. The academy rises. And the family's neighborhood of the heart is **Josefov — the old Jewish Town**: the synagogues, the narrow streets, Simonka's favorite restaurant near the Spanish Synagogue (see `dinner-in-jewish-town-scene-outline.md`). What was once Klára's private inheritance and Gamaliel's private wanting becomes, in Prague, simply *the family's life* — candles, seasons, a city that holds a thousand years of the faith's memory in its stones.

## KLÁRA'S SIDE OF IT

**He follows Klára.** That is the arc's spine — but Klára's own relationship to her Jewishness is its own story, not a prop for his:

- Her heritage comes through **her great-grandmothers** — memory and blood more than practice, for most of her childhood.
- It is braided into pain: her biological father, the houses that ended, the things she does not talk about. Gamaliel's reverence for her heritage must never feel like pressure on her wounds.
- Over the years — the camps, his conversion study (her answering questions she has to research herself, the two of them learning her own inheritance together), Prague, raising daughters — the thread thickens from memory toward practice.

**The quiet reversal:** Klára spent her childhood receiving the Key family's world — their house, their Christmases, their faith filling the rooms around her. Gamaliel's conversion is the love story running the other way: **a Key, at last, moving into *her* inheritance.** The family that took her in produces a son who asks to be taken into what is hers.

## SAGA CONNECTIONS

- **Conversion-toward-belonging** is the Key family pattern: Klára into the family, Gamaliel into Judaism, Palmer Peter into Islam, Terri Anne's $25M act of faith. The Keys convert to *people* before principles.
- **The body as text:** ballet taught Gamaliel that repetition is devotion and submission to a form is freedom — the same grammar his brother later finds in Islamic prayer. For Gamaliel it is the Shema at night, the calendar in the body, the discipline of a chosen life.
- **Josefov:** the dinner scene's "deep sense of mystery and history" is no longer just atmosphere — it is the family's *own* quarter. Their Prague is not Castle-and-Charles-Bridge Prague; it is the Jewish Town.
