# MIKE TELLS PATTI ANNE

## A Prose Scene

**When:** The same night Gamaliel tells Mike what Klára told him about her father. Gamaliel is seven or eight. Klára is still living in her father's home. This is four months before Klára becomes Patti Anne's foster daughter.

**Where:** Mike and Terri Anne's house. Late evening, after dinner, after the kitchen floor has been cleaned, after Gamaliel has been put somewhere safe and quiet.

**POV:** Patti Anne, with Mike as the emotional anchor.

**Core fact:** *Little man* is Patti Anne's nickname for Gamaliel Galvin — her son, her first child, the boy who came before Aleksander, before Palmer Peter and Salena Sarai, before Klára entered the household as a foster daughter. When Mike tells Patti Anne what her little man told him, he is not only reporting a crisis. He is handing a mother the knowledge that her son has been carrying something no child should carry.

---

Patti Anne knows something is wrong before Mike says her name.

It is not one thing. It is the accumulation of small wrongnesses — the house too quiet after dinner, Terri Anne's voice low in the hall, the smell of disinfectant where there should only be dish soap and coffee, Mike standing in the kitchen with his sleeves rolled to his forearms and his face held in that particular stillness Patti Anne has known since childhood.

Mike Key is a calm man. Patti Anne has known his calm her whole life. She has leaned on it, resented it, trusted it, measured the world against it. But this is not ordinary calm. This is the calm he uses when something terrible has already happened and the next thing must be done correctly.

She stops in the doorway.

"Dad?"

Mike looks up.

That is the first confirmation. He does not soften quickly enough. He tries — because he is Mike, because she is his first girl, because even now some part of him wants to protect her from the shape of what is coming — but he is half a second too late. Patti Anne sees the effort. Patti Anne has spent her entire life reading this man's face. She knows when the truth is arriving.

"Where's Gamaliel?" she asks.

She almost says *my little man*, because that is what she calls him when she is not thinking. Her little man. Her solemn, watchful boy. Her tiny dancer with bruised shins and perfect posture and a heart too large for his own chest. But the words catch behind her teeth because Mike's face changes at the sound of the question, and now fear steps fully into the room.

"He's upstairs," Mike says. "Terri Anne's with him. He's safe. He's asleep, or close to it."

"What happened?"

Mike does not answer immediately.

That is the second confirmation.

Patti Anne comes into the kitchen. She sees the bucket near the sink, the cloth folded over the faucet, the floor cleaner put away but not quite where it belongs. She sees a damp patch on the tile where someone has scrubbed hard. She sees Mike's hands — clean now, but red at the knuckles from hot water.

Her body understands before her mind does.

"Was he sick?"

Mike draws a breath.

"After," he says.

"After what?"

Mike looks toward the hallway, toward the stairs, toward the place where Gamaliel has been tucked away from the adult consequences of what he said. Then he looks back at Patti Anne.

"He told me something."

The kitchen goes still around her.

Patti Anne is the oldest of eight sisters. She has been the one who notices, the one who helps, the one who translates adult tension for younger children and child fear for adults. She knows the weight of the sentence *he told me something*. It is never small. Not when Mike says it like that.

"About him?" she asks.

It is the mother's first terror. Always. Before compassion can widen, fear narrows to the child with your blood in his body. Did someone hurt him? Did someone touch him? Did someone threaten him? Did her little man carry his own wound up to his grandfather and break under it?

Mike hears every question she does not ask.

"No," he says gently. "Not him."

Patti Anne closes her eyes for one second, and hates herself for the relief that comes. It is immediate and shameful and human. Not him. Not her little man. The relief is there — and then, almost before it can finish moving through her, the horror rushes in to take its place.

Not him means someone else.

"Who?"

Mike's jaw tightens. Only once.

"Klára."

Patti Anne knows the name. Of course she knows the name. Gamaliel has said it for years — Klára from ballet, Klára at the barre, Klára who turns too fast, Klára who gets corrections from Madame and never cries, Klára who forgot her ribbon, Klára who stood beside him in rehearsal. A classmate. A small blond presence at the edges of Patti Anne's knowledge, made mostly of her son's sentences.

Not family. Not yet.

A girl from school.

A child.

"What about Klára?"

Mike does not look away from her. Patti Anne will remember that later — that he gave her the respect of the whole truth without making her chase it, and the mercy of not making it graphic. He tells her what Gamaliel told him. He uses careful words. Adult words when he must, plain words when plainness is kinder. He tells her that Klára told Gamaliel about her father. He tells her that what is happening in that house is not discipline, not a misunderstanding, not a child exaggerating. He tells her that Gamaliel carried it as long as he could and then came to Mike.

He tells her that after Gamaliel said it, he vomited.

Patti Anne makes a sound then.

Not a sob. Not a word. A small, broken intake of breath that belongs to the part of her that is only mother. The eldest sister, the competent woman, the daughter trained by a lifetime of Key family crises to stand upright when the world tilts — all of that disappears for one exposed second.

Her little man.

Her little man stood in this kitchen and repeated another child's pain in words too small for it, and then his body gave out.

She grips the back of a chair.

Mike notices, because Mike notices everything, but he does not move toward her too quickly. Patti Anne has always needed one second to prove she is still standing. He gives her that second. Then he comes around the table and puts one hand over hers on the chair back.

"He did the right thing," Mike says.

Patti Anne nods, but the nod is jagged.

"He should have told me."

It comes out before she can stop it. Not accusation. Grief. The old ache of motherhood: why did my child carry this past me? Why did he climb the stairs of my father's house instead of finding me? What did I miss? Where was I standing when my little man decided this was too heavy and did not put it in my hands first?

Mike's hand tightens over hers.

"Patti."

She looks at him.

"He came to the adult he could reach in the moment he broke," Mike says. "That is not a measure of who he loves. It is not a failure of yours."

She wants to believe him. Because it is Mike saying it, she almost can.

"He's seven," she whispers.

"I know."

"He's seven, Dad."

"I know."

Now Mike's voice changes — not breaking, exactly, but deepening with the force of what he is containing. Patti Anne hears the fury under it then. Not at Gamaliel. Never at Gamaliel. Not at Klára. Never at Klára. At the man in Klára's house. At the world that made a seven-year-old girl tell a seven-year-old boy. At the fact that his grandson's body had to reject dinner because an adult failed so completely at being a father.

"I called the OPP," Mike says.

Patti Anne stares at him.

"Already?"

"Yes."

Of course already. She knows her father. She knows the man who does not wait when a child is in danger. She knows the particular shape of his love: immediate, practical, leveraged. He has a phone number and a name and enough authority behind his own name to make someone listen after hours. A child is being hurt. Mike has moved.

"What happens now?"

"They'll go to the house. Children's Aid will be involved. There will be an investigation. She'll be removed if what Gamaliel told me is confirmed. Maybe tonight. Maybe early morning. I pushed as hard as I could."

"If?" Patti Anne says, and there is steel in her voice now.

Mike sees it. Something like pride flickers behind the grief in his face.

"Procedural word," he says. "Not my word."

Patti Anne lets go of the chair. She turns away, then turns back because there is nowhere to put her body. Her mind keeps making pictures she does not want: Gamaliel at the table, Gamaliel speaking, Gamaliel vomiting, Klára in a house with a father who should not be allowed near the word.

Father.

The word lands hard.

Patti Anne knows something about fathers who are not what they should be. Randy's failure is not Klára's father's violence; she would never flatten the difference. But the wound is in the same part of the body. A father is supposed to be shelter. A father is supposed to be the person between the child and the harm. When a father becomes the harm, the world inverts.

Mike raised her. Mike chose her. Mike stood where another man did not.

And somewhere across town, a little girl from Gamaliel's ballet class has no Mike in the house.

Not yet.

The thought comes and goes so quickly Patti Anne does not recognize it as prophecy.

"Can I see him?" she asks.

"Yes. But Patti — softly. He is asleep or almost there. Terri Anne got him cleaned up. He was shaking. He needs you calm."

Patti Anne almost laughs, once, without humour.

"Do I look calm?"

"No," Mike says. "But you know how to be calm for a child."

That is true. It has been true since she was small enough to still need calming herself. Firstborn daughters learn early. Patti Anne learned with babies on her hip and sisters crying into her shoulder and adults saying more than they thought she could understand. She knows how to put her own panic on a shelf long enough to kneel by a bed.

But this is not a sister. This is Gamaliel.

Her little man.

"What do I say to him?"

Mike's answer is immediate.

"You tell him he did right. You tell him you love him. You tell him Klára's secret is with adults now. You tell him he doesn't have to carry it anymore."

Patti Anne nods. She presses the heel of her hand to her mouth.

"And if he asks what happens to her?"

Mike is quiet for a beat.

"Then you tell him the truth we can promise. We are going to try to make her safe."

Patti Anne hears what he does not add. They cannot promise the system will be gentle. They cannot promise fast. They cannot promise perfect. Mike can call the OPP, can wake up the machine, can push it with every ounce of Key force behind him — but once the report leaves this kitchen, it enters rooms with forms and thresholds and people whose names Klára does not know.

They can promise effort.

They can promise that the secret is no longer trapped inside children.

For tonight, maybe that is the first miracle.

Patti Anne wipes her face. She had not realized she was crying. Mike pretends not to notice until she is done; then he hands her a towel because tissues are not on the table and Mike is practical even in catastrophe.

She takes it.

"Dad?"

"Yes."

"Thank you."

He frowns as if the gratitude is unnecessary, which it is, and also not enough, which it could never be.

"He's my grandson," Mike says.

Patti Anne looks at him — at the man who is her father in every way that has ever mattered, at the man who has already made the call, already cleaned the floor, already held the boy, already absorbed the first terrible telling so she did not have to watch it happen.

"I know," she says.

Then she goes upstairs.

---

Gamaliel's room is dim. Terri Anne is sitting in the chair near the bed, one hand resting on the blanket as if touch alone can keep a nightmare from entering. She looks up when Patti Anne appears, and her face says everything Mike did not.

Patti Anne crosses the room carefully.

Gamaliel is curled on his side. His hair is damp at the edges from where someone wiped his face. He looks younger asleep than he does awake. Awake, he carries himself like ballet has already made him responsible for every bone in his body. Asleep, he is just a little boy with one hand tucked under his cheek.

Her little man.

Patti Anne kneels beside the bed.

His eyes open before she touches him.

For one second, fear is there. Not fear of her. Fear that he is still in the telling. Fear that the words are still happening. Fear that he has done something wrong by letting them out.

Patti Anne puts her hand on his hair.

"Hi, little man."

His face crumples.

Not all the way. Gamaliel is a child trained to hold position, even in grief. But his mouth trembles, and his eyes fill, and he reaches for her with the blind certainty of a much younger child.

Patti Anne gathers him carefully, mindful of his stomach, mindful of his shaking, mindful of the fact that he has already given up more than a child should have to give. Terri Anne slips out of the chair, not leaving, just making room.

"Grandpa told you?" Gamaliel whispers.

Patti Anne closes her eyes over his head.

"Yes."

"Is Klára in trouble?"

There it is. Not *am I in trouble*. Not first. His first waking fear is for her.

Patti Anne holds him tighter.

"No, sweetheart. Klára is not in trouble."

"Her dad—"

"I know."

He stiffens at that, because *I know* means the secret is real outside his body now. It is in the room. It is in his mother's arms. It is no longer only something whispered between children.

Patti Anne remembers Mike's words. She uses them because they are right.

"You did the right thing," she says. "You did exactly the right thing telling Grandpa."

Gamaliel shakes his head against her.

"I told," he says, and there is shame in it. Child-shame. The kind adults create when they make secrecy feel like loyalty. "She told me and I told."

Patti Anne pulls back just enough to see his face.

"Listen to me, little man. If someone is hurting a child, telling a safe adult is not betrayal. It is rescue."

He blinks.

The word enters him slowly.

Rescue.

He does not know yet that it will become one of the words attached to his life. He does not know there will be another rescue, a physical one, four months from now. He does not know that Klára will one day sleep under Patti Anne's roof, that Willow will sleep on her bed, that Mike will sit in her room years later and tell her she is not losing this family. He does not know any of that.

Tonight he only knows that his mother has not pulled away.

"Grandpa called people," Patti Anne says. "Adults whose job is to help keep children safe. Klára's secret is with adults now. You don't have to hold it by yourself anymore."

His breath catches.

"Will they make him stop?"

Patti Anne cannot promise the whole future. She will not lie to him. Key children are not protected by lies; they are protected by adults who stand beside the truth and do not flinch.

"They are going to try," she says. "Grandpa is going to make sure they try tonight."

Gamaliel absorbs that. His body is still tense, but something in him gives way — not relief exactly, not peace, but the first loosening of a knot that has been pulled too tight for too long.

He presses his face into her shoulder.

"I threw up."

It is said with humiliation so small and ordinary that Patti Anne almost breaks again.

Of all the things to be ashamed of. Of all the things.

She kisses his hair.

"I know."

"On Grandpa's floor."

"Grandpa has had eight daughters and a lot of grandchildren in his house," Patti Anne says, her voice steadier now because this she can give him — the normal, practical mercy of making vomit unimportant. "That floor has survived worse."

From the doorway, Mike makes a sound that might be agreement and might be the beginning of a laugh he will not allow himself to have yet.

Gamaliel turns his head. He sees Mike there, large and steady in the dim hallway.

"Sorry," Gamaliel whispers.

Mike comes in. He crouches beside Patti Anne, bringing himself down to the level of the bed.

"Nothing to be sorry for," he says. "Not one thing."

Gamaliel looks between them — his mother and his grandfather, both there, both knowing, neither angry, neither leaving. Patti Anne sees him trying to understand what has changed. The secret is gone. The love is still here. For a child, this is not obvious until it happens.

Patti Anne brushes his hair back.

"Sleep now," she says. "We'll be here."

"What about Klára?"

"Grandpa is handling it," Patti Anne says.

It is the strongest sentence she has.

Gamaliel believes it. Not fully, not enough to erase the fear, but enough for his eyelids to lower. Enough for his body, exhausted by telling and vomiting and being held, to begin surrendering to sleep.

Patti Anne stays until he is out. She stays longer than that. Mike stays in the doorway. Terri Anne stands beside him. No one says much. There are nights in a family when words become too clumsy and presence has to do the work.

Downstairs, the kitchen floor is clean.

Across town, the phone call Mike made is moving through wires and radios and adult systems toward a house where a little girl does not yet know that a boy from ballet has broken his own small body open to tell the truth for her.

Patti Anne sits beside her son and keeps one hand on his blanket.

Her little man sleeps.

And the secret is no longer his to carry.

## Notes for the Saga

1. **"Little man" belongs to Patti Anne.** It is her private, maternal nickname for Gamaliel Galvin — tender without being cute, rooted in the fact that he has always seemed solemn and older than his years while still being unmistakably her small son.

2. **This scene sits between `gamaliel-tells-mike-scene.md` and the later rescue.** Mike has already called the OPP, or calls them immediately before/after this conversation depending on final timeline preference. The emotional function is the same: Patti Anne learns that Gamaliel carried Klára's disclosure and broke under it.

3. **Patti Anne's wound around fathers is activated here.** She does not equate Randy with Klára's father, but the theme is deliberately adjacent: a father can fail; a father can wound; Mike is the counterexample.

4. **This is an early seed for Patti Anne fostering Klára.** Patti Anne does not decide in this scene to become Klára's foster mother. She may not even consciously imagine it yet. But the first thread is tied here: Klára stops being only "a girl from ballet" and becomes the child her little man tried to save.

5. **The promise is careful.** Patti Anne and Mike do not promise Gamaliel that everything will be fixed. They promise that adults have it now, that he did right, and that he does not have to carry it alone. That is the honest protection this scene can offer.
