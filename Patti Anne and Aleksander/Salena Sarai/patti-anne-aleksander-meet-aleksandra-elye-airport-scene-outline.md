# Patti Anne and Aleksander Meet Their Granddaughter Aleksandra Elye for the First Time — Airport Scene Outline

## Placement

**When:** The Australia leg of Patti Anne and Aleksander's three-continent trip. They have already been to Prague (Gamaliel Galvin and Klára) and Saudi Arabia (Palmer Peter). This is the last stop — Perth.

**Where:** Perth Airport, Western Australia. Arrivals.

**Continuity Lead-in:**
- Prague: They visited Gamaliel and Klára's ballet academy. (In the Simonka Káta timeline, this is also when Patti Anne visited Holic's mansion and Aleksander went the next day.)
- Saudi Arabia: They visited Palmer Peter's compound. Seven women. Three pregnancies. The living room. Best behavior.
- Perth: The last stop. The rest stop. The easy one — or so Salena Sarai said. But there is something waiting at the airport that Patti Anne and Aleksander do not yet know.
- **The last visit:** The last time Patti Anne and Aleksander came to Perth, the child they knew as Aleksander Eli Junior was totally miserable. Withdrawn, unhappy, wrong in a way no one could name. He did not want to be held. He did not want to play. He did not want to be in his own skin. The visit was shadowed by it — a grandchild who could not settle, parents who could not explain why, and grandparents who felt the wrongness without understanding it. Patti Anne noticed. She always notices. But she did not have the shape of it then. She has the shape of it now — because Salena Sarai told Eli, and Eli told no one, and Patti Anne is about to see the answer running toward her in a baby blue dress.

## Characters

- **Patti Anne Key**: Grandmother. Catholic. The family anchor. She has just come from Prague and Saudi Arabia — carrying everything those visits gave her. She is about to see something she has seen before, one generation up, in her own youngest daughter Ashley Erin. She will know exactly what she is looking at.
- **Aleksander**: Grandfather. Patti Anne's partner. Father of Salena Sarai and Palmer Peter. The man the twins are named for — Aleksandra Elye carries his name, feminized. He is about to hold both his granddaughters in his arms and kiss their cheeks, and one of them is wearing his name in a new form.
- **Salena Sarai Key**: Daughter. The perceptive one. She is watching her parents' faces at the arrivals gate — watching for the moment they see. She told Eli she would not hide it. She is not hiding it.
- **Eli**: Salena Sarai's husband. Australian. Standing beside his wife at the airport, steady, present.
- **Aleksandra Elye**: Granddaughter. Twin with Xana Dylan. Wearing a matching baby blue dress and ivory tights — the same as her sister. This is who she is. This is how she is meeting her grandparents.
- **Xana Dylan**: Granddaughter. Twin with Aleksandra Elye. Wearing a matching baby blue dress and ivory tights.

## Scene Outline

### The Arrivals Gate

- Perth Airport. The arrivals hall. Salena Sarai, Eli, and the twins are waiting. The twins are dressed in matching baby blue dresses and ivory tights — identical outfits on two small bodies. Xana Dylan and Aleksandra Elye, side by side, looking toward the doors where the passengers come through.
- Salena Sarai has dressed them deliberately. Not to make a statement. Not to force a confrontation. Because this is what her daughters wear. This is what Aleksandra Elye chose — what she always chooses — and Salena Sarai said she would not hide it. The matching dresses are not a costume. They are a Tuesday. They are an ordinary morning in this household, where both twins put on the same thing and walk out the door together.
- Salena Sarai is calm on the outside. Inside, she is watching the arrivals door the way she watches everything — reading the room before it happens, feeling the shape of the moment before it lands. Eli stands beside her. His hand is on her lower back. He does not say anything. He does not need to.

### They Come Through the Door

- The arrivals door opens and closes, opens and closes. Strangers stream through. And then — Patti Anne and Aleksander.
- They look tired. Not jet-lag tired — the other kind. The kind Salena Sarai warned Eli about. Prague and Saudi Arabia are on their faces — the love, the complexity, the weight of three weeks on two continents visiting two lives that require constant translation. They are carrying bags and carrying more than bags.
- Then they see the twins.

### The Girls Run to Aleksander

- The moment the twins spot their grandfather, they break free. Both of them — Aleksandra Elye and Xana Dylan — run toward Aleksander. Not a walk. Not a toddle. A full, headlong, small-body sprint across the arrivals hall, baby blue dresses flying, ivory tights flashing, arms outstretched. The girls are so excited to see their grandparents — squealing, laughing, pure joy on their faces, the kind of happiness that makes strangers in an airport smile.
- They run to Aleksander. Not to Patti Anne — to Aleksander. The man they are named for. The grandfather whose name one of them wears in a form he has never heard before.
- The contrast with the last visit is immediate and devastating. The last time Patti Anne and Aleksander were here, the child they knew as Aleksander Eli Junior was totally miserable. He would not run. He would not laugh. He did not want to be picked up. He sat apart, withdrawn, unhappy in a way that filled the room with something no one could fix. Aleksander had tried to hold him and the boy had gone rigid — not defiant, just wrong. Trapped in something. The whole visit had been shadowed by it — a grandchild who could not be reached, who sat in his own discomfort like a sentence he couldn't finish.
- Now that same child is sprinting across an arrivals hall in a baby blue dress, ivory tights, arms wide open, face lit up, screaming "Grandpa! Grandpa!" — and she is the happiest person in the airport.
- Aleksander sees them coming. His face — the face that has been carrying Prague and Saudi Arabia, the face that cried at a kitchen table in Prague, the face that told Holic he would dismantle everything — changes. The weight lifts. The tiredness lifts. Everything lifts. His granddaughters are running toward him in matching baby blue dresses and he does what he has always done: he opens his arms.
- Aleksander picks them both up immediately. One in each arm. He does not hesitate. He does not pause. He does not look at their dresses and process what he is seeing. He picks up his granddaughters and he holds them and he kisses them both on their cheeks — one cheek, then the other, then back again, the way grandfathers kiss grandchildren when they have crossed the world to see them.
- Aleksandra Elye in one arm. Xana Dylan in the other. Matching baby blue dresses. Matching ivory tights. Matching granddaughters. He holds them like they are the most natural thing in the world — because to him, in this moment, they are.
- And then it hits him. The child in his left arm — the one pressing her face into his neck, the one giggling, the one whose body is relaxed and warm and *settled* — is the same child who went rigid in his arms the last time. The same child who sat alone and miserable through an entire visit. The same child who could not be reached. That child is gone. This child — this girl, in this dress, with this name he has not heard yet — is here. And she is happy. She is so happy. The difference is so total, so complete, that Aleksander's arms tighten around both of them and he closes his eyes for a moment because he understands, in the way that grandparents understand before anyone tells them, that something has been fixed. Something that was wrong is now right. He does not know the details. He does not need to. He is holding the proof.

### Patti Anne Sees

- Patti Anne is two steps behind Aleksander. She has stopped walking. She is standing in the middle of the arrivals hall, bags at her feet, watching her husband hold two girls in matching dresses.
- She sees Xana Dylan. She sees Aleksandra Elye. She sees the baby blue dresses, the ivory tights, the two small faces pressed against Aleksander's cheeks. She sees two granddaughters.
- And she knows.
- She knew before she got on the plane. Salena Sarai did not tell her directly — but Patti Anne is Patti Anne. She is the woman who raised eight daughters, who raised a transgender child, who watched Ashley Erin become herself. She has seen this before. She has held this before. She knows the shape of it the way she knows the shape of her own hands.
- The recognition hits her not as shock — not as confusion, not as adjustment — but as something deeper. A wave of love so specific and so old that it takes her breath away. She is looking at Ashley Erin. Not literally — but the echo. The same echo Salena Sarai saw in her own kitchen. A child who feels funny in the wrong clothes and settles in the right ones. A child whose name was already reaching toward something. A child in a baby blue dress at an airport, being kissed by a grandfather who did not blink.
- And now she understands the last visit. The misery. The rigidity. The child who could not be held, who could not settle, who sat in wrongness like a locked room. That was a child in the wrong clothes, the wrong name, the wrong everything — and no one knew. Patti Anne remembers it now with perfect, painful clarity: she had sat with that unhappy grandchild on her lap and felt the wrongness radiating off the small body and not known what it was. She knows now. She knew from the moment she saw the baby blue dress. The misery was not a phase. It was a child who had not yet been allowed to be herself. And now she has been. And she is running. And she is laughing. And she is the happiest person in the airport.
- Patti Anne's eyes fill. She does not cry — not yet. But her chin trembles. The Catholic mother from Canada who has crossed three continents in three weeks — who sat through a lunch in front of a run in Prague, who counted three pregnant bellies in Saudi Arabia, who has held everything this family has thrown at her and loved it anyway — stands in Perth Airport and watches her husband hold their granddaughters and feels the thing she has felt at every turn of this family's story: *I have been here before. I know this. I know how to love this.*

### Patti Anne Goes to Them

- Patti Anne walks forward. She reaches Aleksander and the twins. Aleksandra Elye turns in Aleksander's arm and looks at her grandmother. Baby blue dress. Ivory tights. Her grandfather's name, feminized, on her birth certificate and on her lips.
- "Nana!"
- Patti Anne takes Aleksandra Elye from Aleksander's arm. She holds her — close, tight, the way she holds every grandchild, the way she held Klárka Pavlînka, the way she will hold every child this family gives her. She presses her face into Aleksandra Elye's hair and breathes in.
- She does not say: *I know what this is.* She does not say: *This happened before.* She does not say: *Your aunt Ashley Erin.* She does not explain. She does not teach. She does not ask questions.
- She says: "Look at you. Look at my beautiful granddaughter."
- The word — **granddaughter** — lands in the arrivals hall the way it landed at a ten-foot fence in Prague. The same word. The same love. The same Patti Anne. A woman who calls her grandchildren what they are, wherever they are, whatever it costs her to get there.
- Aleksandra Elye smiles. She does not know the weight of the word. She does not know about the fence, or the run, or the patio, or the marking, or the kennel. She does not know about three pregnant bellies in Saudi Arabia or a living room floor. She is a child in a baby blue dress being held by her grandmother in an airport. And her grandmother called her beautiful.

### Salena Sarai Watches

- Salena Sarai is standing ten feet back. She has not moved. Eli's hand is still on her lower back. She is watching her mother hold Aleksandra Elye and she is crying.
- Not grief. Not relief. Something in between — the specific tears of a daughter who feared her parents would be better at this than she is, and who is watching that fear come true in the most beautiful way possible.
- Patti Anne looks up from Aleksandra Elye and sees Salena Sarai. Their eyes meet across the arrivals hall. Patti Anne's face — still holding the grandchild, still wet-eyed, still tender — says everything Salena Sarai was afraid of and everything she hoped for:
- *I see her. I love her. You did right. I'm here.*
- Salena Sarai takes a shaking breath. She walks forward. Patti Anne shifts Aleksandra Elye to one hip — the practiced, effortless move of a woman who has carried children her whole life — and opens her other arm for her daughter.
- Salena Sarai steps into her mother's arms. Mother and daughter and granddaughter. Three generations in an arrivals hall in Perth, at the end of a three-continent trip, at the last stop, the rest stop, the easy one.
- Aleksander stands beside them, Xana Dylan still in his arms, kissing the top of her head. Eli stands behind Salena Sarai, one hand on her shoulder. The family — small, specific, Australian and Canadian and something entirely its own — holds together in the middle of an airport while strangers stream past with their luggage.

### Walking Out

- They walk out of the airport together. Aleksander carries Xana Dylan. Patti Anne carries Aleksandra Elye. Salena Sarai walks between her parents, her arms linked through theirs — a daughter flanked by the people who made her, heading toward the car park, heading toward the home she built on the far side of the world.
- Aleksandra Elye, still in Patti Anne's arms, reaches over and touches Patti Anne's face. A small hand on an old cheek. Patti Anne catches the hand and kisses the palm.
- "Nana's here," she says. "Nana's here."
- The baby blue dress is soft against Patti Anne's arm. The ivory tights are warm. Her granddaughter smells like shampoo and airplane snacks and Australia. And Patti Anne — the woman who has been everywhere, who has seen everything, who has loved through every fence and every floor and every ocean — holds on.

## Tone & Style Notes

- The scene is pure warmth. After Prague and Saudi Arabia — after the run, the marking, the kennel, the three pregnancies, the living room floor — Perth is the exhale. The airport is the landing. This is where Patti Anne and Aleksander stop carrying and start being held.
- Aleksander picking up both girls without hesitation — without processing, without pausing to look at the dresses — is the most important beat. He does not see a question. He sees his granddaughters. He picks them up. He kisses their cheeks. The simplicity is the point.
- Patti Anne's recognition should feel earned — not dramatic. She has done this before with Ashley Erin. The echo is real and specific. But this is not a crisis. This is a baby blue dress at an airport. This is a grandmother calling her grandchild beautiful.
- Salena Sarai crying because her parents are better at this than she feared — and better than she is — is the emotional center. Her fear was never about rejection. It was about being surpassed in love. And she is. And it is the best thing that has ever happened to her.
- The matching dresses and ivory tights should be treated as ordinary — because they are ordinary. That is the whole point. Aleksandra Elye is not performing. She is getting picked up at the airport by her grandparents, wearing what she always wears.

4. **Salena Sarai's tears.** Her fear was not rejection — it was that her parents would be better at this than she is. They are. And it is beautiful.
5. **The contrast with the last visit.** The last time they were here, Aleksander Eli Junior was totally miserable — rigid, withdrawn, unreachable. Now Aleksandra Elye is sprinting across an airport in a baby blue dress, screaming "Grandpa!" The difference is total. The misery was not a phase — it was a child who had not yet been allowed to be herself.
6. **The exhale after Prague and Saudi Arabia.** Perth is the last stop. The rest stop. And it turns out the rest stop has its own gift — a granddaughter they didn't know they had, wearing a baby blue dress and their name.
7. **"Nana's here."** Patti Anne's line — simple, repeated, final. The family anchor, anchoring.
