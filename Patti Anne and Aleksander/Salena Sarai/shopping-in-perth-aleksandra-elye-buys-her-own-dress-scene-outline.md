# Shopping in Perth — Aleksandra Elya Buys Her Own Dress — Scene Outline

## Placement

**When:** After Christmas, before the school decision. Patti Anne and Aleksander have left. The name has formed — **Aleksandra Elya** — and it's sticking. But the St Hilda's application has not yet been confirmed. The family is preparing for a future that isn't guaranteed yet. Aleksandra Elya still shares a wardrobe with Xana Dylan. She still reaches for her sister's clothes. The girls wear matching outfits because that's what twins do, and because it's easier than navigating separate closets. This shopping trip is the first time they go out with the explicit purpose of buying Aleksandra Elya *her own* things — not Xana Dylan's things that she borrows, but things chosen for her, by her, in a store, with her own bag.

**Where:** Perth, Western Australia. A shopping centre near Mosman Park — somewhere bright, air-conditioned, ordinary. Australian summer. The kind of place where families drift from store to store with ice creams and shopping bags and the low hum of a Saturday afternoon.

**Context:** This is not a crisis shopping trip. There is no drama, no confrontation, no moment of public panic. It is exactly what it looks like: two parents taking two daughters to buy clothes. But for Aleksandra Elya, it is the first time the world meets her as herself in a store. The first time a sales assistant says, "Can I help you find something, love?" and the something is for *her*, not for a sister she shares a closet with. The first time she gets to point at a dress and say, "That one," and have it be hers.

There is an undercurrent they don't talk about: **they don't know if St Hilda's will accept them yet.** The application is in. The interviews may not have happened. The school might say yes — or it might say no. And if it says no, they'll need a plan B: a co-ed school, a different all-girls school, something that can accommodate both twins. So this shopping trip carries a quiet weight: they are buying clothes for a life that is forming but not yet confirmed. They are betting — with money, with hope, with the small, brave act of a child choosing her own dress — on a future that the world hasn't promised yet.

## Characters

- **Aleksandra Elya**: The daughter who chose herself. By this point, the name has stuck — she answers to it, she wears it, she is Aleksandra Elya in every room she enters. But she has never bought her own clothes. She has worn Xana Dylan's clothes — borrowed, shared, the twin exchange that has always been natural between them. This trip is the first time she walks into a store with the intention of leaving with something that is hers alone. She is excited — the buzzing, restless excitement of a child going to do a new thing — and she holds Eli's hand.
- **Xana Dylan**: Her twin sister. Equally excited, but for different reasons. Xana Dylan is here for her own clothes too — she needs new things, she's growing, she wants to pick something fun — but she is also deeply aware that this trip is *different* for her sister. She doesn't understand the full weight of it, but she understands the shape: today, Aleksandra Elya gets her own dress. Xana Dylan is happy about this. Twins share everything; today, they are sharing the act of choosing separately.
- **Salena Sarai Key**: Mother. The perceptive one. She is watching everything — the way Aleksandra Elya's eyes move across the racks, the way she hesitates before reaching for something, the way her body language shifts when she finds the right thing. Salena is not steering. She is not suggesting. She is *witnessing* — the way she has always witnessed her children, reading the current under the surface, letting them find the words and the shapes for themselves. She is also watching Xana Dylan — making sure her daughter doesn't feel left out, making sure the twin bond holds even as they branch into separate wardrobes. She is doing the work of a mother who knows that both of her children need to feel seen, just in different ways.
- **Eli**: Father. The gentle anchor. He is the one carrying the shopping bags before they even have anything in them. He is the one who says, "Take your time, girls," and means it. He is not perceptive the way Salena Sarai is — he doesn't read the micro-expressions, the hesitations, the almost-choices — but he reads the big picture: his daughters are happy, and he is happy because they are happy. He is the one who holds the hand of the child who needs holding and lets go of the child who wants to run. He is Australian in his ease — unfazed by the ordinary miracle of a family shopping trip, treating it like exactly what it is: a Saturday, a store, two girls picking out clothes.

## Scene Outline

### The Drive There

- The car ride to the shopping centre. Perth summer — the windows down, the air warm, the sky that specific Australian blue that looks like nothing else on earth. Aleksandra Elya is in the back seat, bouncing slightly — not enough to be restless, just enough to show the energy underneath. Xana Dylan is singing something — a song from school, or a children's tune she's half-remembered, the kind of thing that fills a car ride with noise.
- Eli is driving. Salena Sarai is in the front passenger seat, looking back at the girls in the rearview mirror. She catches Aleksandra Elya's eye and smiles. Aleksandra Elya smiles back — the full, unguarded smile of a child who has nothing to hide anymore.
- "What are we looking for today?" Eli asks, not because he doesn't know, but because asking is part of the ritual. It gives the girls a chance to say it out loud.
- "Clothes!" Xana Dylan shouts.
- "My own clothes," Aleksandra Elya says. Quietly. Not shy — just clear. The way she has been learning to say things clearly since the nightie, since the airport, since the name stuck.
- "Your own clothes," Eli agrees. He says it the way he says everything: as a fact, as something natural, as the most ordinary thing in the world. "That sounds like a good plan."
- Salena Sarai watches Aleksandra Elya's face in the mirror. The child hears "your own clothes" and something in her expression shifts — not dramatically, just a small settling, like a puzzle piece clicking into place. She has heard this before — at home, in the bedroom, in the kitchen, at school — but hearing it in the car, on the way to a store, with the whole family present and moving toward the thing, it lands differently. It's real in a way the bedroom wasn't. It's public in a way the kitchen wasn't. It's a Saturday plan. It's normal.
- Aleksandra Elya leans back in her seat and sings along with her sister. Both girls singing. Both girls bouncing. The car is full of noise and warmth and the kind of happiness that doesn't need to announce itself.

### The First Store — The Names

- The shopping centre is bright and cool and smells like pretzels and floor polish. The kind of Australian mall that exists in every city — the same layout, the same stores, the same echo of footsteps on tile.
- They go into a children's clothing store first. Nothing fancy — the kind of place where the clothes are sorted by size and colour and the sales assistants are patient and unhurried.
- The sales assistant — a woman in her fifties, Perth-born, the kind of person who has seen everything and judges none of it — greets them with: "G'day. Looking for anything in particular?"
- Eli: "Two girls. Growing out of everything. We're in trouble."
- The woman laughs. "Aren't they always. Take your time, love. Sizing's on the wall if you need it."
- She looks at the twins — both in their matching outfits (maybe Xana Dylan's old things, the shared wardrobe still in use) — and says, without missing a beat: "You two look alike! Are you twins?"
- "Yes!" they say together. The twin synchrony — automatic, joyful, the thing that has always defined them.
- "Well, you're gorgeous. What are your names, then?"
- **Xana Dylan** goes first. Confident, clear, the way she says everything: "Xana Dylan." She doesn't add "Key." She doesn't need to. The name is enough. The sales assistant smiles. "Xana Dylan. What a beautiful name."
- **Aleksandra Elya** goes second.
- She doesn't say the old name. She doesn't say "Junior." She doesn't hesitate or look at her parents for permission or help. She steps forward — just half a step, the small forward movement of a child who knows what she's about to say and means it — and she says it:
- *"Aleksandra Elya."*
- Not rushed. Not shy. Not testing it out to see if it fits. The full name — her grandfather's name, feminized, her father's name tucked inside it — spoken softly but with the kind of sweetness that makes the air in the store go still for just a second.
- The sales assistant's face lights up. "Aleksandra Elya. Oh, that's a gorgeous name, love. Both of you are gorgeous."
- And then it hits Salena Sarai and Eli.
- They knew the name. They chose it. They've said it at home, in the kitchen, in the bedroom, at school, at the airport. They've heard it from Patti Anne, from Aleksander, from each other. But they have never heard it from *her* — from Aleksandra Elya herself — spoken to a stranger in a store, in the ordinary world, without anyone prompting her, without anyone holding her hand through it.
- She said it herself. Sweetly. Clearly. Like it was the most natural thing in the world — because to her, it is.
- Salena Sarai's hand goes to her mouth. Just for a second. The perceptive one, the woman who reads rooms and currents and the space between words, is caught completely off guard by the simplicity of her daughter saying her own name.
- Eli feels it too. The quiet shock of hearing the name you gave your child spoken back to you by the child herself — not as a gift you gave her, but as a truth she found.
- They look at each other. Not with words. With that look parents share when something happens that they didn't expect and don't need to explain. *Did you hear that?* *Yes.* *Did she just —?* *Yes.*
- Eli kneels down. Not to the level of a small child who needs lifting — to the level of a girl who just told the world who she is. He pulls both twins into him, one arm around each, and kisses the top of each of their heads.
- Xana Dylan giggles. "Daddy!"
- Aleksandra Elya doesn't giggle. She leans into him. Her head against his chest. The way she leaned into the headband, the way she leaned into the nightie, the way she leaned into her grandmother at the airport — the same settling, the same *this is where I belong.*
- Salena Sarai kneels too. She kisses the top of Aleksandra Elya's head — soft, lingering, the kind of kiss a mother gives when the words are too big for her mouth. Then she kisses Xana Dylan's head too, because this is a twin moment, and you don't leave one twin out of a kiss.
- "You're both so brave," she whispers. She means it for both of them — Xana Dylan for saying her name with the ease she always has, Aleksandra Elya for saying hers for the first time in a room full of strangers and not flinching.
- The sales assistant watches from behind the counter. She doesn't comment. She doesn't need to. She's seen families like this before — not the specifics, not the story — but the shape of it. The shape of parents who love their children and children who know it.
- "Right then," she says, warm and unhurried. "Go on, girls. Pick what you like."
- And the twins go.

### Xana Dylan Chooses First

- Xana Dylan goes straight for colour. Bright pink, electric blue, something with sequins — the kind of clothes a seven-year-old girl chooses when given free rein in a store. She holds things up to herself and asks: "Do you like this one, Daddy?"
- Eli: "If you like it, I like it."
- "But do *you* like it?"
- "I like it when you're happy. So if the dress makes you happy, it's my favourite dress in the world."
- Xana Dylan considers this. Then she nods, satisfied, and adds three more things to her pile. She is efficient about it — the kind of child who knows what she wants and gets it. No second-guessing, no hesitation. Just choice, decision, done.
- Aleksandra Elya watches her sister. She sees the ease of it — the way Xana Dylan just *picks* things, without wondering if they're right, without feeling the weight of each selection. For Xana Dylan, clothes are clothes. Fun, colourful, something to wear. For Aleksandra Elya, clothes have been something else — something loaded, something shared, something she reached for because they felt right when her own things felt wrong.
- Now she gets to choose without reaching for someone else's pile. And she doesn't know how to do it yet.
- She stands by the racks and touches things — a sleeve here, a hem there — but nothing makes the *settled* feeling she got from Xana Dylan's headband on Christmas morning. Nothing clicks. She looks at Salena Sarai, just for a second, and Salena Sarai looks back with the expression she has always had for this child: *I see you. Take your time. I'm not going anywhere.*

### The Dress

- Aleksandra Elya moves deeper into the store. Past the bright colours, past the sequins, past the things Xana Dylan would pick. She goes to the quieter section — softer colours, simpler cuts, the kind of dress that doesn't announce itself but asks to be noticed.
- And then she sees it.
- It's not pink. It's not glittering. It's a dress in a muted colour — maybe lavender, maybe soft blue, maybe something that catches the light in a way that makes it shift between shades. It's the kind of dress that looks like nothing from the outside and everything when you hold it up.
- She takes it off the rack. She holds it against herself. She looks at it.
- And she doesn't need to look at anyone else. She doesn't need to check with Xana Dylan, or Salena Sarai, or Eli. She doesn't need to borrow, or share, or ask permission. She holds the dress and she knows — the way a child knows, without language, without explanation, without doubt — that this is *hers.*
- "I like this one," she says.
- Salena Sarai is there in a moment. Not hovering — just present, the way she has always been present for this child's discoveries. She looks at the dress. She looks at Aleksandra Elya holding it. She sees the set of her daughter's shoulders — relaxed, settled, the same posture she had in the Hello Kitty nightie, the same posture she had in the baby blue dress at the airport. The body saying what the mouth doesn't need to: *yes, this, this is me.*
- "It's beautiful," Salena Sarai says. And she means it — not just the dress, but the choosing, the moment, the child who is learning to want things for herself.
- Eli comes over. He looks at the dress. He looks at Aleksandra Elya. He smiles — the slow, warm smile of a father watching his daughter do something ordinary and enormous at the same time.
- "Looks like you found it," he says.
- Aleksandra Elya nods. She doesn't let go of the dress. She holds it like she held the headband — with both hands, not because she's afraid someone will take it, but because she doesn't want to put it down. It's hers. She chose it. And she is keeping it.

### The Undercurrent — The School Question

- While Aleksandra Elya holds the dress, Salena Sarai's mind drifts — just for a moment — to the thing they haven't named in the store. The application. St Hilda's. The school where both girls would start as themselves, in matching uniforms, where no one would need to ask which one is which or why they're both in dresses.
- The application is in. The interviews haven't happened yet. They don't know if St Hilda's will say yes. They don't know if the school that seemed perfect — all-girls, both twins together, no explanation needed — will open its doors to two children whose enrolment forms still carry names and categories that don't match who they are.
- Salena Sarai doesn't say this out loud. Not in the store. Not in front of the girls. But she thinks it — the way she thinks everything: quietly, perceptively, holding the weight without letting it show.
- Eli catches her expression. He knows what she's thinking. He's been thinking it too. The plan B — the co-ed school down the road, the different all-girls school in a different suburb, the school that *might* understand but might not. The schools where they'd have to explain Aleksandra Elya. The schools where they'd have to make a case for their daughter instead of just enrolling her and letting the uniform do the work.
- He doesn't say anything either. He just reaches for her hand and squeezes it. The squeeze says: *We'll figure it out. Whatever happens, we'll figure it out.*
- Aleksandra Elya doesn't know any of this. She's holding the dress. She's happy. She's seven. The future is a word she hasn't learned to worry about yet.
- Xana Dylan, meanwhile, has found something else — a second dress, or a skirt, or a shirt with stars — and she's showing it to her father with the same boundless enthusiasm she brings to everything. "Daddy, look at this one!"
- Eli's face brightens — the easy, immediate warmth of a father whose daughter is showing him something she loves. "That's a good one," he says. And it is.
- The moment passes. The undercurrent stays where it belongs — below the surface, where parents keep the things their children don't need to carry yet.

### The Fitting Room

- The sales assistant appears — not intrusively, just helpfully — and directs them to the fitting rooms. "Take as many as you like in, love. If you need a different size, just call out."
- Xana Dylan goes first. She emerges in three different outfits, doing a little runway walk for Eli and Salena Sarai, who clap and cheer the way parents are supposed to. She tries on a pink dress, a blue skirt, something with stars — and she likes all of them, and she can't decide, and that's fine, because she's seven and indecision is part of the fun.
- Then Aleksandra Elya goes in with the lavender dress.
- The fitting room door closes.
- Salena Sarai sits on the bench outside. Eli stands beside her. Xana Dylan leans against the wall, still wearing her own clothes, waiting for her sister to come out. The three of them — mother, father, daughter — are doing the ordinary thing of waiting in a fitting room while another daughter tries on a dress.
- The door opens.
- Aleksandra Elya steps out.
- The dress fits. It's the right size — not too big, not too small — and it sits on her shoulders the way clothes are supposed to sit: like they belong there. The colour is right. The cut is right. The fabric is right. Everything is right.
- She stands in the doorway of the fitting room and looks at her family. She doesn't twirl. She doesn't perform. She just stands there — in the dress, in her own clothes, in the thing she chose — and lets them see her.
- Salena Sarai's breath catches. Just slightly. Not because the dress is beautiful — though it is — but because the child in the dress is *settled.* This is the same posture she saw on Christmas morning with the headband, the same posture she saw at the airport in the baby blue dress. The body saying yes. The child saying, without words: *this is who I am.*
- Eli says, simply: "There she is."
- Xana Dylan says: "I like it."
- Aleksandra Elya smiles. Not the full, explosive smile of Christmas morning — the quieter smile of a child who has found something and knows it's hers. She looks down at the dress, then back up at her family, and nods.
- "It's mine," she says.
- "It's yours," Salena Sarai agrees.
- She goes back into the fitting room. Not to take it off — to get her own clothes so she can put the dress back on. She wants to wear it out of the store. She wants to wear it home. She wants to wear it *now.*

### The Checkout

- They gather at the register. Xana Dylan has a pile of things — the pink dress, the blue skirt, the star shirt, three more things she added at the last minute because "but Daddy, what if I change my mind?" Aleksandra Elya has the lavender dress. Just the one. She doesn't need more. The dress is enough.
- The sales assistant scans everything. She rings up the total. She bags the clothes — two separate bags, because they are two separate girls with two separate choices.
- "Here you go, loves," she says, handing the bags to Eli. "Enjoy your day."
- "Thank you," Eli says. He always says thank you. It's one of the things Salena Sarai loves about him — the way he treats every interaction with the same quiet courtesy, as if every person he meets deserves to be thanked for existing.
- Aleksandra Elya holds her bag. Not the big one — the small one, the one with the dress inside. She holds it with both hands, the same way she held the dress in the fitting room, the same way she held the headband on Christmas morning. She is not letting go.
- Xana Dylan holds her own bag. She looks at her sister's bag. She looks at her own. She says: "We both got our own things."
- "We did," Aleksandra Elya says.
- "That's good," Xana Dylan says. And it is. It's good. It's normal. It's what families do on Saturdays.

### The St Hilda's Conversation

- On the drive home, after Xana Dylan has fallen asleep and Aleksandra Elya is tracing the shopping bag's handles with her finger, Salena Sarai turns to Eli. Quietly. The kind of conversation parents have when the children can't hear it.
- "Do you think they'll take them?"
- Eli doesn't pretend not to know what she means. "St Hilda's?"
- "St Hilda's."
- He thinks about it. The way he thinks about everything — not quickly, not slowly, just honestly. "I don't know. I think so. But I don't know."
- "If they don't —"
- "We'll find another school."
- "Another school that —"
- "Another school that will take both of them. As girls. As twins. As themselves. There are other schools, Sal. There's always another school."
- She knows he's right. But St Hilda's was the plan — the all-girls school where the uniform would do the work for them, where both twins could walk in as themselves without anyone needing to explain the gap between the enrolment form and the child standing in front of it. If St Hilda's says no, the gap opens again. They'd have to explain. They'd have to make a case. They'd have to ask for permission instead of just enrolling.
- "I want them to say yes," she says.
- "I know," Eli says. "Me too."
- A pause. The car hums along the road. Aleksandra Elya shifts in the back seat, still awake, still holding the bag.
- "But if they don't —"
- "If they don't, she'll still have the dress," Eli says. "And she'll still be Aleksandra Elya. And we'll still be us. The school doesn't get to decide that."
- Salena Sarai looks at him — really looks at him — and feels the same thing she felt the night she told him about the visit: *this is why I married him.* Not because he has answers. Because he has the right kind of simple. The kind that cuts through the worry and lands on what actually matters.
- She turns back to the road. The dress is in the bag. The bag is in the car. The child is in the back seat. The future is unknown. And for now, that's enough.

### The Ride Home

- The car ride back is quieter than the ride there. Not because anything is wrong — because something is *done.* The shopping trip is complete. The dress is in the bag. The bag is in the car. The girls are tired in the good way — the kind of tired that comes from doing something that matters, even if the mattering is small and ordinary and invisible to anyone outside the family.
- Xana Dylan falls asleep first. Her head on the seat, her bag on her lap, her mouth slightly open — the same posture she has always had when she's exhausted from joy.
- Aleksandra Elya stays awake. She looks at her bag. She opens it and touches the dress — not taking it out, just feeling the fabric, checking that it's still there, still hers.
- "Are you happy?" Salena Sarai asks. She doesn't ask it as a question. She asks it as a gift — the way she has always given her daughter the chance to name her own feelings.
- Aleksandra Elya thinks about it. She is the kind of child who thinks before she speaks — not because she's careful, but because she's honest, and honesty takes a moment to find the right words.
- "Yes," she says. "I'm happy."
- "Good," Salena Sarai says. And she means it — not just the happiness, but the *yes,* the clarity, the child who knows what she feels and can name it.
- Eli catches Salena Sarai's eye in the rearview mirror. He doesn't say anything. He doesn't need to. His hand reaches over and finds hers on the console. She squeezes it. The car drives on through Perth summer — the blue sky, the warm air, the girls in the back seat, the dress in the bag, the ordinary miracle of a family that went shopping and came home with exactly what they needed.

## Tone & Style Notes

- The scene should feel warm, quiet, and grounded. This is not a crisis moment — it's a milestone, but a gentle one. The emotional weight comes from the ordinary: a child choosing her own clothes, a family supporting her without fanfare, a sales assistant who treats them like any other family in a store.
- Xana Dylan's role is crucial. She is not jealous, not confused, not left out. She is her sister's twin — the child who shared her wardrobe without question, who put the headband on her brother's head on Christmas morning, who understands that sharing is what twins do. In this scene, she gets to choose her own things too, and she is happy for her sister. The twin bond holds — it doesn't break, it doesn't strain, it just expands to include two separate wardrobes.
- Eli is the steady centre. He doesn't overthink things. He says "looks like you found it" and "if you like it, I like it" and "there she is." His simplicity is his strength — he is the father who loves his daughters and shows it in the most direct way possible: by being present, by being kind, by being unbothered by the thing that isn't actually a thing.
- Salena Sarai is the perceptive one, but she knows when to hold back. She watches. She witnesses. She says "it's beautiful" and "I see you" without turning the moment into a lesson or a speech. She lets Aleksandra Elya find the words herself.
- The lavender dress is the scene's central image — not because it's special, but because it's *hers.* The dress that Aleksandra Elya chose, not borrowed, not shared, not borrowed from her sister. The first thing that belongs to her alone. The colour should feel soft, quiet, settling — the opposite of the bright pink headband on Christmas morning, but carrying the same weight.
- The sales assistant should feel real — not a plot device, not a symbol, just a Perth woman doing her job on a Saturday afternoon. Her casual acceptance ("you two look alike! are you twins?") is the point: the world is already kinder than we think it is.
- The fitting room moment — the door closing, the family waiting, the door opening, the child stepping out — should carry the same emotional architecture as the Christmas morning headband and the airport baby blue dress. The pattern holds: a child tries something on, the family sees her, the world does not end, the world begins.

## What This Scene Establishes

1. **The name moment.** Aleksandra Elya says her own name to a stranger in a store — sweetly, clearly, without prompting. The first time she speaks it to the outside world. Salena Sarai and Eli are caught off guard, kneel down, and kiss both daughters' heads.
2. **Aleksandra Elya's first independent purchase.** The lavender dress — chosen by her, for her, in a store, with her own bag. The first time she leaves a store with something that is hers alone, not shared with Xana Dylan.
3. **The twin bond holds.** Xana Dylan is not jealous or left out. She gets her own things too. The twins share everything — but they also get to be separate. The wardrobe split is not a rupture; it's a natural next step.
4. **The world is ordinary.** The sales assistant treats them like any other family. No drama, no confrontation, no moment of public judgment. Just a Saturday in Perth. The normalcy is the point — Aleksandra Elya gets to be a girl in a girl's clothing store, and no one blinks.
5. **Eli's quiet strength.** He is the father who says "there she is" and means it. He doesn't overthink. He doesn't panic. He holds the bags and holds the hands and holds the family together with the simplest kind of love.
6. **Salena Sarai's witnessing.** She watches. She sees the settled posture. She says "it's beautiful" and lets the moment breathe. She is the perceptive one, but she knows that perception is not the same as intervention — sometimes the most loving thing a mother can do is stand back and let her child find her own dress.
7. **The St Hilda's uncertainty.** The application is in, the decision is pending. The parents carry this weight quietly while the children don't have to.
8. **The pattern continues.** Headband → nightie → baby blue dress at the airport → lavender dress in the store. Each one is a step forward, a settling, a moment where the body says yes. The pattern is not dramatic — it's quiet, cumulative, the way identity forms in a child who is allowed to try things on and see what fits.
9. **The ride home.** The quiet car, the sleeping twin, the awake child holding her bag. The family driving through Perth summer with everything they need. The scene ends not with a climax but with a completion — the shopping trip is done, the dress is bought, the girl is happy, and the world keeps turning.
