# Salena Sarai Tells Eli About Her Parents' Visit — Scene Outline

## Placement

**When:** Before Patti Anne and Aleksander's trip. Salena Sarai has received word that her parents are making the rounds — Prague first (Gamaliel Galvin and Klára), then Australia (Salena Sarai and Eli), then Saudi Arabia (Palmer Peter). She tells her husband.

**Where:** Salena Sarai and Eli's home in Australia.

---

## Characters

- **Salena Sarai Key**: Patti Anne and Aleksander's daughter. Palmer Peter's twin sister. Ballet-trained at WAAPA in Perth. The perceptive one — the girl who said "Klára loves Gamy" at age three. Now married to Eli and living in Australia. She is the farthest-flung Key by distance, and her parents are coming to her last — after Prague, then Australia. She knows what that itinerary means. She knows what her parents will have seen and felt before they reach her door. Mother of twins — Aleksandra Elya and Xana Dylan — a twin who had twins of her own.
- **Eli**: Salena Sarai's husband. Australian. The man she met and married on the far side of the world from everything she came from. He knows the Key family — he married into it — but knowing the Keys from across an ocean and having Patti Anne and Aleksander in your house are two different things.
- **Aleksandra Elya**: Twin with Xana Dylan. Named for her maternal grandfather and her father — feminized. Attends St Hilda's Anglican School for Girls alongside Xana Dylan. Wears Xana Dylan's clothes — exploring gender identity.
- **Xana Dylan**: Daughter. Twin with Aleksandra Elya. Attends St Hilda's Anglican School for Girls (Junior Kindergarten through Year 12, Mosman Park, Perth).

---

## Scene Outline

### The Conversation Begins

- Evening. Their home. The twins — Aleksandra Elya and Xana Dylan — are asleep, or close to it. The quiet, domestic rhythm of a couple at the end of a day. Salena Sarai has been sitting with the news for a few hours — turning it over, feeling the weight of it, the way she has always felt the weight of family things before she names them out loud.
- She finds Eli — in the kitchen, or on the couch, or wherever he lands at the end of the day — and says it simply:
- "My parents are coming to visit."
- Eli looks up. He knows Patti Anne and Aleksander. He has met them. He has sat with them, eaten with them, navigated the particular experience of being the Australian son-in-law of a Catholic Canadian mother and a quiet, watchful father. He knows what a visit means.
- "When?"
- Salena Sarai tells him the timeline. And then she tells him the rest.

### The Itinerary — Saudi Arabia, Then Prague, Then Australia

- Salena Sarai explains: her parents are not just coming to Australia. They are making the full trip. All three children. All three continents.
- **First: Saudi Arabia.** Palmer Peter. The compound. The women. The children. The living room at the center of nine houses. A Catholic mother walking into her Muslim convert son's plural household.

- **Then: Australia.** Salena Sarai and Eli. The middle stop.
- **Then: Prague.** Gamaliel Galvin and Klára. The ballet academy. The grandchildren. The eldest son, the foster daughter who became family, the school they built.

- Salena Sarai lays it out for Eli not because he needs the geography — he knows where her brothers live — but because the order matters. By the time her parents reach their door, they will have already been through Saudi Arabia and Prague. They will arrive carrying whatever those visits gave them — the joy, the complexity, the exhaustion, the love, the weight.
- "They're coming to us last, Eli. They always come to us last. Because we're the farthest. And because we're the easiest."
- There is no bitterness in it. She says it as a fact. Of her parents' three children, she is the one whose life requires the least adjustment to understand. No plural marriage. No compound. No conversion. Just a dancer who married an Australian and lives on the other side of the world. The gentlest test. The easiest stop.

### Eli's Response

- Eli listens the way Eli listens — fully, without rushing to fill the space. He is Australian. He does not panic about in-laws the way a different man might. But he is not naive either. He has seen what the Key family is — the size of it, the weight of it, the fierce love that runs through every branch.
- "How long are they staying?"
- Salena Sarai tells him.
- "And how are you feeling about it?"
- This is why she married him. Not the question "What do we need to do?" Not "Should we clean the house?" The question underneath: *How are you?*
- Salena Sarai sits with it for a moment. Then she answers honestly — the way she has always answered honestly, because she is the perceptive one, the one who reads rooms, and the first room she learned to read was her own.

### Salena Sarai's Feelings — The Twin Who Reads Everything

- She is happy. She is always happy when her parents come. Patti Anne walking through the door — holding her face, looking at her, the full-body love of a mother who crossed the planet — is one of the best things in Salena Sarai's life. Aleksander behind her, steady, quiet, seeing everything. She loves them. She misses them. She wants them here.
- But.
- She knows what they will have been through before they reach her. Saudi Arabia and Prague. The plural household and the ballet academy.
- Her parents will arrive at her door carrying all of that. And she — the easy one, the uncomplicated one, the farthest but the simplest — will be the place they land.
- "I'm the rest stop, Eli. I'm where they come to breathe."
- She does not say this with resentment. She says it with the particular clarity of a woman who has always seen the shape of things. She is the rest stop. Patti Anne can sit in her kitchen and not have to adjust to anything — just her daughter, her son-in-law, Australia, and the ordinary rhythms of a life that looks, from the outside, like the one Patti Anne would have chosen for her.
- "And that's fine. That's good. I want to be that for them. I just — I want you to know that when they get here, they'll be tired. Not jet-lag tired. The other kind."

### Eli and Salena Sarai — The Practical

- Eli takes it in. He understands. He may not fully grasp the weight of the Saudi Arabia and Prague stops — the grandchildren Patti Anne worries about, the dog thread she might have caught a glimpse of, or the general weight of the academy — but he understands Salena Sarai. And she is telling him: be gentle with them when they arrive.
- They begin the easy, domestic work of preparing. Which room for her parents. What to cook. Whether to plan anything or leave the days open so Patti Anne and Aleksander can simply exist in their daughter's house without an itinerary.
- Salena Sarai says: "Leave it open. They won't want to do things. They'll want to sit."
- Eli: "I can do sitting."
- She laughs. The first real laugh of the conversation. The tension — not heavy, but present — loosens.

### Aleksandra Elya — The Thing They Haven't Said Out Loud Yet

- Before the twin thread, before Palmer Peter, there is something closer. Something in this house. Something that has been happening for weeks — or months — quietly, persistently, without a name yet.
- Salena Sarai looks at Eli. The visit is coming. Her parents are coming. And there is something they will see when they get here.
- "Eli. We need to talk about Junior."
- Aleksandra Elya has been wearing Xana Dylan's clothes. Not once. Not as a game. Not as a dare. Again and again. He reaches for her wardrobe — her dresses, her shirts, her things — and something in him settles when he puts them on. He feels funny in his own clothes. He has said so, in the fragmented, half-formed language of a young child who does not have the words for what he is feeling: "These feel funny." And then he puts on Xana Dylan's things and the "funny" stops.
- Xana Dylan does not mind. She shares without question — the way twins share everything. She has not asked why. To her, it is simply what her twin does.
- Salena Sarai has been watching. She is the perceptive one — the girl who said "Klára loves Gamy" at three, the woman who reads rooms before anyone tells her what's in them. She has seen this. She knows what she is seeing. She may not have the full picture yet — her child is young, still forming, still finding the edges of something — but she knows this is not a phase. She knows this is not a game.
- She also knows who this echoes. **Ashley Erin.** Her aunt. Patti Anne's youngest daughter. Assigned male at birth — original name Mikkel James Junior. A transgender girl. The family has been here before. The family loved Ashley Erin through it — Mike took her to Pride Day baseball games; the whole family held her. But that was the generation above. This is Salena Sarai's child. This is her kitchen. This is her turn.
- "When Mum and Dad get here, Junior is going to be wearing Xana's clothes. That's what he does. That's what he's been doing. And I'm not going to stop him, and I'm not going to hide it, and I'm not going to explain it away."
- Eli listens. He has been living with this too — watching his son, watching his daughter's wardrobe slowly become shared, watching the "funny" arrive and leave and arrive again. Eli is not a man who panics. He is Australian. He is steady. But this is his child, and the steadiness has a tremor underneath it — not of rejection, but of the particular fear of a parent who wants to get it right and does not know the shape of what "right" looks like yet.
- "I know," he says. Simply. Because he does.
- "Mum will see it. She'll see Junior in Xana's clothes and she'll think about Ashley Erin and she'll know exactly what she's looking at. Because Mum sees everything."
- A pause.
- "And she'll love him. The way she loved Ashley Erin. The way she loves everyone. But I want you to be ready, Eli. Because she's going to look at me the way she looked at Ashley Erin's parents — with that face that says *I've been here, I know this road, let me help you carry it.* And I don't know if I'm ready for that yet. Because I'm still figuring out what I'm carrying."
- Eli reaches for her hand. He holds it. He does not offer a solution — because there isn't one. There is only a child who feels funny in his own clothes and settles in his sister's, and two parents who love him and are learning the shape of what that love looks like now.
- "We'll figure it out. Together. And your parents will help."
- Salena Sarai squeezes his hand. "I know. That's the part that scares me. That they'll be better at this than I am. Because they've done it before."

### The Twin Thread

- Before they move on, Salena Sarai adds one more thing. Quietly. The thing she has been holding since she heard about the itinerary.
- "Palmer Peter called me. He told his women they're coming. He warned them to be on their best behavior."
- Eli waits.
- "He's nervous, Eli. My brother doesn't get nervous. But he's nervous about this one." She pauses. "Three of them are pregnant."
- The sentence lands. Eli does not react the way a different man would. He does not say anything about three pregnancies. He says:
- "Is he okay?"
- Salena Sarai — the twin who reads her brother from across an ocean, the girl who said "Klára loves Gamy" before she could spell, the woman whose entire gift is seeing the current between people — says:
- "He's happy. He's really happy. He just wants Mum to be happy too. And he knows that's a lot to ask when she walks in and counts three bellies."
- A beat.
- "I told him they'd be fine. That Mum loves him. That she'll love all of them."
- Eli: "Do you believe that?"
- Salena Sarai: "I believe Mum will try. And for Mum, trying and succeeding are the same thing."

### Closing

- The conversation settles. The evening continues. Eli goes back to whatever he was doing. Salena Sarai stays where she is for a moment — thinking about the itinerary. Prague, Australia, Saudi Arabia. Three children on three continents. One set of parents making the full circle.
- She picks up her phone and texts Palmer Peter. Just two words:
- **"Love you."**
- He responds within a minute. The same two words back.
- The twins — one in Saudi Arabia, one in Australia, separated by oceans and time zones and lives that look nothing alike — hold the thread the way they always have. Briefly. Completely. Without needing to say more.
- Salena Sarai sets the phone down. She looks around her home — the Australian home, the simple home, the home that will be the last stop, the rest stop, the place where Patti Anne and Aleksander come to breathe. Down the hall, Aleksandra Elya and Xana Dylan are sleeping. Her twins. Named for the grandfather who is coming. A twin who made twins. The pattern continuing on the other side of the world.
- She is ready.

## Tone & Style Notes

- The scene is quiet, warm, and intimate. This is a marriage — two people at the end of a day, talking about family. The emotional weight comes from Salena Sarai's perception, not from conflict.
- Eli should feel like a real person — grounded, present, Australian in his ease. He is not a Key. He married into the Keys. His gift is simplicity: "Is he okay?" instead of judgment. "I can do sitting" instead of plans.
- Salena Sarai's role as "the rest stop" is not resentment — it is self-knowledge. She has always been the perceptive one. She knows exactly where she sits in the family's geography and she accepts it with grace and a little humor.
- The twin thread — the text to Palmer Peter — should be the emotional anchor of the scene. Two words. One minute. The whole bond.
- The itinerary (Prague → Australia → Saudi Arabia) mirrors the family's spread across the globe and establishes the emotional arc of Patti Anne and Aleksander's trip: complex → more complex → rest.

## What This Scene Establishes

1. **Eli and the twins.** Salena Sarai's Australian husband. Grounded, gentle, perceptive in his own understated way. A real partner. Their children: Aleksandra Elya and Xana Dylan — twins. A twin who made twins. Aleksandra Elya named for her maternal grandfather and father — feminized.
2. **The full trip itinerary.** Prague (Gamaliel & Klára) → Australia (Salena Sarai) → Saudi Arabia (Palmer Peter). Three children, three continents, one trip.
3. **Salena Sarai as the rest stop.** She is the middle stop but usually the easiest stop — not because she is less loved, but because her life is the one that requires the least translation. She accepts this with clarity and grace.
4. **Aleksandra Elya.** The child who keeps wearing Xana Dylan's clothes. The beginning of a gender identity exploration that echoes Ashley Erin one generation up. Salena Sarai sees it, names it to Eli, and refuses to hide it when her parents visit. Her fear: not that her parents won't accept it, but that they'll be better at this than she is — because they've done it before.
5. **The twin bond across distance.** Palmer Peter called her. He told her about the warning, the pregnancies, his nerves. She told him it would be fine. Two words texted. Two words back. The thread holds.
6. **Patti Anne's love as a constant.** Even from the other side of the world, through the lens of her daughter's perception, Patti Anne's love is the fixed point: "For Mum, trying and succeeding are the same thing."
7. **The emotional arc of the parents' trip.** By the time Patti Anne and Aleksander reach Australia, they will be carrying Saudi Arabia and Prague with them. Salena Sarai knows this and prepares Eli for it.
