# Christmas in Perth — Aleksander Eli Junior Is Miserable — Scene Outline

## Placement

**When:** Christmas. The visit before the airport scene — the last time Patti Anne and Aleksander saw their grandchildren. This is the Christmas where everything is wrong and no one can name it yet.

**Where:** Salena Sarai and Eli's home in Perth, Western Australia. An Australian summer Christmas — heat, sunshine, no snow. The opposite of every Key family Christmas before it.

**Context:** Patti Anne and Aleksander have come to Perth for Christmas. This is a happy visit — they are here to see their daughter, their son-in-law, and their twin grandchildren. Xana Dylan is fine. Aleksander Eli Junior is not. He is sad. He is upset. He is miserable in a way that no one in the house can fix, because no one in the house understands what is wrong — including Junior himself. All he knows is that being a boy feels wrong. Everything about it feels wrong. And Christmas — a holiday built on joy and togetherness — is the worst possible time to feel wrong in your own body, because everyone is watching, everyone is smiling, and you cannot make yourself smile back.

---

## Characters

- **Aleksander Eli Junior**: The child who is wrong. He does not have the words for it yet — he is young, still forming, still trying to understand the shape of the feeling — but it is there, constant and heavy: being a boy is wrong. His name is wrong. His clothes are wrong. The way people look at him and say "he" and "him" and "good boy" is wrong. At Christmas, when the house is full of warmth and presents and family, the wrongness does not go away. It gets louder. Every gendered moment — the boy's pyjamas, the "handsome little man" from a relative, the separation of "the girls" and "the boys" in conversation — is a small cut. By Christmas Day, he is covered in them.
- **Xana Dylan**: His twin sister. She knows something is wrong with her brother. She does not know what. She tries — she sits near him, she offers him her toys, she holds his hand. She is the one person in the house whose presence does not make it worse, because Xana Dylan does not ask him to be anything. She just sits with him.
- **Salena Sarai Key**: Mother. The perceptive one. She can feel the wrongness coming off her child like heat from a road. She has been watching it build for weeks — months — the increasing withdrawal, the sadness that has no trigger, the way Junior flinches when someone calls him "buddy" or "mate" or "little man." She does not have the name for it yet. She is circling it — the way she circles everything, reading the room before she reads the words. But at Christmas, with her parents here, she cannot circle anymore. She has to watch her child be miserable in front of the people she loves most, and she cannot explain why.
- **Eli**: Father. He sees it too. He is not as perceptive as Salena Sarai — not because he is less loving, but because he does not have her gift for reading the current under the surface. What he sees is simpler and no less painful: his child is unhappy. At Christmas. And nothing Eli does — the presents, the food, the swimming pool, the backyard cricket — makes it better.
- **Patti Anne Key**: Grandmother. She has crossed the world to be here. She has carried presents. She has come to hold her grandchildren and eat her daughter's food and sit in the Australian sun and be Nana. Instead, she is watching one of her grandchildren disappear into himself. She notices everything — the flinching, the rigidity, the way Junior pulls away from hugs that should feel good — and she stores it. She does not name it. Not yet. But somewhere deep in the back of her mind, in the place where she has kept every difficult truth this family has ever handed her, something stirs. Something that has Ashley Erin's shape.
- **Aleksander**: Grandfather. He tries the hardest. He is the one Junior was named for — the boy carries his name. Aleksander reaches for his grandson the way he has always reached for his grandchildren: with open arms, with warmth, with the assumption that holding a child fixes things. It does not fix this. When Aleksander picks Junior up, the boy goes rigid. Not fighting — not squirming or crying or pushing away. Just rigid. As if being held is something he has to endure rather than something he wants. Aleksander feels it in his arms — the stiffness, the wrongness — and he does not understand it. It hurts him in a way he cannot articulate. His grandchild does not want to be held by him. Or does not want to be held at all. Or does not want to be held as this. He doesn't know. He sets the boy down gently and watches him walk away, and something in Aleksander's chest tightens.

## Scene Outline

### Christmas Eve — The Wrongness Arrives

- The house is warm. Perth in December is blazing — the sun doesn't set until late, the air smells like eucalyptus and barbecue, and Christmas here is barefoot and sunlit. Salena Sarai has decorated the house. Eli has put up lights. Xana Dylan is vibrating with excitement. The grandparents have arrived.
- Aleksander Eli Junior is quiet. He has been quiet for days — the lead-up to Christmas making it worse, not better. The anticipation that lights up his sister has the opposite effect on him. More family means more eyes. More eyes means more people seeing him as a boy. More presents wrapped in blue. More "what a handsome grandson." More of everything that feels like wearing someone else's skin.
- Christmas Eve dinner. The family gathers. Salena Sarai and Eli have prepared lobster — a proper Australian summer Christmas dinner, nothing like the turkey and stuffing of a Canadian December. The lobsters are laid out on the table, bright red against white platters, with lemon and butter and salads and cold wine. It is beautiful. It is festive. It is the kind of meal that says *we are celebrating.* Eli pours wine for the adults. Xana Dylan chatters about Santa. Junior sits at the table and pushes his food around his plate. He does not touch the lobster.
- Patti Anne watches him from across the table. She does not say anything. She watches the way he holds his fork — not eating, just moving things. She watches his eyes — not sad exactly, but *absent.* As if he has gone somewhere inside himself where the Christmas lights cannot reach.
- She catches Salena Sarai's eye. Salena Sarai looks back. The look between them is brief and loaded: *I know. I don't know what it is. But I know.*
- Aleksander tries. He leans across the table and ruffles Junior's hair. "What's going on, little man? Not hungry?"
- Junior flinches at "little man." It is small — barely visible — but Salena Sarai sees it. Patti Anne sees it. The word lands on the child like a wrong note, and he shrinks.
- "I'm okay," Junior says. He is not okay.

### Christmas Morning — The Presents

- Christmas morning. The Australian sun is already hot at seven a.m. Xana Dylan is up first, tearing into presents with the velocity of a child who has never doubted that Christmas would come. Wrapping paper everywhere. Squealing. Joy.
- Junior opens his presents slowly. Carefully. The way Klára used to open presents — folding the wrapping paper, keeping the ribbons — except Junior is not doing it because he learned not to own what he couldn't carry. He is doing it because he does not want what is inside.
- The presents are for a boy. They are good presents — thoughtful, chosen with love by parents and grandparents who know their grandchild. But they are for a boy. The colours, the toys, the clothes — all of it calibrated to the child they think he is. Every unwrapped gift is another confirmation of the thing that is wrong.
- Junior holds a shirt — a boy's shirt, the kind of thing Eli would wear in miniature — and looks at it. He does not say "I don't like it." He does not throw it. He folds it neatly and sets it aside and reaches for the next present. His face is flat. His hands are steady. He is performing Christmas the way a child performs when the alternative is ruining it for everyone else.
- Xana Dylan, mid-wrapping-paper-explosion, notices. She stops. She goes to her brother and sits next to him. She does not say anything. She takes one of her own new things — a headband, pink and glittering — and puts it on his head without a word.
- Junior does not take it off. For a moment — one small moment — something in his face eases. The headband sits on his head and the wrongness quiets, just slightly, just enough for him to almost smile.
- Then Eli, not understanding, laughs gently and says, "That's your sister's, mate." He reaches to take it off.
- Junior's hand flies up and holds the headband on his head. His eyes fill with tears. He does not explain. He cannot explain. He holds the headband with both hands and the tears come and he does not know why a headband is the hill he is willing to die on, except that it is the only thing in this room that feels right.
- The room goes quiet. Xana Dylan looks at her parents. Eli's hand hovers. Salena Sarai says, softly: "Leave it, Eli."
- Eli leaves it. Junior sits on the floor in a pile of wrapping paper, wearing a pink glittering headband, crying silently on Christmas morning.
- Patti Anne, sitting in the armchair with her tea, watches. Her heart is breaking in a way she recognizes. She has seen this before. She has sat in a room and watched a child be wrong in their own body and not have the words. She has held Ashley Erin through this — years of it, the slow unfolding, the naming, the becoming. She knows the shape. She does not say it out loud. It is not hers to say. But she knows.

### Christmas Day — The Rigidity

- The rest of Christmas Day is a study in a child trying to survive joy. The house is full of laughter and food and family. Xana Dylan runs in and out of the sprinkler in the backyard. Eli barbecues. Salena Sarai and Patti Anne sit in the shade and talk about nothing and everything — except the thing they are both circling.
- Junior stays inside. He does not want to swim. He does not want to play cricket. He does not want to take off his shirt and run through the sprinkler because taking off his shirt means being seen, and being seen means being seen as a boy, and being seen as a boy is the thing that is eating him alive.
- Aleksander goes inside to find him. He finds Junior sitting on the floor of his bedroom, surrounded by his new Christmas presents — untouched, still in their boxes — wearing the pink headband Xana Dylan gave him that morning.
- Aleksander sits on the floor next to him. He does not ask what's wrong. He has asked three times today and gotten "I'm okay" each time. Instead he just sits. Two Aleksanders on a bedroom floor on Christmas Day in Perth.
- After a while, Aleksander opens his arms. An offer. The same open arms that will one day scoop up two girls in baby blue dresses at an airport. But today, when Junior looks at the arms and then climbs into them, the small body goes rigid. Stiff. Enduring.
- Aleksander holds him anyway. He holds the rigid little body against his chest and he does not let go and he does not understand. He feels the wrongness — it radiates through the child's entire frame, a tension that has no source and no release — and he thinks: *something is wrong with my grandchild and I cannot fix it.*
- He kisses the top of Junior's head. The pink headband is in the way. He kisses it anyway — headband and all — and says quietly: "I love you. Whatever it is. I love you."
- Junior does not respond. He sits rigid in his grandfather's arms and stares at the wall and endures being loved as the wrong thing.

### Boxing Day — Patti Anne and Salena Sarai

- The day after Christmas. The house is quieter. Eli has taken both twins to the park — Xana Dylan running ahead, Junior trailing behind, headband still on. Aleksander has gone with them, trying again, always trying.
- Patti Anne and Salena Sarai are alone in the kitchen. The coffee is hot. The morning light comes through the window at the wrong angle — Australian light, too bright, too early, the opposite of Canadian December.
- Salena Sarai does not look at her mother. She looks at her coffee. Her voice is quiet.
- "Something is wrong with Junior, Mum."
- Patti Anne waits. She does not fill the silence. She lets her daughter find it.
- "He's been like this for months. Not just this week. It's getting worse. He doesn't want to play with the other boys. He doesn't want to wear his clothes. He cried this morning because Eli tried to take Xana's headband off his head." Her voice cracks. "He's five years old and he's miserable and I don't know why."
- Patti Anne sips her coffee. She sets it down. She looks at her daughter — the perceptive one, the reader of rooms, the girl who said "Klára loves Gamy" at three — and she sees a mother who is circling the truth and cannot land on it because landing on it makes it real.
- Patti Anne does not say: *It's because he's not a boy.* She does not say: *This is Ashley Erin.* She does not diagnose her grandchild across a kitchen table on Boxing Day.
- She says: "You're watching him. You're paying attention. That's the most important thing right now."
- Salena Sarai looks up. Her eyes are red. "Do you think something is really wrong?"
- Patti Anne reaches across the table and takes her daughter's hand. She holds it the way she has held every hand in this family — firmly, warmly, without letting go.
- "I think something is really *right,* and it's trying to come out. And when it does, you'll know. And you'll love him through it. The same way I loved —"
- She stops. She does not say Ashley Erin's name. She does not finish the sentence. She squeezes Salena Sarai's hand instead.
- Salena Sarai stares at her mother. The perceptive one. The reader of rooms. She heard the name her mother did not say. And in this moment — in the bright, wrong-angled light of an Australian Boxing Day morning — the shape of it lands.
- She does not say it out loud. Not yet. But she knows now. She knew before Patti Anne almost said it. She has known for months — circling and circling, reading the room her child lives in, seeing the current underneath. She just needed her mother to almost say the thing so she could let herself believe it.
- Patti Anne watches her daughter's face change. She sees the landing happen — the moment Salena Sarai stops circling and starts seeing. She does not push. She does not explain. She holds her daughter's hand and drinks her coffee and lets the truth settle the way truth settles in this family: slowly, with love holding the edges.

### The Visit Ends — The Wrongness Stays

- The rest of the visit is careful and gentle and shadowed. Junior wears the pink headband every day. No one takes it off again. Xana Dylan gives him a pair of her socks — purple, with stars — and he wears them under his shoes where no one can see, except Xana Dylan knows, and he knows she knows, and the secret is the first thing that has made him smile all week.
- Aleksander tries every day to hold him. Some days the rigidity is less. Most days it is the same.

### The Goodbye — New Year's Day Morning

- New Year's Day morning. Patti Anne and Aleksander are leaving. The bags are at the door. The taxi is coming. The Australian sun is already bright and wrong — a summer New Year, the opposite of everything Canadian.
- At the front door, Aleksander kneels. Junior stands in front of him — the pink headband, the purple star socks hidden under his shoes, the flat face that has smiled exactly twice in two weeks. Aleksander opens his arms. Junior climbs in. The small body goes rigid — the same rigidity, the same enduring — but at the very end, just before Aleksander lets go, Junior leans in. Just slightly. Just for a moment. His small body leans into his grandfather's chest instead of going stiff, and Aleksander feels it — the fraction of an inch between enduring and wanting — and his breath catches.
- Patti Anne kneels next to Aleksander. She looks at Junior — the pink headband, the hidden socks, the child who cannot be reached — and she says: "Nana loves you. Nana will always love you."
- Junior nods. He does not say it back. But he lets her kiss his cheek, and he does not flinch.
- They leave. The taxi pulls away. The house is quiet.
- At the airport, walking to the gate, Patti Anne takes Aleksander's hand. They do not speak for a long time. Then Aleksander says, quietly: "Something is wrong with that boy."
- Patti Anne squeezes his hand. "Something is right," she says. "And it's coming."
- They fly home to Canada carrying the weight of it — a grandchild who could not be held, a daughter who is circling the truth, and the unfinished sentence Patti Anne did not say in the kitchen on Boxing Day.

### New Year's Day — The Children's Bedroom (After the Grandparents Leave)

- The house is different with Patti Anne and Aleksander gone. Quieter. Emptier. The weight of performing for two weeks — of being watched, of being the wrong thing in front of people who love you — lifts slightly. But the wrongness does not lift. The wrongness stays.
- Eli is in the kitchen, cleaning up from the morning. Xana Dylan is in the backyard.
- Salena Sarai goes to the children's bedroom. Junior is there — sitting on the floor between the two small beds, still wearing the pink headband, the purple star socks hidden under his shoes. He is not playing. He is not reading. He is sitting in the space between his bed and his sister's bed, in the room where half the things are his and half are Xana Dylan's, and he is on Xana Dylan's side.
- Salena Sarai sits down on the floor next to him. She does not sit on the bed. She sits on the carpet, at his level, close enough to touch but not touching. She has been circling this moment for months — reading the room her child lives in, watching the wrongness build, listening to Patti Anne almost say a name on Boxing Day. She cannot circle anymore. It is New Year's Day. Her parents have just left. A new year. And her child is sitting on the floor of his bedroom looking like the world is ending.
- She asks him. Simply. Gently. The way Patti Anne would ask — the way Patti Anne taught her to ask, without even knowing she was teaching:
- "Baby, is there anything wrong?"
- Junior does not answer immediately. He looks at the floor. His hands are in his lap. The pink headband glitters. The silence stretches — five seconds, ten, fifteen — and Salena Sarai does not fill it. She waits. She has always been the perceptive one. But perception is not just seeing. It is knowing when to be quiet.
- Then Junior starts crying.
- Not the silent tears of Christmas morning. Not the flat, enduring sadness of the last two weeks. This is different. This is a dam breaking. The tears come with sound — the jagged, hiccupping sobs of a young child who has been holding something too big for too long and cannot hold it anymore.
- Salena Sarai does not move. She does not reach for him. She lets him cry. She stays on the floor next to him and she waits for the words, because she knows — the way she has always known — that the words are coming. They are behind the tears. They have been behind everything.
- Junior speaks through the sobs. Broken sentences. Fragments. The language of a child who does not have the vocabulary for what is happening inside:
- "I feel funny."
- A breath. A sob.
- "I feel funny all the time."
- Another breath. His hands press against his chest — against himself, against the body that does not match.
- "I don't want to wear my clothes. I want to wear Xana's clothes."
- The words land in the children's bedroom like stones dropped into still water. Each one sends a ripple. Each one is the truth — a truth that has been sitting inside this child for months, maybe longer, pressing against the walls of a container that was never the right shape.
- "Xana's clothes feel right. My clothes feel funny. Everything feels funny. I don't know why."
- He looks up at Salena Sarai. His face is wet. The pink headband is crooked on his head. His eyes are asking the question he cannot form: *What is wrong with me?*
- Salena Sarai looks at her child. The perceptive one. The reader of rooms. The woman who said "Klára loves Gamy" at three years old because she could see what no one else was saying. She has been reading this room for months. She read it on Boxing Day when her mother almost said a name. She has known — circling, circling, always circling — and now her child has said it out loud, and the circling stops, and the truth is here, on the floor of a children's bedroom on New Year's Day, in a pair of purple star socks and a pink headband.
- The grandparents are gone. The house is empty. It is just Salena Sarai and her child on the floor. And maybe that is why it happened now — not with the grandparents there, not with the audience, not with the extra eyes and the extra love and the extra weight. The grandparents left, and the pressure lifted just enough for the truth to come out. Junior could not say it with Patti Anne and Aleksander watching. He could only say it to his mother, alone, on the floor, after everyone else had gone.
- She reaches for her child. She pulls Junior into her arms — gently, slowly, letting him come to her rather than scooping him up. He does not go rigid. Not this time. He folds into her — small, shaking, sobbing — and she holds him the way she has always held him, except now she is holding the truth too.
- "There is nothing wrong with you," she says. Her voice is steady. Her eyes are not — they are full, overflowing — but her voice is steady because her child needs it to be steady right now. "There is nothing wrong with you, baby. Nothing. Do you hear me?"
- Junior nods into her chest. The sobs are slowing. The words are out. The biggest thing he has ever said is out of his body and in the room and his mother is holding him and she did not let go.
- "If you want to wear Xana's clothes, you can wear Xana's clothes. If Xana's clothes feel right, then they are right. You are right. Whatever you feel is right."
- She holds him for a moment longer. Then she pulls back and looks at him — really looks at him. His face is blotchy. The pink headband is crooked. The purple star socks are peeking out from under his shoes. He is still in his boy's clothes — the clothes that feel funny, the clothes that feel wrong, the clothes he has been wearing for the entire visit like a costume he cannot take off.
- Salena Sarai says: "Do you want to change right now?"
- Junior looks at her. His eyes are wide. He nods. Fast. Desperate. The way a child nods when they have been waiting for permission they did not know how to ask for.
- Salena Sarai stands. She goes to Xana Dylan's side of the closet — the side Junior has been sitting next to, the side of the room he has been gravitating toward for months. It is evening. The day is over. She does not reach for a shirt or shorts. She reaches for a nightie.
- Xana Dylan's Hello Kitty nightie. Pink and white, with Hello Kitty's face printed across the front, soft cotton, the kind of nightie a little girl wears to bed and loves so much it gets washed a hundred times and stays soft. It is Xana Dylan's favourite. It smells like her. It is the most girl thing in the room.
- Salena Sarai brings it to Junior. She kneels in front of him on the bedroom floor. "Arms up," she says. The same words she has said a thousand times at bedtime — the same routine, the same gentle instruction. But this time it is different. This time she is undressing her child out of the wrong clothes and dressing her child into the right ones.
- She lifts the boy's shirt over his head. She pulls it off and sets it aside. She unbuttons the boy's shorts and slides them down. She peels off the boy's socks — not the purple star ones, the other ones, the boy ones underneath. Every piece she removes is a layer of wrong coming off. Junior stands on the bedroom floor in his underwear, small and bare, and for a moment the room holds its breath.
- Then Salena Sarai lifts the Hello Kitty nightie over Junior's head. She guides his arms through the sleeves — gently, carefully, the way you dress a child who needs to be handled with tenderness. She pulls it down over his body. The pink and white cotton settles against his skin. Hello Kitty smiles from the front.
- Junior looks down at himself. He looks at the nightie. He touches the fabric with both hands — feeling it, holding it against his body, pressing it flat against his stomach as if checking that it is real. That it is there. That it is on him and not on Xana Dylan and no one is reaching to take it off.
- His face changes. Not a smile — not yet. Something quieter. Something deeper. The wrongness — the wrongness that has been on him like a second skin for months, through Christmas Eve lobster and Christmas morning presents and Christmas Day rigidity and Boxing Day silence and a grandfather's arms he could not relax into — the wrongness eases. Not all at once. Not completely. But the Hello Kitty nightie is on his body and the boy's clothes are on the floor and something in him unclenches for the first time in as long as he can remember.
- Then the smile comes. Small. Real. The kind of smile that starts in the eyes.
- Salena Sarai sees it and her heart breaks and heals in the same moment. Her child is smiling. In a Hello Kitty nightie. On the floor of a bedroom in Perth. On New Year's Day. After the grandparents left. After the worst Christmas of a small life. Her child is smiling because the right clothes are on the right body and the world makes sense for the first time.
- She pulls Junior into her arms again — and this time, in the Hello Kitty nightie, the small body does not go rigid. It melts. Soft. Warm. Settled. The way a child feels when they are finally, finally home in their own skin.
- "There you are," Salena Sarai whispers into her child's hair. "There you are."

### Xana Dylan Meets Her Sister

- The bedroom door is open. It has been open the whole time. And Xana Dylan — who was in the backyard, who was playing, who was doing the things children do when they do not know the world is changing inside their house — appears in the doorway.
- She stops. She looks at her twin. Her twin is wearing her Hello Kitty nightie. Her twin's face is wet from crying. Her twin is in their mother's arms. And her twin is smiling.
- Xana Dylan does not ask why. She does not ask what happened. She does not say "that's mine." She does not hesitate.
- She walks across the bedroom floor and wraps her arms around her twin — around her sister — and hugs her. A real hug. A full-body, small-arms, face-pressed-into-shoulder hug. The kind of hug that says everything a child cannot put into words: *I don't know what just happened but you're my person and I'm here and you look happy and that's all I need.*
- Junior — in the Hello Kitty nightie, in Xana Dylan's arms — starts crying again. But this is different crying. This is not the jagged sobs of a child breaking open. This is the soft, overwhelmed crying of a child who has been seen by the one person whose seeing matters most. Not a parent. Not a grandparent. Her twin. Her sister. The other half of her.
- Xana Dylan holds on. She does not let go. She pats her twin's back — the instinctive, clumsy patting of a child who has watched adults comfort each other and is doing her best version of it — and says, simply:
- "You look pretty."
- Two words. A child's words. The most important words spoken in this house all Christmas — more important than Patti Anne's unfinished sentence on Boxing Day, more important than Aleksander's "I love you, whatever it is," more important than Salena Sarai's "there is nothing wrong with you." Because these words come from the twin. From the sister. From the person who shared a womb and a bedroom and a life and who has just, without knowing it, without understanding the full weight of what she is doing, welcomed her sibling home.
- *You look pretty.*
- Salena Sarai watches her two daughters hold each other on the bedroom floor. Two girls. Twins. One in a Hello Kitty nightie, one in her play clothes. She does not say anything. She does not need to. The room has said it for her.
- She has two daughters now. She has always had two daughters. It just took until New Year's Day for the second one to arrive.

### Eli Comes to the Bedroom

- Eli appears in the doorway. He has heard the crying — the first round and the second — from the kitchen. He has been standing in the hallway, listening, not sure whether to come in, the way fathers sometimes hover at the edges of moments they know belong to the mother first.
- Now he is in the doorway. He sees the scene: Salena Sarai on the floor. The two girls holding each other. The Hello Kitty nightie. The boy's clothes in a pile on the carpet. His child — the child he has watched be miserable for months, the child who would not swim, would not play cricket, would not smile at the lobster dinner — wearing a pink and white nightie and being hugged by her sister.
- Eli's face does the thing it did when Salena Sarai looked at him on Boxing Day — the complicated reshuffling of a father catching up. But it is faster this time. The evidence is right in front of him — not a theory, not a conversation, not a mother's intuition. Two girls on the floor, one in a Hello Kitty nightie, and the one in the nightie is smiling for the first time since Christmas.
- He does not ask what happened. He does not stand in the doorway and process. He does not need Salena Sarai to explain.
- Eli walks into the bedroom. He kneels on the floor next to his daughters. He opens his arms — the same way Aleksander opened his arms on Christmas Day, except this time there will be no rigidity — and he says:
- "Come here, sweetheart."
- The word lands. *Sweetheart.* Not mate. Not buddy. Not little man. Sweetheart. Eli has never called this child sweetheart before. He did not plan it. It came out — the way the right word comes out when a father finally sees what he is looking at.
- Junior looks up at her father. Her face crumbles — not with sadness but with the overwhelming relief of being seen by the last person in the room. Mum saw. Sister saw. And now Dad sees.
- She climbs into Eli's arms. The Hello Kitty nightie bunches against his chest. She wraps her small arms around his neck and presses her face into his shoulder and holds on — the way she held on to the headband on Christmas morning, except this time she is holding on to her father, and he is holding on to her, and neither of them is letting go.
- Eli holds his daughter. His eyes are closed. His jaw is tight — the tightness of a man who is feeling too much and does not have the language for it, but whose arms know what to do even when his mouth does not. He holds her and he feels what Aleksander could not feel on Christmas Day — a child who is not rigid, who is not enduring, who is soft and warm and melted into him because the right word was said and the right clothes are on and the world, for this child, has just started making sense.
- "I love you," he says. Simply. The way Eli says everything — without performance, without preamble. "I love you so much."
- Xana Dylan, not wanting to be left out, wraps herself around Eli's other side. Salena Sarai puts her hand on Eli's back. The four of them — a father, a mother, and two daughters — hold each other on the bedroom floor in Perth on New Year's Day, in a pile of boy's clothes and a Hello Kitty nightie, and the wrongness is gone.
- Eli opens his eyes and looks at Salena Sarai over the top of their daughters' heads. His eyes are wet. His face says what his mouth cannot:
- *I didn't see it. I should have seen it. But I see it now.*
- Salena Sarai reaches across and takes his hand. One squeeze. The same language Patti Anne and Aleksander speak — the private shorthand of two people who have weathered everything together. One squeeze: *You're here now. That's enough.*

### That Night — Salena Sarai and Eli's Bedroom

- The girls are asleep. Both of them — in the children's bedroom, in matching nighties now, because Salena Sarai found a second one in Xana Dylan's drawer and dressed them both for bed. Two daughters, two nighties, two small bodies curled toward each other the way twins sleep. The house is quiet.
- Salena Sarai and Eli are in their bedroom. The door is closed. The conversation that has been waiting behind every look, every squeeze, every unfinished sentence since the children's bedroom — the conversation that could not happen in front of the girls — begins.
- It is deep. It is serious. It is the hardest conversation they have ever had — harder than any fight, any disagreement, any decision about money or work or where to live. Because this conversation is about their child, and what they missed, and how long they missed it, and what that missing cost.

**Eli's Shame:**
- Eli speaks first. He is sitting on the edge of the bed, forearms on his knees, looking at the floor. His voice is low — not the steady, easy voice Salena Sarai fell in love with. This voice is rough. Broken at the edges.
- "I should have seen it."
- He says it and the shame fills the room. Not embarrassment — shame. The deep, gut-level shame of a father who watched his child suffer and did not understand why. Who ruffled his hair and called him "mate" and tried to take the headband off on Christmas morning. Who said "that's your sister's" to a child who was drowning.
- "Every time I called him buddy. Every time I said mate. Every time I tried to get him to play cricket or take his shirt off for the sprinkler. I was — I was making it worse. Every single time. And I didn't know."
- His hands are clasped together. His knuckles are white. Eli is not a man who falls apart. He is Australian. He is steady. He fixes things. But he cannot fix the months of wrongness he contributed to without knowing, and the shame of that — the specific shame of a good father who was accidentally cruel — sits on him like a weight he cannot put down.
- "He went rigid in my arms, Sal. On Christmas Day. My kid went rigid when I held him. And I thought — I thought he was just being difficult. I thought it was a phase. I thought —" He stops. He cannot finish the sentence because the end of it is: *I thought my child didn't love me.* And the truth is worse and better at the same time: his child loved him. His child just couldn't be held as the wrong thing.
- "I'm his father. I'm supposed to see. I'm supposed to protect him — her. I'm supposed to —" His voice breaks. Eli puts his face in his hands.

**Salena Sarai's Guilt:**
- Salena Sarai is sitting against the headboard, knees drawn up. She lets Eli speak. She lets the shame come out. She does not rush to comfort him — not yet — because she has her own weight to put down first.
- "You think you should have seen it. I *did* see it."
- The words land in the bedroom like a confession.
- "I saw it, Eli. I've been seeing it for months. The clothes. The way she flinched. The sadness that had no trigger. I'm the perceptive one — that's what everyone says about me. 'Salena sees everything.' I said 'Klára loves Gamy' when I was three. I can read a room before I walk into it. And I read my own child's room — I read it correctly — and I did nothing."
- Her guilt is different from Eli's shame. His is the guilt of not seeing. Hers is the guilt of seeing and not acting. She knew. She circled and circled. She watched her child suffer through an entire Christmas — the lobster dinner, the presents, the rigidity, the silent tears — and she circled. She waited for her mother to almost say a name on Boxing Day before she let herself believe what she already knew.
- "My mother saw it in five days. Five days, Eli. She almost said Ashley Erin's name on Boxing Day. She saw what I've been watching for months and she nearly named it over coffee. And I — I needed my mother to almost say it before I could let myself know it."
- She presses her forehead against her knees. Her voice is muffled.
- "What kind of mother sees her child in pain and waits? What kind of mother needs permission from her own mum to believe what she's looking at?"
- The guilt is the mirror of her gift. The perception that makes her who she is — the girl who reads rooms, who sees currents, who knows — is the same thing that makes the guilt unbearable. She saw. She knew. And she circled instead of landing, because landing meant it was real, and real meant her child was suffering, and suffering meant she had let it happen.

**Together — The Guilt and the Shame Meet:**
- The bedroom is quiet for a long time. Two parents on a bed. One who didn't see. One who saw and didn't act. Both carrying the same thing: the knowledge that their child was miserable and they could have ended it sooner.
- Eli lifts his head from his hands. He looks at Salena Sarai. "You didn't do nothing. You watched. You paid attention. You were there tonight. You asked. You held her. You put the nightie on."
- "After months, Eli. After months."
- "And she told you. She told *you.* Not me. Not your mum. You. She waited until it was just the two of you on the floor and she told you because you're the person she trusts most in the world. That's not nothing. That's not guilt. That's — that's the whole thing."
- Salena Sarai looks at him. The tears come — the tears she has been holding since the children's bedroom, since Xana Dylan said "you look pretty," since Eli said "sweetheart." She has been strong for her child and now, in the privacy of their bedroom, she lets herself not be strong.
- Eli moves to her side of the bed. He puts his arm around her. She leans into him. Two parents holding each other the way their daughters held each other an hour ago — learning the shape of a new truth, finding the ground beneath it.
- "We missed it," Eli says. "We both missed it in our own ways. And now we don't miss it anymore. That's where we are."
- Salena Sarai nods into his shoulder. "That's where we are."

**The School Conversation:**
- The guilt and the shame settle — not gone, not resolved, but set down for now, in the way that parents set down impossible things so they can pick up the next necessary thing. And the next necessary thing is practical.
- "School starts in February," Salena Sarai says. She lifts her head. Her face is streaked but her voice is shifting — from grief to logistics, from guilt to action. This is the part she can do something about. The past is the past. February is coming.
- The twins are enrolled. They were enrolled as a boy and a girl. Aleksander Eli Junior and Xana Dylan. A son and a daughter. That enrolment no longer reflects the truth.
- "We need to talk about school."
- Eli nods. He sits up straighter. This is terrain he can meet her on — decisions, plans, the concrete shape of what comes next.
- "She can't go as Aleksander Eli Junior. Not anymore. Not after tonight."
- "No," Eli agrees. "She can't."
- They talk about options. Co-ed schools where the transition would need to be explained, managed, accommodated. Schools that might understand. Schools that might not. The weight of a child walking into a classroom as someone different than the name on the enrolment form.
- And then Salena Sarai says: "What about an all-girls school?"
- The idea lands in the room and stays. An all-girls school. Both twins together. Both enrolled as girls. No explanation needed — or at least, a different kind of explanation. A school where their daughter would simply be a girl among girls. Where the uniform is the same for everyone. Where no one would look at her and see the gap between what she was enrolled as and what she is.
- "St Hilda's," Salena Sarai says. She has been thinking about this longer than tonight — maybe since Boxing Day, maybe since before that. The perceptive one, circling. "Junior Kindergarten through Year 12. Mosman Park. It's one of the best schools in Perth."
- Eli: "For both of them?"
- "For both of them. Together. Both girls. Both in the same uniform. Both starting fresh."
- Eli is quiet. He is thinking about it — the practicality, the cost, the logistics of changing an enrolment, of changing a name, of walking into a school office and saying *we have two daughters.* It is a big thing. It is an enormous thing. But he has just held his child in a Hello Kitty nightie and felt the difference between rigid and settled, and he knows — the way fathers know when the evidence is in their arms — that the big thing is the right thing.
- "St Hilda's," he says. Testing the name. "All girls."
- "All girls."
- "And her name?"
- Salena Sarai pauses. She shakes her head slowly. "I don't know yet. Not tonight."
- The name is not ready. It is forming — somewhere deep, the way names form, the way the truth about her child formed, circling and circling before it landed. But tonight is not the night for the name. Tonight was the night for the nightie. Tonight was the night for "there you are" and "you look pretty" and "sweetheart." The name will come. It will come the way everything in this family comes — slowly, with love holding the edges.
- "We'll figure it out," Eli says. "Before February."
- "Before February," Salena Sarai agrees.
- "February," Salena Sarai says. "St Hilda's. Two girls. Two sisters. And a name we haven't found yet."
- Eli takes her hand. "Two daughters," he says.
- "Two daughters."
- They sit with it — the school, the February that is coming, the name that is still forming, the life that is about to change — and for the first time since Christmas Eve, since the lobster dinner, since the flat face and the rigid arms and the wrongness that filled every room, the future feels possible. Not easy. Not simple. But possible. And right.
- In the children's bedroom, two girls sleep in matching nighties, curled toward each other, breathing in unison. One of them does not have her name yet. But she has the nightie. She has her sister. She has her parents. And the name will come.
- In the bedroom, Salena Sarai presses her face into Junior's hair and closes her eyes and thinks of her mother's unfinished sentence on Boxing Day. *I think something is really right, and it's trying to come out.* It came out. On New Year's Day. On the floor of a children's bedroom. In a pink headband and purple star socks. After the grandparents left. In the quiet her mother's absence made room for.
- And it is right.

### The Days After — The Wrongness Lifts

- Junior wears Xana Dylan's clothes the next day. Not the headband — a full outfit. Xana Dylan's shirt, Xana Dylan's shorts, Xana Dylan's shoes. Xana Dylan helps pick them out, the two of them standing in front of her side of the closet like it is the most ordinary thing in the world — because to Xana Dylan, it is.
- Junior walks out of the bedroom and into the kitchen wearing his sister's clothes. Eli sees. His face does something complicated — surprise, adjustment, the quick reshuffling of a father who is catching up — and then he says: "Morning. Breakfast?"
- Salena Sarai stands behind Junior with her hand on his shoulder. She looks at Eli. The look says everything: *This is happening. This is right. We'll talk later.*
- Junior smiles. A real smile. The first real smile since the visit began.
- That evening, after the children are asleep, Salena Sarai calls Patti Anne. It is morning in Canada — the time zones work for once. She tells her mother what happened. The children's bedroom. The crying. The words. *I feel funny. I want to wear Xana's clothes.*
- Patti Anne listens. She does not interrupt. When Salena Sarai finishes, there is a long pause — the width of an ocean in it.
- Then Patti Anne says: "You did everything right, baby. Everything."
- Salena Sarai cries. On the phone, in her kitchen in Perth, in the quiet house, she cries the way she has not cried in front of anyone — the tears of a mother who has just held her child through the biggest moment of a small life and needs her own mother to tell her it was enough.
- Patti Anne stays on the line. She does not hang up. She holds the phone and holds her daughter's crying the way she has held everything this family has ever given her — across an ocean, across a time zone, across the distance between a mother in Canada and a daughter in Australia and a grandchild on the floor of a bedroom in Perth.
- "Something is right," Patti Anne says. "And it came out."
- The next time Patti Anne and Aleksander come to Perth, two girls in matching baby blue dresses and ivory tights will run across an arrivals hall into Aleksander's arms. And everything will make sense.

## Tone & Style Notes

- The scene should ache. Not with drama — with the specific, quiet pain of watching a child be wrong in their own body and not having the words. Junior's misery is not performative. It is the deep, wordless wrongness of a person who does not fit the container they have been put in.
- Xana Dylan's gestures — the headband, the socks — are the purest things in the scene. A twin who does not understand what is happening but instinctively gives her sibling the right things. She does not ask why. She just gives.
- Patti Anne's restraint is key. She recognizes what she is seeing — she has been through Ashley Erin — but she does not name it. It is not her place. She almost says it on Boxing Day and stops. She gives Salena Sarai the space to land on it herself. This is the discipline of a mother who has learned that some truths have to be found, not told.
- Aleksander's pain is physical — he feels the rigidity in his arms. His grandchild does not want to be held by him, or does not want to be held as this, and the difference between those two things is the difference between rejection and revelation. He does not know which it is. It breaks his heart either way.
- The pink headband is the scene's central image. A glittering, silly, ordinary thing — and the only object in the house that makes the child's face ease. When Eli reaches to take it off and Junior holds it with both hands, that is the moment the truth becomes visible.
- The final line — "Something is right. And it's coming." — ties directly to the airport scene. Patti Anne knew. She always knows.

## What This Scene Establishes

1. **The misery.** Aleksander Eli Junior is not going through a phase. He is a child trapped in the wrong container — wrong name, wrong clothes, wrong pronouns, wrong everything. Christmas amplifies it because Christmas demands joy, and joy requires being yourself, and he is not allowed to be himself yet.
2. **Xana Dylan's instinct.** The twin gives the headband. The twin gives the socks. The twin does not ask why. This is the foundation for the relationship they will have as Aleksandra Elya and Xana Dylan — two sisters who share everything.
3. **Patti Anne's recognition.** She sees the shape of it — Ashley Erin — and holds the name back. Her restraint is love. Her almost-sentence on Boxing Day is the catalyst that lets Salena Sarai finally land on the truth.
4. **Aleksander's arms.** The rigidity — the child enduring being held as the wrong thing — will contrast directly with the airport scene, where Aleksandra Elya runs into his arms and melts into his chest. The difference is total. The difference is everything.
5. **The headband as turning point.** The first visible moment of the child reaching for what is right — and refusing to let it be taken away.
6. **The unfinished sentence.** Patti Anne does not say Ashley Erin's name. She does not need to. Salena Sarai hears it anyway. The truth lands in the silence between them.
7. **The setup for the airport.** Every detail of this Christmas — the rigidity, the misery, the headband, the star socks, the "something is right and it's coming" — pays off when two girls in baby blue dresses sprint across arrivals.
