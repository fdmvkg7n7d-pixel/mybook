# Patti Anne Starts Rhythmic Gymnastics — Age Five — Scene Outline

## Purpose of This File
This is a planning / scene-outline file for **Patti Anne Key beginning rhythmic gymnastics at age five**.

This scene establishes the origin of Patti Anne’s athletic discipline: the moment she first holds an apparatus, first enters a gymnasium, and first discovers that her oldest-child seriousness can be translated into controlled, graceful motion. It is the beginning of the path that will later lead to her Vancouver competition at age nine and a half.

## Core Premise
At **five years old**, Patti Anne is already the steady eldest sister, managing the household rhythms for her parents, helping with the three-year-old conjoined twins Jessica Isabella and Rachel Darcy, and carrying an adult-like responsibility that Mike and Terri Anne both admire and quietly worry about. 

Mike enrolls her in a **rhythmic gymnastics class** near the Carlisle mansion — not because he wants her to compete, but because he wants her to have something that belongs only to her. Something that is not about managing, helping, or watching younger siblings. Something that is about her own body, her own concentration, her own grace.

The first class is small, local, and low-pressure. Patti Anne walks in holding Mike’s hand, dressed in a simple leotard, and is handed a **ribbon** for the first time. The moment she makes the ribbon trace a circle in the air — watching the color follow her hand, watching the arc obey her wrist — she understands, for the first time, that she can be in control of something beautiful.

## Beat Sheet

### 1. The Decision at Home
- The scene opens in the Carlisle mansion. Patti Anne is five, already moving through the house with a little adult’s efficiency: picking up toys, fetching diapers, signing simple words to the twins because Mike and Terri Anne have begun using signs with them.
- Mike watches her. He has noticed that Patti Anne does not play the way other children play. She organizes. She helps. She asks if the twins need water, if Mommy is tired, if Daddy has finished his coffee.
- Terri Anne agrees that Patti Anne needs something outside the house — something that is not about the twins, not about helping, and not about being the oldest.
- Mike suggests a gymnastics class. Not because he wants her to be an athlete, but because he wants her to use her body for her own joy.
- Patti Anne asks if the twins can come. Mike says no. This is only for her.
- Patti Anne is confused but intrigued. She has never had something that was only for her.

### 2. The Drive to the Studio
- Mike drives Patti Anne to the Burlington Gymnastics Club. It is not elite, not competitive, not pressure-filled. It is a community space with mats, mirrors, and a high ceiling.
- Patti Anne sits in her booster seat, holding her small gym bag. She has never had a class without siblings, without sharing, without being responsible for someone else.
- She asks Mike if she will be good at it. Mike says: *"You don't have to be good. You just have to try."*
- Patti Anne processes this. She is five, and she is already used to being good at things — good at helping, good at being calm, good at watching the twins. The idea of trying without being good is new.

### 3. Entering the Gymnasium
- The club smells of chalk, floor polish, and the faint sweetness of rubber mats. Patti Anne holds Mike’s hand at the door.
- Other children are arriving with their parents. Patti Anne watches them — some are crying, some are running, some are clinging. She stands still, assessing the room the way she assesses the nursery at home.
- Mike kneels to her level. He says: *"You are not the oldest here. You are just Patti Anne. That is enough."*
- The instructor greets them. She is warm, unhurried, and does not treat Patti Anne as precocious or special. She treats her as a five-year-old who has come to learn.

### 4. The First Apparatus — The Ribbon
- The instructor begins the class with a simple warm-up: stretching, jumping, learning to point toes. Patti Anne follows with the concentration she brings to everything. She does not giggle. She does not run around. She watches and copies.
- Then the instructor hands out apparatuses for the first time. To Patti Anne, she gives a **ribbon** — a long, weighted stream of fabric on a stick.
- The instructor demonstrates: a slow circle, a figure eight, a simple arc through the air. The ribbon follows the hand like paint trailing behind a brush.
- Patti Anne takes the ribbon. She grips the stick carefully. She makes her first circle. The ribbon wobbles, tangles, then untangles. She makes a second circle. It is slightly better. By the third, the arc is clean.
- She watches the color trace the air. She sees that the ribbon does what her hand tells it to do. For the first time in her life, she controls something that responds to her exactly — without crying, without needing a diaper, without requiring her to fetch or manage or calm.
- She does not smile. She focuses. But her eyes are wide.

### 5. Mike Watching from the Parent Area
- Mike sits in the parent viewing area, behind a glass partition or on a bench along the wall. He watches Patti Anne with the same steady attention he will later bring to Jessica Isabella’s Ottawa competition, Rachel Darcy’s Mohawk College recital, and Patti Anne’s own Vancouver competition.
- He sees her concentration. He sees the way she does not look around for approval, the way she simply tries again when the ribbon tangles. He sees the oldest-child discipline already present, but now directed inward, toward her own body and her own motion.
- He does not wave. He does not call out. He simply watches. His presence is enough.
- A nearby parent might remark: *"She’s very focused for five."* Mike smiles and says: *"She has a lot of practice."*

### 6. The Class Continues
- The instructor introduces the hoop, the ball, and the clubs in quick succession — not for mastery, but for play. Patti Anne tries each. The hoop rolls away from her; she chases it, catches it, and brings it back with the same efficiency she brings to fetching a toy for the twins. The ball wobbles on her arms; she adjusts. The clubs click together; she tries again.
- She is not naturally gifted in the way a prodigy would be. She is naturally serious. She treats each mistake as information. She corrects. She tries again.
- The instructor notices. She does not praise Patti Anne with empty enthusiasm. She says: *"You have good control. We can build on that."* Patti Anne nods, as if this is a contract she has signed.

### 7. The Car Ride Home
- After class, Patti Anne walks out of the club holding her ribbon. The instructor has let her keep it for practice at home. She grips it in the car like a prize.
- Mike asks what she thought. Patti Anne answers with characteristic seriousness: *"I want to do it again."*
- She says it not as a child asking for a treat, but as a person who has found a tool that fits her hand. Mike hears the difference.
- She then asks, with sudden worry: *"Can I still help with the twins?"* Mike reassures her: she is not being replaced, and she is not being punished. She is being given something extra. The twins will be there when she gets home. But this — the ribbon, the gym, the motion — is hers.

### 8. Arriving Home
- Terri Anne greets them at the Carlisle mansion. The twins are napping or playing in the nursery. Patti Anne walks in with her ribbon and shows her mother what she learned.
- She demonstrates a circle. The ribbon wobbles in the living room, catching a lamp and almost knocking it over. Terri Anne laughs and moves the lamp. She tells Patti Anne they will find a safe place to practice.
- Patti Anne asks if she can go back next week. Terri Anne says yes.
- Mike and Terri Anne exchange a look over Patti Anne’s head. They have given her something she did not know she needed: a space where her competence is not about managing other people, but about mastering herself.

### 9. The Ribbon in the Nursery
- That evening, Patti Anne visits the twins’ nursery. Jessica Isabella and Rachel Darcy, age three, are in their crib or beds, conjoined, watching their older sister with the same fascinated attention they give everything.
- Patti Anne does not show off. She does not perform. She simply lets the ribbon trace a slow, quiet circle in the air above their crib. The color drifts like a falling leaf. The twins watch, silent, tracking the movement with four eyes.
- Patti Anne has already learned that the ribbon is not only for the gym. It is also a language she can share — on her own terms, in her own time, with her own control.
- She puts the ribbon away before the twins can grab it. She kisses them goodnight. She is the oldest sister, and she is also a gymnast. Both are true.

## Key Emotional Beats
- Mike sees Patti Anne’s oldest-child burden at age five and chooses to intervene early.
- The first rhythmic gymnastics class is not about competition or talent; it is about giving Patti Anne a space that is only hers.
- The ribbon becomes the first apparatus she masters — or tries to master — and it teaches her that control can be beautiful, not only responsible.
- Mike’s quiet watching from the parent area establishes the pattern that will recur across all his daughters’ performance milestones: presence without pressure.
- Patti Anne’s worry that she will lose her role as helper reveals how deeply she has already absorbed her oldest-sister identity.
- The scene ends with Patti Anne integrating the two parts of herself: she is still the sister who kisses the twins goodnight, but she is also the girl who has a ribbon that belongs only to her.

## Recurring Motifs
- **The ribbon:** the first apparatus; a thread of color that obeys her hand; the beginning of control as grace.
- **The gymnasium mirrors:** Patti Anne sees herself moving alone, not reflected against siblings or parents.
- **Mike’s bench in the parent area:** the first of many times he will watch her from a distance.
- **The car ride:** Patti Anne’s declaration *"I want to do it again"* as the hinge between trying and committing.
- **The nursery at night:** the ribbon shared quietly with the twins, not as performance but as love — Patti Anne controlling the boundary between her own activity and her sisterhood.
