## THE REACTION

Mikkel James — age **9–10** at the time of *The Jungle Girl*'s production and release, identical twin to Jemahr Pierce — reacts not with confusion, not with pride, but with **fury**. He is the first to yell. He is the loudest. He is the one who does not care about nuance, context, or the difference between a review and an attack. He sees a critic write that his baby sister's movie is "bad," and he responds as a boy who has been taught that family is the only fortress that matters.

The yelling begins at home. Mikkel James reads a review headline on Georgi's laptop — he is not supposed to, but he is curious, and he is a twin, and twins do not leave questions unanswered. The words are ugly. He does not read the whole article. He does not need to. He sees the score, the dismissive tone, the word "exploitation," and he storms into the living room where Jemahr Pierce is doing homework, and he says, "They gave her a bad rating. They're wrong. They're wrong and I hate them."

Jemahr Pierce looks up. In the twin language that requires no words, they agree. The yelling begins.

It happens at the breakfast table, when a newspaper is left open. It happens at school, when a classmate repeats something overheard from a parent. It happens at a grocery store, when a stranger recognizes Jordan Lisa from a set photo and says something careless. Mikkel James does not have the vocabulary to argue aesthetics. He has the vocabulary of a brother: **"She's my sister. You don't know her. You don't get to say that."**

The yelling is not always articulate. Sometimes it is simply: "Shut up!" Sometimes it is: "You didn't even watch it!" Sometimes it is tears he cannot explain, rage he cannot name, because he is nine years old and his baby sister is being called a victim on the internet, and he knows she is not a victim. She is a girl who ran through a jungle and climbed a tree and saved a baby monkey. He was there. He saw the dailies. He held the stuffed monkey she brought home from set.

Ashley Erin and Georgi do not punish the yelling. They know what it is. They know that Mikkel James and Jemahr Pierce are trying to build a wall around Jordan Lisa with their voices, because they are not old enough to build one with anything else. Georgi sits Mikkel James down once and says, "The critics are doing a job. They are wrong, but they are doing a job. You cannot yell at everyone." Mikkel James looks at him and says, "Then why do you let them say it?" Georgi has no answer. No one has an answer.

At the premiere, Mikkel James wears a tiny suit and stands on the red carpet with his hand in Jemahr Pierce's hand. When a reporter asks, "What do you think of the controversy?" Mikkel James says — loud, clear, unafraid — "My sister is the best part of the movie. You write that down." The reporter does not write it down. But Jemahr Pierce squeezes his hand, and that is enough.

## EMOTIONAL BEATS

1. **The discovery:** He reads the headline alone. The betrayal is not just that the review is bad — it is that the world has dared to judge his sister without loving her first. He does not have a framework for this. He has anger.
2. **The twin mobilization:** He tells Jemahr Pierce. They do not discuss. They agree instantly. This is the twin bond at its most primal: two boys becoming one shield.
3. **The yelling montage:** Home, school, public. He is not disciplined for it because his parents recognize the fury for what it is — love in the only language he has. But they do try to teach him other languages. He is not ready to learn.
4. **The premiere:** He stands in the suit, small and furious and proud. He speaks to a reporter. He is not performing. He is testifying. He is nine years old and he is telling the truth.
5. **The quiet aftermath:** He builds the pillow fort with Jemahr Pierce. They invite Jordan Lisa inside. They watch the movie together on a laptop. No yelling. No critics. Just the three of them, and the jungle, and the monkey, and the love that does not need to be loud to be real.
