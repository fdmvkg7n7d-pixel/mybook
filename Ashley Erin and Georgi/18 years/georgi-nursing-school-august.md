# SCENE OUTLINE: GEORGI LEAVES FOR LONDON (AUGUST)

**Characters:** Ashley Erin Key (17 — turns eighteen in October), Georgi Dimitrov (17 — turned seventeen in April). Dr. Stoyan Dimitrov, Dr. Veska Dimitrova. Possible light presence/reference: Mike and Terri Anne Key; Raya.
**Setting:** The Dimitrov apartment (his last night); Toronto Pearson International Airport (departure day); London, England — referenced, not seen.
**Timeframe:** **Late August, roughly two months after graduation and the hotel.** Georgi is leaving for London, England, to begin his nursing degree (BScN) — or a related program the saga settles on. He will be an ocean away. Ashley Erin has one more year of high school. This is a real goodbye.

---

## THE CONTEXT

He is not twenty minutes away. He is not a bus ride, not a Saturday coffee, not the same skyline. He is London — an ocean, a time zone, a five-hour flight. He will be gone for his entire degree. She will graduate high school without him. She will walk across her own stage next June, and he will not be in the third row the way she was in the third row for his.

They have known this was coming. He applied to programs in Canada and the UK. The London offer was the best one — the one his parents told him he had to take. *We didn't leave Bulgaria so you could settle,* Dr. Stoyan said. *We left so you could go anywhere.* And Georgi, who has spent four years watching his parents work below their training in a country that still doesn't quite count them, understood. You don't waste the opportunity your parents bled for.

But knowing it's coming is not the same as it being here.

They have been together for three years and two months. They have been each other's person for four years. They have had one night in a hotel room and a thousand ordinary days. They have said *marriage, kids, the whole thing* — and now the whole thing is about to include an ocean.

---

## PART ONE: THE LAST NIGHT — THE DIMITROV APARTMENT

### The Dinner

- The night before his flight. Dr. Veska cooks a full Bulgarian dinner — the kind she made the first time Ashley Erin came over, when she was thirteen and Georgi was fourteen and they were just two kids who'd met in a kitchen. Banitsa. Shopska salad. Something grilled. The same dishes, four years later.
- Dr. Veska is managing her emotions by feeding everyone. She piles food onto Ashley Erin's plate. She fusses over Georgi in Bulgarian. She is a mother whose only child is leaving the continent, and she is holding it together with food and busy hands.
- Dr. Stoyan is quieter. He watches his son across the table. He has spent four years working jobs below his training so this boy could have choices. Now the choice is London, and the pride and the grief are the same thing.
- Ashley Erin sits at the Dimitrov table — a table she has sat at a hundred times — and tries to memorize it. The scuff on the leg. The way the light hits the wall. The particular sound of Dr. Veska's slippers on the kitchen floor.

### The Small Things

- After dinner, Georgi and Ashley Erin do not go out. They do not make a production of the last night. They sit on the floor of his mostly-empty room — the same floor where they watched the Halloween horror movie, where they did homework, where they built a friendship that became a love that became a life.
- He gives her something. Not a gift — a thing to keep. The Bulgarian-English dictionary from his first month at UCC. The one he used when he was fourteen and still translating the world in his head. He doesn't need it anymore. He wants her to have it.
- She gives him the photo-booth strip from the arcade — the one she has kept in her wallet since their fifteenth date. She has the other copy. She wants him to have this one. *So you don't forget what I look like.* — *I'm not going to forget what you look like.* — *I know. Take it anyway.*
- They do not say the big things. They have already said them — in the hotel room, in the alcove, in a hundred small moments. Tonight is not for speeches. Tonight is for sitting on the floor of his empty room, her head on his shoulder, the city quiet outside, memorizing the ordinary.

### The Parents' Goodnight

- Dr. Veska hugs Ashley Erin at the door — the same fierce, maternal hug Terri Anne gave Georgi in April. She says something in Bulgarian. Ashley Erin doesn't need a translation.
- Dr. Stoyan shakes her hand — the first time he has ever done that. He has always treated her like a daughter. Tonight he treats her like family you don't say goodbye to lightly. *We will see you soon,* he says. It is not a question.

## PART TWO: THE AIRPORT — DEPARTURE DAY

### The Drive

- The drive to Pearson. Georgi in the front with his father. Ashley Erin in the back with Dr. Veska, who holds her hand without comment.
- No one says much. The highway is the same highway they have driven a hundred times. The sky is the same sky. But everything is about to change.

### The Terminal

- Pearson Airport, international departures. The particular chaos of late August — families, students, people leaving and people arriving. The Dimitrovs and Ashley Erin find a corner near the security line.
- Dr. Veska is crying openly now. She has earned it. She holds Georgi's face in both hands and says something in Bulgarian — a blessing, a prayer, a mother's last words before her son crosses an ocean. He answers in Bulgarian. His voice cracks.
- Dr. Stoyan shakes his hand — the same grip he gave him at graduation, the same grip Mike Key gave Georgi in April. Then he pulls him into a hug. *We are proud of you. Your mother and I — we are so proud.*
- And then the Dimitrovs step back. Deliberately. They give Ashley Erin the last moment. They know what she is to him. They have known since the first Bulgarian dinner, when two thirteen-year-olds sat at their table and didn't know yet what they were to each other.

### The Goodbye — Prose Passage

> The airport was loud and bright and full of people who were not them, and they stood in the middle of it, and neither of them knew how to start.
>
> They had done harder things. They had done the first date and the winter formal and the burned Valentine's dinner and the hotel room in June. They had said *I love you* in his parents' kitchen. They had said *marriage, kids, the whole thing* over fries and ice cream. They had done the big things.
>
> But this was a big thing too, and the big things were not easier the second time.
>
> He was leaving. Not the city — the continent. He was leaving the country, the time zone, the daily rhythm they had shared since the year his family landed and Raya pulled him into a kitchen and said *this is my friend Ashley Erin.* He was leaving, and she was staying, and there was an ocean between those two things.
>
> "I don't know how to do this," she said.
>
> "Neither do I."
>
> "You're supposed to say something reassuring. That's your job."
>
> He almost smiled. "I'm not going to lie to you. You know I'm not."
>
> She did know. He had never lied to her. Not once, in four years. Not about the small things and not about the big ones. He was not going to tell her it would be easy, because it wouldn't be. He was not going to tell her the time would fly, because it wouldn't. He was going to tell her the truth, and the truth was: this was hard, and they would survive it, but the surviving would hurt.
>
> "I love you," he said. "I've loved you since I was fourteen and didn't know what to call it. I loved you at the movies, and at the winter formal, and at Christmas, and in the hotel room. I loved you this morning, and I'll love you tomorrow, and I'll love you every day I'm in London, and I'll love you when I come back. That's not a speech. That's just the truth."
>
> She was crying. She had promised herself she wouldn't cry — not here, not in the middle of the terminal, not with his parents watching — but she was crying, and she didn't care.
>
> "You're going to be a nurse," she said.
>
> "Yeah."
>
> "You're going to be the best nurse in London. And then you're going to come home, and you're going to be the best nurse in Toronto."
>
> "Yeah."
>
> "And I'm going to be here. Waiting. I'm not going anywhere."
>
> He looked at her — the girl he had met in a kitchen at thirteen, the girl who had been his first friend in a new country, the girl who had never once made him feel like an outsider. She was seventeen and she was crying in an airport and she was the most beautiful thing he had ever seen.
>
> "I know," he said. "You've never gone anywhere. That's the whole point of you."
>
> He pulled her into him. The same way he had at the winter formal. The same way he had in the hotel room. The same way he had a hundred times in a hundred ordinary moments. She pressed her face into his shoulder and breathed him in — the cheap detergent, the faint trace of the Dimitrov apartment, the smell of someone who is about to get on a plane and carry you with him.
>
> "I'm proud of you," she said, into his shirt.
>
> "For what?"
>
> "For all of it. For making it here. For London. For —" she pulled back, looked at him. "For being you."
>
> He kissed her. Not the careful kiss of the first date, or the joyful kiss of the winter formal, or the deep kiss of the hotel room. The airport kiss — the one that says *I am leaving, and I am coming back, and those two things are both true.*
>
> "Text me when you land," she said.
>
> "You're not my mom."
>
> "No. But I'm the person who loves you, and I want to know you got there safe."
>
> He couldn't argue with that.
>
> He let go of her. He picked up his bag. He walked toward security — and then he stopped, turned, looked at her one more time. She was standing with his parents. Dr. Veska had her arm around her. They looked like a family.
>
> He raised his hand. She raised hers.
>
> And then he walked through, and he was gone.

### The Aftermath — The Terminal

- Dr. Veska keeps her arm around Ashley Erin for a long moment after Georgi disappears through security.
- Dr. Stoyan, quiet as always, says: *He will come back. He is a Dimitrov. We always come back.*
- They walk out of the terminal together — the three of them. The Dimitrovs and the girl who is not their daughter but might as well be.
- Ashley Erin does not cry in the car. She waits until she is home, in her room, in the Key mansion, and then she sits on her bed and looks at the Bulgarian-English dictionary he gave her and cries — not the quiet tears of the hotel room, but the full, unguarded cry of someone who has just watched the person they love cross an ocean.

## PART THREE: THE FIRST DAYS

### The Text

- She texts him when she knows his flight lands: *Are you there?*
- An hour passes. Two. She knows there's customs, baggage, the chaos of arrival. She waits.
- Then: *I'm here. London is loud. I miss you already.*
- She reads it three times. She texts back: *Miss you too. Go be a nurse.*
- *Working on it.*

### The New Rhythm

- The time difference is five hours. When she wakes up, he is already in the middle of his day. When she goes to sleep, he is five hours ahead, already in tomorrow.
- They learn to schedule calls — not the casual, spontaneous calls of the Toronto years, but deliberate calls, planned around classes and clinicals and the gap in the clock.
- She keeps the Bulgarian-English dictionary on her nightstand. She doesn't use it. She just likes knowing it's there.
- She starts her senior year. The hallways feel different without him in the next building. The winter formal is coming. She doesn't have a date. She doesn't want one.

### The Open Items

- They do not have a plan for when they will see each other next. Christmas is four months away. Four months is a long time. Four months is an eternity when you are seventeen and the person you love is on another continent.
- But they have a plan for the big things: he will come back. She will wait. The promise they made in the hotel room — *marriage, kids, the whole thing* — is still the promise. The ocean doesn't change it.
- It just makes it harder.

## WHY THIS SCENE MATTERS

- **It is their first real separation.** Not a different neighborhood, not a different school — a different continent. This is the first major test of the relationship, and the scene establishes that they will pass it, but not easily.
- **It closes the Toronto chapter of Georgi's life.** He arrived at fourteen knowing no one. He leaves at seventeen with a nursing school future on another continent, his parents proud, and the girl who has been his person the whole way through. The arc from immigrant kid to international student is complete.
- **It echoes the saga's immigration theme.** Dr. Stoyan left Bulgaria so Georgi could have choices. Georgi leaving for London is the fulfillment of that sacrifice, not a betrayal of it. The Dimitrovs — who spent four years struggling to be counted in Canada — watch their son walk into a future they built for him.
- **It deepens the Dimitrovs' relationship to Ashley Erin.** Dr. Veska's arm around her in the terminal, Dr. Stoyan's handshake — these are the gestures of people who have decided she is family, with or without a ring.
- **It parallels and inverts the graduation/hotel arc.** The hotel was the first time they came together fully. The airport is the first time they come apart. Both are acts of love. Both are choices.
- **The Bulgarian-English dictionary is a perfect artifact.** He used it when he was fourteen and translating the world. Now he doesn't need it — he has found his language, his place, his person. He leaves it with her. She keeps it on her nightstand.
- **It plants the long-distance arc.** The saga needs to decide what happens in the years between this departure and the adoption of the twins at thirty-three. This scene sets the table for that — the calls, the visits, the waiting, the eventual reunion.
