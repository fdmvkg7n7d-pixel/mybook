# SCENE OUTLINE: GEORGI LEAVES FOR LONDON (AUGUST)

**Characters:** Ashley Erin Key (17 — turns eighteen in October), Georgi Dimitrov (17 — turned seventeen in April). Dr. Stoyan Dimitrov, Dr. Veska Dimitrova. Possible light presence/reference: Mike and Terri Anne Key; Raya.
**Setting:** The Dimitrov apartment (packing); Pearson International Airport (departure); Toronto and London, late August.
**Timeframe:** **Late August, roughly two months after graduation and the hotel.** Georgi starts his business program in London, England in September. He is leaving the city and the country. Ashley Erin has one more year of high school. This is the end of the UCC chapter and the beginning of their first real test: long distance.

---

## THE CONTEXT

Georgi is leaving Toronto. He is moving across an ocean to London for his university degree. For four years — since the week his family landed from Bulgaria, since Raya's kitchen at thirteen — they have shared a daily orbit. UCC events. Weekends. The easy, unspoken knowledge that the other person is *right there* — a text away, a bus ride, a walk across the neighborhood. He was the new kid who knew no one, and she was the person Raya handed him, and for four years that geometry has held.

Now he is leaving for England. He will have classes and a new social circle and a schedule separated by a five-hour time difference. She will have her last year of high school — the one where you are supposed to be the senior, the one who knows everything — and the person who has been her person since thirteen will not be in the next classroom, the next building, the next neighborhood. He will be thousands of miles away.

The scene is not just a goodbye to a place, but a transition in the nature of their love. It is the movement from the effortless proximity of childhood sweethearts to the intentional effort of a long-distance partnership. It is the small grief of a chapter ending and the frightening openness of a new, distant one.

---

## THE OUTLINE

### Part One: Packing — The Dimitrov Apartment

- Ashley Erin is at the Dimitrov apartment, helping Georgi pack. This is different from previous packing; this is for a move across the sea. The suitcases are larger, the stakes higher.
- Dr. Veska drifts through, offering food, fussing over her son in Bulgarian. She is proud and sad in equal measure, and she does not hide either.
- Dr. Stoyan stands in the doorway of Georgi's room at one point, watching his son pack. He says something quiet — in Bulgarian, maybe, or in careful English — about how he left Bulgaria so Georgi could have the best possible start, and how London is the place to do it. Georgi nods. His father nods back.
- Ashley Erin helps Georgi sort through the small artifacts of four years: ticket stubs, a photo of them from the winter formal, a Bulgarian-English dictionary from his first month at UCC. He pulls out the photo-booth strip from their fifteenth date — the arcade, four frames of them making faces. She still has hers in her wallet.
- They find the playlist she made him for Valentine's Day — the one he played guitar for her after the burned dinner. Neither says anything. They don't have to.

### Part Two: The Airport — Departure

- Pearson Airport is sterile and loud: the hum of rolling luggage, the announcements in multiple languages, the specific tension of an international departure.
- Dr. Veska hugs him — fiercely, longer than he would allow if anyone else were watching. She says something in Bulgarian. He answers in Bulgarian. Ashley Erin doesn't understand the words, but she understands the tone.
- Dr. Stoyan shakes his hand — the same grip he gave him at graduation, the same grip Mike Key gave Georgi in the living room in April. *We are proud of you.*
- And then the Dimitrovs step back to let the couple have a moment. Dr. Veska's eyes are wet, and she hugs Ashley Erin too before she goes. *You take care of him.* — *I will.*
- And then it is just the two of them. At the gate. With his passport in hand and his new life waiting on a flight.

### Part Three: The Goodbye — Prose Passage

> They stood in the terminal — the air conditioned chill of the airport pressing in around them — and neither of them knew how to do this. They had done harder things. They had done the first date and the winter formal and the burned Valentine's dinner and the hotel room in June. They had said *I love you* in his parents' kitchen while his mother pretended not to hear. They had said *marriage, kids, the whole thing* over fries and ice cream in their underwear. They had done the big things.
>
> But this was a small thing, and the small things were harder to name.
>
> He was not just moving to a different room. He was leaving the city. He was leaving the country. He would be across an ocean, in a city where he knew no one, in a time zone that would make their phone calls a puzzle of midnight and morning. But he would not be at UCC. He would not be in the next building, the next neighborhood, the daily rhythm they had shared since the year his family landed and Raya pulled him into a kitchen and said *this is my friend Ashley Erin.* For four years, the geometry of their lives had been a single, shared thing — two orbits locked around the same center. Now his orbit was shifting. Not just different. Far.
>
> "It's not a big deal," she said, though her voice sounded small against the roar of the terminal.
>
> "It's a big deal," he said.
>
> "You're just... you're just in London."
>
> "I know."
>
> "You're not — you're not *gone*."
>
> "I know."
>
> She looked at him. At the boy she had known since she was thirteen — the boy who had never once made her a problem, who had asked her father for permission in a living room she had grown up in, who had booked two beds because her *no* mattered as much as her *yes*. He was standing there with a passport and a ticket, and she was supposed to let him go, and she didn't want to.
>
> "I don't know how to do this," she said.
>
> "Do what?"
>
> "Be here while you're there."
>
> He crossed the small space between them. He put his hands on her shoulders — the steady, un-flashy hands of a man who would one day build a legacy, the hands that had unzipped her dress in June and trembled doing it. They weren't trembling now.
>
> "You're not alone," he said. "You're going home. And I'm going to be there. And tomorrow you'll text me, and I'll text you back, and on Saturday we'll FaceTime, and it'll be the same us we've been since we were fourteen."
>
> "But you won't be at UCC."
>
> "No."
>
> "And I won't see you at school events."
>
> "No."
>
> "And you'll have business lectures and networking events and —"
>
> "Ashley Erin."
>
> She stopped.
>
> "I'm just a flight away," he said. "I'm not going anywhere. I've been your person since I was fourteen years old and didn't know anyone in this country. That doesn't change because I'm in London."
>
> She looked up at him. "Promise?"
>
> "Promise."
>
> He pulled her into him — the same way he had at the winter formal, the same way he had in the hotel room, the same way he had a hundred times in a hundred ordinary moments. She pressed her face into his shoulder and breathed him in — laundry detergent and the airport air and the faint, permanent trace of the Dimitrov apartment.
>
> "I'm proud of you," she said, into his shirt.
>
> "For what?"
>
> "For all of it. For making it here. For London. For —" she pulled back, looked at him. "For being you."
>
> He didn't say anything. He didn't have to. He kissed her forehead — the same forehead he had leaned against in the dark of the hotel room, in the after of everything — and held her for a long, quiet moment.
>
> "Go home," he said, eventually. "Text me when you land."
>
> "You're not my dad."
>
> "No. But I'm the person who loves you, and I want to know you got home safe."
>
> She couldn't argue with that.

### Part Four: The Drive Home

- She drives home. Or her driver takes her. Or she sits on the bus. The city scrolls past the window. Same streets. Same skyline. Same Toronto.
- But something has shifted. She is seventeen and she is in love and the boy she loves is no longer a bus ride away. He is a flight away. He is a time zone away.
- She does not feel left behind. She feels like someone standing on one side of a bridge, watching the person she loves take the first step across. She will cross too. She knows this. But for now, she is still on this side, and he is on the other, and the bridge between them is five thousand miles wide.
- She texts him: *Home.*
- He texts back: *Just boarded. I miss you already.*
- She reads it twice. She texts back: *Me too.*
- And then: *Saturday. FaceTime. Same time.*
- *Same time.*

## THE AFTER

- Ashley Erin starts her senior year. Georgi starts his first semester in London.
- They find a new rhythm — a long-distance rhythm. FaceTime dates. Late-night texts. He tells her about the grey skies of London; she tells him about the drama at the Key mansion. Dr. Veska still sends her home with food, but now she asks for photos of Georgi in the city.
- It is not the same. It is not supposed to be the same. But it is still *them*, and that is the thing that doesn't change.

---

## WHY THIS SCENE MATTERS

- **It is the end of the UCC chapter.** Georgi arrived at fourteen knowing no one; he leaves at seventeen with a business degree future and the girl who has been his person the whole way through. This scene closes that arc cleanly.
- **It introduces the theme of distance.** The separation is now geographic and structural. Learning to love each other across an ocean is a significant developmental step for their relationship, proving that their bond is not just based on proximity but on a fundamental, intentional choice.
- **It echoes and inverts earlier departures.** The first date ended with him watching her car leave. Christmas Eve ended with him leaving the mansion. The morning after the hotel ended with them walking out together. Now she is the one staying, and he is the one leaving, and the goodbye is expansive and heavy and true.
- **It gives the Dimitrovs a final grace note in the UCC era.** Dr. Stoyan in the doorway, Dr. Veska making the bed, the handshake and the hug — these are the same parents who sat in the third row at graduation, and this is the last time they hand their son off to the next chapter.
- **It establishes the "stability" of the relationship.** By surviving the long-distance university years, they prove that their devotion is "load-bearing," setting the stage for their later marriage and the challenges of adoption.
- **Ashley Erin's "I'm proud of you" is a quiet parallel to Georgi's "you're good"** after he watched *This Land* for the first time. They have always been each other's best audience.
