# Britany Madelena Kupchenko: Reaction to *The Jungle Girl*

## Scene / Character Beat

---

## THE REACTION

Britany Madelena — age **12–13** at the time of *The Jungle Girl*'s production and release — does not merely like the movie. She **loves** it. She loves it without reservation, without complication, without the need to interrogate it. For Britany, *The Jungle Girl* is color, movement, adventure, and the triumph of a girl who looks like her baby sister conquering a world that was not built for her. It is art, and her sister is the artist, and that is all Britany needs to know.

She watches the film with her whole body. She leans forward during the running sequences, gasps during the falls, laughs at the small moments of jungle humor, and applauds at the end — alone in the screening room, clapping for her sister. She is not embarrassed. She does not understand why anyone would be.

When the shirtless controversy erupts, Britany is confused before she is angry. She does not see what the critics see. She sees a child in a jungle, wearing what a child in a jungle would wear. She sees her sister's bravery, her sister's beauty, her sister's power. The idea that this is "inappropriate" does not compute for Britany. To her, the critics are speaking a language she does not speak, about a world they have not visited.

Her reaction is not intellectual. It is **pure**. She makes posters for her locker. She draws Jordan Lisa in her jungle costume, surrounded by flowers and animals, and pins the drawings to her bedroom wall. She talks about the movie to anyone who will listen — teachers, friends, strangers on the bus. She is not performing loyalty; she is expressing joy. Her joy is loud, messy, unapologetic.

When a friend at gymnastics says, "My mom says that movie is weird," Britany does not argue. She simply says, "Your mom didn't see the movie. Jordan Lisa is my sister. She was amazing." Then she does a cartwheel and walks away, already singing the jungle theme under her breath. She does not need to win the argument. She knows what she knows.

But there is one moment of quiet. In the weeks after the worst reviews, Britany finds Jordan Lisa crying in the playroom. Jordan Lisa is seven; she has heard something, a fragment of a conversation, a word she does not fully understand. Britany sits beside her and does not explain the controversy. She does not explain what "exploitation" means or why grown-ups argue about shirts. Instead, she puts on *The Jungle Girl* — the movie, not the conversation — and they watch it together. Jordan Lisa falls asleep halfway through, curled against Britany's side. Britany watches the rest alone, holding her sister, and she decides: *This is ours. The movie, the jungle, the joy. Not theirs. Ours.*

## EMOTIONAL BEATS

1. **The screening:** Pure, uncomplicated joy. She reacts with her whole body — gasps, laughter, applause. No shame, no filtering. She is the audience the movie dreams of.
2. **The creative aftermath:** She draws, she sings, she makes things. The movie becomes a creative spark for her own projects. She is not just a fan; she is an artist responding to art.
3. **The confusion:** She does not understand the criticism. It is like hearing a language she has not learned. She does not get angry immediately; she gets lost. Then she decides she does not need to learn the language. She already knows the truth.
4. **The comforting:** The quiet moment with Jordan Lisa. She does not solve the problem with words. She solves it with presence, with the movie, with sisterhood. This is the moment she earns her role as the "fun" older sister — not because she is always laughing, but because she knows how to bring joy back when it has been stolen.
