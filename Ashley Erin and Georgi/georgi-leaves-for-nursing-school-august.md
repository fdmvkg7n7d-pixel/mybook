# SCENE OUTLINE: GEORGI LEAVES FOR NURSING SCHOOL (AUGUST)

**Characters:** Ashley Erin Key (17 — turns eighteen in October), Georgi Dimitrov (17 — turned seventeen in April). Dr. Stoyan Dimitrov, Dr. Veska Dimitrova. Possible light presence/reference: Mike and Terri Anne Key; Raya.
**Setting:** The Dimitrov apartment (packing); Georgi's new place — a shared residence room or small apartment near campus (move-in day); Toronto, late August.
**Timeframe:** **Late August, roughly two months after graduation and the hotel.** Georgi starts his BScN program in September. He is staying in Toronto — he is not leaving the city — but he is leaving the daily rhythm they have shared for four years. Ashley Erin has one more year of high school. This is the end of the UCC chapter.

---

## THE CONTEXT

Georgi is not leaving Toronto. His nursing program is here — U of T, or Ryerson, or whichever Toronto school the saga settles on — and he will be twenty minutes away, not twenty hours. Ashley Erin knows this. She repeats it to herself. It is not a long-distance relationship. It is not a goodbye.

But it is also not the same. For four years — since the week his family landed from Bulgaria, since Raya's kitchen at thirteen — they have shared a daily orbit. UCC events. Weekends. The easy, unspoken knowledge that the other person is *right there* — a text away, a bus ride, a walk across the neighborhood. He was the new kid who knew no one, and she was the person Raya handed him, and for four years that geometry has held.

Now he is moving into a residence room or a small apartment near campus. He will have classes and clinical rotations and a schedule that does not bend around hers. She will have her last year of high school — the one where you are supposed to be the senior, the one who knows everything — and the person who has been her person since thirteen will not be in the next classroom, the next building, the next neighborhood. He will be in the same city. But the same city is not the same thing.

The scene is not a tearful farewell. It is a quieter, truer thing: the small grief of a chapter ending, even when the story continues.

## THE OUTLINE

### Part One: Packing — The Dimitrov Apartment

- Ashley Erin is at the Dimitrov apartment, helping Georgi pack. She has done this before — Bulgarian dinners, homework at the kitchen table, the Halloween horror movie, the sick day soup. This apartment has been a second home. Now his things are in boxes.
- Dr. Veska drifts through, offering food, fussing over her son in Bulgarian. She is proud and sad in equal measure, and she does not hide either.
- Dr. Stoyan stands in the doorway of Georgi's room at one point, watching his son pack. He says something quiet — in Bulgarian, maybe, or in careful English — about how he left Bulgaria so Georgi could do exactly this. Georgi doesn't know what to say. He nods. His father nods back. That is enough.
- Ashley Erin helps Georgi sort through the small artifacts of four years: ticket stubs, a photo of them from the winter formal, a Bulgarian-English dictionary from his first month at UCC. He pulls out the photo-booth strip from their fifteenth date — the arcade, four frames of them making faces. She still has hers in her wallet.
- They find the playlist she made him for Valentine's Day — the one he played guitar for her after the burned dinner. Neither says anything. They don't have to.

### Part Two: Move-In Day

- Move-in day is ordinary and chaotic: parents hauling boxes, nervous first-years, the particular smell of residence hallways. Georgi's room is small and bare — a bed, a desk, a window. Dr. Veska insists on making the bed. Dr. Stoyan checks the smoke detector.
- Ashley Erin helps him unpack: books on the shelf, clothes in the closet, the photo-booth strip pinned to the corkboard above his desk. She places it carefully. He watches her do it.
- Dr. Veska hugs him — fiercely, longer than he would allow if anyone else were watching. She says something in Bulgarian. He answers in Bulgarian. Ashley Erin doesn't understand the words, but she understands the tone.
- Dr. Stoyan shakes his hand — the same grip he gave him at graduation, the same grip Mike Key gave Georgi in the living room in April. *We are proud of you.*
- And then the Dimitrovs leave. Dr. Veska's eyes are wet — the same as graduation, the same as every milestone — and she hugs Ashley Erin too before she goes. *You take care of him.* — *I will.*
- And then it is just the two of them. In his new room. With his new life. And she has to go home.

### Part Three: The Goodbye — Prose Passage

> They stood in the small room — his room now, not theirs, not yet — and neither of them knew how to do this. They had done harder things. They had done the first date and the winter formal and the burned Valentine's dinner and the hotel room in June. They had said *I love you* in his parents' kitchen while his mother pretended not to hear. They had said *marriage, kids, the whole thing* over fries and ice cream in their underwear. They had done the big things.
>
> But this was a small thing, and the small things were harder to name.
>
> He was not leaving the city. He was not leaving her. He would be twenty minutes away — a bus ride, a text, the same skyline. But he would not be at UCC. He would not be in the next building, the next neighborhood, the daily rhythm they had shared since the year his family landed and Raya pulled him into a kitchen and said *this is my friend Ashley Erin.* For four years, the geometry of their lives had been a single, shared thing — two orbits locked around the same center. Now his orbit was shifting. Not far. Just... different.
>
> "It's not a big deal," she said.
>
> "It's a big deal," he said.
>
> "You're twenty minutes away."
>
> "I know."
>
> "You're not — you're not *leaving.*"
>
> "I know."
>
> She looked at him. At the boy she had known since she was thirteen — the boy who had never once made her a problem, who had asked her father for permission in a living room she had grown up in, who had booked two beds because her *no* mattered as much as her *yes*. He was standing in a room that was his now, not theirs, and she was supposed to leave, and she didn't want to.
>
> "I don't know how to do this," she said.
>
> "Do what?"
>
> "Go home. Leave you here."
>
> He crossed the room. He put his hands on her shoulders — the steady, un-flashy hands of a future nurse, the hands that had held the smallest, most fragile lives in clinical rotations she hadn't seen yet, the hands that had unzipped her dress in June and trembled doing it. They weren't trembling now.
>
> "You're not leaving me," he said. "You're going home. And I'm going to be here. And tomorrow you'll text me, and I'll text you back, and on Saturday we'll get coffee, and it'll be the same coffee we've been getting since we were fourteen."
>
> "But you won't be at UCC."
>
> "No."
>
> "And I won't see you at school events."
>
> "No."
>
> "And you'll have clinical rotations and night shifts and —"
>
> "Ashley Erin."
>
> She stopped.
>
> "I'm twenty minutes away," he said. "I'm not going anywhere. I've been your person since I was fourteen years old and didn't know anyone in this country. That doesn't change because I live in a different room."
>
> She looked up at him. "Promise?"
>
> "Promise."
>
> He pulled her into him — the same way he had at the winter formal, the same way he had in the hotel room, the same way he had a hundred times in a hundred ordinary moments. She pressed her face into his shoulder and breathed him in — laundry detergent and the new-room smell and the faint, permanent trace of the Dimitrov apartment.
>
> "I'm proud of you," she said, into his shirt.
>
> "For what?"
>
> "For all of it. For making it here. For nursing school. For —" she pulled back, looked at him. "For being you."
>
> He didn't say anything. He didn't have to. He kissed her forehead — the same forehead he had leaned against in the dark of the hotel room, in the after of everything — and held her for a long, quiet moment.
>
> "Go home," he said, eventually. "Text me when you get there."
>
> "You're not my dad."
>
> "No. But I'm the person who loves you, and I want to know you got home safe."
>
> She couldn't argue with that.

### Part Four: The Drive Home

- She drives home. Or her driver takes her. Or she sits on the bus — the same bus route she has taken a hundred times from his old neighborhood, but not from here, not from this new address that she will have to learn.
- The city scrolls past the window. Same streets. Same skyline. Same Toronto.
- But something has shifted. She is seventeen and she is in love and the boy she loves is in a different room now, in a different chapter, and she is still in the old one — one more year of high school, one more year before she follows him into whatever comes next.
- She does not feel left behind. She feels like someone standing on one side of a bridge, watching the person she loves take the first step across. She will cross too. She knows this. But for now, she is still on this side, and he is on the other, and the bridge between them is only twenty minutes wide.
- She texts him: *Home.*
- He texts back: *Good.*
- And then, a moment later: *I miss you already.*
- She reads it twice. She texts back: *Me too.*
- And then: *Saturday. Coffee. Same place.*
- *Same place.*

## THE AFTER

- Ashley Erin starts her senior year. Georgi starts his first semester.
- They find a new rhythm — different from the UCC rhythm, but still theirs. Coffee on Saturdays. Texts between classes. He comes to the Key mansion for Sunday dinners; she comes to his residence room with takeout and listens to him complain about anatomy. Dr. Veska still sends her home with food.
- It is not the same. It is not supposed to be the same. But it is still *them*, and that is the thing that doesn't change.

---

## WHY THIS SCENE MATTERS

- **It is the end of the UCC chapter.** Georgi arrived at fourteen knowing no one; he leaves at seventeen with a nursing school future and the girl who has been his person the whole way through. This scene closes that arc cleanly.
- **It is their first real separation — even though he stays in Toronto.** The distance is not geographical. It is structural: different schools, different schedules, different daily orbits. Learning to love each other across that gap is its own quiet skill, and this scene plants the first seed of it.
- **It echoes and inverts earlier departures.** The first date ended with him watching her car leave. Christmas Eve ended with him leaving the mansion. The morning after the hotel ended with them walking out together. Now she is the one leaving, and he is the one staying, and the goodbye is small and unglamorous and true.
- **It gives the Dimitrovs a final grace note in the UCC era.** Dr. Stoyan in the doorway, Dr. Veska making the bed, the handshake and the hug — these are the same parents who sat in the third row at graduation, and this is the last time they hand their son off to the next chapter.
- **It strengthens the bridge to the adoption arc.** Georgi's nursing training — the clinical rotations, the NICU specialization, the small fragile lives — begins here. The reader who knows he will one day be a neonatal nurse holding his own adopted twins feels the weight of that origin.
- **Ashley Erin's "I'm proud of you" is a quiet parallel to Georgi's "you're good"** after he watched *This Land* for the first time. They have always been each other's best audience.
