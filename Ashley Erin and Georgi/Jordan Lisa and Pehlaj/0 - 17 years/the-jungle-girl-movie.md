## THE PRODUCTION

*The Jungle Girl* is the most high-profile and controversial project in the history of Ashley Erin and Georgi Kupchenko's production company. The company bought the rights to the property and produced the film itself, casting their youngest adopted daughter, **Jordan Lisa Kupchenko** (age seven), in the lead role.

The film is a visually lush, physically demanding survival-adventure story about a young child stranded in a remote jungle environment. Like the 1980 film *The Blue Lagoon* (starring a 14-year-old Brooke Shields), *The Jungle Girl* places a very young actor at the center of a narrative that demands physical endurance, emotional vulnerability, and scenes of isolation and danger — all filmed on location in tropical, remote settings.

The decision to produce the film internally, through the family company, and to cast their own seven-year-old daughter, makes *The Jungle Girl* a lightning rod for critical and public debate. It tests the boundaries between a family's creative mission and its protective obligations.

## FAMILY DYNAMICS & THE DECISION

The decision to make *The Jungle Girl* is not a simple one. It emerges from the intersection of multiple family forces:

- **Ashley Erin's creative vision:** She sees the project as an extension of her own childhood work on *This Land* — a story about a child surviving and thriving in a world not built for her. She believes in Jordan Lisa's capacity to handle the material.
- **Georgi's business discipline:** He assesses the project strategically — the rights acquisition, the budget, the distribution potential, the risk profile. His MBA-trained mind sees the film as a sound investment that also happens to star his daughter. But the collision of "investment" and "daughter" is not simple for him.
- **Jordan Lisa's own desire:** At seven, she has already been training in ballet and acting. She wants to do what her mother did. She does not fully understand the scale of what she is asking for.
- **Hadleigh's reaction:** Despite her usual protective instincts, Hadleigh loves the movie. She is proud of Jordan Lisa's performance and defends the film fiercely against anyone who questions her sister's talent or the family's choice. Her emotional intelligence allows her to hold two truths: she sees the risk, but she also sees Jordan Lisa's joy and capability — and she chooses to stand by her sister publicly and privately. She is not uneasy; she is her sister's first and loudest champion.
- **Britany's creative excitement:** Britany (age 12–13 at the time) is thrilled for her sister. She sees the glamour, the adventure, the *art* of it. She loves the film unreservedly — the colors, the movement, the story of a girl surviving by her own strength. Britany does not see the cost because, for her, there is none. The movie is beautiful, and her sister is beautiful in it. She celebrates it with her whole heart.
- **The twins' (Mikkel James and Jemahr Pierce) protective rage:** At age 9–10, the twins are old enough to understand that critics are attacking their baby sister. They do not read reviews for nuance — they see headlines, they hear phrases like "exploitation" and "inappropriate," and they react with fierce, protective anger. Mikkel James and Jemahr Pierce yell at critics (in person at press events, or through outbursts at home when they see negative coverage), refusing to accept that anyone has the right to call their sister's work bad. They are not confused; they are defensive. Their twin bond amplifies their shared fury: Jordan Lisa is theirs to protect, and the critics are wrong, and they will say so loudly.
