# Jordan Lisa Is Cast in *The Jungle Girl* — Scene Outline

## SCENE INFORMATION

- **Working Title:** The Decision to Cast Jordan Lisa
- **Project:** *The Jungle Girl*
- **Timeframe:** Jordan Lisa Kupchenko is **seven years old**. Ashley Erin and Georgi have already bought the rights through **AEG**, their family production company.
- **Primary Location:** The Kupchenko family home in London, with references to the production office and Jordan Lisa's casting video.
- **Core Characters:**
  - **Ashley Erin Key-Kupchenko** — mother, former child performer, producer, creative force behind the adaptation.
  - **Georgi Kupchenko** — parent, business head of the production company, calm strategist, protective father.
  - **Jordan Lisa Kupchenko** — seven years old; ballet-trained, eager, emotionally secure, desperate to prove she can do what her mother once did.
  - **Hadleigh Patterson Kupchenko** — Jordan Lisa's older biological sister; protective, emotionally intelligent, already attuned to the seriousness in the adults' voices.
  - **Britany Madelena Kupchenko** — Jordan Lisa's older biological sister; expressive, delighted by art and spectacle, quick to turn the news into celebration.
- **Off-page / Referenced Characters:** Casting director **Graham Todd**, child-performance coach, child welfare consultant **Abbie Boot**, pediatrician, stunt/safety coordinator, on-set teacher, Mikkel James, Jemahr Pierce.
- **Core Question:** Is casting Jordan Lisa an act of belief in her talent, or a failure of parental protection?

---

## SCENE PURPOSE

This scene establishes the exact private moment where Ashley Erin and Georgi decide Jordan Lisa will star in *The Jungle Girl*. It should not feel like an impulsive act of family favoritism. It should feel like a decision made under pressure from four directions at once:

1. **Jordan Lisa's genuine talent and desire.**
2. **Ashley Erin's creative conviction and memories of being protected through her own childhood work.**
3. **Georgi's business calculation and fatherly caution.**
4. **The ethical burden of putting a seven-year-old at the center of a physically demanding feature film.**

The scene must preserve the ambiguity that later fuels the controversy: Ashley Erin and Georgi are not careless villains, but they are also not neutral observers. They love Jordan Lisa, they believe in her, and that love may make them more vulnerable to rationalizing the risk.

---

## CONTEXT BEFORE THE SCENE

- **AEG** has acquired the rights to *The Jungle Girl* after months of negotiation.
- The project was originally imagined as a bold child-centered survival film with international theatrical potential.
- The company held a legitimate casting search. Several child actors tested well, but none carried the physical stillness, emotional openness, and untrained ferocity Ashley Erin wanted.
- Jordan Lisa has been around the production office, hearing fragments of the story. She knows there is a jungle girl. She knows her mother believes the part matters.
- Jordan Lisa asks to read for it. At first, Ashley Erin says no.
- Georgi also says no — not harshly, but firmly. Their daughter is seven. There is a difference between ballet class, school plays, camera tests, and carrying a feature film.
- Jordan Lisa does not tantrum. She prepares. She learns the sides, practices quietly, and asks again.
- Ashley Erin finally allows a private, low-pressure casting video under strict conditions: no promise, no audience of executives, no costume testing, no physical demands, and the right to stop at any moment.

---

## THE SCREEN TEST THAT CHANGES EVERYTHING

The scene can begin after the casting video rather than during it, letting the test exist as a charged recent memory.

Key details from the casting video:

- Jordan Lisa performs a scene where the child protagonist realizes she is alone in the jungle for the first time.
- She does not overact. She goes quiet.
- Instead of theatrical crying, she listens to the imagined jungle around her — insects, birds, leaves, water — and lets fear arrive slowly.
- The casting director stops taking notes.
- Ashley Erin watches with a producer's eye for about ten seconds, then with a mother's face for the rest of it.
- Georgi watches the monitor instead of Jordan directly, because the screen gives him just enough distance to think.
- When the scene ends, Jordan Lisa looks toward her parents, not asking *Was I good?* but *Did you see me?*
- Nobody in the room says the obvious thing immediately: she is the best person they have seen.

## SCENE BEATS

### Beat 1: The House After the Test

- Evening in the London house. The children are awake elsewhere in the house — close enough that Ashley Erin and Georgi can hear their ordinary noise, but far enough away that the adults can speak privately.
- Jordan Lisa is still excited from the casting video, moving between her siblings and her own restless questions. She does not know the adults are about to have the real conversation.
- Ashley Erin and Georgi sit at the kitchen table or in Georgi's office with the test footage paused on a monitor, keeping their voices low because the children are not sleeping.
- The paused image is ordinary and devastating: Jordan Lisa's small face turned toward an imagined sound in the trees.
- Ashley Erin says nothing for a long time.
- Georgi finally asks the only question that matters: *"If she were not ours, would you cast her?"*

### Beat 2: Ashley Erin's First Answer

- Ashley Erin answers too quickly: *"Yes."*
- Then she corrects herself, because the speed of the answer scares her.
- She admits that if Jordan Lisa were any other child, she would fight for her. She has the face, the stillness, the physical discipline from ballet, and the emotional openness the story needs.
- Ashley Erin also knows that talent alone is not a sufficient reason to cast a child. Mike and Terri Anne never put her into a project like *The Jungle Girl*; when Ashley Erin worked as a child, she was protected, supervised, and never treated as a resource to be spent.
- Ashley Erin says the line that anchors the scene: *"If we say yes, we have to protect her at least as fiercely as I was protected."*

### Beat 3: Georgi's Ledger

- Georgi does not dismiss the performance. He agrees Jordan Lisa was extraordinary.
- But he separates the question into columns, the way he does when he is frightened:
  - What the film needs.
  - What Jordan wants.
  - What the law allows.
  - What the public will say.
  - What their family can live with ten years from now.
- He is most afraid of the last column.
- He says they cannot let the family company become a loophole. If Jordan Lisa is cast, the safeguards must be stricter than any outside studio would require, not looser.
- Georgi's key line: *"If she is our daughter, the standard is not lower. It is higher."*

### Beat 4: The Costume and Vulnerability Problem

- Ashley Erin brings up the part neither of them wants to soften: the story's survival realism includes long stretches where the child is dressed for heat, water, mud, and isolation rather than ordinary city life.
- They do **not** discuss this as spectacle. They discuss it as vulnerability, consent, camera ethics, and future consequences.
- Ashley Erin says they cannot hide behind words like *authentic* if the result makes their child unsafe or ashamed later.
- Georgi insists on written boundaries before casting is finalized:
  - no improvising costume decisions on set;
  - no unnecessary exposure for the sake of marketing or shock;
  - child welfare consultant **Abbie Boot** with authority to halt filming;
  - a closed set for sensitive physical or survival sequences;
  - camera angles planned in advance;
  - Jordan Lisa's right to say no without being treated as unprofessional;
  - daily check-ins with a child psychologist or welfare advocate;
  - no promotional material that turns the costume controversy into the selling point.
- Ashley Erin agrees, but the agreement does not erase the fear.

### Beat 5: Jordan Lisa's Want

- Georgi asks whether Jordan Lisa truly wants the role or wants to please Ashley Erin.
- This lands hard.
- Ashley Erin remembers Jordan Lisa saying, after the casting video, *"I didn't pretend to be brave. I pretended I had to become brave."*
- That line convinces Ashley Erin that Jordan Lisa understands the emotional center of the role better than many adults.
- Still, Ashley Erin refuses to let desire alone decide it. A seven-year-old can want something real and still need adults to protect her from the scale of it.

### Beat 6: The Outside Wall

- Georgi proposes the rule that makes the decision possible: they must invite outside people to tell them no.
- Before final casting, the footage will be reviewed by:
  - the casting director;
  - an independent child performance coach;
  - child welfare consultant **Abbie Boot**;
  - the pediatrician;
  - the stunt/safety coordinator;
  - legal counsel specializing in child performers;
  - the on-set teacher.
- Each person must have the authority to raise objections in writing.
- Ashley Erin initially bristles because it feels like asking strangers for permission to believe in her daughter.
- Then she realizes that is exactly the point.
- Georgi says: *"If everyone around us is paid to agree with us, then nobody is protecting her."*

### Beat 7: The Decision

- They watch the casting video again, this time without speaking.
- Jordan Lisa on the monitor hears the imaginary jungle and decides not to cry.
- Ashley Erin cries instead — silently, angrily, because she knows the performance is real and because she knows what it will cost to say yes responsibly.
- Georgi takes her hand.
- The decision is careful but real:
  - *"She is the strongest one."*
  - *"With every safeguard."*
  - *"And if she stops wanting it, we stop."*
- Ashley Erin closes the laptop before the image of Jordan Lisa can become a business asset again. Georgi goes back to the bathroom, where he is already supervising the girls' evening bath routine, and Ashley Erin follows a moment later so they can tell Jordan Lisa before the house has time to turn the decision into a secret.

### Beat 8: Telling Jordan Lisa in the Bathtub

- The scene moves into the girls' ordinary evening bath routine. **Georgi is already in the bathroom supervising** while Jordan Lisa is in the bathtub with **Hadleigh Patterson** and **Britany Madelena** — not staged, not ceremonial, just the familiar domestic chaos of sisters sharing warmth, water, chatter, and post-screen-test excitement.
- **Hadleigh Patterson is washing Jordan Lisa's hair** with careful older-sister tenderness, one hand shielding Jordan's eyes while Britany keeps up a bright stream of chatter beside them.
- Keep the staging gentle and domestic: the emotional focus is on faces, voices, Hadleigh's caretaking hands, towels stacked nearby, and Georgi sitting close enough to supervise without intruding on the sisters' rhythm.
- Ashley Erin comes into the bathroom carrying the seriousness of the decision on her face. Hadleigh notices first that her mother has come in with *news*. Her hands slow in Jordan Lisa's hair before she goes quiet. Britany is still talking until she sees Ashley Erin's expression.
- Ashley Erin does not make a grand announcement. She keeps it simple because Jordan is seven:
  - *"We watched your casting video again."*
  - *"You listened better than anyone else we saw."*
  - *"If you still want to do it, we want you to be our jungle girl."*
- Jordan Lisa freezes for half a second, processing the words. Then she asks the most childlike, practical question possible — something like: *"For the real movie?"*
- Georgi answers clearly: *"For the real movie. But only with rules that protect you. School, rest, safety, and you can tell us no."*
- Ashley Erin repeats the boundary in child language: *"The movie is never more important than you."*
- Jordan Lisa nods solemnly — then the joy hits all at once. She scrambles out of the bathtub in a burst of seven-year-old excitement, with Ashley Erin or Georgi immediately catching her in a towel as she throws herself into their arms.
- Jordan Lisa hugs Ashley Erin and Georgi, breathless and radiant, telling them: *"I'll be the best jungle girl ever. I'll be the best jungle girl ever."*
- Hadleigh immediately understands the weight of it. She stays close, protective and proud, watching Jordan Lisa's happiness without trying to take it over.
- Britany erupts too — joy, pride, a delighted gasp, already imagining the jungle, the posters, the adventure. Her happiness gives Jordan permission to be loudly, uncomplicatedly happy before the adult seriousness swallows the room.
- Jordan Lisa looks from Ashley Erin to Georgi to Hadleigh to Britany. The role becomes real because her family sees her, not because the company chose her.
- Hadleigh says something protective and proud — *"Then I'm going to watch every day I can."*
- Britany says something bright and possessive — *"You're going to be brilliant."*
- Georgi lets the sisters celebrate for a moment, but he watches Jordan's face carefully, making sure the joy is hers.

---

## FINAL IMAGE

The final image is not a boardroom decision or a paused casting video. It is Jordan Lisa wrapped safely in the immediate aftermath of her bathtub leap, hugging Ashley Erin and Georgi while her sisters celebrate around her, still utterly seven years old. Not a star. Not a headline. Not a controversy. Just their daughter, being told impossible news in the safest room the family can give her.

Ashley Erin watches Jordan Lisa's happiness and makes herself a private promise: *I will not spend you.*

This vow is sincere. The later controversy will test whether sincerity was enough.

## EMOTIONAL DYNAMICS

### Ashley Erin

- She is split between artistic recognition and maternal fear.
- She sees Jordan Lisa's gift because she has lived inside that kind of gift herself.
- She is aware that seeing the gift clearly does not free her from the duty to protect the child carrying it.
- Her memory of being protected by Mike and Terri Anne becomes the moral measuring stick for every choice.

### Georgi

- He is not the dream-killer, and he is not the permissive business parent.
- His love appears as procedure: lists, conditions, independent review, exit clauses, rest days, school hours, safety plans.
- He understands that paperwork cannot love a child, but it can prevent adults from pretending love is enough.

### Jordan Lisa

- She is not present for most of the adult decision, which matters.
- The scene should not make her responsible for the ethical burden.
- Her desire is real. Her talent is real. Her age is also real.
- The adults must carry the adult weight.

## CONTINUITY NOTES

- Jordan Lisa is **seven** when she stars in *The Jungle Girl*.
- This is her first big-budget feature and the breakthrough that launches her early acting career.
- **AEG**, Ashley Erin and Georgi's production company, has bought the rights and produces the film internally.
- The film later becomes controversial because of the age of the lead, the physical demands, the family-company conflict of interest, the survival costuming, and the press cycle.
- Hadleigh later loves the finished movie and defends Jordan Lisa fiercely.
- Britany loves the movie unreservedly as art and adventure.
- Mikkel James and Jemahr Pierce react with protective rage toward critics who attack Jordan Lisa.
- The private casting video is later leaked and made into a documentary by adult Jordan Lisa and Pehlaj, her future husband from Goa, India.

---

## THEMES

- **Talent is not consent.** Jordan Lisa's ability does not remove Ashley Erin and Georgi's obligation to protect her.
- **Love can distort judgment.** Their belief in Jordan Lisa is genuine, but the fact that it is genuine does not make it automatically safe.
- **Safeguards as love.** Georgi's insistence on outside review and hard boundaries becomes one of the scene's central expressions of parenthood.
- **The protected-child mirror.** Ashley Erin measures every decision against how carefully Mike and Terri Anne protected her when she was young.
- **The beginning of the controversy.** The public debate begins later, but the moral fault lines are already present in this room.
