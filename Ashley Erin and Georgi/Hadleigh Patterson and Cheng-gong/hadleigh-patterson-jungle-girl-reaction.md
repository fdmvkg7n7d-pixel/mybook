## THE REACTION

Hadleigh Patterson — the eldest of the five adopted children, age **13–14** at the time of *The Jungle Girl*'s production and release — reacts in a way that surprises even herself. She loves the movie. She is not uneasy, not worried, not quietly critical. She is **proud**.

This is not a failure of her protective instincts. It is a triumph of her emotional intelligence. Hadleigh sees what Jordan Lisa is capable of. She sees the joy in her sister's eyes on set, the discipline in the dailies, the quiet confidence of a child who knows she is doing something extraordinary. Hadleigh's early childhood — marked by neglect and uncertainty — taught her to read danger. But it also taught her to recognize safety when she sees it. And on the set of *The Jungle Girl*, surrounded by her parents and her family, Jordan Lisa is safe.

Hadleigh watches the finished film in a private family screening, sitting between Britany and Jordan Lisa herself. She does not flinch at the shirtless scenes. She does not see exploitation. She sees **her sister surviving** — a child alone in a jungle, finding strength, finding herself. It is the same story Hadleigh has lived, transposed into a different language.

When the controversy breaks — when critics call the film exploitative, when child-advocacy groups attack the shirtless requirement — Hadleigh becomes **the family's first public defender**. At school, at gymnastics, in conversations with teachers and friends, she speaks plainly and fiercely: "My sister is an actor. She was safe. She was loved. She was doing what she wanted to do. You didn't see the set. You didn't see the joy. You saw a headline."

She does not yell. She does not need to. She is calm, precise, unmovable. Her classmates who mock the film find themselves facing a girl who will not back down. Her teachers who express concern receive a response so mature it silences them. Hadleigh has been the family's emotional guardian since she was five; she does not abandon that role now. She simply redirects it — from protection to **advocacy**.

But there is a private moment, too. One night, after a particularly harsh review, Hadleigh slips into Jordan Lisa's bedroom. She finds her sister already asleep, clutching a stuffed animal from the jungle set. Hadleigh sits on the edge of the bed, watching her breathe, and she allows herself one quiet truth: *I would have protected her if she had needed it. But she didn't. She flew.* That is the only sorrow she permits herself — the sorrow of a protector whose charge no longer needs protection. And even that is mixed with pride.

## EMOTIONAL BEATS

1. **Private screening:** Anticipation, then surprise at her own lack of anxiety. The film is beautiful. Jordan Lisa is beautiful in it. She laughs at the funny parts, holds her breath at the dangerous parts, and cries — quietly, alone — at the ending.
2. **Public defense:** Calm, unshakeable. She does not seek arguments but does not avoid them. She becomes the family's designated spokesperson at school and in her social circles.
3. **The bedroom vigil:** The private moment of letting go. She allows herself to feel the sadness of no longer being the primary protector. Then she tucks the blanket around Jordan Lisa and leaves.
4. **The aftermath:** She continues to defend the film for years. When Jordan Lisa is fifteen and embarrassed by clips from *The Jungle Girl*, Hadleigh is the one who says: "Don't be ashamed. You were seven. You were magnificent. That is still true."
