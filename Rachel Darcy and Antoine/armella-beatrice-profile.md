## ACTIVITIES

### River Way Ranch Camp — Armella Beatrice's Schedule

Armella Beatrice attends **River Way Ranch Camp** (Sanger, California), the premier co-ed overnight summer camp in the foothills of Sequoia/Kings Canyon National Parks, alongside **Larissa Leola**, **Armando Cortez Junior**, and **Kella Nakeisha**. Armella and Larissa are paired as a camp pair — one of the identical twins and the eldest singleton. At camp, Armella is Larissa's little sister — not Kella's twin, not half of a pair. That is the whole point.

Armella and Kella attend River Way together — same camp, same session — but they are not paired with each other. Camp is where they learn, in a sustained way, that being identical does not mean being interchangeable. They see each other at meals, at swimming, at evening activities. But their days are their own.

**Armella's Majors (3): theater / performing arts, ropes course, photography**

- **Theater / performing arts (Major)** — The stage, shared with Kella. But where Kella majors in theater and takes the lead roles, Armella approaches it differently. [How she approaches it — backstage, directing, writing, ensemble, tech — TBD. The twins are in the same program, the same shows, but in different places inside them. That difference is one of the first signals of who they are becoming apart.]
- **Ropes course (Major)** — High ropes, zip line, the challenge course in the trees. Shared with Larissa. The eldest singleton and one of the younger twins, clipped into the same line, trusting the same harness. Armella, who shares a face with her twin, learns at the top of the ropes course that her body can do things Kella's body doesn't have to.
- **Photography (Major)** — The visual record. Armella sees the world through a lens. She shares this minor with Larissa (who minors in photography), but Armella takes it as a major — it is her primary art, the thing she carries around camp, the thing she is known for. While Kella is on stage being seen, Armella is behind the camera, doing the seeing.

**Armella's Minors (3): swimming, horseback riding, gymnastics**

- **Swimming (Minor)** — The family constant. Shared with Larissa, Armando, and Kella. The lake, the dock, the four eldest in the water.
- **Horseback riding (Minor)** — She rides, but it's not her passion. Connects her to her aunt Fallon Glenda. She is the only one of the four who minors in riding rather than majors — for Armella, the horse is pleasant, grounding, but not the thing she reaches for.
- **Gymnastics (Minor)** — The Key family's most dominant physical language. Shared with Kella. They are in the same gym, moving through the same stations, identical bodies in identical leotards — and then they leave the gym and go to different majors, different friends, different camp lives. Gymnastics is where they are still the same; everything else is where they learn to be different.

**Not taking:** waterskiing, wakeboarding, rock climbing, mini-bikes, arts & crafts, hiking overnights (among others). Waterskiing belongs to Larissa and Kella. Wakeboarding, rock climbing, and mini-bikes belong to Armando. Arts & crafts belongs to Kella. Armella leaves them to it.

### River Way — The Eldest Four's Territory

River Way is where the four eldest become their own unit — paired across birth-order lines (Armella + Larissa, Kella + Armando). For Armella, it is the place where she is Larissa's little sister, where she is the photographer, where she is not defined by sharing a face. She and Kella are together at camp but deliberately separate — and in that separation, they discover the first outlines of who they are when the other one isn't looking.

**The four's shared activity:** Swimming (Minor for all).

## ARC AND CORE THEME

Armella Beatrice is one of the seven children at the heart of the saga's most profound act of love — the gift Jessica gave Rachel so that Rachel could be a mother. Like everyone in this family, Armella Beatrice is proof of the same conviction: that family is not defined by biology alone, that love is what makes a parent, and that *"no one in this family does anything alone."*

She is also one of identical twins who learns, at camp, to be seen alone — behind the camera, on the ropes course, paired with the eldest sister instead of her other half. She loses her father at seven. She carries the loss the way her twin carries it: in the same face, in the same house, in different ways. And she has the lens: the tool that lets her frame the world instead of being framed by it.
