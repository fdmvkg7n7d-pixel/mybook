### French Woods — Marcos Eduarte's Schedule

Marcos Eduarte attends **French Woods** (Hancock, New York), the prestigious performing and visual arts sleepaway camp in the Catskills, alongside his triplet sisters Melissa Lizette and Annabella Nicki. The three attend together, for multiple summers, from early childhood through their teens. Like all French Woods campers, they choose their own fully elective schedules — each camper selects three majors and three minors each session. As a young child (ages 7–11), Marcos is on the main Festival campus with his sisters. At age 12, he has the option to shift to the sister camp, **French Woods Sports & Arts Center** (ages 12–17), where the program includes competitive sports alongside arts and technology. Even if he moves to the Sports & Arts side, the two campuses share a lake and facilities; he is never far from Melissa and Annabella.

**Marcos's Majors (3): ballet, baseball, martial arts**

- **Ballet (Major)** — Taken together with Melissa and Annabella. This is the one class the three never split: same studio, same barre, same teacher. Three bodies who began in the same womb, moving through the same positions — a silent language only they share. For Marcos, ballet is not his heart but his foundation: the turnout, the balance, the discipline of the body — all of it feeds the athlete he is becoming. He is the boy at the barre between his sisters, and he never misses it. Ballet teaches him things baseball needs: footwork, core control, the ability to explode from stillness.
- **Baseball (Major)** — His obsession, now with a dedicated summer training ground. At the Sports & Arts Center (ages 12+), baseball is a competitive program — coaching, drills, games. At the younger Festival campus, he takes whatever baseball-adjacent programming exists and fills the gaps with every sport that builds the skills. French Woods doesn't replace his year-round baseball; it supplements it, in a setting where his sisters are nearby and camp life holds him in a rhythm bigger than the diamond.
- **Martial Arts (Major)** — The thing he does when he's not on a diamond. Discipline, agility, a combat edge. Martial arts gives Marcos a different kind of stillness — the kind before a strike — and a different kind of respect for bodies, including his own. In a family built on the principle that *your body is yours*, martial arts is the physical expression of that: you learn what your body can do, and you learn when not to use it.

**Marcos's Minors (3): swimming, gymnastics, horseback riding**

- **Swimming (Minor)** — Taken together with Melissa and Annabella. The family constant. All eight Key sisters swim; all seven of Rachel's children swim. At French Woods, the triplets are in the water together, the way they have been since before they were born.
- **Gymnastics (Minor)** — The Key family's most dominant physical language. Marcos keeps it in the rotation — not as a competitor, but as a mover. The vault, the rings, the floor routine build exactly the kind of athleticism baseball rewards: explosive power, body control, spatial awareness. Plus, it connects him to his aunt Jessica Isabella, the original Key gymnast, whose burn scars he has seen his whole life and whose sport he now touches with his own hands.
- **Horseback Riding (Minor)** — Connects him to his aunt Fallon Glenda and gets him outdoors. For a kid who spends most of his sports life on manicured fields and gym floors, horseback riding is the wild card — the animal you can't drill, the partner you have to read. It is also the one minor he shares with Annabella (who minors in it too); Melissa leaves it to them.

**Not taking:** circus, yoga, drawing. Circus belongs to Melissa — her signature, her flying. Yoga belongs to Melissa and Annabella — the Sabrina Bryna lineage, the quiet centered practice. Marcos leaves them to it. Drawing belongs to Annabella alone — the visual, private art that is hers in a family of movers. Marcos sets it down without regret.

### French Woods — The Triplets' Shared Summer

French Woods is the triplets' territory — the place where, for a few weeks each year, the three of them step out of the sprawling seven-sibling dynamic and become simply the three they were born as. Rachel and Antoine, like so many arts-and-sports families, send them not only for the training but for the independence: a place where children build their own days, choose their own disciplines, and live among other kids who are just as serious and just as strange.

For Marcos, French Woods occupies an interesting space. He is the triplet who dreams of professional baseball — not dance, not gymnastics, not medicine, not the family's arts. But French Woods is where he goes with his sisters every summer, and it is where he is known not as *the baseball kid* but as *one of the three*. His majors — ballet, baseball, martial arts — tell the story: the shared art, the driving ambition, the discipline. His minors keep him tied to the family's physical language (gymnastics, swimming) and his aunt Fallon's world (horseback riding). He is the only boy among the triplets, the only athlete in a family of artists, and the only Key-d'Aboville child who will one day wear his full hyphenated name on the back of a jersey — but every summer, he is also just Marcos, at the barre, between his sisters.

**The triplets' shared activities:** Two classes the triplets take together, always:
- **Ballet (all three, Major).** Same studio, same barre, same teacher. The Rachel Darcy inheritance claimed together.
- **Swimming (all three, Minor).** The family constant. The water holds all three the way it held their mother and all her sisters.

## ARC AND CORE THEME

Marcos Eduarte is one of the seven children at the heart of the saga's most profound act of love — the gift Jessica gave Rachel so that Rachel could be a mother. Like everyone in this family, Marcos Eduarte is proof of the same conviction: that family is not defined by biology alone, that love is what makes a parent, and that *"no one in this family does anything alone."*

He is also the triplet chasing a different dream — baseball in a family of dancers — and the brother who, every summer in the Catskills, walks into camp side by side with his two sisters. At the barre between them. In the water with them. The three who always come as a set — even when one of them is looking toward a diamond the other two will never play on.
