# SCENE OUTLINE: THE TRIPLETS' FIRST BALLET LESSON

## Placement

**When:** A Saturday morning in late August, 2066 (all three are six years old).
**Where:** LA Key—Rachel Darcy's ballet studio in Los Angeles—the same studio where the triplets have grown up, watching classes and wandering the halls since they could walk.
**Characters Present:** Melissa Lizette, Marcos Eduarte, Annabella Nicki (all age six), Rachel Darcy Key (their mother and studio owner), Madam Catherine (children's ballet teacher), and a handful of other young students and their parents. No other Key family members are present—Larissa Leola lives in Perth, Australia; Armando Cortez Junior lives in Bangkok; and Kella Nakeisha and Armella Beatrice are at gymnastics that day.
**Context:** The triplets have spent their entire young lives in Rachel's studio—sitting in the corner during adult classes, napping on mats, watching dancers at the barre. Now they are finally old enough to take their first formal lesson in the space that has always been their second home. Rachel's studio, LA Key, is a proper facility with six different studios. Parents watch from a separate observation room with a one-way mirror, so children can focus without distraction. Antoine died when the triplets were one year old (2062), so he is not present. Only Rachel is there to watch—no other Key family members attend. Larissa Leola lives in Perth, Australia; Armando Cortez Junior lives in Bangkok; and Kella Nakeisha and Armella Beatrice are at gymnastics that day. Family note: Armando Cortez Junior is a creep and does not communicate with Rachel Darcy, Kella Nakeisha, and Armella Beatrice. However, Larissa Leola, Kella Nakeisha, and Armella Beatrice are very excited to hear about the triplets' first ballet class.

---

## Scene Purpose

This scene establishes:

- **The triplets' first formal lesson** in the studio they've known their entire lives—finally being allowed to participate rather than just watch.
- **The natural dynamic between Melissa, Marcos, and Annabella** as they move from being observers to dancers in their mother's world.
- **Rachel Darcy's dual role as mother and studio owner**—watching her children step into the space she's built, with pride and protectiveness. She watches alone from the observation room.
- **The comfort and familiarity** of being in their mother's studio—the barre they've touched a thousand times, the mirrors they've peered into, the sounds and smells they've grown up with.
- **The foundation of their bond as triplets in dance**—three bodies who began in the same womb, now learning to move together in the same positions in the same place they've always known.
- **The introduction of Madam Catherine as a recurring teacher** who will guide the triplets through their dance education at Rachel's studio for years to come.

---

## Emotional Core

**From familiar comfort to active participation.**

The scene charts the triplets' journey from watching ballet their whole lives to finally being allowed to do it themselves. There's no nervousness about a new place—they know this studio intimately. Instead, there's the excitement of finally being big enough to join in, the comfort of being in their mother's world, and the joy of moving from observer to dancer in the space that has always been their second home.

---

## Tone

Playful, warm, gently humorous, and tender. The class is designed for young children, so the energy is light and encouraging rather than rigorous. The emotional register moves from anticipation to concentration to delight.

## Scene Outline

### Beat 1 — A Familiar Space

Rachel Darcy's ballet studio is not new to the triplets. They've grown up here—sitting in the corner during adult classes, napping on mats in the hallway, wandering the six studios since they could walk. They know the sound of pianos playing during class, the smell of rosin and wooden floors, the way light comes through the windows in the morning. They've watched hundreds of dancers at the barre, memorized the combinations from their perch on the floor.

Today is different. Today they are dressed in their first ballet outfits—Melissa in a pink leotard and tights, Marcos in a white t-shirt and black shorts, Annabella in a lavender leotard—and they are finally old enough to step onto the floor themselves.

As they enter the studio, there's no nervousness about a new place. Melissa runs straight to "her" spot by the mirror—the one she's always watched from. Marcos tests the springiness of the floor in the same spot he's bounced on since he was three. Annabella stands in the corner she's always occupied during classes, watching the room with familiar eyes.

Rachel watches them with a mixture of pride and tenderness. This is her studio, her world, her art—and now her children are stepping into it. Not as observers this time, but as dancers.

### Beat 2 — Madam Catherine

**Madam Catherine** has been teaching children's classes at Rachel's studio for years. She's watched the triplets grow up from infants in Rachel's arms to toddlers tumbling across the floor to now, finally, six-year-olds ready for their first real class. She will become a recurring presence in the triplets' dance education, teaching them across multiple years at Rachel's studio.

Madam Catherine greets the small class with warmth and humor. She's experienced with young children and knows how to hold their attention through playfulness and encouragement rather than strictness. She's taught many of the other children in this class and knows their families well.

Madam Catherine introduces the basic concepts: ballet is about making your body beautiful, like a princess or a prince. She asks the children to stand tall like ballerinas and ballerinos, with their chins up and their backs straight.

Rachel moves to the parent observation room—a separate space with a one-way mirror that allows parents to watch without distracting the children. She watches alone. The triplets know this setup; they've seen parents watching from behind the mirror countless times. Now it's their turn to be the ones in front of it.

### Beat 3 — First Position and Plié

Madam Catherine demonstrates **first position**: heels together, toes turned out. The triplets have seen this a thousand times. Melissa gets it almost immediately, her feet naturally finding the shape she's watched dancers form at this very barre. Marcos struggles to keep his heels together, his toes splaying in different directions—he's watched this move his whole life but his own feet don't cooperate yet. Annabella watches Melissa and Marcos first, then carefully places her feet, adjusting until they match her brother and sister's.

Then they learn **plié**—bending the knees while keeping the back straight. The room fills with the sound of twenty six-year-olds counting "one, two, three, four" as they bend and straighten. Melissa pliés with natural grace, having memorized the movement from watching. Marcos bends too deeply, losing his balance and wobbling. Annabella moves slowly and deliberately, matching her brother's rhythm.

Rachel laughs quietly from the observation room, recognizing her own dancer's eye in her children's movements—the same way she learned to see ballet so many years ago.

### Beat 4 — Relevé and Balance

Next comes **relevé**—rising up onto the balls of the feet. This is harder than anything they've practiced. Many of the children wobble and topple over. Melissa rises up easily and holds the position with quiet concentration—she's watched this move countless times, and now her body gets to do it. Marcos pushes up on his toes but immediately falls back down, giggling. Annabella rises halfway, testing her balance, then rises the rest of the way, holding still for a moment before coming back down.

Madam Catherine moves through the room, gently correcting posture and encouraging the children. She knows the triplets—she's taught their older siblings, she's watched them grow up in this studio. She notices how they watch each other, how they mirror each other's movements, how they seem to share a silent language even in this new environment.

### Beat 5 — Skipping and Running in Ballet

To keep the energy up, Madam Catherine leads the class in **skipping** across the floor in ballet style—light, bouncy, with pointed toes. The children love this part. Melissa skips with natural rhythm, her arms floating gracefully. Marcos skips with athletic energy, his movements bigger and louder. Annabella skips more quietly, her steps small and careful.

Then they practice **running** in ballet style—on the balls of their feet, light and quick. The studio fills with the patter of tiny feet, the sound of laughter, the energy of children discovering they can move differently.

Next comes **crawling**—moving across the floor on hands and knees, exploring how their bodies move at different levels. Melissa crawls with deliberate grace, her back straight. Marcos crawls with speed, treating it like a race. Annabella crawls slowly, observing how the floor feels beneath her knees and palms. Madam Catherine encourages them to notice how crawling connects to ballet—the awareness of space, the control of movement, the strength in their core.

Rachel watches from the observation room, her heart full.

### Beat 6 — A Simple Combination

Madam Catherine puts together a very simple combination: first position, pliés, and then a small skip across the floor. She breaks it down step by step, and the children practice slowly.

The triplets move through the combination together. They are not perfectly synchronized—not yet—but they are trying, watching each other, adjusting. Melissa leads with confidence. Marcos follows her rhythm. Annabella watches both of them, then moves in her own time.

When they finish the combination, Madam Catherine applauds, and the children clap for themselves.

### Beat 7 — The Reverence (Bow/Curtsy)

At the end of class, Madam Catherine teaches the children **reverence**—the traditional ballet bow or curtsy that marks the end of class. The boys bow, the girls curtsy. Marcos bows with exaggerated formality, making Melissa giggle. Annabella curtsies slowly and gracefully. Melissa curtsies with natural elegance.

Rachel stands up from the observation room and joins the other parents as they gather at the end of class. The triplets beam with pride.

### Beat 8 — After Class

After class, Rachel meets her three children in the studio. She kneels down to them and tells them how proud she is. She asks them what they liked best. Melissa says she loved the mirror. Marcos says he loved the skipping. Annabella says she loved watching her brother and sister.

Rachel smiles and tells them they can come back next week if they want to. All three say yes immediately.

They head to Rachel's office to change. The triplets change out of their ballet outfits and into their normal clothes—Melissa into a pink dress, Marcos into jeans and a t-shirt, Annabella into leggings and a sweater. Rachel helps them with buttons and zippers while they chatter about the class.

It's just the four of them—Rachel and her triplets. No older siblings to share the moment. Larissa is in Perth, Kella and Armella are at gymnastics, and Armando is in Bangkok but doesn't communicate with Rachel, Kella, or Armella (he's a creep). But Rachel doesn't mind. This is her and her three babies, taking their first steps into ballet together.

Rachel knows Larissa, Kella, and Armella will be so excited to hear about this when she tells them later today. They'll want all the details.

As they leave the studio, hand in hand, Rachel watches her three children—her triplets—and thinks about the years ahead. French Woods. Ballet as a major. The inheritance she is passing down, not as pressure, but as gift.
