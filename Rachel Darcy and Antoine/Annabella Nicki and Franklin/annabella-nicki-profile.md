## ACTIVITIES

### French Woods — Annabella Nicki's Schedule

Annabella Nicki attends **French Woods** (Hancock, New York), the prestigious performing and visual arts sleepaway camp in the Catskills, alongside her triplet siblings Melissa Lizette and Marcos Eduarte. The three attend together, for multiple summers, from early childhood through their teens. Like all French Woods campers, they choose their own fully elective schedules — each camper selects three majors and three minors each session. As the youngest of all seven Key-d'Aboville children, Annabella Nicki is the baby of the family at home — but at French Woods, she is simply one of the three, choosing her own days from a catalog of possibilities.

**Annabella's Majors (3): ballet, drawing, yoga**

- **Ballet (Major)** — Taken together with Melissa and Marcos. This is the one class the three never split: same studio, same barre, same teacher. Three bodies who began in the same womb, moving through the same positions — a silent language only they share. For Annabella, ballet is not performance — that's Melissa, under the lights. It is inhabitation. She dances inside the movement, not for an audience but for the movement itself. Where Melissa turns ballet toward the stage, Annabella turns it inward. The Rachel Darcy inheritance, received quietly.
- **Drawing (Major)** — Annabella's primary language. Visual, private, patient, hers alone in a family of movers. At French Woods, where every other activity is physical and performed and shared, drawing is the room she enters by herself. Charcoal, graphite, ink — the marks she makes on paper are the one art in her life that no one else can step into. She does not perform her drawings. She keeps them in a portfolio, or pins them to her bunk wall, or gives them away to the people she loves without saying what they cost her to make.
- **Yoga (Major)** — The Sabrina Bryna lineage, shared with Melissa. Quiet, centered, deliberate. For Annabella, yoga is not counterbalance to a theatrical life (the way it is for Melissa) — it is the natural extension of who she already is. A girl who watches. A girl who moves slowly and means it. The mat is where she learns that stillness is not absence — it is a practice, the same as drawing, the same as ballet. Yoga also ties her to her aunt Sabrina Bryna, who practices from a body with cerebral palsy — and Annabella, the youngest of seven, the one who watches, notices this connection before anyone explains it to her.

**Annabella's Minors (3): horseback riding, circus, swimming**

- **Horseback Riding (Minor)** — Her quiet bond with animals. In a family where her aunt Fallon Glenda rides, Annabella discovers she loves the barn: the smell of hay, the weight of a horse leaning into a brush stroke, the way an animal that large can be gentle. She is not a competitive rider. She is the girl who stays after the lesson to cool down a horse that isn't hers, talking to it the whole time. She shares this minor with Marcos — the one activity, aside from the two shared ones, where the youngest triplet and her brother overlap.
- **Circus (Minor)** — A thread of daring. Annabella does not fly the way Melissa does — Melissa is the one on the trapeze, in the silks, under the lights. But Annabella takes circus as a minor because Melissa does, and because there is something in it she needs: the discovery that her quiet body can also take risks. She might prefer the grounded apparatus — lyra, low trapeze — or the precision arts like juggling. Whatever she chooses, circus is where Annabella learns that being quiet doesn't mean being timid.
- **Swimming (Minor)** — Taken together with Melissa and Marcos. The family constant. All eight Key sisters swim; all seven of Rachel's children swim. At French Woods, the triplets are in the water together, the way they have been since before they were born. For Annabella — the youngest, the one who is used to being last — swimming is the place where she is simply one of three bodies in the water, indistinguishable from her siblings, held.

**Not taking:** gymnastics, baseball, martial arts. Gymnastics is the family's most dominant physical language — and the one Annabella quietly steps away from. In a family of gymnasts (Jessica Isabella, Samira, Sahara, Kiley Rose — and Melissa and Marcos both take it as minors), Annabella is the one who does not. That absence is its own quiet statement: she does not have to carry everything the family carries. Baseball and martial arts belong to Marcos; she leaves them to him.

### French Woods — The Triplets' Shared Summer

French Woods is the triplets' territory — the place where, for a few weeks each year, the three of them step out of the sprawling seven-sibling dynamic and become simply the three they were born as. Rachel and Antoine, like so many arts-and-sports families, send them not only for the training but for the independence: a place where children build their own days, choose their own disciplines, and live among other kids who are just as serious and just as strange.

For Annabella Nicki — the youngest of seven, the baby of the family — French Woods offers something specific: a space where she is not defined by being the last, the smallest, the one everyone looks after. At camp, in a cabin with girls her own age, choosing her own schedule from a catalog of possibilities, she is simply Annabella. Not the youngest triplet. Not the seventh child. Herself. Her majors — ballet, drawing, yoga — are the disciplines of someone who turns inward. Her minors — horseback riding, circus, swimming — are the places where she reaches out: toward animals, toward risk, toward her siblings in the water.

**The triplets' shared activities:** Two classes the triplets take together, always:
- **Ballet (all three, Major).** Same studio, same barre, same teacher. The Rachel Darcy inheritance claimed together.
- **Swimming (all three, Minor).** The family constant. The water holds all three the way it held their mother and all her sisters.

## ARC AND CORE THEME

Annabella Nicki is one of the seven children at the heart of the saga's most profound act of love — the gift Jessica gave Rachel so that Rachel could be a mother. Like everyone in this family, Annabella Nicki is proof of the same conviction: that family is not defined by biology alone, that love is what makes a parent, and that *"no one in this family does anything alone."*

She is also the youngest of them all — the baby of seven — who grows up knowing she belongs not just to Rachel and Antoine, but to Melissa and Marcos, the two who shared the womb with her and every summer after. As a Temple University student following Rachel Darcy’s footsteps through dance, she becomes the triplet whose adult life turns toward quiet movement, drawing, and embodied study. At French Woods, in the Catskills, she learns who she is when she's not the youngest. She takes ballet beside her brother and sister in the same studio. She draws — alone, quietly, in a language no one else in the family speaks. She steps away from gymnastics, the family's most dominant physical language, and no one in her family asks her to explain it. The absence is enough. She is enough.


She is also the youngest — five years old when her father dies. She will remember almost nothing of him directly: a voice, a hand, a room she can no longer place. Her father will be, for her, a story other people tell. And yet the loss is no less real for being reconstructed. It is simply a different kind of grief: the grief of not knowing what you lost.
