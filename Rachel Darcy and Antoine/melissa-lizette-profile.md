## ACTIVITIES

### French Woods — Melissa Lizette's Schedule

Melissa Lizette attends **French Woods** (Hancock, New York), the prestigious performing and visual arts sleepaway camp in the Catskills, alongside her triplet siblings Marcos Eduarte and Annabella Nicki. The three attend together, for multiple summers, from early childhood through their teens. Like all French Woods campers, they choose their own fully elective schedules — each camper selects three majors and three minors each session.

**Melissa's Majors (3): ballet, circus, yoga**

- **Ballet (Major)** — Taken together with Marcos and Annabella. This is the one class the three never split: same studio, same barre, same teacher. Three bodies who began in the same womb, moving through the same positions — a silent language only they share. For Melissa, ballet is the Rachel Darcy inheritance taken all the way to the stage. Where Annabella inhabits the dance and Marcos learns from it, Melissa performs it. She is the triplet most at home under the lights.
- **Circus (Major)** — Melissa's signature discipline. Theatrical, physical, daring — the thing no other Key child does. Flying trapeze, silks, lyra, acrobatics. It is ballet turned upside down: the same discipline of the body, but in three dimensions, with risk built in. Circus is where Melissa discovers she is not only a dancer but a performer — someone who can hold an audience with her body in flight.
- **Yoga (Major)** — The Sabrina Bryna lineage. Quiet, centered, deliberate. Yoga grounds Melissa — balances the theatrical fire of circus and the formal discipline of ballet with something inward. In a family where her aunt Sabrina Bryna has practiced yoga from a body with cerebral palsy, Melissa's yoga is also a thread of connection: the cousin who moves in ASL and the niece who moves on a mat, speaking different dialects of the same language.

**Melissa's Minors (3): gymnastics, swimming, drawing**

- **Gymnastics (Minor)** — The Key family's most dominant physical language. Melissa keeps it in the rotation — not as a passion, but as a connection. The vault, the beam, the floor routine: these are the movements her mother's twin sister Jessica mastered, the movements her cousins Samira and Sahara grew up inside. She does not need to be great at it. She needs to share it.
- **Swimming (Minor)** — Taken together with Marcos and Annabella. The family constant. All eight Key sisters swim; all seven of Rachel's children swim. At French Woods, the triplets are in the water together, the way they have been since before they were born.
- **Drawing (Minor)** — Her visual outlet. Where Annabella majors in drawing — lives in it — Melissa uses it as a minor: sketchbook in her bunk, charcoal on her hands, a way of seeing the circus rig before she climbs it. Less a discipline than a companion to her other arts.

**Not taking:** baseball, martial arts, horseback riding. Baseball and martial arts belong to Marcos; she leaves him to them. Horseback riding she leaves to Annabella — the quiet bond between the youngest triplet and the animals.

### French Woods — The Triplets' Shared Summer

French Woods is the triplets' territory — the place where, for a few weeks each year, the three of them step out of the sprawling seven-sibling dynamic and become simply the three they were born as. Rachel and Antoine, like so many arts-and-sports families, send them not only for the training but for the independence: a place where children build their own days, choose their own disciplines, and live among other kids who are just as serious and just as strange.

**What French Woods is:** A 170-acre campus in Hancock, New York — a fully elective summer camp for ages 7–17, famous for its performing and visual arts programming (theater, music, dance, circus, visual arts, film, magic) and, at its sister campus the French Woods Sports & Arts Center (ages 12–17), sports, technology, horseback riding, and more. Campers build their own daily schedule from hundreds of possible activities. The camp's ethos — *choice, independence, and immersion* — mirrors the way the Key family raises its children: your body is yours, your time is yours, and you are trusted to choose what you love and pursue it seriously.

**How it works for the triplets:** As young children (ages 7–11), all three attend the main French Woods Festival campus — Melissa, Annabella, and Marcos together in the same session, sharing cabins, meals, and camp traditions. As they reach age 12, Marcos has the option to shift to the Sports & Arts Center sister campus; Melissa and Annabella remain on the Festival side. The campuses share a lake and some facilities, so even when they are in different programs, the triplets are never truly apart — they see each other at meals, at evening activities, and in the casual overlap of camp life.

**The triplets' shared activities:** Two classes the triplets take together, always:
- **Ballet (all three, Major).** Same studio, same barre, same teacher. The Rachel Darcy inheritance claimed together.
- **Swimming (all three, Minor).** The family constant. The water holds all three the way it held their mother and all her sisters.

**Why it matters for the saga:**
1. **The triplets, defined together.** In a family of seven children broken into singletons, twins, and triplets, French Woods is the one place where Melissa, Marcos, and Annabella are known as *the three who always come as a set*. It is where their bond as triplets — distinct from their roles as younger siblings to Larissa, Armando, Kella, and Armella — solidifies into something of its own.
2. **A different kind of inheritance.** Unlike the twins Samira and Sahara, who were spotted by a Vaganova teacher and swept into the most elite training in the world, the triplets choose a broader, more exploratory path — the camp designed for kids to try everything, not specialize in one thing. Like their cousins, they live in their bodies. But at French Woods, they decide how.
3. **Connection to the cousins.** Samira and Sahara train at Vaganova and the Bolshoi — Russia, the pinnacle of classical ballet. The triplets spend their summers in rural New York. The contrast is deliberate and rich: two different philosophies of art and childhood, two different answers to the same family question (*what do you do with a gift?*), both held within the same sprawling Key family.
4. **A home away from LA.** If the family ultimately relocates east — toward Halifax, toward the original Key hub — French Woods is already there, already familiar, already an anchor.
5. **Three siblings, nine majors.** Melissa (circus, yoga), Marcos (baseball, martial arts), Annabella (drawing, yoga) — each triplet has two majors that are theirs alone, and one major (ballet) they share. The minors overlap where they choose to. The result is three distinct people who chose, on purpose, to keep one room where they are still undivided.

## ARC AND CORE THEME

Melissa Lizette is one of the seven children at the heart of the saga's most profound act of love — the gift Jessica gave Rachel so that Rachel could be a mother. Like everyone in this family, Melissa Lizette is proof of the same conviction: that family is not defined by biology alone, that love is what makes a parent, and that *"no one in this family does anything alone."*

She is also one of the three — the performer, the one who climbs the silks and holds the stage, who dances beside her brother and her sister in the same studio every summer. At French Woods, Melissa is not the fifth-born or the middle triplet. She is a girl who chooses circus, who centers herself in yoga, who takes the Rachel Darcy inheritance of ballet and turns it toward the lights.


She is also the fifth-born — five years old when her father dies. She will remember almost nothing of him directly: a voice, a hand, a room she can no longer place. Her father will be, for her, a story other people tell. And yet the loss is no less real for being reconstructed. It is simply a different kind of grief: the grief of not knowing what you lost.
