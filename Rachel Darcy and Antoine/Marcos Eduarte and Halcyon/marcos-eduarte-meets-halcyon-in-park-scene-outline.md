# SCENE OUTLINE: MARCOS EDUARTE MEETS HALCYON IN THE PARK

## Placement

**When:** The same day Marcos Eduarte signs his professional contract with the Toronto Blue Jays out of high school.  
**Where:** Clarence Park near Rogers Centre in Toronto; later, the decision to take Halcyon to the Carlisle mansion in Ontario.  
**Characters:** Marcos Eduarte Key-d'Aboville, Halcyon. Possible brief background presence from family/security/park visitors.  
**Marcos's age:** Approximately eighteen.  
**Halcyon's age:** Seventeen.  
**Context:** Marcos has just stepped into professional baseball. He has money, a contract, public expectations, and the Blue Jays' development plan ahead of him. He goes to Clarence Park because he needs quiet. Halcyon is living in Clarence Park when he meets her.

---

## Scene Purpose

This scene establishes:

- Marcos meeting Halcyon on the exact day he crosses the threshold between childhood and professional baseball.
- Halcyon, seventeen, as a person with agency, not a rescue object.
- Marcos's instinct toward quiet observation rather than flashy heroics.
- The contrast between his new contract/security and Halcyon's unstable housing situation.
- Marcos buying Halcyon pizza as a concrete, immediate act of care rather than a sweeping rescue.
- Marcos telling Halcyon he signed with the Toronto Blue Jays as a pitcher.
- Halcyon being very frightened to go off with him, even when he offers safety.
- Marcos explaining that his Blue Jays contract has a moral clause, meaning he cannot afford to do anything coercive, exploitative, or reckless.
- The beginning of a relationship that will challenge Marcos to understand care without control.

---

## Emotional Core

**Recognition before rescue.**

Marcos does not meet Halcyon because he is looking for someone to save. He meets her because he notices her — her organization, her alertness, her refusal to be invisible. His first help is deliberately small: food, specifically pizza, because food can be accepted without requiring her to surrender control.

Halcyon does not become his girlfriend immediately in this first beat. She is seventeen, so the scene should stay age-appropriate and plant the beginning: curiosity, caution, a first conversation, and the strange feeling that both of them are standing at the edge of very different lives.

---

## Tone

Quiet, grounded, wary, and intimate. No melodrama. No instant fairy tale. The park should feel real: open air, benches, grass, paths, stadium-adjacent foot traffic, city noise, and the private geography Halcyon has made for survival.

---

## Scene Outline

### Beat 1 — After the Contract Noise

Marcos has signed with the Toronto Blue Jays.

Everyone around him is proud. Rachel is emotional. His siblings are excited. The name **KEY-D'ABOVILLE** has begun its path toward a professional jersey.

But Marcos feels overloaded.

He goes to Clarence Park with his glove or a baseball, not to practice seriously, but to breathe. Rogers Centre and the Blue Jays machinery are still close enough to feel real, but the park gives him open space without requiring him to perform.

### Beat 2 — Marcos Notices Order

He sees Halcyon before he understands her situation.

What catches his attention is not simply that she is living in the park. It is the order around her:

- her bag placed within reach;
- her blanket folded or rolled in a deliberate way;
- her shoes positioned carefully;
- perhaps a book, notebook, or small personal object protected from dirt;
- the way she watches movement without seeming afraid of every person.

Marcos recognizes discipline when he sees it.

### Beat 3 — Halcyon Notices Him Noticing

Halcyon catches him looking.

She does not shrink. She challenges him with a look or a direct line.

Possible dialogue:

> "If you're going to stare, at least be interesting."

Marcos is caught off guard, but he does not get defensive.

He answers honestly.

> "I wasn't staring at you. I was looking at how you set everything up."

That answer matters. It tells Halcyon he saw her choices, not only her hardship.

### Beat 4 — The First Conversation

Their first conversation should be brief and edged with caution.

Halcyon may ask about the baseball or glove. Marcos tells her plainly that he pitches, and that earlier that day he signed with the **Toronto Blue Jays** as a pitcher. She may not know or care what that means at first. This is important: she meets him outside the worship of baseball.

Possible exchange:

> Halcyon: "You any good?"  
> Marcos: "They think I might be."  
> Halcyon: "That's not what I asked."

This is the first time she cuts through the professional language around him.


### Beat 5 — The Pizza

Marcos buys Halcyon pizza.

This should be practical and grounded, not romanticized. He does not make a performance out of feeding her. He asks what she wants, buys it, and lets her eat without staring at her or demanding gratitude.

The pizza gives the scene a concrete human anchor:

- cardboard box warmth;
- melted cheese;
- paper napkins;
- Halcyon eating carefully because she is hungry but does not want to look desperate;
- Marcos learning to sit with silence instead of filling it.

This is the first sign that Marcos can offer something useful without turning it into a grand rescue.

### Beat 6 — Marcos Does Not Offer Too Much

Marcos may want to offer money, a ride, a hotel, something immediate. But he checks himself.

He asks instead:

> "Do you need anything right now?"

Halcyon is allowed to refuse.

If she accepts anything beyond the pizza, it should be specific and practical — water, a phone charge, information — not a sweeping rescue.

The key is consent.

### Beat 7 — Names

Marcos gives his name.

> "Marcos."

Halcyon gives hers.

> "Halcyon."

He may repeat it once, not mockingly, but because the name lands strangely and beautifully.

> "Halcyon."

She watches to see whether he will make a joke. He does not.

### Beat 8 — The Contract Comes Out

At some point, Halcyon realizes or asks why he is carrying a baseball glove around like it means something.

Marcos tells her he just signed with the Toronto Blue Jays as a pitcher.

He also tells her something important: the contract has a **moral clause**. He explains it simply — if he does something exploitative, coercive, criminal, or publicly damaging, he can lose everything he just signed.

This is not meant to pressure Halcyon into trusting him. It is meant to give her one concrete reason why he is not going to force her, trap her, or use her situation carelessly.

Halcyon does not respond the way everyone else has.

She might say:

> "So today is supposed to be the best day of your life, and you're sitting alone in a park."

Marcos has no easy answer.

This is why she matters.

### Beat 9 — Marcos Does Not Call Rachel

Marcos eventually has to leave. Family may be waiting. The Blue Jays may have calls. His new life is already pulling at him.

But he cannot simply walk away from Halcyon sleeping in Clarence Park.

He does **not** call Rachel Darcy first. This is important. Marcos is newly signed, seventeen, overwhelmed, and trying to make an adult decision before he fully understands the weight of it. He chooses to act in the moment rather than step away and ask his mother to manage it.

This makes the choice riskier and more emotionally complicated. He is not hiding Halcyon out of malice, but he is also not doing the fully careful thing yet.

### Beat 10 — The Offer

Marcos returns to Halcyon and does not command, pressure, or assume.

He offers one concrete possibility:

> "My family has a house outside the city. Carlisle. You could come there tonight if you want. A room. Food. A door that locks. No one will make you explain everything before you sleep."

Halcyon is very frightened to go off with him. This fear should be taken seriously. It is not irrational. She is seventeen, unhoused, and being invited away from the public visibility of Clarence Park by a boy she only just met, even if he seems kind.

She asks questions:

- Who knows?
- Is his mother there?
- Is this a trick?
- Can she leave if she wants?
- What happens to her things?
- Why should she believe he is safe?

Marcos answers carefully. No, Rachel does not know yet. No, it is not a trick. Yes, Halcyon can leave. Her things come with her. No one touches them without asking. He tells her that his mother is Rachel Darcy and that Carlisle is his family home, but he is honest that he has not called Rachel first.

He also tells her again that his Blue Jays contract has a moral clause. He does not use it as a magic solution, but as one more factual anchor:

> "If I hurt you or force you or lie about this, I don't just hurt you. I lose everything I signed today. I know that doesn't mean you have to trust me. But I need you to know I'm not asking you to disappear with me. My mother knows. You can say no. You can leave."

### Beat 11 — Halcyon Chooses

Halcyon chooses to go, but the choice is frightened and cautious, not easy.

This is important: Marcos does not "take" her from the park as if she has no agency. He brings her because she agrees, even though Rachel has not been told yet.

She gathers her belongings herself. Marcos may carry something only if she permits it.

The movement from the park to the car should feel enormous, even if it is physically simple. For Marcos, it is the first act of adulthood after signing. For Halcyon, it is the first step toward a place she does not yet trust.

### Beat 12 — Toward Carlisle

The scene ends with Marcos and Halcyon leaving Clarence Park for the Carlisle mansion.

Rogers Centre remains behind them — the symbol of Marcos's new public future. Carlisle waits ahead — the Key family fortress, where Halcyon will be given food, privacy, a room, and time before anyone asks for her whole story.

The final emotional note is not rescue. It is threshold.

Marcos has signed with the Blue Jays.
Halcyon has said yes to one night of safety.
Neither of them understands yet how much this will change their lives.

## What This Scene Establishes

1. Marcos meets Halcyon on the same day he signs with the Toronto Blue Jays.
2. Halcyon is living in Clarence Park when he meets her.
3. Their relationship begins with caution and recognition, not instant romance.
4. Marcos is drawn to her order, honesty, and refusal to flatter him.
5. Halcyon has agency and boundaries from the beginning.
6. Marcos's new money and professional status create ethical/emotional complexity rather than easy solutions.
7. Marcos buys Halcyon pizza as his first concrete act of care.
8. Marcos tells Halcyon he signed with the Toronto Blue Jays as a pitcher.
9. Marcos tells Halcyon his contract has a moral clause, using it as a factual reassurance rather than pressure.
10. Halcyon is very frightened to go off with Marcos, and the scene treats that fear as reasonable.
11. This scene becomes the foundation for Halcyon later becoming his girlfriend, with the relationship handled carefully and age-appropriately because she is seventeen.
12. Marcos brings Halcyon to the Carlisle mansion only after she chooses to go, but he does **not** call Rachel Darcy first, making the decision riskier and more impulsive.
