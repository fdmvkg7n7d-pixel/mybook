# KELLA NAKEISHA AND CHARLIE JANYA IN RENO — MITCH MACLEAD

## Scene Outline — Beats, Dialogue Cues, and Thematic Notes

**POV:** Kella Nakeisha and Charlie Janya (alternating)
**Tone:** Bright and ordinary at first — two cousins who want to get away from Las Vegas for a Saturday — then sharp, intrusive, and electric with possibility
**Location:** Reno, Nevada — a shopping district, stores, the street. Reno is about 30 minutes from Mound House (where the Moonlite BunnyRanch is located) and about 4 hours north of Las Vegas (where the cousins attend UNLV).
**Ages:** Kella Nakeisha and Charlie Janya are both ~18–20 (UNLV students).
**Time in saga:** ~2075–2077

## CONTENT NOTE

This scene is about **an encounter, a card, and a refusal** — not about sex work itself. The man is Mitch MacLead, who works for the Moonlite BunnyRanch. There is no specific approach — he sees them, he talks to them, he gives them his card. The cousins tell him to fuck off. They do not stop anywhere on the drive back to Las Vegas. They do not tell anyone else. The drama is in the refusal, the silence in the car, and the slow, unspoken rethinking that begins before they even reach their dormitory. The saga's handling standard applies: **no graphic content.** The focus is on the cousins' agency, their bond, and the question that follows them home.

## BEFORE THE SCENE — WHAT THE COUSINS CARRY INTO RENO

*Establish, briefly, the ordinary world of two identical-twin cousins who drove to Reno to shop.*

- Kella Nakeisha and Charlie Janya are at UNLV together. They are identical-twin cousins — each is an identical twin in her own family (Kella shares a face with Armella Beatrice; Charlie Janya shares a face with Rebecca Brazilia), but they do not share a face with each other. They are cousins, born the same year (~2057), who chose the same university.
- They want to get away from Las Vegas for a Saturday. That is the whole purpose — a drive north to Reno, stores, lunch, the ordinary pleasure of being young and together and somewhere that isn't campus. They are not looking for anything. They are not expecting anything.
- They do not know they are about to meet Mitch MacLead.

## BEAT 1 — THE SHOPPING

*Two cousins, a Saturday in Reno, the ordinary pleasure of being young and together.*

- Kella and Charlie Janya are shopping in Reno. They are trying things on, laughing, eating, doing what young women do on a day off from university.
- **Dialogue cue (Charlie Janya, holding up something):** "Would you wear this?"
- **Dialogue cue (Kella, laughing):** "Absolutely not. But you would."
- **Thematic note:** This is the last ordinary moment. The scene needs to establish the cousins' bond — their ease with each other, their humor, their confidence — before Mitch MacLead enters the frame. The reader needs to feel what is about to be interrupted.

## BEAT 2 — MITCH MACLEAD

*There is no specific approach. He sees them. He talks to them.*

- **Mitch MacLead.** Bald, green eyes, 210 pounds, 6'1". He works for the Moonlite BunnyRanch in Mound House. He is not lurking. He is not following them. He is not calculating. He is in Reno, they are in Reno, he sees them, and he talks to them. There is no specific approach — it is just a man who sees two young women and decides to say something.
- **His read:** Two young women. Beautiful. Confident. The type that does well at the Ranch. He thinks about the money they could make — for themselves, and for the Ranch. And he wants them. Both of them. At the same time. That is what Mitch MacLead wants — to sleep with both of them, together, at the same time. The business pitch is the vehicle. The desire is personal.
- **Dialogue cue (Mitch, easy, direct, not aggressive):** "I work at the BunnyRanch. You two would make a lot of money there."
- **The cousins' reaction:** They are not frightened. They are not flattered. They are Key women. They know what this is, and they know who they are.

**Thematic note:** Mitch is not a villain. He is not a predator. He is a man who works at the BunnyRanch, who sees an opportunity, and who says it out loud. He is bald, green-eyed, 210 pounds, 6'1" — a physical presence, but not a threatening one. The scene should not make him a monster. It should make him a person who made a calculation about two women's bodies and their potential earnings, and who presented that calculation to them as if it were a fact.

## BEAT 3 — THE REFUSAL AND THE CARD

*The cousins tell him to fuck off. He gives them his business card.*

- Kella and Charlie Janya do not hesitate. They do not look at each other for permission. They do not soften it. They are Key women. They tell him to fuck off.
- **Dialogue cue (Kella, flat, not angry, not amused):** "Fuck off."
- **Dialogue cue (Charlie Janya, equally flat):** "What she said."
- **Mitch's response:** He does not argue. He does not push. He hands them his business card — his name, the Ranch's name, a phone number — and he walks away. He knows better than to push. He also knows that sometimes women call.
- **Dialogue cue (Mitch, handing over the card, leaving):** "Think about it."
- **The cousins take the card.** They do not throw it away. They do not tear it up. Charlie Janya puts it in her wallet. They watch him walk away. They do not look at each other yet. They are processing.

**Thematic note:** The refusal is clean. The card is taken. These two facts coexist: the cousins said no, and the card is now in their possession. The card is the thing that follows them home. It is the physical object that sits in the room with them later, in the dormitory, in the dark.

## BEAT 4 — THE DRIVE BACK

*They do not stop anywhere. They do not tell anyone else.*

- The cousins finish their shopping — or they don't. They get in the car. They drive back to Las Vegas. **They do not stop anywhere** — no gas station, no drive-through, no rest stop, nowhere. They drive straight back.
- **They do not tell anyone else.** Not on the drive, not when they get back, not to their roommates, not to their families, not to anyone. This is between the two of them. The only people who know what happened in Reno are Kella and Charlie Janya.
- **In the car:** They do not talk about Mitch on the way home — or they talk around it, or they joke about it, or they are quiet. The card is in Charlie Janya's wallet, in the car with them.
- **Dialogue cue (Charlie Janya, in the car, looking out the window):** "He wasn't wrong about the money."
- **Dialogue cue (Kella, driving, eyes on the road):** "I know."

**Thematic note:** The drive back is the bridge between the ordinary day and the private rethinking. The cousins do not stop anywhere because they do not want to be in the world right now — they want to be in their room, in the dark, where they can think. They do not tell anyone else because this is not yet something that belongs to anyone else. It belongs to the two of them, and to the card, and to the dark.
