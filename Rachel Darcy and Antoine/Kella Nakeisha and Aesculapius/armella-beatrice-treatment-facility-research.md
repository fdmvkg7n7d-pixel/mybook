# ARMELLA BEATRICE — THE TREATMENT ARC

## Story Development Notes

---

## THE ARC IN BRIEF

At **fifteen**, Armella Beatrice Key-d'Aboville does something impulsive, careless, and reckless — something she does not think through, something that causes real harm to the people she loves most. She is with **Todd Benstin**, her identical twin Kella Nakeisha's boyfriend, who believes he is with Kella. The twins share a face; Todd cannot tell them apart. Armella does not correct him. She does not stop. She does not think.

**Kella catches them.** She walks in. She sees it with her own eyes. She is very emotional, and she very quickly breaks up with Todd. Todd is very confused. Rachel Darcy is pretty angry — because Armella Beatrice is not thinking.

Rachel calls **Jessica Isabella** — her identical twin, her conjoined twin, the sister who donated her eggs so that Rachel could be a mother, the biological mother of both the child who caused the harm and the child who was harmed. Jessica is a neurosurgeon in Halifax. She understands twin dynamics, adolescent brain development, and the weight of what has happened.

The family decides: **Armella will go to residential treatment outside Los Angeles.** Not as punishment — as therapy. To give Kella space. To give Rachel a break from managing both twins in the same house. To allow Armella to focus on treatment away from the environment where the incident occurred. The evidence-based treatment model is **PSB-CBT™** (Problematic Sexual Behavior – Cognitive Behavioral Therapy), which has a **2% recidivism rate at 10 years** for youth who complete treatment.

**Armando Cortez Junior** — the eldest son, ~21, the brother with his own planned dark arc — intersects with this storyline. How, exactly, the author will decide.

The saga's handling standard applies throughout: **harm kept entirely off-page and non-graphic.** The focus is the family **noticing, believing, protecting, and responding** — and the saga's intergenerational question: *does the family repeat a pattern, or does the family break the chain on purpose?*

## THE SCHOOL — VIEWPOINT SCHOOL, CALABASAS

**Armella, Kella, and Todd all attend Viewpoint School** — an independent, coeducational, college-preparatory day school in Calabasas, California (K–12; ~1,200 students; 7:1 student-teacher ratio; 80-acre campus on Mulholland Highway; founded 1961). Viewpoint is the social world where the twins and Todd know each other, where Kella and Todd's relationship began, where the social dynamics of the incident will have consequences.

**For Kella:** Viewpoint is where she pursues theater and performing arts — the stage at school is an extension of the stage at River Way Ranch Camp. It is where she is seen, where she is recognized, where she is *herself.* After the incident, Viewpoint becomes a place where she must navigate the social fallout — classmates who may know, whispers in the hallway, the question of whether Todd is still at the same school.

**For Armella:** Viewpoint is where she pursues photography and the visual arts — she is the one behind the camera, the one who observes rather than performs. After the incident, Viewpoint becomes a place she must leave (for residential treatment) and later return to — a place where her absence will be noticed, where her return will be complicated.

**For Todd:** Viewpoint is where he met Kella, where their relationship began, where he is now the boy who was with the wrong twin. He is confused, ashamed, and still at the same school as Kella — or perhaps his family moves him to a different school. The author will decide.

**The school's role in the story:** Viewpoint is not just a backdrop — it is the social ecosystem where the incident has consequences beyond the family. Classmates, teachers, counselors, the school's response to the crisis — all of this is part of the story. Viewpoint's emphasis on character development (*"respect, integrity, responsibility, and optimism"*) creates a specific context for how the school might respond to what happened.

## THE INCIDENT

Armella Beatrice is with Todd Benstin. Todd believes he is with Kella Nakeisha — his girlfriend, Armella's identical twin. The twins share a face. Todd cannot tell them apart.

Armella knows this — or she should know this. She does not correct him. She does not stop. She does not think.

This is not premeditation. This is not malice. This is not a calculated deception. This is a fifteen-year-old acting **impulsively, carelessly, recklessly** — in the moment, failing to consider consequences, failing to hold other people's feelings in mind, failing to see what she is doing and what it will cost.

Rachel Darcy's characterization is exactly right: *Armella Beatrice is not thinking.*

The saga's handling standard: **the incident itself is kept entirely off-page and non-graphic.** What the reader sees is the aftermath — the discovery, the family's response, the treatment, the healing. What happened is known to the author and to the characters. It is not shown.

## THE DISCOVERY — KELLA CAUGHT THEM

Kella walks in. She sees it with her own eyes.

This is not secondhand knowledge. This is not a rumor. This is not a confession after the fact. Kella **witnessed the betrayal in real time.** She saw her boyfriend with her identical twin. She saw the face she shares being used — or confused, or not corrected. She saw the moment of betrayal as it occurred.

Kella is very emotional. She quickly breaks up with Todd. There is no deliberation. There is no *"let me think about this."* There is no attempt to work it out. Kella is done. She is betrayed — romantically, familially, identity-based — and she ends it on the spot.

Todd is very confused. He did not know — or he did not realize until Kella walked in and her reaction told him everything. He is fifteen. He liked a girl. He could not tell her apart from her identical twin. Something happened. Now he does not know what he felt, who he was with, or who he is.

Armella is caught by her twin — the person she most resembles, the person whose face she shares, the person she betrayed. There is no denying it. There is no explaining it away. Kella saw. Kella knows.

*The discovery scene is a fulcrum. Everything before it leads to this moment. Everything after it flows from this moment. The author will write it with care, with precision, and with the saga's standard: off-page, non-graphic, but emotionally true.*

## THE THREE

### Kella Nakeisha — Very Betrayed

Kella feels **very betrayed.** This is not confusion, not guilt, not uncertainty. It is a clean, sharp, specific wound: she knows what happened, she knows who did it, and she knows she was wronged.

She has clarity — unlike Todd (confused) and unlike Armella (impulsive). Kella does not need to be convinced that what happened was wrong. She needs to be believed, protected, and supported.

She needs her own therapist — not the same clinician treating Armella. Her therapy is about betrayal, identity, trust, and rebuilding. She did nothing wrong.

She needs the family to choose her — at least in this moment. The Key family's principle is *"no one does anything alone,"* but in this crisis, one sister harmed the other. Rachel must hold both of them, but Kella needs to feel, unequivocally, that her mother is on her side.

She needs a new mode of differentiation — something that makes Kella unmistakably Kella. The saga's twin motif demands that Kella find a way to be *seen* as herself, not as half of a pair, not as the face that was confused.

**Kella's wounds:**

- **Romantic betrayal:** Her boyfriend was with her twin.
- **Familial betrayal:** Her twin was with her boyfriend.
- **Identity wound:** Her face — the face she shares with Armella — was the instrument of this betrayal. Kella, who chose theater (being seen, being recognized, being *herself* on stage), was not recognized.
- **Camp separation revisited:** At River Way, they learned to be individuals. Now it feels like it wasn't enough.
- **Theater vs. Photography:** The boyfriend *looked* at Armella and *saw* Kella. Kella, the one who performs, who stands in the light — was not recognized. Her twin, the one who hides behind the lens, was mistaken for her. The irony is devastating, and Kella feels it as betrayal, not as poetry.

### Todd Benstin — Very Confused

Todd Benstin is **very confused.** He is not angry, not certain, not accusatory. He is lost.

His confusion operates on multiple levels:

- **Perceptual:** He literally could not tell them apart. Two people share a face, and he trusted the face.
- **Emotional:** Was what he felt for *"Kella"* actually what he felt? If he couldn't tell them apart, does he even know who he's attracted to? Is attraction to a face the same as attraction to a person?
- **Moral:** Did he do something wrong? He was with his girlfriend — or he thought he was.
- **Relational:** Can he look at Kella the same way? Can he look at Armella? Can he be in the same room as either of them?

Todd is not simply a victim and not simply a perpetrator. He is a confused fifteen-year-old caught in a situation the saga's twin motif was practically designed to produce.

Kella broke up with him quickly. He did not have a chance to explain. He is now single, confused, ashamed, and cut off from the person he thought he was in a relationship with. He may benefit from his own counseling — not PSB-specific, but adolescent therapy focused on identity, relationships, and processing an experience that doesn't have a clean category.

*His confusion deserves gentleness — from the family, from the narrative, from the author.*

### Armella Beatrice — Impulsive, Careless, Reckless

Armella Beatrice was **not thinking.** She was in the moment. She failed to consider consequences. She did not plan this. She did not set out to hurt Kella. She was impulsive, careless, reckless — and she caused real harm.

**What this is not:**

- Not premeditation. Not malice. Not predation. Not identity theft.

**What this is:**

- A failure of judgment. A failure of impulse control. A failure of care. A failure of risk assessment.
- Accountability — she still did it, even if she wasn't thinking.
- Harm — Kella was betrayed. Todd was confused. The family is in crisis.
- Treatable — impulsivity, carelessness, and recklessness are all things a fifteen-year-old can learn to manage.

Armella is the photographer — the one who sees, the one behind the camera. And yet, in this moment, she failed to see. She failed to observe the consequences of her actions. This is a profound irony, and it is also a profound opportunity for growth: Armella is learning to see — not just through a lens, but through empathy, accountability, and foresight.

## RACHEL DARCY'S RESPONSE

**Rachel is pretty angry.** Specifically, she is angry at Armella Beatrice, and the reason is specific: *Armella Beatrice is not thinking.*

This is a particular kind of parental anger. Rachel is not calling Armella evil. She is calling her thoughtless — impulsive, careless, reckless. This characterization suggests:

- Armella did not plan this.
- Armella was in the moment — she froze, or she was curious, or she wanted to be seen, or she simply didn't think.
- Armella failed to consider consequences — for Kella, for Todd, for herself, for the family.
- This is a failure of judgment, not malice.

Rachel's anger is protective of Kella — she is choosing her daughter who was betrayed. And it is protective of Armella — by framing this as *"not thinking"* rather than *"deliberate betrayal,"* Rachel is leaving room for Armella to learn, to grow, to be held accountable without being defined as a monster.

Rachel is a widowed mother of seven. She lost Antoine when the twins were ~9. She has held this family together through grief, through the challenges of raising multiples, through the complexities of identity. And now, at fifteen, one twin has harmed the other through the very face they share. Her anger is valid. Her response is protective. Her love for both daughters is unconditional.

Rachel calls **Jessica Isabella** — her identical twin, her conjoined twin, the sister who donated her eggs so that Rachel could be a mother. This is significant on multiple levels:

## THE TREATMENT — RESIDENTIAL, OUTSIDE LOS ANGELES

The family decides: **Armella will go to residential treatment outside the LA area.** Not as punishment — as therapy.

**Why outside LA:**

- Gives Kella necessary space — she does not have to see Armella at home while processing the betrayal
- Gives Rachel a break from managing both twins in the same house during the acute phase
- Allows Armella to focus on treatment away from the environment where the incident occurred
- Provides specialized, intensive care in a structured setting
- Consistent with the family's resources
- The Key family's principle of *"no one does anything alone"* is honored through regular family visits, family therapy sessions, and the family's involvement in treatment planning

**Timeline:** Residential treatment typically lasts **3–6 months** for adolescent PSB-CBT. Armella could return home by age 15.5 or 16, depending on progress.

**The departure** is a significant narrative moment — Armella leaving home, Rachel's anger mixed with love, Kella's relief mixed with complicated feelings, the triplets' confusion, Armella's own feelings about being sent away.

**The return** is another significant moment — reintegration, ongoing boundaries between the twins, the new normal.

### The Evidence Base — PSB-CBT™

The treatment model is **PSB-CBT™ (Problematic Sexual Behavior – Cognitive Behavioral Therapy)**, developed at the University of Oklahoma Health Sciences Center. It is the gold-standard evidence-based treatment for youth with PSB.

- **Ages:** 3–18 (grouped: preschool, school-age 7–12, adolescent 13–18)
- **Recidivism rate:** **2%** at 10-year follow-up for youth who complete treatment
- **Format:** Group + family therapy; typically 16–20 sessions for school-age, adapted for adolescents
- **Caregiver involvement:** Required — maximum benefits occur when the primary caregiver is actively engaged
- **Curriculum:** Empathy, critical thinking, decision-making, sex laws, sexual education, consent, rule-setting, healthy family dynamics, communication, sexual health

**For Armella specifically, treatment focuses on:**

1. **Impulse control** — learning to slow down, to pause, to think before acting
2. **Empathy development** — understanding what Kella felt, what Todd felt, what the family felt
3. **Accountability** — owning what she did, even though it wasn't planned
4. **Identity work** — understanding why she was in that moment, what she wanted, why she didn't think

A 2022 systematic review found that residential treatment for juveniles with PSB has **sexual recidivism rates averaging 5.20%** — comparable to outpatient programs. The comparable rates suggest that less restrictive interventions may be sufficient for most youth, but residential placement is appropriate when the home environment needs time to stabilize, as it does here.

## ARMANDO CORTEZ JUNIOR — THE INTERSECTION

**Armando Cortez Junior** — the eldest son, ~21, the second-born of the seven — has his own planned dark arc, and it **intersects with this storyline.**

His profile notes: *"A darker arc around age ~14 is planned but undefined."* And: *"The author is still deciding its nature and may connect it to the family's buried 'Historican' history."*

At ~21, when Armella's crisis unfolds, Armando is an adult with his own unresolved darkness. The intersection could take many forms:

**Recognition:** Armando may see himself in Armella — not in the specific behavior, but in the impulsivity, the carelessness, the recklessness, the failure to think. He may know what it's like to be the one who messed up, and he may be able to reach Armella in a way Rachel or the therapists cannot.

**Protection:** Armando may become protective of Armella — advocating for her, ensuring she's not treated as a monster, ensuring the family doesn't abandon her. His protection may be fierce, because he knows what it feels like to need protection.

**Complication:** Armando's own unresolved darkness may complicate the family's response. His presence during this crisis may trigger his own unhealed wounds, or may cause the family to see patterns they didn't see before.

**The intergenerational question:** Armando's intersection may reveal that Armella's behavior is not an isolated incident but part of a pattern — a pattern that runs through the family, that Armando has been carrying, that the family needs to address systemically. This connects to the saga's deepest question: *does the family repeat a pattern, or does the family break the chain on purpose?*

**The older brother:** Armando may visit Armella at the treatment facility. He may sit with Kella when she can't sleep. He may talk to Todd when Todd doesn't know who else to talk to. He may be the one who drives Armella to the airport, or the one who picks her up when she comes home.

**Narrative twins:** Armando and Armella share more than a family name. He is the child with a dark arc at ~14. She is the child with a crisis at 15. They may be mirrors for each other — two siblings whose arcs reflect and illuminate each other.

The specifics of Armando's dark arc and how it intersects are for the author to develop. This document notes the intersection as canonical.

## THE TWIN DIMENSION

Armella Beatrice's identical twin, **Kella Nakeisha**, is not just affected by this arc — she is **at the center of it.**

This is not a generic scenario. This is specific to the twin relationship and exploits the very thing the saga has been building: two girls who share a face, who have spent their lives learning to be seen as separate, who went to camp together but were deliberately not paired, who chose different majors (Kella: theater, the spotlight; Armella: photography, behind the camera).

Now that careful individuation has been weaponized — not by malice, but by impulsivity. The boyfriend could not tell them apart. The face they share, which the saga treats as both gift and burden, has become the instrument of harm.

The saga's twin motif deepens:

- **Jessica & Rachel** were conjoined and separated. One received the reproductive system; the other did not. The asymmetry was anatomical and irreversible. Now Armella & Kella face a different asymmetry: one has betrayed the other, and the betrayal is inseparable from the face they share.
- **Samira & Sahara** danced in Moscow and St. Petersburg — same art, different cities. Their separation was geographic and chosen. Armella & Kella's separation is now emotional and forced.
- **Brandi & Pandora** — Brandi is blind; Pandora is the photographer. One sees, one doesn't. Armella is the photographer; Kella is the performer. One sees, one is seen. The boyfriend looked at the one who sees and saw the one who is seen. The saga's visual/seeing motif collapses into confusion.

At River Way Ranch Camp, Kella and Armella learned to be separate — different majors, different pairings, different lives within the same camp. Armella was paired with Larissa Leola; Kella with Armando. Armella majored in theater/performing arts, ropes course, and photography. Kella majored in horseback riding (English), theater/performing arts, and waterskiing. They were in the same camp, the same session — but not paired with each other. That separation was a gift. Now it feels like it wasn't enough.

## THE FATHER'S DEATH — A CONTEXTUAL THREAD

Antoine d'Aboville died when Armella was ~9. At 15, she has been fatherless for roughly half her life. This does not cause what happened — the evidence base is clear that adolescent impulsivity and recklessness have multiple contributing factors and are not reducible to a single event. But the loss is part of Armella's story, and the saga treats grief as something that lives in the body long after the mind has processed it.

Armando was ~13 when Antoine died — one year before his own planned dark arc at ~14. Kella and Armella were ~9. Larissa was ~14. The grief fell differently on each of them, and it may still be falling.

The connection between the father's death and Armella's arc — whether causal, correlational, or simply concurrent — is for the author to decide.
