# RACHEL IS PRETTY ANGRY — FALLEN GLENDA DOESN'T CARE

**POV:** Rachel Darcy Key (primary), with Fallon Glenda Key (secondary)
**Tone:** Rachel: steel meeting grief. A mother's anger, a mother's fear, a mother's love. Fallon: calm, steady, unbothered — the woman who became a mother at thirteen and learned not to judge what women do with their bodies.
**Location:** [TBD — Rachel's home in Los Angeles; Fallon Glenda's home in the San Francisco Bay Area; or a shared space where the sisters talk. The scene could be a phone call, a visit, or a family gathering.]
**Ages:** Rachel Darcy is ~50–55. Fallon Glenda is ~50–55. Kella Nakeisha and Charlie Janya are ~18–20.
**Time in saga:** ~2075–2077 — after Kella and Charlie Janya have made their decision about the Bunny Ranch.

## CONTENT NOTE

This scene is about **two mothers responding to their daughters' choices.** Rachel is angry. Fallon is not. The drama is in the contrast — the two sisters, who grew up in the same house, who were raised by the same parents, who share the Key family's deepest values, and who respond to their daughters' choices in completely different ways. Neither response is wrong. Both responses are true. The saga's handling standard applies: **no graphic content.** The focus is on the mothers, their relationship, and the question of what it means to love a daughter who makes a choice you would not have made.

## BEFORE THE SCENE — WHAT EACH MOTHER CARRIES

*Establish, briefly, the weight each sister brings into this moment.*

**Rachel Darcy:**
- Rachel is a widowed mother of seven. She lost Antoine when the twins were ~9. She has held this family together through grief, through the Armella/Todd incident, through the complexities of raising multiples. She is already carrying a lot.
- Rachel is a dancer. She built her family through her sister's egg gift and surrogacy. She understands the body — her own body, the body she shares (or shared) with Jessica, the bodies of her children.
- Rachel is pretty angry. She is angry because Kella is her daughter, and Kella has made a choice that Rachel does not understand, and Rachel's anger is the shape of her love and her fear.

**Fallon Glenda:**
- Fallon Glenda is a physician. She became a mother at thirteen — the hardest possible beginning — and she chose Charlie, and she built a life that was not the life anyone expected.
- Fallon Glenda knows what it is to make a choice that the world does not understand. She knows what it is to have a body that the world has opinions about. She knows what it is to be a young woman making a decision that changes everything.
- Fallon Glenda doesn't care. She is not angry. She is not surprised. She is Fallon Glenda, who has already lived through the thing that Rachel is afraid of, and who came out the other side with her daughter and her partner and her life intact.

## BEAT 1 — RACHEL'S ANGER

*Rachel tells Fallon what Kella has decided. Rachel is pretty angry.*

- Rachel and Fallon are talking — on the phone, in person, at a family gathering. Rachel tells Fallon what Kella has decided. Rachel's anger is specific, and it is not just about the Bunny Ranch.
- **Dialogue cue (Rachel, to Fallon):** "She's going to work at the Bunny Ranch. My daughter. At the Bunny Ranch. I am pretty angry, Fallon. I am pretty angry."
- **Rachel's interior:** She is thinking about Kella — the child who was born through Jessica's egg gift, the child she carried in her heart if not in her body, the child who is now making a choice that Rachel cannot reconcile with the life she imagined for her. She is thinking about what people will say. She is thinking about what it means. She is thinking about whether she failed.
- **Rachel's anger is not just about the Ranch.** It is about the loss of the life she imagined. It is about the fear that Kella is making a mistake. It is about the fact that Rachel has already lost so much — Antoine, the ordinary life, the family she thought she was building — and now her daughter is choosing something Rachel cannot protect her from.

**Thematic note:** Rachel's anger is the anger of a mother who loves her daughter and who does not understand her daughter's choice. It is not the anger of a mother who is ashamed. It is the anger of a mother who is afraid. The scene should honor that distinction.

## BEAT 2 — FALLEN GLENDA'S RESPONSE

*Fallon doesn't care.*

- Fallon listens to Rachel. She does not interrupt. She does not offer advice. She lets Rachel finish. And then she says the thing that Rachel needs to hear and does not want to hear.
- **Dialogue cue (Fallon Glenda, calm, steady):** "I don't care."
- **Rachel's reaction:** Rachel is not expecting this. She is expecting Fallon to agree with her, to share her anger, to validate her fear. Fallon does none of these things.
- **Dialogue cue (Rachel, not believing it):** "You don't care? Fallon, Charlie Janya is going to work at the Bunny Ranch. Your daughter. You don't care?"
- **Dialogue cue (Fallon Glenda):** "I care about Charlie Janya. I care about whether she is safe, whether she is choosing this for herself, whether she knows she can come home. I don't care about the Bunny Ranch. I was thirteen when I had Lÿsa. The world had a lot to say about that, too. I'm not going to be the world for my daughter."

**Thematic note:** Fallon's response is the response of a woman who has already lived through the thing Rachel is afraid of. Fallon was thirteen. She was a mother. The world had opinions. Fallon made her choices anyway. She is not going to be the world for her daughter. This is not indifference — it is the deepest form of love: *I trust you to choose, and I will be here no matter what you choose.*

## BEAT 3 — THE CONTRAST

*Two sisters, two mothers, two responses.*

- Rachel and Fallon are sisters. They grew up in the same house. They were raised by the same parents — Mike and Terri Anne, who took in Emma, who fostered Klára, who held the family together through everything. They share the Key family's deepest values: *no one does anything alone. The family notices, believes, protects, and responds.*
- But they are also different women, with different lives, different losses, and different daughters. Rachel is a widowed mother of seven, holding everything together. Fallon is a physician, a mother of four, a woman who chose her partner and built a life that was not expected.
- **Dialogue cue (Rachel, quieter now):** "I just don't want her to get hurt."
- **Dialogue cue (Fallon Glenda):** "I know. I don't want Charlie Janya to get hurt either. But she's a Key woman. She'll choose, and she'll be okay, and we'll be here."

**Thematic note:** The scene should not resolve the contrast. Rachel's anger is valid. Fallon's acceptance is valid. They are both loving their daughters the best they can, from the lives they have lived, with the tools they have. The saga's recurring theme — that the Key family holds difference without breaking — is fully present here.

## BEAT 4 — THE FORWARD HOOK

*The conversation ends. The mothers return to their daughters.*

- Rachel and Fallon end the conversation. They will talk again. They will see each other at family gatherings. They will hold their daughters the way they know how.
- **Rachel's forward hook:** Rachel will talk to Kella. She will be angry, and she will be honest, and she will love her daughter. She will not understand, but she will not abandon.
- **Fallon's forward hook:** Fallon will talk to Charlie Janya. She will ask the only question that matters: *Is this what you want?* And she will listen to the answer.

**End image:** Two sisters, two mothers, two phones (or two rooms, or two chairs at a family gathering), each holding her daughter's choice in her own way. Rachel is pretty angry. Fallon doesn't care. Both of them love their daughters more than anything in the world.
