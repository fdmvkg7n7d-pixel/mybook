# JESSICA ISABELLA AND EMMA — AFTER THE CALL

## Scene Outline — Beats, Dialogue Cues, and Thematic Notes

**POV:** Jessica Isabella Key and Emma (alternating; the two of them alone)
**Tone:** Quiet. Private. Grief meeting love. The conversation two partners have after they hang up the iPad and look at each other and the tears come.
**Location:** The **kitchen nook** of Jessica and Emma's home in **Halifax, Nova Scotia.** The iPad is off. The call with Rachel is over. The two of them are alone in the nook.
**Ages:** Jessica Isabella is **~48.** Emma is **~48.**
**Time in saga:** ~2072 — immediately after the iPad call with Rachel.

---

## CONTENT NOTE

This scene is the **private conversation** — Jessica and Emma, alone in their kitchen nook in Halifax, processing what Rachel just told them. The incident is named between them, plainly, without graphic detail. The drama is in the relationship: Jessica as the egg donor, the woman whose eggs made both Kella and Armella possible, the woman who is not their mother but who loves them; and Emma as Jessica's wife, the co-mother of Samira and Sahara (living in Russia) and Eliot David and Kalvin LaMarcus (sixteen, identical twins, at home or nearby). Both women cry. The saga's handling standard applies: **off-page, non-graphic, but emotionally true.**

## BEFORE THE SCENE — WHAT JESSICA AND EMMA CARRY INTO THE CONVERSATION

*Establish the state they are in when the iPad goes dark.*

- Jessica and Emma have just finished the iPad call with Rachel. They have heard what happened. They have offered their support. They have told Rachel they are here — day or night, on iPad, whenever she needs them. The call is over.
- The iPad is off. The screen is dark. The two of them are alone in their kitchen nook in Halifax, and the weight of what they just heard is settling in.
- **Jessica's interior:** She is a neurosurgeon. She is also Rachel's identical twin — her conjoined twin, the sister she was joined to at birth, the sister who gave Rachel the eggs that made Kella and Armella possible. She knows these children are not hers. She has always known. But they came from her eggs, and she loves them, and one of them has hurt the other, and she is sitting in her kitchen nook in Halifax and she cannot hold them.
- **Emma's interior:** She is Jessica's wife. She is also the co-mother of Samira and Sahara — their daughters, who are living in Russia, far from home. She is also the co-mother of Eliot David and Kalvin LaMarcus — their sixteen-year-old identical twins. She is also Patti Anne's paternal half-sister (both fathered by Randy Fritz). She is part of this family's most intimate bonds, and she is thinking about Kella and Armella — two fifteen-year-old girls who lost their father when they were nine, and who are now living through this — and she feels sorry for them.

## BEAT 1 — THE IPAD GOES DARK

*The call ends. The two of them are alone in the kitchen nook. The tears come.*

- Jessica puts the iPad down. Or Emma puts the iPad down. Or one of them closes the cover. The screen goes dark. The call is over.
- For a moment, neither of them speaks. They are sitting together in the kitchen nook — the small, warm space where they have their coffee in the morning, where they have their quiet conversations, where they have lived their life together — and they are processing.
- **And then the tears come.** Both of them. Not one at a time — both of them, at the same time, the way tears come when two people who love each other have just heard something terrible and there is no one else in the room to hold it together for.
- **Jessica cries.** The neurosurgeon, the woman who holds herself together in operating rooms, the woman who told Rachel that Armella's brain is not broken but unfinished — she cries. She cries because the eggs were hers. She cries because the children are Rachel's. She cries because she is in Halifax and they are in Los Angeles and she cannot hold them.
- **Emma cries.** Jessica's wife, the co-mother of four children, the woman who is always steady, the woman who holds the family together — she cries. She cries because she is thinking about Kella and Armella — two fifteen-year-old girls who lost their father when they were nine, who are now living through this betrayal, who are sitting in a kitchen in Los Angeles holding Bluey plush dolls that nine-year-old sisters gave them — and Emma feels sorry for them. She feels sorry for them in a way that is deep and specific and true.
- **They hold each other.** In the kitchen nook. In Halifax. With the iPad dark and the tears coming. They hold each other because that is what they do. That is what they have always done.

**Thematic note:** The tears are not weakness. The tears are the thing that happens when two people who love each other have just heard something terrible and there is no one else in the room. Jessica and Emma cry together in their kitchen nook, and the crying is the first honest thing that happens after the call. It is the body saying what the mind has not yet processed.

## BEAT 2 — JESSICA PROCESSES

*Jessica says what she is thinking. Emma listens.*

- Jessica speaks first. She is a neurosurgeon — she processes by naming things, by making them clinical, by turning feelings into facts. But she is also Rachel's twin, and the egg donor, and the woman who loves two children who are not hers. And she has just been crying, and the clinical distance is not working the way it usually does.
- **Dialogue cue (Jessica, her voice still wet):** "Both of them are Rachel's. I know that. I have always known that. But they came from my eggs, Emma. Kella came from my eggs. Armella came from my eggs. And one of them hurt the other, and I am sitting here in Halifax and I cannot —"
- **Emma's response:** Emma does not interrupt. She lets Jessica speak. She lets the neurosurgeon be a person for a moment. She lets the egg donor be a woman who loves two children who are not hers.
- **Dialogue cue (Jessica, continuing):** "I told Rachel her brain is not broken. I told her Armella's brain is unfinished. And I believe that. I do. But I also — I also feel it, Emma. I feel it in my body. The eggs were mine. The children are Rachel's. But I feel it."
- **Emma's response:** Emma reaches for Jessica's hand. She holds it. She is present. That is what she has.

**Thematic note:** Jessica's processing is the processing of a woman who occupies a unique position in this family. She is the egg donor — not the mother, not the aunt, not the stranger. She gave the eggs as a gift. The children are Rachel's. But the eggs were hers, and she feels the weight of that. Emma is the only person in the world who can hold this with her.

## BEAT 3 — EMMA SPEAKS

*Emma says what she is thinking. She is thinking about Kella and Armella, and she is thinking about Antoine.*

- Emma speaks. She is not a physician. She is not a twin. She is Jessica's wife, and she is the co-mother of four children, and she is the woman who has held this family's most intimate bonds alongside Jessica for decades. And she is thinking about Kella and Armella — and she is thinking about Antoine.
- **Dialogue cue (Emma, her voice still wet):** "I feel so sorry for them, Jessica. Kella and Armella. They lost Antoine when they were nine. Nine years old. And now this. At fifteen. They have been carrying grief for six years, and now this is on top of it, and I just — I feel so sorry for them."
- **Jessica's response:** Jessica hears this. She knows Emma is right. Kella and Armella lost their father when they were nine. They have been fatherless for six years. And now, at fifteen, one of them has hurt the other, and the family is in crisis, and the grief they have been carrying since childhood is sitting in the room with everything else.
- **Dialogue cue (Jessica, quietly):** "I know. I know you do. I do too."
- **Emma's interior:** She is thinking about what it means to lose a father at nine. She is thinking about what it means to be fifteen and fatherless and living through a betrayal like this. She is thinking about Kella — the twin who was betrayed, who walked into a room and saw her boyfriend with her face — and she is thinking about Armella — the twin who caused the harm, who was not thinking, who is now sitting in a kitchen holding a Bluey plush doll that a nine-year-old gave her. Emma feels sorry for both of them. She does not choose one over the other. She feels sorry for both of them, because both of them are fifteen-year-old girls who lost their father when they were nine, and both of them deserve to be held.

**Thematic note:** Emma's grief for Kella and Armella is specific. It is not the grief of the egg donor (that is Jessica's). It is the grief of a woman who sees two children — two girls, fifteen years old, fatherless since nine — and who feels sorry for them in a way that is deep and specific and true. Emma does not know Kella and Armella the way Rachel knows them. She is not their mother. But she loves them, and she feels sorry for them, and that love and that sorrow are real.

## BEAT 4 — THEIR CHILDREN — THEY DO NOT TELL THEM

*They talk about their children — and they decide not to tell them. This is not their children's crisis to carry.*

- Jessica and Emma talk about their children. They have four: **Samira Chaka** and **Sahara Hannan** — their daughters, identical twins, who are **living in Russia** (Samira at the Bolshoi Ballet Academy in Moscow, Sahara at the Vaganova Ballet Academy in St. Petersburg). And **Eliot David** and **Kalvin LaMarcus** — their sons, identical twins, **sixteen years old**, conceived via Jessica's eggs and a known donor (Dr. Rémi Arsenault), who are at home or nearby.
- **The question:** Do they tell them? Do they call Samira and Sahara in Russia? Do they sit down with Eliot David and Kalvin LaMarcus at home? Do they tell their children that something has happened between their cousins?
- **Dialogue cue (Emma, thinking out loud):** "Do we tell them?"
- **Dialogue cue (Jessica, after a pause):** "No. I don't think so."
- **Emma's interior:** She thinks about it. She thinks about Samira and Sahara, far away in Russia, focused on their ballet, living their own lives. She thinks about Eliot David and Kalvin LaMarcus, sixteen, identical twins, living their own lives. She thinks about what it would mean to call them and say *something happened between Kella and Armella* — and she thinks about what it would mean for them to hear that, to worry about their cousins from a distance where they can't do anything about it, to carry a burden that is not theirs to carry.
- **Dialogue cue (Emma, deciding):** "You're right. They're cousins. This is Rachel's family's crisis. It's not theirs. If they hear about it eventually, we'll tell them the truth at the level they can hold. But we don't initiate it. We don't put this on them."
- **Dialogue cue (Jessica):** "Agreed. Samira and Sahara are in Russia. They're dancing. They're living their lives. Eliot David and Kalvin LaMarcus are sixteen. They're identical twins — but their twin relationship is their own. They don't need to process what happened between Kella and Armella through the lens of their own twinhood. That is not their story."
- **Emma's interior:** She thinks about her own father — Randy Fritz, who disowned her at sixteen because of who she loved. She has moved past that. She is over it. She does not need to process intergenerational patterns through her children. She has already done that work. The question of whether the family repeats or breaks the chain is not Emma's question anymore — it's Rachel's, it's Kella's, it's Armella's. Emma's children do not need to carry that.
- **Dialogue cue (Emma, quietly):** "We hold this. Just the two of us. We hold it for Rachel, and we hold it for ourselves. And we do not put it on our children."
- **Dialogue cue (Jessica):** "Just the two of us."

**Thematic note:** Jessica and Emma decide not to tell their children. This is not secrecy — it is protection. Samira, Sahara, Eliot David, and Kalvin LaMarcus are Kella and Armella's cousins — not siblings, not half-siblings in any social sense. This is Rachel's family's crisis, not theirs. Telling cousins about a crisis in another household is not protective — it is an unnecessary burden. The Key family's principle of *"no one does anything alone"* applies to the people who are directly involved — Rachel, Kella, Armella, the triplets, the immediate family. Jessica and Emma are part of the support network. Their children are not. And Emma — who has already broken her own chain, who is over her father disowning her — does not need to process intergenerational patterns through her children. She holds this with Jessica. Just the two of them. That is enough.

## BEAT 5 — THE TWO OF THEM

*The conversation ends. They are still partners. They are still here. The tears have stopped.*

- The conversation is over. Jessica has processed. Emma has spoken. They have talked about Rachel, about Kella, about Armella, about Antoine's death, about Samira and Sahara in Russia, about Eliot David and Kalvin LaMarcus at home. They have cried together. They have made a plan.
- **Dialogue cue (Emma, quietly, the tears dried):** "We're going to be okay."
- **Dialogue cue (Jessica, quietly):** "We are. Rachel is going to be okay. Kella is going to be okay. Armella is going to be okay. It's going to take time. But they are Key women. They hold each other."
- **Dialogue cue (Emma):** "And we hold them."
- **Dialogue cue (Jessica):** "And we hold them."
- **End image:** The two of them, alone in their kitchen nook in Halifax, the iPad dark, the call over, the tears dried, the plan made. They are partners. They are here. They are going to call their daughters in Russia. They are going to sit down with their sixteen-year-old sons. They are going to stay available on iPad, day or night, whenever Rachel needs them. They are going to hold this family from a distance, the way they have always held it — with love, with honesty, with the quiet, steady presence of two women who chose each other and who choose this family, every day, without condition.
