# THE DEPARTURE — SAN FRANCISCO AND THE TREATMENT CENTER

## Scene Outline — Beats, Dialogue Cues, and Thematic Notes

**POV:** Primarily Rachel Darcy Key (with shifts to Kella Nakeisha, Armella Beatrice, Fallon Glenda, and the triplets)
**Tone:** Joy meeting grief. The triplets are happy — they see their cousins, their aunt, their uncle. They do not understand why they are here. Kella and Armella are very emotional — the twins break their silence, hug each other, and cry. Rachel holds everything.
**Location:** **San Francisco** — Fallon Glenda and Charlie's home. Then the **limousine** to **San Francisco International Airport (SFO)**. Then the flight to **Atlanta, Georgia**, where Armella will enter residential treatment at **Skyland Trail**.
**Ages:** Rachel Darcy is **~48.** Kella Nakeisha and Armella Beatrice are **~15.** The triplets (Melissa Lizette, Marcos Eduarte, Annabella Nicki) are **~9.** Fallon Glenda is **~39.** Charlie is **~39** (same school year as Fallon Glenda; Charlie's birthday is in February, Fallon Glenda's is in December). Fallon and Charlie's daughters: **Lÿsa Mandy** (~26), **Rebecca Brazilia** (~15), **Charlie Janya** (~15), **Calida Dawn** (~8).
**Time in saga:** ~2072 — after the family meeting, after the treatment facility has been chosen.

---

## CONTENT NOTE

This scene is the **departure** — Rachel driving her five children to San Francisco, leaving four of them with Fallon Glenda and Charlie, and then flying with the fifth (Armella) to **Skyland Trail** in **Atlanta, Georgia** for residential treatment. The drama is in the separation: the triplets' joy, the twins' tears, the hug that breaks the silence, the limousine, the women talking, the Giants game. The saga's handling standard applies: **off-page, non-graphic, but emotionally true.**

## BEFORE THE SCENE — THE DRIVE FROM LA TO SAN FRANCISCO

*Establish the drive — six hours, five children, one mother.*

- Rachel has packed the car. She has packed for five children — Kella, Armella, Melissa, Marcos, Annabella. She has packed clothes, toiletries, school books, the Bluey plush dolls (the ones Melissa and Annabella gave to Kella and Armella, the ones they are now sleeping with), Marcos's things, everyone's things.
- The drive from Los Angeles to San Francisco is about six hours. Five children in the car. One mother driving. The house they are leaving behind — the house where everything happened — is in the rearview mirror.
- **In the car:** The triplets are excited. They are going to see their cousins! They are going to see Aunt Fallon Glenda and Uncle Charlie! They do not totally understand why they are going to San Francisco — they know something happened, they know their mother told them something true at the level they could hold, but they do not understand the full weight of it. They are nine. They are excited.
- **Kella is quiet.** She is in the car, and she is quiet. She knows why they are going. She knows she is being left in San Francisco while Armella goes to treatment. She knows her mother is taking Armella and leaving her. She is not speaking to Armella — the twins have not spoken since the kitchen — but she is in the car with her, and the silence is loud.
- **Armella is quiet.** She is in the car, and she is quiet. She knows where she is going. She knows she is going to treatment. She knows she is leaving the house, leaving the triplets, leaving Kella. She is holding the Bluey plush doll that Annabella gave her. She is not speaking to Kella. She is not speaking to anyone.

## BEAT 1 — ARRIVING IN SAN FRANCISCO

*They arrive at Fallon Glenda and Charlie's home. The triplets are so happy.*

- Rachel pulls into the driveway. Fallon Glenda and Charlie's home in San Francisco. The door opens before Rachel has finished parking — Fallon Glenda is there, Charlie is there, the cousins are there.
- **The triplets explode with joy.** Melissa Lizette, Marcos Eduarte, and Annabella Nicki — nine years old, bursting out of the car, running toward their cousins, their aunt, their uncle. They are so happy. They do not totally understand why they are here, but they are here, and their cousins are here, and this is wonderful.
- **Dialogue cue (Melissa, running):** "Rebecca! Charlie Janya! We're here!"
- **Dialogue cue (Marcos, running):** "Uncle Charlie!"
- **Dialogue cue (Annabella, running, holding Calida Dawn's hand):** "Lÿsa! Look how big you are!"
- **Fallon Glenda welcomes them.** She is at the door, arms open, the physician and the sister and the mother who has said yes. She hugs the triplets. She hugs Kella. She hugs Armella. She hugs Rachel.
- **Charlie welcomes them.** He is steady, warm, present. He high-fives Marcos. He hugs the girls. He looks at Rachel and nods — the quiet acknowledgment of a man who knows what his sister-in-law is carrying.
- **The cousins:** Rebecca Brazilia and Charlie Janya — Fallon Glenda's identical twins, fifteen years old, the same age as Kella and Armella — are there. They are Kella and Armella's cousins, born the same year. They know something has happened (their parents have told them something, at the level they can hold, or they have not — the author will decide). They are here, and they are welcoming their cousins.
- **Lÿsa Mandy** — Fallon Glenda's eldest, ~26 — is there. She is the big cousin, the one who can help with the younger ones.
- **Calida Dawn** — Fallon Glenda and Charlie's fourth daughter, ~8 — is there. She is close to the triplets' age. She is excited to have playmates.

**Thematic note:** The triplets' joy is the first thing the reader sees in this scene. They are nine years old, and they are so happy to see their cousins, their aunt, their uncle. They do not totally understand why they are here, and they do not need to. They are happy. And that happiness is real, and it is good, and it is the thing that makes the separation that is coming feel even harder.

*The triplets run into the house. They are home — not their home, but a home.*

- The triplets run into the house. They are exploring, laughing, finding their rooms, finding their cousins, finding the toys and the food and the love that Fallon Glenda and Charlie have prepared for them.
- **Melissa and Annabella** find their room — they will share, the way they share a bedroom at home. The Bluey plush dolls are with them — or they are with Kella and Armella, depending on where the dolls are now. The author will decide.
- **Marcos** finds his room, or his space, or the couch, or wherever a nine-year-old boy settles in. He is happy. He is here.
- **The triplets do not totally understand why they are in San Francisco.** They know something happened. They know their mother told them something true. But they are nine, and they are with their cousins, and they are happy, and the full weight of why they are here is not theirs to carry. Not yet. Maybe not ever.

**Thematic note:** The triplets' happiness is not ignorance — it is childhood. They are nine years old, and they are in a house full of cousins and love and food and toys, and they are happy. The reason they are here is being carried by the adults. The triplets are being held. That is the point.

## BEAT 3 — THE TWINS BREAK THEIR SILENCE

*Kella and Armella are very emotional. They hug each other and start crying.*

- The triplets have run inside. The house is full of noise and joy and cousins. And Kella and Armella are standing outside — or in the driveway, or on the porch — and they are looking at each other.
- **The twins have not spoken since the kitchen.** They have not looked at each other. They have not acknowledged each other. The silence between them has been the loudest thing in every room they have been in. And now, standing outside Fallon Glenda's house in San Francisco, on the day that everything changes, the silence breaks.
- **Kella looks at Armella.** Armella looks at Kella. They see the face they share. They see the face that was used. They see the face that was betrayed. And they see each other — not the face, but the person behind it. The sister. The twin. The person they have shared everything with since birth.
- **They are very emotional.** Both of them. The emotion comes all at once — the grief, the anger, the love, the fear, the loss, the everything. They are fifteen years old, and they are about to be separated by two thousand miles, and they do not know when they will see each other again, and they do not know if things will ever be the same.
- **They hug.** Kella and Armella hug each other. They wrap their arms around each other — the twin who was betrayed and the twin who caused the betrayal — and they hold on. They hold on the way only twins can hold on, the way only two people who share a face and a history and a life can hold on.
- **They start crying.** Both of them. They cry into each other's shoulders. They cry the way fifteen-year-old girls cry when they are holding the person they love and the person they are angry at and the person they are losing all at the same time. They cry and they hold on and they do not let go.
- **Rachel watches.** Rachel watches her daughters hug each other for the first time since the discovery. She watches the silence break. She watches the tears come. She watches the two faces — the face that was used and the face that was used — pressed together, crying, holding on. Rachel does not speak. She does not need to. She is witnessing the first crack in the wall between them, and she is holding space for it.
- **Fallon Glenda watches.** Fallon Glenda watches her nieces — the twins, the fifteen-year-olds, the ones who have been through more than any fifteen-year-old should have to go through — and she watches them hold each other, and she feels the weight of it.

**Thematic note:** The hug is the emotional center of the entire scene. The twins have not spoken since the kitchen. They have not looked at each other. They have been separated by the worst thing that has ever happened between them. And now, at the moment of separation — the moment when Armella is leaving, when Kella is staying, when the two thousand miles are about to open between them — they break. They hug. They cry. They hold on. This is not forgiveness — not yet, not fully — but it is the first crack in the wall. It is the face they share, reminding them that they are still sisters, still twins, still connected, even when everything else is broken.

## BEAT 4 — KELLA HELPS ARMELLA TO THE LIMOUSINE

*Kella walks Armella to the limousine. Armella is crying. Kella is crying. They say goodbye.*

- The hug ends — or it does not end, not fully. Kella and Armella let go of each other, but they do not let go completely. Kella takes Armella's hand — or Armella takes Kella's hand — and they walk together toward the limousine.
- **The limousine** is waiting in the driveway. It is a black car, long and elegant — the kind of car the Key family uses, because the Key family can afford it, and because Rachel wants her daughter to travel to treatment in comfort and dignity, not in the back of a taxi. The driver is standing by the door, waiting.
- **Kella helps Armella to the limousine.** She walks her twin to the car. She opens the door. She helps her in. She is taking care of her sister — the sister who hurt her, the sister she is angry at, the sister she loves — because that is what twins do. That is what the face they share means. Even when everything is broken, you help your sister into the car.
- **Armella is crying.** She is holding the Bluey plush doll in one hand and she is crying. She gets into the limousine. She sits down. She looks up at Kella through her tears.
- **Kella is crying.** She is standing at the door of the limousine, looking at her twin, and she is crying. She does not say anything — or she says something, something small, something the author will decide.
- **Dialogue cue (Kella, quietly, to Armella):** "I love you. But I couldn't forgive you right now."
- **Dialogue cue (Armella, quietly, through tears):** "I know. I love you too."
- **Kella closes the door.** She closes the door of the limousine. She steps back. She watches the car. She is crying.

**Thematic note:** Kella helping Armella to the limousine is the act of a sister who has been betrayed and who is still a sister. She is angry. She is hurt. She is not speaking to her twin. And she is still helping her into the car. This is the Key family's deepest instinct — *no one does anything alone* — performed by a fifteen-year-old girl who is hurting and who is still showing up for her sister. The hug broke the silence. The walk to the limousine is the first step back.

## BEAT 5 — THE LIMOUSINE — RACHEL AND ARMELLA TALK

*Rachel and Armella are in the limousine. They talk. The silence breaks.*

- Rachel gets into the limousine. She sits next to Armella — or across from her, the author will decide. The driver closes the door. The limousine pulls out of the driveway. They are heading to San Francisco International Airport (SFO). They are heading to Atlanta.
- **Armella is crying.** She is holding the Bluey plush doll and she is crying. She is looking out the window — or she is not. She is fifteen years old and she is leaving her home, her siblings, her twin, her life, and she is going to a treatment center in Atlanta, and she is crying.
- **Rachel reaches for her daughter.** She puts her hand on Armella's hand — or her arm around Armella's shoulder — or she simply sits close. She is here. She is present. She is not leaving.
- **They talk.** The silence breaks — not the silence between the twins (that was broken by the hug), but the silence between Rachel and Armella. The silence that has been in the car since LA, since the confrontation, since the argument, since the crack. They talk.
- **Dialogue cue (Rachel, quietly, to Armella):** "I'm here. I'm not going anywhere. I'm going to be in the hotel down the street from Skyland Trail. I'm going to be there for every session, every call, every moment. You are not alone. Do you hear me?"
- **Dialogue cue (Armella, through tears):** "I hear you."
- **Dialogue cue (Rachel):** "And Kella — she helped you to the car. She hugged you. She's hurting too, Armella. She's hurting, and she still showed up for you. That's your sister. That's the face you share. Don't forget that."
- **Dialogue cue (Armella, quietly):** "I won't forget."
- **They talk more.** About the treatment. About what it will be like. About how long it will take. About the Bluey plush doll. About the triplets. About Kella. About the face they share. About the thing that happened and the thing that comes next. They talk the way a mother and daughter talk when they are alone in a car, driving to the airport, flying across the country, heading toward something hard and something hopeful.
- **The limousine arrives at SFO.** The driver opens the door. Rachel and Armella get out. They collect their bags. They walk into the airport. They are still talking — or they are quiet now, the quiet that comes after talking, the quiet that is comfortable instead of heavy.

**Thematic note:** The conversation in the limousine is the first real conversation between Rachel and Armella since the confrontation. It is not the argument. It is not the naming. It is not the crack. It is the conversation that comes after — the conversation where a mother tells her daughter *I am here, I am not leaving, you are not alone* and the daughter hears it and believes it. This is the conversation that Armella will carry with her into Skyland Trail. This is the conversation that will hold her when the treatment is hard and the days are long and the face she shares feels far away.

## BEAT 6 — KELLA GOES INSIDE

*Kella goes back into the house. She is crying. Fallon Glenda is there.*

- Kella is standing in the driveway. The limousine is gone. It has turned the corner. It has disappeared down the street. Armella is in the car, heading to the airport, heading to Atlanta, heading to treatment. Kella is standing in the driveway, and she is crying.
- **Fallon Glenda is beside her.** Fallon Glenda stands next to Kella — her arm around her, or her hand on her shoulder, or just beside her — and she watches the limousine go too.
- **Dialogue cue (Fallon Glenda, quietly, to Kella):** "Come inside. I've got you."
- **Kella goes inside.** She goes inside the house that is not her house, with the aunt who promised to hold her, with the cousins who are her age, with the triplets who are happy and do not understand. She goes inside, and she is crying, and the door closes behind her.
- **The triplets see her.** Melissa, Marcos, and Annabella see their sister Kella come inside, crying. They do not understand why she is crying. They know something is wrong. They are nine. They are confused. But they are also Key children, and they know that when someone in the family is crying, you go to them.
- **Melissa goes to Kella.** Or Annabella goes to Kella. Or both of them go. They go to their sister and they hug her — the way nine-year-olds hug, with their whole bodies, with no reservation, with the pure, uncomplicated love of children who do not understand the details but who understand the feeling.
- **Kella holds them.** She holds her little sisters, and she cries, and they hold her, and the house is full of love and confusion and the sound of nine-year-old voices saying *"It's okay, Kella, it's okay"* — even though it is not okay, and even though they do not know that.

**Thematic note:** Kella going inside is the moment the separation becomes real. The limousine is gone. Armella is gone. Rachel is gone. Kella is in a house that is not her house, with an aunt who is not her mother, with cousins who are not her siblings, with triplets who are happy and do not understand. And she is crying. And the triplets hold her. And the house holds her. And she is not alone.

## BEAT 7 — THE WOMEN TALK

*Fallon Glenda, Kella, Rebecca Brazilia, and Charlie Janya talk. They become very emotional.*

- Later — after the triplets have settled, after Charlie has taken them out, after the house is quieter — Fallon Glenda, Kella, Rebecca Brazilia, and Charlie Janya sit down together. The four of them. The aunt and the three fifteen-year-old girls.
- **Fallon Glenda** — the physician, the sister, the mother who promised to hold Kella.
- **Kella** — the twin who was betrayed, the fifteen-year-old who just watched her sister and her mother drive away in a limousine.
- **Rebecca Brazilia and Charlie Janya** — Fallon Glenda's identical twins, Kella's cousins, born the same year, the same age, the same face shared between them.
- **They talk.** They talk about what happened. They talk about Kella and Armella. They talk about the hug in the driveway. They talk about the face that Kella shares with Armella and the face that Rebecca Brazilia shares with Charlie Janya. They talk about what it means to be an identical twin when one twin hurts the other. They talk about what it means to watch your cousin go through something terrible and not be able to fix it.
- **They become very emotional.** All four of them. Fallon Glenda cries — the physician, the sister, the woman who holds everyone else, finally lets herself cry. Kella cries — she has been crying all day, and she cries more. Rebecca Brazilia and Charlie Janya cry — they are fifteen years old, and they are watching their cousin fall apart, and they are holding her, and they are feeling the weight of the face they share with each other and the face their cousin shares with her twin, and it is all too much, and they cry.
- **Dialogue cue (Kella, to Rebecca Brazilia and Charlie Janya):** "You don't know what it's like. To look at someone and see your own face and know that they did something terrible and they look exactly like you. You don't know what that's like."
- **Dialogue cue (Rebecca Brazilia, quietly):** "We know what it's like to share a face. We know what it's like to be identical. We don't know what you went through. But we know what it's like to look at someone and see yourself. And we're here. We're here for you."
- **Dialogue cue (Charlie Janya, quietly):** "We're not going anywhere. We're right here."
- **Fallon Glenda holds them all.** She holds her niece and her daughters, the three fifteen-year-old girls who are crying in her living room, and she holds them the way only an aunt can — not as a mother, not as a peer, but as the woman who is old enough to hold it and young enough to understand it.

**Thematic note:** This is the scene where the twin motif deepens. Kella shares a face with Armella. Rebecca Brazilia shares a face with Charlie Janya. Three fifteen-year-old girls, two sets of identical twins, sitting in a living room in San Francisco, talking about what it means to share a face when one face has been used and the other face has been betrayed. Fallon Glenda holds them all. She is the adult in the room. She is the aunt. She is the sister. She is the one who promised Rachel she would hold Kella, and she is holding her. And she is holding her own daughters too, because that is what mothers do.

## BEAT 8 — THE GIANTS GAME

*Charlie and Calida Dawn take the triplets to a San Francisco Giants game.*

- While the women are talking — while Fallon Glenda, Kella, Rebecca Brazilia, and Charlie Janya are in the living room, being emotional, holding each other — Charlie does what Charlie does. He takes the younger children out.
- **Charlie takes Melissa Lizette, Marcos Eduarte, and Annabella Nicki to a San Francisco Giants game.** The triplets. Nine years old. They do not understand why they are in San Francisco. They do not understand why Kella is crying. They do not understand why Armella and Rachel left in a limousine. But they understand baseball. They understand hot dogs. They understand Uncle Charlie and the Giants and a day at the ballpark.
- **Calida Dawn goes too.** Calida Dawn — Fallon Glenda and Charlie's fourth daughter, ~8, close to the triplets' age — goes with them. She is the playmate, the cousin, the girl who is close enough in age to be their friend and old enough to help Charlie manage three excited nine-year-olds at a baseball game.
- **The triplets are so happy.** They are at a Giants game! They are with Uncle Charlie! They are with Calida Dawn! They are eating hot dogs and drinking soda and cheering for the Giants and they are nine years old and they are happy and they do not understand why they are here and they do not need to.
- **Dialogue cue (Marcos, at the game, excited):** "Uncle Charlie! Look! A home run!"
- **Dialogue cue (Charlie, steady, warm):** "I see it, buddy. I see it."
- **Dialogue cue (Annabella, to Calida Dawn):** "This is the best day ever."
- **Dialogue cue (Calida Dawn, smiling):** "I know, right?"
- **Charlie watches the triplets.** He watches them cheer and eat and laugh and be nine years old at a baseball game, and he feels the weight of what is happening at home — the women talking, the tears, the grief — and he holds it, the way he always holds it. Quietly. Steadily. Without anyone asking him to. He is the man who chose Fallon Glenda and Lÿsa Mandy. He is the man who built a family. He is the man who takes three confused, happy nine-year-olds to a Giants game while the women in his life hold each other through something terrible. That is what he does. That is who he is.

**Thematic note:** The Giants game is the counterpoint to the women's conversation. While Fallon Glenda, Kella, Rebecca Brazilia, and Charlie Janya are in the living room, talking about betrayal and identity and the face they share, Charlie is at the ballpark with the triplets, eating hot dogs and watching baseball. This is not avoidance — this is care. Charlie knows that the triplets do not need to be in the room for that conversation. They need to be nine years old. They need to be at a Giants game. They need to be happy. And Charlie is the man who gives them that.

## BEAT 9 — RACHEL AND ARMELLA FLY TO ATLANTA

*Rachel and Armella are on the plane. They are flying to Atlanta. Skyland Trail is waiting.*

- Rachel and Armella are on the plane. They have boarded at SFO. They are flying to **Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta International Airport**. The flight is about four and a half hours. They are sitting together — Rachel in the window seat, Armella in the aisle, or the other way around, the author will decide.
- **Armella is holding the Bluey plush doll.** She is looking out the window — or she is not. She is fifteen. She is flying across the country to a treatment center she has never been to. She is leaving everything she knows. She is scared. She is sad. She is hopeful. She is all of those things at once.
- **Rachel is beside her.** Rachel is not leaving. Rachel is staying in a hotel near Skyland Trail. Rachel will be there for every session, every call, every moment. Rachel said *"you are not alone"* and she meant it.
- **They talk more.** Or they are quiet. Or they sleep. The author will decide. They are on a plane, flying across the country, and they are together, and that is what matters.
- **The plane lands in Atlanta.** Rachel and Armella get off the plane. They collect their bags. They go to the car — a rental, a taxi, a rideshare — and they drive to **Skyland Trail**, the treatment center. They arrive. They check in. Armella is admitted. Rachel meets the treatment team. She meets the clinicians. She meets the people who will hold her daughter for the next 12 weeks.
- **Rachel says goodbye to Armella — for now.** She is not leaving Armella. She is staying in a hotel nearby. She will be here for family therapy sessions. She will be here for phone coaching. She will be here. But Armella is going inside, and Rachel is going to the hotel, and they are separating — not for the first time today, but in a different way. This is the separation of treatment: the mother steps back so the clinicians can step in.
- **Dialogue cue (Rachel, to Armella, at the door of Skyland Trail):** "I am here. I am not going anywhere. I am in the hotel down the street. I will be here for every session, every call, every moment. You are not alone. Do you hear me?"
- **Dialogue cue (Armella, quietly):** "I hear you."
- **Armella goes inside.** She walks through the door of Skyland Trail, holding the Bluey plush doll, and the door closes behind her. Rachel stands outside for a moment. Then she walks to the car. She drives to the hotel. She checks in. She sits on the bed. And she cries — or she does not. The author will decide.

## END IMAGE

**Three cities. Three images. One family.**

- **San Francisco — Fallon Glenda's house:** Fallon Glenda, Kella, Rebecca Brazilia, and Charlie Janya are in the living room, holding each other, crying, talking about the face they share and the face that was used and the face that was betrayed. The triplets are at the Giants game with Charlie — wait, no, Charlie is at the game, so the women are alone. The triplets are at the game, happy, eating hot dogs, cheering for the Giants, not understanding why they are here and not needing to. Kella is held. The triplets are held. Fallon Glenda is holding everyone.

- **Atlanta — Skyland Trail:** Armella is walking through the door of a treatment center that might help. She is holding a Bluey plush doll that a nine-year-old gave her. She is fifteen years old. She is scared. She is hopeful. Her mother is in a hotel down the street. She is not alone.

- **Atlanta — the hotel:** Rachel is sitting on a hotel bed, alone, having left four children in San Francisco and admitted the fifth to treatment. She is holding the weight of everything. She is a widowed mother of seven. She is doing the hardest thing she has ever done. She is not alone — Fallon Glenda is holding four of them in San Francisco, Jessica and Emma are on iPad, the wider family is mobilizing — but right now, in this hotel room, it is just her. And she is holding it. And she is steady. And she will be here tomorrow, and the day after that, and every day until Armella comes home.

**The twins are separated by two thousand miles.** The face they share is in two different cities. And both of them are held — Kella by Fallon Glenda and the cousins and the triplets, Armella by the clinicians at Skyland Trail and her mother in the hotel down the street — and both of them are loved, and both of them are Key women, and neither of them is alone.
