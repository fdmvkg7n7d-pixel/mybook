**POV:** Multiple — Kella Nakeisha, Armella Beatrice, the triplets (Melissa Lizette, Marcos Eduarte, Annabella Nicki), and their cousins

## BEAT 1 — KELLA AT SCHOOL (VIEWPOINT, CALABASAS)

Rachel drives her — or Kella drives herself, or she takes a rideshare, the author will decide. She walks through the gates of Viewpoint, and she is back in her old life. The same hallways. The same classrooms. The same faces. The same life she had before the discovery.
- **Kella is trying to be normal.** She is trying to pay attention in class. She is trying to talk to her friends — her real friends, the friends she has had at Viewpoint for years, the friends who do not know what happened. She is trying to eat lunch and laugh at jokes and be a fifteen-year-old girl who is not carrying the weight of a betrayal that happened in her own bedroom. She is not succeeding.

They see Kella and they see the same Kella they have always seen. They do not know that she is a different person now. They do not know that she is carrying something that none of them can understand.
- **Kella is alone at Viewpoint.** Her cousins Rebecca Brazilia and Charlie Janya are in San Francisco. She is not with them during the school day. She is alone at Viewpoint, navigating the hallways, the classrooms, the faces of people who do not know what happened. She is alone with the face she shares, the face that is everywhere — in the mirror, in every reflection, in every photograph on the wall.
- **Dialogue cue (Friend, at lunch, casually):** "Hey Kella, where were you last week? We missed you."
- **Dialogue cue (Kella, not looking up):** "I was in San Francisco. Family stuff."
- **Dialogue cue (Friend):** "Cool. Everything okay?"

- **Kella's interior:** She is sitting in the cafeteria at Viewpoint, eating lunch, with her friends around her, and she is thinking about Armella. She is thinking about Armella in Atlanta, in a treatment center, in a school that is part of the treatment center, learning DBT skills, learning to think, learning to feel. She is thinking about the face they share, and how she sees it every time she looks in the mirror, every time she catches her reflection in a window, every time she walks past a photograph. She is thinking about what it means to be Kella without Armella. She does not know what that means. She has never been Kella without Armella. She is learning.

**Thematic note:** Kella at Viewpoint is Kella trying to be normal in the place where she used to be normal. She is fifteen, and she is carrying something that none of her friends understand, and she is alone with it, and the face she shares with Armella is everywhere — in the mirror, in every reflection, in every photograph on the wall. She is learning to be Kella without Armella. She does not know how. She is learning.

## BEAT 2 — ARMELLA AT SCHOOL (SKYLAND TRAIL)

*Armella goes to school at Skyland Trail. She is learning DBT skills. She is learning to think.*

- Armella goes to school. Not a regular school — the on-site accredited school at Skyland Trail, where the adolescent clients continue their education while in residential treatment. The school is fully accredited. The teachers are licensed. The curriculum is real. But it is also part of the treatment — the teachers are trained in DBT, and the classroom is an extension of the therapeutic environment.
- **Armella is learning DBT skills in the classroom.** She is learning mindfulness — the practice of being present, of noticing what she is feeling without acting on it. She is learning emotion regulation — the practice of managing her emotions, of not being controlled by them. She is learning distress tolerance — the practice of surviving hard moments without making them worse. She is learning interpersonal effectiveness — the practice of communicating clearly, of asking for what she needs, of saying no when she needs to say no.
- **Armella is also learning regular subjects.** She is learning math, English, science, history. She is keeping up with her schoolwork. She is not falling behind. Skyland Trail is designed to ensure that adolescents do not lose academic ground while in treatment.
- **Armella is in a classroom with other adolescents.** She is not the only one. There are other fifteen-year-olds in this classroom, other adolescents who are in residential treatment, other adolescents who are learning DBT skills, other adolescents who are trying to figure out how to think, how to feel, how to be.
- **Dialogue cue (Teacher, in class):** "Armella, can you tell me what mindfulness means? In your own words?"
- **Dialogue cue (Armella, thinking):** "It means... noticing. Noticing what I'm feeling without acting on it. Noticing what's happening without trying to change it. Just... being here."
- **Dialogue cue (Teacher):** "That's exactly right. Just being here. That's the hardest part, isn't it?"
- **Dialogue cue (Armella, quietly):** "Yeah. It is."
- **Armella's interior:** She is sitting in a classroom in Atlanta, learning about mindfulness, and she is thinking about Kella. She is thinking about the hug in the driveway. She is thinking about *"I love you but I couldn't forgive you right now."* She is thinking about what it means to be mindful of the harm she caused. She is thinking about what it means to sit with the guilt without acting on it, without deflecting it, without minimizing it. She is learning. It is hard. She is learning.

**Thematic note:** Armella at school is Armella learning the skills she needs to become a different person. Not a different face — she will always share Kella's face — but a different way of being in the world. DBT is teaching her to think before she acts, to feel before she deflects, to see the consequences before she ignores them. This is the work. This is the 12 weeks. This is the thing that might change her life.

**Thematic note:** The triplets at school are the triplets being children. They are nine. They are back in Los Angeles, in their school, with their friends, and they are happy. *Rachel calls the children. The family stays connected across the distance.*

- Rachel calls from Atlanta. She calls Kella. She calls the triplets. She calls Fallon Glenda. She stays connected — across the two thousand miles, across the 12 weeks, across the distance.
- **Rachel calls Kella.** They talk about school — Viewpoint, Calabasas, the ordinary things. They talk about homework, friends, the things that fifteen-year-old girls talk about with their mothers. They do not talk about Armella — or they do, carefully, gently, the way they talk about the thing that is always in the room.
  - **Dialogue cue (Rachel, on the phone):** "How was school today?"
  - **Dialogue cue (Kella):** "Fine. We had a test in history. I think I did okay."
  - **Dialogue cue (Rachel):** "I'm proud of you. You're doing really hard things, Kella. I'm proud of you."

- **Rachel calls the triplets.** They talk about school. They talk about their new friends. They talk about the Giants game. They talk about everything nine-year-olds talk about. They are happy. Rachel listens. Rachel holds the phone and listens to her youngest children be happy, and she holds the weight of why they are not at home, and she holds it steady.

**Thematic note:** The phone calls are the thread that holds the family together across the distance. Rachel is in Atlanta. Kella and the triplets are in San Francisco. This is what the Key family does. They hold each other. Even from two thousand miles away. Even through a phone. Even when everything is broken.
