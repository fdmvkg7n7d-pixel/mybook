# RACHEL CALLS JESSICA ISABELLA AND EMMA

## Scene Outline — Beats, Dialogue Cues, and Thematic Notes

**POV:** Primarily Rachel Darcy Key (with brief shifts to Jessica Isabella's and Emma's perception on the other end of the line)
**Tone:** Steel meeting love. A mother calling her twin — her conjoined twin, the sister who donated her eggs so that Rachel could be a mother — and her twin's wife. The call is practical and emotional at the same time.
**Location:** Rachel is at home in Los Angeles. Jessica Isabella and Emma are in Halifax (or wherever they are based — TBD). The call is by **iPad** — a video call. Jessica and Emma do not travel to Los Angeles. They stay where they are and speak with Rachel through the screen.
**Ages:** Rachel Darcy is **~48.** Jessica Isabella is **~48** (Rachel's identical twin). Emma is **~48.**
**Time in saga:** ~2072 — after Kella has told Rachel, after Rachel has confronted Armella, and after Kella is safe in Rachel's bedroom.

The drama is in the relationships: Rachel calling Jessica (her conjoined twin, the sister who donated her eggs so that Rachel could have these children), and Emma being on the line too (Jessica's wife, the co-mother of Samira and Sahara, the woman who is part of this family's most intimate bonds). The saga's handling standard applies: **off-page, non-graphic, but emotionally true.**

## BEFORE THE SCENE — WHAT RACHEL CARRIES INTO THE CALL

*Establish the weight Rachel brings into this conversation.*

- Rachel has just held Kella. She has just confronted Armella. She has just argued with her daughter and held her ground and heard the first honest thing (*"I wasn't thinking"*). She is angry, tired, and clear.
- Rachel is now calling Jessica Isabella — her identical twin, her conjoined twin, the sister who donated her eggs so that Rachel could be a mother. Jessica is the **egg donor** — the sister whose eggs made both of these children possible. But Jessica knows — clearly, without ambiguity — that Kella and Armella are **Rachel's children.** Not hers. Rachel is calling the woman who gave her the means to be a mother.
- Rachel is also calling Emma — Jessica's wife, the co-mother of Samira and Sahara. Emma is part of this family's most intimate bonds, and Rachel is including her in this call because this is a family crisis, not just a Rachel-and-Jessica conversation.

---

## BEAT 1 — THE CALL CONNECTS

*Rachel dials. Jessica and Emma answer.*

- Rachel calls. Jessica answers — or Emma answers — or they are together and one of them picks up and the other is immediately there. They are a couple. They answer as a unit.
- **Dialogue cue (Jessica, answering):** "Rachel?"
- **Dialogue cue (Rachel, steady):** "I need you both. Are you together?"
- **Dialogue cue (Jessica):** "Emma's here. What happened?"
- **Rachel's interior:** She hears Jessica's voice — the voice of her twin, the woman she was joined to at birth, the woman who gave her the eggs that made Kella and Armella — and she feels the weight of what she is about to say. She also hears Emma's presence on the line — steady, quiet, there.

**Thematic note:** Rachel calls them together. She does not call Jessica alone. This is a deliberate choice: this crisis involves the biological mother of both children, and it involves the biological mother's wife. The family does nothing alone — and Rachel is modeling that from the first phone call.

---

## BEAT 2 — RACHEL TELLS THEM

*Rachel names what happened. Plainly. Without graphic detail.*

- Rachel tells Jessica and Emma what happened. She is direct. She is restrained. She names the facts without re-describing the incident.
- **Dialogue cue (Rachel):** "Armella was with Todd. He thought she was Kella. She didn't stop him. Kella walked in and saw it. Kella is in my bedroom right now. Armella is in their room. I've talked to both of them."
- **Jessica's reaction:** Jessica is a neurosurgeon. She is also Rachel's twin — the sister whose eggs made these children possible, who loves them as her sister's children, as her nieces. Her reaction is both clinical and sororal. She does not panic. She does not shout. She asks the right questions.
- **Dialogue cue (Jessica):** "Is Kella safe?"
- **Dialogue cue (Rachel):** "Yes. She's in my room. She's safe."
- **Dialogue cue (Jessica):** "And Armella?"
- **Dialogue cue (Rachel):** "She's in their room. I've talked to her. She argued. She's fifteen. She said 'I wasn't thinking.' That's the first honest thing she's said."
- **Emma's presence:** Emma is on the line. She is quiet. She is listening. She is Jessica's wife, and she is also a mother — she and Jessica raised Samira and Sahara together. She knows what it is to hold a family through a crisis. She does not need to speak yet. She is there.

**Thematic note:** Rachel's telling is the act of a sister and a mother who trusts the people on the other end of the line. She does not soften it. She does not dramatize it. She names the facts and she names what she has already done (held Kella, confronted Armella, made sure everyone is safe). She is reporting, not collapsing.

## BEAT 3 — JESSICA'S RESPONSE

*Jessica processes. She is a physician. She is Rachel's twin. She knows these children are not hers.*

- Jessica processes what Rachel has told her. She is a neurosurgeon — she understands adolescent brain development, impulsivity, the neurological underpinnings of *"not thinking."* She is also Rachel's identical twin, her conjoined twin, the sister who donated her eggs so that Rachel could be a mother. But she knows — clearly, without ambiguity — that Kella and Armella are **Rachel's children.** Not hers. They are Rachel's in every way that counts. Jessica gave the eggs as a gift — *"a wonderful gift between sisters"* — but she did not give motherhood. That belongs to Rachel.
- **Dialogue cue (Jessica):** "Rachel. They are your children. You are their mother. I know that. I have always known that. But they came from my eggs, and I need to know what you need from me — as a physician, as your sister, as the person who gave you the means to have them."
- **Rachel's response:** Rachel hears this and feels the weight of what Jessica is offering. Jessica is not claiming the children. She is not overstepping. She is offering herself — her medical expertise, her twin bond, her presence — without trying to be something she is not. She is the egg donor. She is the sister. She is the physician. And she is here.
- **Dialogue cue (Rachel):** "I need you here. Or I need you on the line. Or I need you to tell me what you know about a fifteen-year-old brain that doesn't think. I need you."
- **Dialogue cue (Jessica):** "I can tell you this: a fifteen-year-old brain is not finished. The prefrontal cortex — the part that thinks ahead, that weighs consequences, that says *stop* — is not fully developed until the mid-twenties. Armella's brain is not a broken brain. It is an unfinished brain. That does not excuse what she did. But it means she can learn. She can grow. Her brain is still building itself."

**Thematic note:** Jessica's clarity is one of the most important things in this scene. She knows these children are not hers. She has always known. The eggs were a gift — the most profound gift one sister could give another — but the motherhood is Rachel's. Jessica offers what she can: her medical expertise, her twin bond, her presence. She does not try to be a mother to children who already have one. This is the egg donor's love: it gives, and then it steps back, and it holds from the place where it stands.

## BEAT 4 — EMMA SPEAKS

*Emma, who has been listening, speaks.*

- Emma has been on the line the whole time. She has been quiet. She has been listening. And now she speaks — not as a physician, not as a biological mother, but as Jessica's wife, as the co-mother of Samira and Sahara, as the woman who has held this family's most intimate bonds alongside Jessica for decades.
- **Dialogue cue (Emma, quiet, steady):** "Rachel. We're coming. Or we're staying on the line. Or we're doing whatever you need. But I want you to know — Samira and Sahara are Kella and Armella's half-sisters. They are going to want to know. And I am going to tell them, in the right way, at the right time. You don't have to carry that part."
- **Rachel's interior:** Rachel hears Emma and feels the weight of what Emma is offering. Emma is not just Jessica's wife. She is the co-mother of Samira and Sahara — the twins who are Kella and Armella's biological half-sisters and social cousins. Emma is offering to carry the part of this crisis that involves telling the next generation, the cousins, the half-sisters. She is offering to hold that so Rachel doesn't have to.
- **Dialogue cue (Rachel):** "Thank you."

**Thematic note:** Emma's offer is the saga's deepest instinct made concrete: *no one does anything alone.* Emma is not the biological mother of Kella or Armella. She is not Rachel's sister. She is Jessica's wife. And she is stepping in — not to take over, not to fix, but to carry one specific part of the crisis so that Rachel has one less thing to hold. This is what the Key family does. This is what love looks like in practice.

## BEAT 5 — THE PLAN

*The three women make a plan. Jessica and Emma do not travel to LA.*

- Rachel, Jessica, and Emma make a plan. The plan is practical: what happens next, how they stay connected, how they support Rachel from a distance. Jessica and Emma do not travel to Los Angeles. They stay where they are — in Halifax, or wherever they are based — and they speak with Rachel through the iPad screen.
- **Dialogue cue (Jessica):** "I'm not going to fly to LA. I'm going to stay here, and I'm going to be on this iPad whenever you need me. Day or night. You call, and I'm here. Emma and I are here."
- **Dialogue cue (Rachel):** "Okay."
- **Dialogue cue (Emma):** "We'll figure out Samira and Sahara. They're old enough to understand that something is happening with their cousins, and they're old enough to wait for the details. You don't have to carry that part."
- **The plan:** Jessica and Emma will stay where they are. They will be available on iPad — day or night, whenever Rachel needs them. Rachel will hold the house. Fallon Glenda will be called separately (or has already been called). The wider family network will be mobilized. The support is real, even across the distance.
- **Dialogue cue (Rachel, as the call ends):** "I love you both. Thank you for picking up."
- **Dialogue cue (Jessica):** "Always."
- **Dialogue cue (Emma):** "Always."

**Thematic note:** The call ends the way it began — with all three women present, all three women committed, all three women holding this family together. Jessica and Emma do not travel to LA. They do not need to. The iPad is enough — the screen is enough — because the connection is real. The family does nothing alone, even across a continent. The plan is practical. The love is not. Both things are true.
