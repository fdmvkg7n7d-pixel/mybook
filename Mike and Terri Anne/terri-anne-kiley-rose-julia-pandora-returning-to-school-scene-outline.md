# Terri Anne, Kiley Rose, and Julia — Pandora Paige Returning to School Without Brandi Jessica — Scene Outline

## SCENE INFORMATION

- **Characters:** Terri Anne Key, Kiley Rose Key (twenty-one years old), Julia (eighteen years old)
- **Offstage / referenced characters:** Pandora Paige Key (six years old), Brandi Jessica Key (missing), Mike Key, Sabrina Bryna Key, the teachers and administrators at Convent of the Sacred Heart, Pandora's classmates
- **Location:** Kiley Rose and Julia's townhouse, likely the kitchen or living room — the domestic space where impossible decisions are made
- **When:** Early January 2052, after the new year, before Pandora would be expected back at school for the second term at Convent of the Sacred Heart
- **Status:** New scene outline
- **Tone:** Devastating practicality, the impossible collision of a child's grief and a school's schedule, three women trying to build a bridge over an abyss

## SCENE PURPOSE

This scene outlines **the conversation between Terri Anne, Kiley Rose, and Julia about whether and how Pandora Paige should return to Convent of the Sacred Heart without her identical twin sister Brandi Jessica.**

Brandi Jessica has been missing since December 26. The school's winter break is ending. Sacred Heart expects Pandora Paige back. And three women must decide what to do with a six-year-old who has been wetting the bed, eating only applesauce, and clinging to Julia's neck so hard her fingernails leave marks — whether to send her back into a world that will look at her and see the ghost of the girl who isn't there.

The scene is about the impossible arithmetic of parenting through crisis: the school will not wait, the term will not pause, the other children will ask questions, and Pandora is an identical twin walking into a building where every person knows her face — because it is also Brandi Jessica's face. She cannot walk into Sacred Heart without the whole school knowing that one of the twins is missing. She cannot walk into Sacred Heart without seeing the empty chair, the empty hook, the empty place in line where her sister has stood beside her since the first day of kindergarten.

Terri Anne brings the practical mother's perspective — the one who has raised children through crises before, who knows that keeping Pandora home indefinitely is its own kind of harm. Kiley Rose brings the protective mother's anguish — the one whose body produced both twins, who cannot bear to release the one she still has into a world that took the other. Julia brings the anchor's clarity — the one who has been holding Pandora through every night terror, who knows exactly how fragile the girl is and also knows that Julia herself cannot be Pandora's only world forever.

No one is right. No one is wrong. They are three women trying to solve a problem that has no solution.

## SCENE ARC / BEATS

### Beat 1: The Question on the Table
- It is early January. The Christmas decorations have been taken down — not with any ceremony, but with the hollow efficiency of people who cannot bear to look at them anymore. The townhouse is quiet in the way it has been quiet since December 26: not peaceful, but held, as if the walls are holding their breath.
- Terri Anne, Kiley Rose, and Julia are in the kitchen. Coffee cups on the table. A calendar open to January. The winter term at Convent of the Sacred Heart begins in four days. Pandora's name is on the class list. So is Brandi Jessica's.
- **The question on the table is simple and impossible: Does Pandora go back to school?**
- No one has said it yet. It has been sitting in the room for days, growing heavier, the way unspeakable things do. Terri Anne is the one who finally says it, because Terri Anne is the one who says the things that need to be said in this family.

> *"We need to talk about Pandora and school. The term starts Monday."*

- The silence that follows is not surprise. It is the silence of three women who have been avoiding the question and have just had it placed in front of them like a glass of water they are not sure they can swallow.

### Beat 2: Kiley Rose's Response — Keep Her Home
- Kiley Rose's answer is immediate and visceral:

> *"She's not going."*

- She says it the way she says everything about Pandora now — fast, protective, a door slamming shut. Kiley Rose has spent the last ten days watching her surviving daughter come apart. She has seen the wet sheets. She has seen the applesauce. She has seen the hands that will not let go of Julia's shirt. She has heard the question — *"When is Brandi coming home?"* — asked seventeen different ways, and she has answered every one of them with *"We're looking, baby. We're looking,"* and the answer has never been enough, and Kiley Rose cannot imagine sending this child into a school building where she will be separated from Julia, separated from home, separated from the only things that are keeping her tethered to the world.

- *"She can't go back there,"* Kiley says. *"Not without Brandi. Not to that school. Every person in that building knows them. Every teacher. Every girl in her class. She walks into that building and every single person is going to look at her and see Brandi. They're going to ask her where her sister is. They're going to ask her questions she doesn't have answers to, and she is going to fall apart, and I am not going to let that happen."*

- Her voice is shaking. Not from anger — from fear. The fear that if Pandora leaves this house, something else will take her. The fear that the world outside this townhouse is the world that took Brandi Jessica, and it is not safe, and Pandora is not safe in it, and the only thing Kiley Rose can control is whether her daughter walks out the front door.

### Beat 3: Terri Anne's Response — She Has to Go Back
- Terri Anne does not argue. She does not contradict. She listens. She lets Kiley Rose finish. And then she says, gently, the way you say things to someone you love that they do not want to hear:

> *"Kiley. She has to go back."*

- Kiley Rose's head comes up. Her eyes are hard. Terri Anne does not flinch.

- *"I know,"* Terri Anne says. *"I know you want to keep her here. I know you want to wrap her in this house and never let her out. I would want the same thing. But keeping her home is not protecting her. It's freezing her. She is six years old, and she has spent the last ten days learning that the world is a place where her sister disappears and she cannot leave Julia's arms. If she stays home, she learns that the world is also a place she cannot re-enter. She learns that she is broken and the world is not safe and she has to hide. And that — that is not what Brandi would want for her. And it is not what she needs."*

- Terri Anne speaks from experience. She has raised children through crisis. She knows what happens when you let grief become the architecture of a child's life — the walls close in, the doors lock, and the child stops growing because the world has been reduced to the size of the room where they feel safe. Safety is not the same as healing. Safety is the precondition. Healing requires movement.

- *"She needs routine,"* Terri Anne says. *"She needs structure. She needs to get up in the morning and put on her uniform and go to a place that is not this house, where people care about her, where she has a desk and a teacher and a schedule. She needs to be Pandora at school, not just Pandora the twin who was left behind."*

### Beat 4: The Empty Chair
- Julia has not spoken. She has been sitting with her hands around her coffee cup, listening, her eyes moving between Kiley and Terri Anne. But now she speaks, and what she says is not about whether Pandora should go back. It is about what will happen when she does.

> *"The chair."*

- One word. Kiley and Terri Anne look at her.

> *"Brandi's chair. Her desk. Her hook in the coatroom. Her place in line. Pandora has walked into that school beside Brandi every single day since kindergarten. Every morning, they walk in together. Every morning, they hang their coats side by side. Every morning, they sit down at their desks and they are two. And now she's going to walk in and there's going to be an empty chair where Brandi sits, and she is going to have to walk past it. Every day. Every morning. Every class."*

- Julia's voice does not crack. But it is close. She has been the one holding Pandora. She knows the geography of this child's grief better than anyone — the way she reaches for a hand that is not there in the night, the way she turns to say something to an empty space beside her, the way she sleeps on the left side of the bed because the right side is Brandi's. Julia knows that the empty chair at Sacred Heart will not be an absence. It will be a presence. A wound that opens every morning at 8:15 and does not close until 3:00.

- *"The other girls are going to ask,"* Julia continues. *"Six-year-olds ask. They don't know how not to. 'Where's your sister? Where's Brandi? Why isn't she here?' And Pandora is going to have to answer. Or not answer. And either way, she's going to be the girl whose twin disappeared at Christmas, and every person in that building is going to look at her and see the other one. Because they look exactly the same. She is going to walk into that school carrying Brandi's face on her own, and people are going to see Brandi in her and they are going to be sad, and Pandora is going to see them being sad about her sister on her face, and I don't —"*

- She stops. She presses her palm flat on the table.

> *"I don't know how to prepare her for that."*

### Beat 5: What the School Needs — The Practical Reality
- Terri Anne brings them back to the practical. Someone has to. She has already spoken to Sacred Heart — quietly, carefully, without giving details that the school does not need and the family cannot afford to have circulating.

- *"I called Sister Margaret,"* Terri Anne says. *"I told her that Brandi Jessica will not be returning for the winter term. I did not tell her why. I told her there has been a family emergency and that Brandi is unavailable. I told her that Pandora will be returning alone and that the school needs to be prepared."*

- She lists what the school needs:
  - **The teacher needs to know** so she can speak to the class before Pandora arrives — not the details, but enough that the girls know not to bombard Pandora with questions.
  - **The empty desk needs to be handled.** Terri Anne asks whether Brandi's desk should be removed before Pandora returns, so the absence is not as stark, or whether removing it would be erasing Brandi, which is its own cruelty.
  - **The coatroom hook** — Brandi's name is above her hook, next to Pandora's. Does it stay or go?
  - **The attendance** — when the teacher calls the roll, she will call Brandi Jessica's name out of habit. Every teacher at Sacred Heart knows both twins by name. The sound of Brandi's name in that classroom could break Pandora before the day begins.
  - **The other parents** — Sacred Heart is a small, close community. The parents talk. If they know one twin is missing, the questions will reach the girls through their daughters. The family needs to decide how much information to give the school, knowing that anything they share will be repeated.

- Kiley Rose listens to the list and her face changes. The anger goes out of it. What replaces it is worse: the dawning recognition of how many ways this can hurt Pandora, how many landmines are waiting in a building she thought was safe, how a school — a place of structure and routine and familiarity — can become a field of small daily devastations.

### Beat 6: Kiley Rose Breaks — The Image of Pandora Walking In Alone
- Something in the list undoes Kiley Rose. Not the desk. Not the attendance. It is the image — the simple, unavoidable image — of Pandora walking into Sacred Heart alone.

> *"She's never walked into that school by herself."*

- Kiley's voice is small. Not the fierce, protective voice from before. A smaller, cracked voice, the voice of a twenty-one-year-old mother who had twins so they would never have to be alone, who nursed them in the same crib so they would always feel each other, who watched them learn to walk holding hands, who dropped them off every morning and watched them walk through the front door of Sacred Heart side by side, matching uniforms, matching backpacks, matching faces, and the sight of them was the most beautiful thing she had ever seen because they were two, they were always two, that was the whole point of twins, that was the gift, that was the thing that made the impossible pregnancy worth it — they would never be alone.

> *"Every morning since kindergarten, they walk in together. They hold hands on the steps. They have this thing where they squeeze each other's hand three times before they go through the door — one, two, three — and Brandi always goes first and Pandora always follows. And now she's going to walk up those steps by herself and there's no hand to squeeze and no one to follow, and she's going to walk through that door alone, and I can't —"*

- Kiley Rose stops. Her hand comes up to cover her mouth. Her shoulders shake. She does not make a sound. She has been the one coordinating the search, the one on the phone, the one holding the investigation together, and now she is a twenty-one-year-old mother breaking over the image of her daughter climbing stairs without her sister.

- Terri Anne moves first. She does not go around the table — she reaches across it, takes Kiley's hand, and holds it. Not to comfort. To anchor. The way you hold someone who is about to go under.

- Julia moves second. She comes around the table and stands behind Kiley. She does not hug her — she puts both hands on Kiley's shoulders, her thumbs pressing into the tight muscles of Kiley's neck, and she holds her there, standing, not letting her fall.

- They hold her like that — Terri Anne's hand, Julia's hands — until Kiley's breathing evens out. She does not wipe her eyes. She sits with the tears on her face and the image of those stairs in her mind, and she does not pull away from their hands.

### Beat 7: Julia's Concern — Separation
- When Kiley can speak again, Julia says what she has been holding:

> *"There's something else. If she goes back, I can't be there."*

- This is the truth that has been sitting under the whole conversation. Julia is Pandora's anchor. Julia is the one Pandora reaches for in the night, the one whose shirt she grips, the one she will not be put down for. Julia is not Pandora's legal parent. Julia is not a student at Sacred Heart. Julia cannot sit in the classroom with Pandora. Julia cannot walk the halls with her. Julia cannot hold her hand through the school day. If Pandora goes back to Sacred Heart, she goes back without Julia.

- *"She has never been away from me for a full school day since this happened,"* Julia says. *"Not once. The longest she's been apart from me is the day I went to the studio — five hours — and she screamed the whole time. Mike held her. Mike is wonderful with her. But she screamed. And school is seven hours. Seven hours in a building where she has never been without Brandi. Seven hours without me. Without Kiley. Without anyone she can reach for when it gets bad."*

- Julia's voice is steady but her hands have not left Kiley's shoulders, and her thumbs are still pressing into the tight muscles, and that tells Terri Anne everything: Julia is terrified. Not of the school. Not of the questions. Of what will happen to Pandora when the person she has been using as a life raft is not in the building.

- *"What if she panics?"* Julia asks. *"What if she starts crying and she can't stop? What if she wets herself? She's been wetting the bed, Terri. Every night. She doesn't have control of her body right now. What if she's in class and she can't hold it? What if the other girls see? What if she's humiliated on top of everything else?"*

- The questions are not theoretical. They are the questions of someone who has been washing the sheets, holding the shaking body, murmuring *"You don't have to be sorry"* at three in the morning. Julia knows what Pandora's grief looks like from the inside, and she is looking at a school building and seeing a seven-hour stretch in which every one of Pandora's vulnerabilities could be exposed in front of twenty classmates.

### Beat 8: Terri Anne's Bridge — The Plan
- Terri Anne does not dismiss Julia's fears. She does not say, *"She'll be fine."* She does not minimize. She listens, and then she builds a bridge — piece by piece, the way Terri Anne has always built bridges in this family.

> *"What if I take her?"*

- Kiley and Julia look at her.

> *"What if I take her to school on the first day? I walk her in. I walk her to her classroom. I stay until she's settled. And if she needs me — if she panics, if she starts to cry, if she can't stay — the school calls me and I come get her. No questions. No pressure. She tries. And if she can't do it, she comes home, and we try again the next day. But she tries."*

- Terri Anne's plan is not rigid. It is a bridge with handrails. Pandora does not have to cross the whole bridge on the first day. She has to step onto it. Terri Anne will hold her hand while she does.

- *"And I'll talk to Sister Margaret about the attendance,"* Terri Anne continues. *"Brandi's name should be removed from the roll before the first day. The teacher should be briefed. The girls should be told — gently, age-appropriately — that Brandi is not coming back this term and that they should not ask Pandora about it. And the desk—"*

- She pauses. The desk.

> *"I think we leave the desk. For now. Removing it is... it's like saying she's not coming back. And we're not saying that. We're not saying that to Pandora, and we're not saying that to the school. The empty desk is hard. But removing it is harder."*

- Kiley Rose does not agree or disagree. She sits with the image of the empty desk — Brandi's desk, the desk next to Pandora's, the desk where Brandi sat every day drawing horses and whispering to Pandora across the gap — and she feels the weight of both choices. Leave it: the wound is visible, every day, every class. Remove it: the wound is covered, but the covering is a kind of giving up. Neither choice is right. Neither choice is merciful.

### Beat 9: Kiley Rose's Condition — The Uniform
- Kiley Rose is quiet for a long time. The coffee in her cup has gone cold. Then she speaks, and her voice is not the fierce voice from before and not the cracked voice from the stairs. It is the voice of a mother who has made a decision she does not want to make.

> *"If she goes back, she wears her own clothes. Not the uniform."*

- Terri Anne and Julia look at her.

> *"The uniform is — they wear the same uniform. Identical. Every morning, they put on the same dress, the same tights, the same shoes. They stand next to each other in the same outfit and they look like one person split in two. And if Pandora puts on that uniform and walks into that building without Brandi beside her wearing the same one, she is going to look in the mirror and see half of herself missing. The uniform is not just clothes. It's proof that they're a pair. And I am not sending her to school dressed like half of a pair. She wears her own clothes. She wears something that is just hers."*

- Terri Anne nods slowly. She understands. The uniform is a symbol. It says: *I am one of two. I have a twin. We match.* Sending Pandora to school in the uniform without Brandi is sending her into the building branded by her own absence. Her own clothes — a dress that is not the Sacred Heart plaid, a sweater that no one else is wearing — make her Pandora. Just Pandora. Not one of a pair missing its other half.

- Julia nods too. She has seen what the red dress did to Pandora — the way she carried Brandi's Christmas dress like a relic. Clothes are not neutral in this child's grief. The uniform would be a daily reminder that she and Brandi used to match, and now she is alone in the matching, and the matching is a lie.

### Beat 10: What They Will Tell Pandora
- The final question: what do they say to her? How do they tell a six-year-old that she has to go back to the place where she and her sister were always together, and she has to go alone?

- Terri Anne: *"We tell her the truth. We tell her that school is starting and she is going back, and that it will be hard, and that it's okay if it's hard. We tell her that Brandi's desk will be there, and that she can look at it or not look at it, and either one is okay. We tell her that the other girls might ask questions and she can answer them or not answer them, and either one is okay. We tell her that if she needs to come home, she tells the teacher, and one of us will come get her. No questions. No consequences. She tries, and if she can't, she comes home."*

- Julia: *"I'll tell her the night before. I'll hold her when I tell her. I'll let her cry. I'll let her say she doesn't want to go. And then I'll tell her that I love her and that Brandi loves her and that going back to school doesn't mean leaving Brandi behind. It means being brave the way Brandi would want her to be brave."*

- Kiley Rose does not speak for a moment. Then:

> *"I'll tell her that Brandi went to school every day with her best friend beside her. And that now it's Pandora's turn to be brave for both of them."*

- The words land heavily. *For both of them.* It is a burden. It is also a gift — the gift of purpose in a situation that has none. Pandora has been helpless for ten days: helpless to find her sister, helpless to stop the grief, helpless to do anything but cling. Sending her to school gives her something to do. Something that is hers. Something that is not just surviving.

### Beat 11: Closing Image — The Calendar
- The conversation is over. The decision has been made. Terri Anne closes the calendar. The first day of the winter term is circled — a small, red circle on a Monday in January. Four days from now.

- Julia goes upstairs to check on Pandora, who is asleep in Kiley and Julia's bed, the red dress balled against her cheek, her small body curled on the left side of the mattress because the right side is Brandi's.

- Terri Anne puts the coffee cups in the sink. She stands at the counter for a moment, her hands resting on the edge, her eyes on the window where the snow is falling on the city.

- Kiley Rose sits at the table for a long time after Terri Anne and Julia have gone. She sits with the empty calendar in front of her and the red circle on Monday and the knowledge that in four days, her daughter will walk up the steps of Convent of the Sacred Heart alone, and there will be no hand to squeeze and no one to follow, and the world will keep going even though it should have stopped.

- She puts her head in her hands. She does not cry. She is too tired to cry. She sits in the quiet kitchen and breathes, and the calendar sits on the table, and Monday is coming, and there is nothing any of them can do to stop it.
