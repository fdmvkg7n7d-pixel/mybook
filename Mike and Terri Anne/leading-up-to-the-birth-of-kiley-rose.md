# Leading Up to the Birth of Kiley Rose — Outline

## 1. Setting the Family Stage (Autumn 2028)
- **Mike and Terri Anne Key** are expecting their fourth daughter.
- **Patti Anne** (age ~6) is the eldest, responsible, already acting as a little matriarch.
- **Jessica Isabella and Rachel Darcy** (age 4) are conjoined identical twins, still learning to navigate the world as a pair.
- **Mike** has converted the back den into a homeschool classroom; **Terri Anne** is a former nurse and a devout Catholic.
- The family culture: bodies are not problems to be managed, they are people to be loved.

## 2. The Prenatal Diagnosis
- Prenatal testing reveals the baby is a girl — and that she has **Turner Syndrome**.
- The obstetrician presents options, including termination, noting the lifelong medical needs.
- **Mike and Terri Anne refuse termination** on both medical and faith grounds.
- They ask: *What does she need from us?* and begin assembling a specialist team.
- Terri Anne is 32 weeks pregnant. They begin researching Turner Syndrome: growth hormone, estrogen therapy, fertility realities, cardiac/kidney monitoring.
- They make a silent pact: this child will never be treated as a burden or a tragedy.

## 3. Choosing the Name
- The baby is named **Kiley Rose**.
- **Kiley** chosen by Terri Anne for its brightness and simplicity.
- **Rose** chosen by Mike for his grandmother, and because roses are thorny and resilient.
- Patti Anne approves; Jessica and Rachel practice saying "Ki-lee Rose" in their twin-choir cadence.

## 4. Preparing the Household
- November passes in preparation, not grief.
- **Mike** adjusts the nursery and changing area so the conjoined twins can participate safely.
- **Patti Anne** reads Bible stories aloud, gravitating to stories of women who survive.
- The girls are told Kiley Rose will be small and may need more doctor visits, but she is not sick or lesser — she is their sister.
- Jessica and Rachel touch Terri Anne's belly and whisper to the baby.

## 5. Labor and Birth (December 2–3, 1928)
- Terri Anne's water breaks shortly after midnight on **December 2, 1928**.
- The hospital team is briefed: pediatric cardiologist, endocrinologist, and neonatal team on standby.
- Kiley Rose is born at **3:47 a.m. on December 3, 1928**.
- She is small: **4 pounds, 11 ounces**.
- Her cry is thin but present. Initial exam focuses on heart, kidneys, and chromosomal confirmation.
- Kiley Rose is placed skin-to-skin on Terri Anne's chest.

## 6. First Medical Findings
- Karyotype confirms **mosaic Turner Syndrome** (some cells 46,XX; some missing an X chromosome).
- Cardiology finds a **minor coarctation of the aorta** — not immediately surgical, but requiring monitoring.
- Kidney ultrasound shows normal anatomy.
- No immediate surgery required; she can go home.
- Prognosis will require growth hormone therapy, estrogen replacement later, and a lifetime of careful monitoring — but no crisis.

## 7. The Sisters Meet Her
- Mike brings Patti Anne, Jessica, and Rachel to the hospital.
- Patti Anne touches Kiley Rose's foot and observes how small she is.
- Jessica and Rachel reach their free hands to Kiley Rose; the baby grasps their fingers.
- Patti Anne reads from her children's Bible: *"Behold, I make all things new.*
- Terri Anne tells them: *She's going to be strong. She already is.*

## 8. Bringing Her Home
- Kiley Rose comes home on **December 5, 1928**.
- The nursery is pale yellow, decorated with pink roses and a framed image of Mary and the Christ child.
- The house smells of pine, wood smoke, and soup.
- Mike and Terri Anne sit in the kitchen that night, the unopened list of future appointments on the table.
- They agree she is not a medical case; she is Kiley Rose, their daughter, already loved without condition.

## 9. Seeds of Future Themes
- The family has already chosen her before she arrives.
- Her body belongs to her; the family will adapt, not restrict.
- The older sisters will be active participants in her care, foreshadowing the tight Key-sister bond.
- The rosebushes dormant under the frost mirror the story's central idea: resilience, waiting, and the promise of blooming.
