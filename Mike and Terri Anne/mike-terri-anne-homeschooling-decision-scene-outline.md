# SCENE OUTLINE: MIKE & TERRI ANNE DECIDE TO HOMESCHOOL (THE FOUNDING DELIBERATION)

**Characters:** **Mike Key** and **Terri Anne Williams-Key**. In the next room: their three children — **Patti Anne Key** (≈4, approaching Junior Kindergarten age), and the identical twins **Jessica Isabella Key** & **Rachel Darcy Key** (≈2, recently separated from their conjoined birth). No other children yet.

**Setting:** The Carlisle mansion — specifically the kitchen table, the load-bearing surface of this family. Late evening. The three girls are asleep upstairs. The house is quiet.

**Timeframe:** **~2026.** Patti Anne is four — the age at which Ontario’s **Junior Kindergarten (JK)** door opens (a child may begin JK the September of the year she turns four), and the question can no longer be deferred. Jessica and Rachel are two, fresh enough out of their separation surgery that the family’s life still bends around their specialists. This is the conversation in which Mike and Terri Anne decide, on purpose, to be their daughters’ first and primary educators — and to keep that door open for every child who comes after.

---

## SYNOPSIS

This scene captures the founding educational decision of the Key household: the evening Mike and Terri Anne, with only three small children in the house, talk their way to homeschooling as a deliberate philosophy rather than a stopgap. It is not a rejection of schools. It is an assertion of *what they believe education is for, and who gets to decide when the hardest truths enter their children’s lives.* The pillars articulated here become the architecture beneath everything that follows — the continuous, term-free learning that lets the family migrate to Paris and Cap d’Agde; the kitchen-table transparency that will later seat eight daughters to hear an itinerary or a diagnosis; the “your body is yours” principle; and the eventual, case-by-case release of each daughter into the institutional world (Dalhousie, U of T, Stanford, UCC) when *that* child is ready, not when a calendar says so.

## THE CONTEXT

Patti Anne is turning four — the September Junior Kindergarten window is opening for her. Somewhere in the stack of mail on the Carlisle counter is the brochure for the local public school, and a quiet, polite letter from the board asking whether the eldest Key child will be registering for JK in the fall. It is not a crisis. It is a question — and in this house, questions that touch the children get answered on purpose, at this table, together.

What sits under the question is heavier than a brochure. Jessica and Rachel are two years old. They began life as one body — one shared liver, one shared reproductive system — and were surgically separated in infancy at **McMaster Hospital** in Hamilton, where they were also born. The separation saved them. It also left them inside a long medical season: specialists, scans, the slow audited proof that two halves of a divided liver are both regenerating and functioning, the occupational therapy of two toddlers learning to live in bodies that are finally, fully their own. That season does not fit inside a school’s attendance policy. It never will.

So the question on the table is not really *where does Patti Anne go for Junior Kindergarten.* It is: **whose calendar runs this family’s life?** And the answer Mike and Terri Anne arrive at — the answer that will hold for decades and eight daughters — is that the calendar belongs to the family. The world is the classroom. And they are the ones who decide.

## THE SCENE

### Beat 1 — The question lands

- It is late. The three girls are asleep — Patti Anne in her room, the twins together in theirs, the way they have slept since the separation, close enough to find each other in the dark. Mike is at the kitchen table. Terri Anne has the brochure and the board’s letter. She sets them down between them the way she sets everything down: plainly, without performance.
- She does not ask *should we send her.* She asks the larger thing: *“What do we believe education is for?”* Mike recognizes the shape of the conversation immediately. This is not logistics. This is first principles. This is the table where they decide who their family is.

### Beat 2 — They weigh the school honestly

- They are not reflexive enemies of the institution. Mike names, fairly, what a good school gives: other children, other adults, structure, the friction of a world that does not love you the way your family does. Terri Anne agrees. They are not afraid of their daughters learning to stand in rooms full of strangers. They plan for that, in fact.
- But Terri Anne names what the school would cost, and it is here that the conversation turns. A school runs on its calendar: terms, bells, attendance windows, snow days, statutory holidays that do not move for a McMaster Hospital appointment. **A school cannot bend around a two-year-old’s liver scan.** A school will not hold the room while a mother explains, in the way only a mother can, what the scar on a separated twin’s belly means. A school will teach the body on the school’s schedule, and this family has already decided — quietly, before this night — that the body belongs to the child.

### Beat 2.5 — They compare their own schooling (with deeper personal cost)

- Mike speaks first, his voice quieter than usual. He attended **Hillfield Strathallan College**, the elite private school in Hamilton. He acknowledges the advantages — rigorous academics, strong extracurriculars, doors that opened because of the name on the diploma — but then he names the real wound: the loneliness. His mother’s frequent business trips meant he was often the last boy picked up at the end of the day. He still remembers the sting of watching other mothers arrive while his own car remained empty, the quiet shame of having to explain to teachers that “she’ll be here soon.” The school’s rigid schedule had no room for a child whose parent’s work life didn’t match the bell. The hierarchy rewarded the boys whose families were always present; it quietly punished the ones whose mothers were elsewhere.
- Terri Anne listens, then shares her own story from the **normal public schools of North York**. She speaks with the same plain honesty. The schools gave her a broad mix of kids and taught her how to navigate different personalities, but her parents had strict rules: **no sport teams**. She watched friends join soccer, volleyball, and track while she sat on the sidelines, the exclusion feeling both arbitrary and permanent. The one place her grandmothers fought for her was **Scouting**. They insisted she attend until her paternal grandmother died when Terri Anne was thirteen. That loss ended the one structured activity that had given her a sense of belonging and purpose outside the classroom. After that, there was only school and home — no teams, no camps, no real outlet for physical confidence or leadership.
- Mike reaches across the table and takes her hand. For a moment neither of them speaks. Then Terri Anne says softly, “I don’t want our girls to feel that kind of exclusion.” Mike nods. “And I don’t want them waiting in an empty classroom for a parent who can’t come.” 
- Together they realize something important: neither of them is rejecting school because it failed them personally. They are choosing homeschooling because they want something *more* than what either of their own educations provided. They want the flexibility their wealth can buy, the individualized attention the twins will need, and the ability to weave faith, truth-telling, and bodily autonomy into daily life instead of treating them as afterthoughts. Their different schooling backgrounds — one marked by lonely afternoons at an elite college, the other by forbidden teams and the early end of Scouting — become the mirror in which they see exactly what they *don’t* want to replicate — and what they *do* want to create for their daughters.

### Beat 3 — The twins become the turning point

- Mike does not pretend the decision is abstract. He says it straight: **the reason this is even a question is Jessica and Rachel.** They are two. They are in and out of McMaster Hospital. They are learning to be separate people inside separate bodies, and the people best placed to hold that education — patiently, daily, without making it a crisis — are the two people at this table.
- This is the beat where homeschooling stops being a preference and becomes a *conviction.* They are not keeping the girls home to hide them. They are keeping the girls home so that the hardest, most particular truths of their lives — *you began as one body; you are now two; half a liver is enough; one of you can carry children one day and one of you cannot* — can be told by the people who love them, at the moment each girl is ready to hear it. In this house, **no child is excluded from the truth just because telling it requires more care.** That rule is older than this night. This night, they realize the rule has a corollary: *if you are going to be the ones who tell the truth, you have to be the ones who hold the room.*

### Beat 4 — Mike names the freedom

- And then Mike — the wealthiest man in the world, the man who chose a grounded life over an empire’s demands — names the thing only their position allows them to name. **They do not have to choose.** The thing that constrains every other family — the school calendar, the commute, the job that will not flex, the leave that will not be granted — does not touch them. Mike’s wealth and his OPP-consultant’s flex are not the *reason* for the decision; they are the *permission* to make it.
- He articulates the first pillar plainly: **the world is the classroom, and the calendar belongs to the family.** If they want to spend a month learning the history of a Normandy beach by standing on it, they can. If a specialist appointment reshapes a Tuesday, the lesson reshapes with it. Learning here is continuous. It does not stop for a term, because a child’s life does not stop for a term.

### Beat 5 — Terri Anne names the values

- Terri Anne takes it deeper, because Terri Anne always takes it deeper. The calendar is the *how.* She wants to name the *what* — what their daughters will actually be taught, and by whom, and toward what.
- She sets out the values that will become the spine of the household:
    - **Faith woven in, not bolted on.** They are Catholic. The girls will learn the world *through* their faith, not beside it — the Mass, the seasons of the Church, the moral grammar that says a person is more than what they produce. Religion is not a class on Friday. It is the air in the house.
    - **“Your body is yours.”** The hardest and most load-bearing one. The girls will learn that their bodies belong to them — to care for, to protect, to understand, to say *stop* about, to one day give or withhold. The twins’ bodies make this urgent. But it is true for all of them. Terri Anne is the one who says, quietly, that this is the lesson she will not outsource to anyone.
    - **The truth rule, lived.** No child is told a comforting lie because the truth is hard. The kitchen table is where it happens. Which means: the kitchen table has to be where they *are.*
    - **Each child, individually.** Patti Anne is not the twins. The twins are not each other. Whatever comes after — and they already know more children may come — each girl gets the education fitted to *her.* (This is the seed that will one day make Mike learn ASL for a deaf daughter, build a desk chair for a daughter with cerebral palsy, take a burn-scarred daughter to the beach where nobody stares.)

### Beat 6 — The philosophy holds still long enough to be named

- There is a moment where neither of them is arguing anymore. They are looking at what they have built. Mike says it back to her, slowly, as if writing it down — the five things:
    1.  **The world is the classroom; the calendar is ours.**
    2.  **No child is excluded from the truth.**
    3.  **Your body is yours.**
    4.  **Faith is the air, not a subject.**
    5.  **Each child is taught as herself — and we hold the room.**
- Terri Anne nods. That is the school. They are the school.

### Beat 7 — The decision, and its one important boundary

- The decision is made — but Mike, who thinks in systems, adds the boundary that will keep the philosophy honest for the next two decades. **This is not a vow never to send a child to school.** It is a vow to be their daughters’ first and primary educators, and to revisit each girl’s path — *on her own timeline* — when the institutional world has something to offer *that child* that the home does not. When Patti Anne is ready for a room full of strangers, she will get one. When Jessica wants medicine, she will go to Dalhousie. When a sister finds ballet, or a school in Halifax, or a university in Stanford, the door opens — because *she* is ready, not because a form says she is due.
- This is the line that saves the philosophy from becoming a cage. The home educates. The world waits. And the girl decides when she walks out — with the porch light on behind her.

### Beat 8 — The first commitment

- They do not leave it abstract. Terri Anne names the first concrete practice, and it is the one that will echo for the rest of the saga: **transparency at this table.** When there is a decision that touches the children — a trip, a move, a diagnosis, a plan — they will sit the girls down and explain it, in the language each one can receive. Starting now. Starting with Patti Anne, tomorrow morning, in words a four-year-old can hold: *you are going to learn here, with us, for now. And when you are ready for something else, we will talk about that too.*
- The brochure and the letter go into a drawer — not thrown away, not answered with defiance. Filed. The board will be told, politely, that the Key children are receiving satisfactory instruction at home. (Mike’s resources, and the family’s legal right in Ontario, make this frictionless. The paperwork is the easy part. The philosophy is what they spent the night building.)

*Grace note:* Before they leave the table, Mike says something small that Terri Anne will remember years later — that the day is coming when this kitchen table will seat more children than they can yet imagine, and the decision they are making tonight is not for three girls. It is for the whole table, whoever ends up sitting at it. (He does not yet know about Kiley Rose, or Sabrina, or the eight. But the architecture he is laying down tonight is built to hold them all.)

## THE PHILOSOPHY (RECAP)

The five pillars Mike and Terri Anne articulate at the Carlisle kitchen table, ~2026 — the load-bearing educational worldview of the Key household:

1.  **The world is the classroom; the calendar belongs to the family.** Learning is continuous, not term-bound. This is the principle that later allows the two-month European migration, the Rio years, and the seamless accommodation of any appointment, trip, or season of recovery.
2.  **No child is excluded from the truth just because telling it requires more care.** The defining family rule. It requires, as its corollary, that the parents be the ones present to tell it.
3.  **Your body is yours.** The girls are taught that their bodies belong to them — to understand, protect, and consent about. Made urgent by the twins’ separation at McMaster Hospital; held for every daughter who follows.
4.  **Faith is the air in the house, not a subject on the timetable.** Catholic formation woven through daily life rather than isolated as instruction.
5.  **Each child is taught as herself, and the parents hold the room.** Individualized, attentive education — the seed of every future accommodation (ASL for a deaf daughter, an ergonomic chair for a daughter with cerebral palsy, a burn-friendly beach, a guide dog at twelve).

**And the one boundary that keeps it honest:** homeschooling is the *default and the foundation*, not a cage. Each daughter is released into the institutional world — a school, a conservatory, a university — when *she* is ready and when the institution offers something the home cannot. The home educates; the world waits; the girl decides.

## WHY THIS SCENE MATTERS

- **It is the founding architectural decision of the household.** Everything the saga later treats as “just how the Keys are” — the continuous learning, the term-free travel, the kitchen-table truth-telling, the case-by-case release into schools and universities — is *built* in this conversation, on purpose, before there are even four children.
- **It grounds the homeschooling in conviction, not eccentricity.** The decision is not “we’re rich and different.” It is rooted in the twins’ medical reality (born and separated at McMaster Hospital), the truth rule, bodily autonomy, faith, and a deliberate theory of what education is *for.* That grounding is what makes the later transitions out feel earned rather than contradictory.
- **It foreshadows the whole saga.** Mike’s closing line — that the table will one day hold more children than they can imagine — seeds the eight sisters. The five-pillar philosophy is the scaffolding under the European migration (~2041), the ASL desk-chair accommodations, and the eventual, individualized launch of each daughter into Dalhousie, U of T, Stanford, UCC, and beyond.
- **It establishes Mike and Terri Anne as a thinking partnership.** Mike names the *how* (freedom, the calendar, the system); Terri Anne names the *what* and the *why* (values, the body, the truth, the faith). The household they build is the product of both minds. This is the marriage’s operating manual, written one quiet night over an unfiled brochure.
