# Mike and Terri Anne Visit French Woods — Session 3 Visiting Day Scene Outline

## SCENE INFORMATION

- **Characters:** Brandi Jessica Key (8), Pandora Paige Key (8), Kiley Rose Key, Julia, Mike Key, Terri Anne Key, Maya (counselor), Sloan (9), Rilee (10), Mr. and Mrs. Hayashi, French Woods camp director
- **Onstage animal:** None (Burden does not arrive until age 14)
- **Locations:** French Woods campus — Birch Cabin, gymnastics building, dance pavilion, circus pavilion, Yoga studio, swimming pool, camp theater (Charlie Brown performance), cafeteria, lake/dock, camp office (Hancock, New York)
- **When:** The last day of Session 3, Brandi Jessica and Pandora Paige's first summer at French Woods (July 2053). Parent Visiting Day. No classes are held on Visiting Day.
- **Status:** New scene outline
- **Tone:** Family pride, generational strength, devastating precision — the Key family's power exercised not through volume but through fact

## SCENE PURPOSE

This scene captures Session 3 Visiting Day — the first time Mike and Terri Anne accompany Kiley Rose and Julia to French Woods. After the Session 2 confrontation with the Hayashis, Kiley Rose and Julia decided that next time, the whole family would come. Not as a threat. As a statement of fact: this is who we are, and this is how many of us there are.

It establishes:
- That Mike and Terri Anne's presence changes the atmosphere simply by existing. They do not need to confront the Hayashis. Their arrival — four adults who move as a unit, who clearly belong to each other — is enough to make the Hayashis nervous.
- That Mike does not confront Mr. Hayashi about the camp. He doesn't need to. Instead, he informs Mr. Hayashi that he is buying his firm — a business decision that has been in motion for six months, but one whose delivery is shaped by what the Hayashis did to his granddaughter.
- That Mike's power is not loud or threatening. It is factual, precise, and already in motion. *"I'm not threatening you. I don't threaten people. I'm informing you. There's a difference. And the difference is that a threat is something that might happen. This has already happened."*
- That the Hayashis, who were confident enough to complain to the camp director and confront Kiley Rose and Julia at Session 2, are now facing consequences they never imagined.
- That Mike and Terri Anne are warm, proud, and completely at ease with their granddaughters. Their strength with the Hayashis and their tenderness with the twins are not contradictory — they are the same quality, directed differently.
- That the twins are overjoyed to see their grandparents — this is the first time Mike and Terri Anne have visited them at camp.
- That the Hayashis watch from a distance and understand that the family they picked a fight with is larger, more unified, and more certain than they imagined.
- That Sloan and Rilee see the Key family together for the first time — four generations of certainty in one place — and the seeds of doubt from Session 2 continue to grow.

## BEATS

### Beat 1: Arrival — Four Adults, One Family

- It is Session 3 Visiting Day. No classes are held. The campus is full of families.
- Two cars pull into the French Woods parking lot. Kiley Rose and Julia step out of the first. Mike and Terri Anne step out of the second.
- Mike is a tall, broad-shouldered man in his sixties. He moves slowly but with absolute presence — the kind of man who fills a room without trying. Terri Anne is beside him, elegant and unhurried, her hand resting on his arm. They are both wearing the kind of casual confidence that comes from decades of being exactly who they are.
- Julia sees them and smiles. "They came."
- Kiley Rose: "I told you they would. Mom wasn't going to miss this."
- They walk onto the campus together — four adults in a loose group, clearly a family. They are not performing unity. They are simply unified.
- Other families notice them. Not because of the Key name — most people here don't know it. Because of the way they move together. Because of the way Mike puts his hand on Kiley Rose's shoulder as they walk. Because of the way Terri Anne scans the campus the way a general surveys a field — not with aggression, but with the calm certainty that she already knows everything she needs to know.

### Beat 2: The Reunion

- The twins are waiting on the Birch Cabin steps, just like last time. Brandi's cane is beside her. Pandora is scanning the path.
- Pandora sees them first. She sees all four adults and her face splits open.
- Pandora: "GRANDMA! GRANDPA!"
- She takes off running. Brandi hears Pandora's footsteps and then hears the sound — Mike's deep laugh, Terri Anne's warm voice — and her face lights up the same way.
- Brandi stands and walks toward them by sound. She doesn't run — she doesn't need to. She knows exactly where they are.
- Mike crouches down and catches both girls at once. He picks them up, one in each arm, the way he's been doing since they were born.
- Mike: "Look at you two. Getting bigger every time I see you."
- Pandora: "I grew another half inch."
- Brandi, reaching for his face: "You grew a beard."
- Mike laughs. "Your grandmother says it makes me look distinguished. I think she means old."
- Terri Anne: "I said it makes you look like someone who remembers to shave less often."
- The girls laugh. Terri Anne wraps her arms around both of them. She holds Brandi a beat longer — not because Brandi needs more, but because Terri Anne needs to feel her. This is the granddaughter who survived cancer. This is the granddaughter who is blind and at sleepaway camp and walking to class by herself.
- Terri Anne, quietly, just to Brandi: "How are you, sweetheart?"
- Brandi: "I'm good, Grandma. I'm really good."
- Terri Anne: "I know you are. I never doubted it."
- Kiley Rose watches her parents hold her daughters. Julia puts her arm around Kiley Rose's waist. This is what family looks like. This is what it has always looked like.

### Beat 3: Catching Up

- They find a spot on the grass near the cabin. Six people instead of four this time.
- The twins tell them about Session 3 — the new classes, the new routines, the things that are different from Session 2.
- Brandi tells them about the beam. "Pandora and I sit on it sometimes. After classes. It's like our bunk but up high."
- Mike: "You walked it on Visiting Day. Your mama sent us the photograph."
- Brandi beams. "You saw it?"
- Terri Anne: "It's on our refrigerator. Has been since the day she sent it."
- Pandora tells them about the silks, about Circus, about her Gymnastics. She describes everything vividly — for Brandi, but also for her grandparents, who are hearing it for the first time in person.
- Brandi tells them about Sloan and Rilee. Briefly, calmly, the way you describe something that happened a long time ago.
- Brandi: "On the first day of Session 2, two sisters said I shouldn't be in their classes. Their parents complained to the camp. Their parents confronted Mama and Julia on Visiting Day."
- Mike's face goes still. Not angry — still. The way a mountain is still. Terri Anne's expression does not change, but her hand finds Mike's.
- Mike: "And what happened?"
- Kiley Rose: "I told them who we are. I told them our parents are Mike and Terri Anne Key."
- A beat. Mike looks at Terri Anne. Terri Anne looks at Kiley Rose. Something passes between them — not surprise, not anger, just the quiet recognition that their daughter handled it exactly the way they raised her to.
- Mike: "Good."
- Terri Anne: "And now we're here. So they can see for themselves."
- She says it simply. Not as a threat. As a fact.

### Beat 4: The Girls Show Their Families Their Classes

- After catching up, the twins lead the four adults around the campus — the same tour they gave Kiley Rose and Julia on Session 2 Visiting Day, but bigger now. Grander. Four adults walking behind two eight-year-olds, one of whom taps a cane and counts her steps.
- They visit the dance pavilion. Brandi shows Mike and Terri Anne the tactile strip on the barre. Terri Anne runs her fingers along it and nods slowly — she has heard about it, but feeling it is different.
- Brandi does her barre combination. Mike watches without blinking. Terri Anne stands very still.
- They visit the gymnastics building. Brandi walks the beam again — this time without hesitation, without counting out loud. She just walks it. Mike doesn't clap. He nods once, the way he nods when something is exactly right.
- The twins sit on the beam together. Brandi reaches for Pandora's hand. Their feet dangle.
- Kiley Rose takes another photograph. This one has all four adults in the foreground, looking up at the twins on the beam. She sends it to Mike and Terri Anne's phones so they have it too.
- Pandora shows them Gymnastics and Circus — the floor exercise, the silks. Terri Anne watches the silks with her hand over her heart. Mike watches with his arms crossed and his jaw set, the way he watches anything that makes him proud and terrified at the same time.
- They do Partner Yoga in the studio. All four adults on the mat this time — Kiley Rose and Julia on one pair of mats, Mike and Terri Anne on another, the twins in the center. Six people breathing together. The studio is full of Key women.
- They go to the swimming pool. Brandi and Pandora swim for all four adults. Mike stands at the edge of the pool with his hands in his pockets, watching his granddaughters slice through the water.

### Beat 4B: Charlie Brown — The Performance

- On Visiting Day, families are invited to watch the session's theater production. This session, it's "You're a Good Man, Charlie Brown." Brandi Jessica is Charlie Brown.
- The four adults take their seats in the camp theater. It is a modest space — a pavilion with a stage, folding chairs, a simple set. The audience is full of Visiting Day families.
- Kiley Rose and Julia did not know Brandi was in the play. They certainly didn't know she was the lead. This is the first they are hearing of it.
- Julia, leaning over, her voice catching: "She's Charlie Brown?"
- Kiley Rose: "She didn't tell us." Her hand is already gripping Julia's.
- The lights dim. The show begins.
- Brandi Jessica walks onto the stage. She does not use her cane — she has mapped the stage by touch, by count, by memory. She walks to center stage and stops exactly where she is supposed to stop. Her face is turned toward the audience. Her eyes do not focus — they never do — but she is facing them. She knows where they are.
- Mike sits very still. His hands are on his knees. He does not move.
- Brandi speaks her first line. Her voice is clear and small and perfectly timed. She is Charlie Brown — the boy who always loses, who keeps trying anyway, who gets up every morning knowing the football will be pulled away and kicks it anyway.
- The audience laughs. Brandi waits. She has learned to wait by listening — she can hear the laugh building and receding, and she knows when the silence is ready for the next line.
- Kiley Rose is already crying. Not loud, not dramatic — just tears running down her face, her jaw tight, her hand gripping Julia's so hard it hurts. This is her daughter. This is her daughter who lost her eyes, who survived cancer, who was kidnapped and beaten, and she is standing on a stage in front of a hundred people performing a musical and she cannot see any of them.
- Julia is not crying yet. Julia is holding Kiley Rose's hand and watching with the fierce, protective focus of a mother who stood between her child and a man who said that child didn't belong. But her breath is shallow. Her chest is tight. She is holding it together the way she always holds it together — by being the wall.
- Brandi sings. The song she wrote in Songwriting class — the one about her family, about Retinoblastoma, about Partner Yoga during chemo — is not in the show. But the voice that sings is the same voice that wrote it. It is a voice that knows something about getting up after something has been taken away.
- The song hits Terri Anne. She has been still and composed all day — the elegant grandmother, the watchful wife, the woman who does not flinch. But when Brandi sings, something breaks open. Her hand goes to her mouth. Her shoulders shake once, hard, and then she pulls herself back. Mike puts his arm around her. She presses her face into his shoulder for just a moment — just one moment — and then she straightens up and watches the rest with tears running silently down her face.
- Mike does not speak. He does not cry. But his eyes are bright and his jaw is set and his arm around Terri Anne is the only thing keeping them both in their seats. This is his granddaughter. This is the girl who survived. She is standing on a stage she cannot see, singing for an audience she cannot see, and she is not afraid. Mike has watched a lot of brave people in his life. He has never been more moved.
- Pandora is not in the play. She is sitting in the audience with the other campers — she chose not to perform. But she watches Brandi with the expression she always wears when Brandi does something extraordinary: not surprise, not pride, just the quiet certainty of a twin who always knew. Her eyes are wet. She doesn't wipe them.
- Wren plays Snoopy. She is energetic and funny and bounces around Brandi like a puppy. Brandi reacts to Wren's voice, to her footsteps, to the timing they have rehearsed together. They move like they have been doing this for years.
- Daisy plays Lucy. She delivers her lines with confidence and volume, the van Pelt authority in full force. Brandi responds to the sound of her voice like she responds to every voice — by orienting toward it, by listening, by being exactly where she needs to be.
- The show is not perfect. Brandi misses a cue once — she waits a beat too long before a line, because she cannot see the other actor's gesture. The Musical Director, sitting in the front row with the score, makes a tiny conducting motion, and Brandi picks up the rhythm again. No one in the audience seems to notice.
- During the final number, Brandi stands center stage with the whole cast around her. She sings the last note. She does not know where the spotlight is — but it is on her. It is on the eight-year-old blind girl who learned an entire musical by ear and by touch and by count, and she is standing in the light she cannot see and she is singing like she belongs there.
- Because she does.
- The audience applauds. Brandi cannot see the clapping but she can feel it — the vibration in the floor, the sound filling the pavilion. She bows. She has been taught where to bow and how deep. She does it perfectly.
- Mike stands up. He is the first person on his feet. He is clapping and his face is wet and he does not care who sees it. Terri Anne is standing beside him, still crying, clapping with both hands. Julia has both arms around Kiley Rose, who is sobbing into Julia's shoulder, not quietly anymore, not trying to hide it — her daughter just performed a musical on a stage she cannot see.
- Four adults, standing, clapping, crying, for an eight-year-old girl who cannot see them standing for her.
- The curtain call ends. Brandi walks off stage. She walks to the edge of the stage, finds the steps by count, and comes down into the audience. She hears the crying before she reaches them.
- Brandi: "Why is everybody crying?"
- Kiley Rose pulls her into her arms. Julia wraps around both of them. Terri Anne is there, Mike is there, four adults holding one small girl who just played Charlie Brown.
- Brandi: "Mama? Why are you crying?"
- Kiley Rose: "Because you were so good."
- Brandi: "It was just the play."
- Kiley Rose: "It was not just the play."
- Brandi feels the shaking in her mother's arms. She feels the tears on Julia's cheek. She feels Terri Anne's hand on the back of her head and Mike's hand on her shoulder. She does not understand, not yet, why this is different from the beam or the barre or the pool. But she understands that they are proud of her, and that is enough.
- Mike, his voice rough: "You did something today, Brandi Jessica. You got up on a stage you couldn't see and you performed for an audience you couldn't see and you didn't miss a beat. Do you know how many grown adults can't do that?"
- Brandi: "I missed one cue."
- Mike laughs. It comes out broken and wet. "You missed one cue. And you picked it right back up. That's not failure. That's professionalism."
- Terri Anne, wiping her eyes: "We are so proud of you, sweetheart."
- Brandi: "The director picked me. I didn't even audition. There are no auditions here. I just signed up and he said I should be Charlie Brown."
- Julia: "He was right."
- Brandi: "I asked him if he knew I was blind. I wanted to make sure he understood what he was signing up for."
- Kiley Rose pulls back and looks at her daughter. "You asked him that?"
- Brandi: "Yes. And he said he knew. He said that's why he wanted me."
- The four adults look at each other. The camp director. The Musical Director. The man who looked at a blind eight-year-old and saw Charlie Brown.
- Mike: "That man is a good director."
- Terri Anne: "We should send him a note."

### Beat 5: The Hayashis See the Keys

- Across campus, the Hayashis arrive for Visiting Day. Mr. and Mrs. Hayashi greet Sloan and Rilee. The girls are happy to see their parents.
- Sloan, looking around the campus: "Mom, Dad — over there. That's the family. The blind girl's family."
- Mrs. Hayashi looks. She sees the group from Session 2 — Kiley Rose and Julia — and then she sees the two adults she doesn't recognize. Mike, tall and broad and standing like the ground belongs to him. Terri Anne, elegant and watchful, her arm through her husband's, her eyes on the twins in the pool.
- Mrs. Hayashi: "There are more of them."
- Mr. Hayashi follows her gaze. He sees Mike. He sees the way Mike moves — not aggressively, not performatively, just with the effortless authority of a man who has never had to prove he belongs anywhere.
- Mr. Hayashi: "Who are they?"
- Sloan: "That's her grandma and grandpa. They came this time."
- A beat. Mrs. Hayashi is quiet. She remembers Kiley Rose's words from Session 2: *"Our parents are Mike and Terri Anne Key. You may have heard of them."* She didn't recognize the name then. She may have looked it up since.
- Mr. Hayashi: "They brought reinforcements."
- Mrs. Hayashi, quietly: "That's not reinforcements. That's a family."
- They watch from across the campus. The Keys are at the pool now — four adults and two children, laughing, easy, completely at home. Mike lifts Pandora out of the water. Terri Anne holds a towel out for Brandi. Kiley Rose and Julia sit at the pool's edge with their feet in the water, leaning against each other.
- It is not a display. It is not a performance. It is just a family being a family. But the Hayashis see what they see: four adults who move together, who protect each other without words, who are clearly the center of something the Hayashis walked into without understanding.
- Mrs. Hayashi: "We should go to lunch."

### Beat 6: Mike and Mr. Hayashi

- At some point during the Visiting Day, Mike separates from the group. He tells Terri Anne he's going for a walk. Terri Anne knows exactly what he's doing. She does not stop him. She does not warn Kiley Rose. She just watches him go with the same calm expression she has worn all day.
- Mike finds Mr. Hayashi near the main building. The other man is standing with his wife, watching Sloan and Rilee play on the grass with some other campers.
- Mike walks up. He does not rush. He does not raise his voice. He puts his hands in his pockets and stands beside Mr. Hayashi the way you stand beside a man at a bus stop — casual, unhurried, like you have all the time in the world.
- Mike: "Mr. Hayashi."
- Mr. Hayashi turns. He recognizes Mike from across the campus — the tall grandfather, the one who moves like the ground belongs to him. Up close, Mike is even more still. Even more certain.
- Mr. Hayashi: "Yes?"
- Mike: "My name is Mike Key. Kiley Rose is my daughter. Brandi Jessica and Pandora Paige are my granddaughters."
- Mr. Hayashi stiffens. He knows where this is going. Or he thinks he does.
- Mr. Hayashi: "Mr. Key, if this is about the camp situation —"
- Mike: "It's not about the camp situation. I'm not here to discuss my granddaughters. I don't discuss my granddaughters with people who have a problem with them."
- A beat. Mr. Hayashi is quiet.
- Mike: "I'm here to let you know that I'm buying your firm."
- The words land quietly. The way a key turns in a lock. No force. Just precision.
- Mr. Hayashi stares at him. "What?"
- Mike: "Hayashi Development. You're a real estate firm. Small but profitable. Good portfolio in the tri-state area. Key Corporation has been looking to acquire one of your properties — the Harrison Street building. We've wanted it for a while. It's the last piece we need for that corridor. Your numbers are solid. Your properties are well-maintained. Buying the whole firm is the cleanest way to get it."
- Mr. Hayashi: "You can't just — my firm is not for sale."
- Mike: "Everything is for sale, Mr. Hayashi. The only question is the price. And I'm offering a very fair one. Your partners have already been contacted. The offer was delivered this morning."
- Mr. Hayashi's face goes pale. His mouth opens and closes.
- Mr. Hayashi: "This is because of the camp. Because we complained about your granddaughter."
- Mike: "This is because Key Corporation needs the Harrison Street building and buying your firm is the most efficient way to get it. I've been looking at your portfolio for six months. The numbers finally made sense."
- A beat. Mike lets the silence sit.
- Mike: "Once the acquisition goes through, we'll keep the Harrison Street property. The rest of your portfolio — the Westchester parcels, the Greenwich listings, the commercial holdings — those will be sold. I'm not in the business of holding properties I don't need."
- The meaning of this lands slowly, and then all at once. Mike is not just buying the firm. He's taking the one property he wants and dismantling the rest. Everything Mr. Hayashi built, scattered to other buyers.
- Mr. Hayashi: "You're going to break up my company?"
- Mike: "I'm going to acquire the asset Key Corporation requires and liquidate the remainder. Your partners will be fairly compensated. Your clients will be transitioned smoothly. It's a clean transaction, Mr. Hayashi. Cleaner than most."
- Mike: "The fact that you stood in front of my daughter and told her that my eight-year-old blind granddaughter — who survived cancer — didn't belong in a ballet class? That didn't affect the offer. The offer is the same whether you complained or you didn't."
- Another beat.
- Mike: "But I'd be lying if I said it didn't affect the way I'm telling you."
- Mr. Hayashi: "This is — you can't —"
- Mike: "I can. I am. The paperwork is already in motion. Your partners are receptive. The board will vote next week. You'll retain a minority stake in the Harrison Street property if you want it, or you can cash out entirely. Either way, Key Corporation will hold the building by the end of the quarter, and the rest of your portfolio will be on the market."
- He says all of this the way a man reads a grocery list. Calm. Factual. Without malice.
- Mike: "You have every right to advocate for your children's safety, Mr. Hayashi. My daughter said that to you, and she meant it. But I want you to understand something. When you pick a fight with my family, you're not picking a fight with a mother at a summer camp. You're picking a fight with people who build things. Who acquire things. Who own things. And who protect their own. Without any single doubt."
- He takes his hands out of his pockets.
- Mike: "I'm not threatening you. I don't threaten people. I'm informing you. There's a difference. And the difference is that a threat is something that might happen. This has already happened."
- He turns to leave. Then he pauses.
- Mike: "Your daughters seem like bright kids. Sloan and Rilee. It'd be a shame if they learned that the way to handle a problem is to try to make it someone else's."
- Mike walks away. He does not look back. He walks back to the dock, where his family is still standing by the lake, and he puts his arm around Terri Anne and kisses the top of her head.
- Terri Anne: "How did it go?"
- Mike: "As expected."
- Terri Anne: "Good."
- Kiley Rose watches her father. She knows what he just did. She may not know the specifics, but she knows the posture — the way he comes back from a conversation lighter, not heavier. The way a man walks when he has delivered a message and the message has been received.
- She does not ask. She will ask later, in the car. For now, she just watches her daughters play at the edge of the lake, and she knows that her father has taken care of it. The way he has always taken care of everything.

### Beat 7: Lunch in the Cafeteria

- The cafeteria is full of Visiting Day families. Each cabin at its own table.
- The Key family sits at the Birch Cabin table — all six of them. Brandi between Mike and Kiley Rose. Pandora between Terri Anne and Julia. The table is full. There is no room for anyone else, and no one tries to sit there.
- The Hayashis sit at the other end of the Birch Cabin table with Sloan and Rilee. The same arrangement as Session 2 — two families at opposite ends of the same table.
- But this time, the table feels different. The Keys take up more space. Not because they are loud or demanding. Because there are six of them, and they are completely at ease, and the warmth at their end of the table radiates outward.
- Mike looks down the table. He sees the Hayashis. He doesn't stare. He doesn't glare. He just looks — the way a man looks at something he has already assessed and found unimportant.
- Mrs. Hayashi feels the look. She keeps her eyes on her plate.
- Mr. Hayashi clears his throat. "How are your classes going?"
- Sloan: "Fine."
- Rilee: "Good."
- Neither of them mentions Brandi Jessica. Neither of them looks down the table.
- At the Key end, the twins are telling their grandparents about the camp food. Mike is pretending to be horrified by the menu. Terri Anne is telling the twins about the meals she used to pack for Kiley Rose when she went to camp. Julia is laughing. Kiley Rose is leaning her head on Julia's shoulder.
- It is the loudest, warmest, most alive end of the table.
- Sloan glances at them once. Then she looks back at her own plate.

### Beat 8: The Dock at the Lake

- After lunch, the Keys walk to the lake. The waterfront recreation area. The dock.
- Brandi leads them — she knows this route by now. She counts her steps and taps her cane.
- Mike walks behind her. Not too close. Not too far. Close enough to catch her if she falls, far enough to let her walk.
- Terri Anne walks beside Pandora. Pandora is talking a mile a minute — describing the dock, the water, the canoes, the way the lake sounds in the early morning when no one else is at the waterfront.
- They stand on the dock. Six people looking out at the lake.
- Mike: "This is a good place."
- Brandi: "I like it here. The lake doesn't have lanes like the pool, so I have to listen harder. But it feels freer."
- Mike: "That sounds like something your mama would say."
- Kiley Rose: "It sounds like something you would say, Dad."
- Mike smiles. The smile is small and private and proud.
- Terri Anne puts her arm around Brandi. "Tell me about the beam again. The one you and Pandora sit on."
- Brandi tells her. She describes it the way she experiences it — the leather under her hands, the width under her shoes, the way the air feels different four feet off the ground, the way Pandora's shoulder feels beside hers.
- Terri Anne listens. She does not interrupt. She does not say "that's amazing" or "I'm so proud of you." She just listens, because Brandi is telling her something true, and the truest thing a grandmother can do is receive it.
- When Brandi finishes, Terri Anne says: "Your grandfather walked a balance beam once. In college. He fell off."
- Mike: "I did not fall off. I dismounted poorly."
- Kiley Rose: "You fell off, Dad."
- Mike: "The beam was narrower than I expected."
- The twins laugh. Julia laughs. Kiley Rose laughs. Even Terri Anne laughs — a quiet, elegant laugh that she hides behind her hand.
- On the dock, in the afternoon sun, the Key family laughs together. It is not a performance. It is not a message to the Hayashis, who are somewhere on the other side of campus. It is just joy.

### Beat 9: The Camp Director Sees the Keys

- The camp director is walking between the buildings, checking on Visiting Day, making sure everything is running smoothly.
- She sees the Key family on the dock. All six of them. She recognizes Kiley Rose and Julia from Session 2 — the mothers who stood their ground in her office. And she sees the two new adults — the grandparents, clearly, from the way the twins cling to them, from the way the older woman holds the blind girl's hand, from the way the older man makes everyone laugh.
- She also sees the Hayashis, across the campus, staying close to the main building, keeping their distance from the lake.
- She makes a note: the Keys brought family. The Hayashis did not. She does not need to know the details to understand the dynamic.

### Beat 10: Goodbye

- Visiting Day ends in late afternoon. Families say goodbye at the same marked drop-off points.
- Mike crouches in front of both girls. He puts one hand on Brandi's shoulder and one on Pandora's.
- Mike: "You two are the bravest girls I know. And I know a lot of brave girls. Your mama. Your grandma. Your Julia. But you two — you're something special."
- Pandora: "We learned it from you, Grandpa."
- Mike: "You learned it from yourself. I just watched."
- Terri Anne hugs them each separately. Long, tight hugs. When she hugs Brandi, she whispers: "You sit on that beam as much as you want, sweetheart. High places are where you belong."
- Brandi whispers back: "I will, Grandma."
- Kiley Rose and Julia say their goodbyes — the smooth stones again, the letters, the promise to write.
- The four adults walk back toward the parking lot. Kiley Rose turns once and waves. Brandi hears the turn and raises her hand in the right direction.
- Pandora: "Session 4. Then we come home."
- Brandi: "I know."

### Beat 11: The Hayashi Sisters That Night

- That night, after lights out, Sloan and Rilee are quiet in their bunks. Their parents have left.
- Sloan whispers: "Did you see how many of them there were?"
- Rilee: "Four adults. Just for the two of them."
- Sloan: "The grandpa was tall."
- Rilee: "The grandma looked like she could take on anybody."
- A pause.
- Sloan: "Mom didn't say anything to them this time."
- Rilee: "No."
- Sloan: "Do you still think she shouldn't be in our classes?"
- A long pause.
- Rilee: "I don't know. She walked the beam again today. She didn't even count out loud this time. She just walked it."
- Sloan: "And she sat on it with Pandora. Like it was a couch."
- Rilee: "Like it was their bunk."
- They are quiet after that. The seeds from Session 2 have grown. Sloan and Rilee are not defending Brandi — not yet — but they are no longer certain she doesn't belong.
