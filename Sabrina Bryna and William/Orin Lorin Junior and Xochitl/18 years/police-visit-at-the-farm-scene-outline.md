# Police Visit at the Farm — Scene Outline

## Placement

**When:** The next morning (Saturday) after Danica arrives at the farm.
**Location:** Sabrina Bryna and William’s horse and rabbit farm, Quebec.
**Focus:** The police arriving because Danica’s parents reported her missing, conducting a wellness check, and — because Quebec law is stricter than the rest of Canada — explaining her options as a minor: return home, accept a DPJ (youth protection) intervention, or petition the court for **simple emancipation** under the *Civil Code of Québec*.

---

## Characters

- **Danica (Xochitl)** — 16 years old.
- **Orin Lorin Junior** — 19 years old.
- **Officer Bouchard** — Male SQ (Sûreté du Québec) officer.
- **Officer Tremblay** — Male SQ officer.
- **Officer Roy** — Female SQ officer, takes the lead with Danica.

---

## Legal Context (Author Note)

Unlike most Canadian provinces, **Quebec keeps a minor under parental authority until age 18** (the age of majority under the *Civil Code of Québec*). The Quebec youth-runaway resource *Jeunes en fugue* states the rule plainly: *“Before you turn 18, you may not leave the place where you live without the consent of the people responsible for you.”* If a minor runs away, police are expected to locate them and, in principle, return them home; the *Youth Protection Act* (loi sur la protection de la jeunesse) may also be triggered, bringing the **DPJ — Direction de la protection de la jeunesse** — into the file.

However, Quebec law also gives a sixteen-year-old a real legal door out: **simple emancipation** under articles 167–176 of the *Civil Code of Québec*. There are two routes:

1. **Through the tutor (parent)** — *Art. 167.* The tutor, with the agreement of the tutorship council, files a Declaration of Simple Emancipation with the **Curateur public**. This requires the parents’ cooperation, which Danica will not have.
2. **By petition to the court** — *Art. 168.* **A minor may apply alone.** The Superior Court of Quebec may grant simple emancipation, after hearing the opinion of the tutor and (where applicable) the tutorship council, where there are **serious reasons** and emancipation is **in the minor’s best interest**.

The effects (*Art. 170–171*): emancipation does not end minority, but it **releases the minor from the obligation to be represented**, allows her to **establish her own domicile**, and **ends the parental authority** of her father and mother. She still needs her tutor’s assistance for acts beyond simple administration (large contracts, real estate, etc.).

Officer Roy — the most experienced of the three — knows all of this and explains it to Danica clearly, in plain language. The officers do **not** drag Danica home. They are satisfied she is safe with a known adult at a known address; they will file a report, notify the DPJ for a follow-up wellness assessment, and let the legal process take its course.

## Scene Outline

### Beat 1: The Police Arrive

The next morning, a marked Sûreté du Québec cruiser pulls up the long gravel drive. Three officers get out. It is a quiet Saturday morning. They knock on the front door.

Orin answers. Officer Bouchard and Officer Tremblay stand in front; Officer Roy stands slightly behind them.

> “We’re looking for Danica,” Officer Bouchard says. “Her parents reported her missing last night.”

Orin stays calm.

> “She’s here. She’s safe. She came on her own. I know she’s sixteen — I’m not hiding her, and I’m not going to lie to you.”

Officer Bouchard nods slowly.

> “That’s the right answer. We still need to come in and speak with her.”

### Beat 2: Separate Interviews

The officers explain, politely but clearly, that this is a *fugue mineure* — a minor’s runaway file — and that under Quebec law they need to (a) confirm Danica is safe, (b) hear from her directly, and (c) document everything for the report that will be sent to the DPJ.

Officer Bouchard takes Orin into the living room. Officer Tremblay and Officer Roy go to the kitchen with Danica.

Danica is still wearing Orin’s shirt and housecoat over her ivory tights. She looks tired but composed.

### Beat 3: Officer Roy Explains Quebec Law to Danica

Officer Roy is gentle but direct. She sits across the kitchen table from Danica with her notebook closed for a moment.

> “Danica, I want to be honest with you about how this works in Quebec, because it’s different from what you might have heard about other provinces. Okay?”

Danica nods.

> “In Quebec, you’re a minor until you’re eighteen. Until then, the law says your parents have authority over you and that you’re supposed to live where they live. If you run away, the police are supposed to find you and bring you home. That’s the default rule.”

Danica’s face tightens. Officer Roy raises a hand, kindly.

> “But — and this matters — the law also gives you choices. I’m going to lay them out, and then you tell me what you want.”

She lists them on her fingers:

1. **Go home.** The officers can drive her back to her parents’ house today. The file closes.
2. **Stay, with youth protection involved.** Because Danica is alleging her parents made racist remarks about her boyfriend and that she does not feel safe or respected at home, Officer Roy will refer the file to the **DPJ**. A youth-protection worker will come out, assess the situation, speak with her parents, and decide whether there are grounds to open a protection file. While that assessment is happening, the DPJ can authorize her to stay at the farm if it’s deemed safe.
3. **Apply for simple emancipation.** Under article 168 of the *Civil Code of Québec*, **a minor sixteen or older can petition the Superior Court alone**, without her parents’ agreement, if there are serious reasons and it is in her best interest. If granted, she can establish her own domicile and is no longer under her parents’ authority. She’ll need a lawyer — legal aid can help — and it takes time, but it is a real option.

> “You don’t have to decide right now,” Officer Roy says. “Today I just need to know you’re safe, and I need to know what to put in the box on my form.”

### Beat 4: Danica Chooses

Danica is quiet for a long moment. Then:

> “I’m not going home. Not today. Not until something changes.”
>
> “Okay,” Officer Roy says, opening her notebook. “Then I’m going to mark this as a wellness check completed, minor located and safe, refusing to return, referred to the DPJ. A youth-protection worker will call here, probably Monday. Do you want me to write down that you’re interested in learning about emancipation?”
>
> “Yes. Please.”

Officer Roy writes it down.

> “I’ll leave you a number for legal aid, and a pamphlet. The DPJ worker can also tell you more.”

### Beat 5: Danica Shows Officer Roy Where She Slept

Officer Roy asks, almost in passing, where Danica slept the night before. Danica stands up and leads her down the hallway to the spare bedroom.

She opens the door and shows Officer Roy the bed, which is still unmade.

> “I slept here,” Danica says quietly. “Alone.”

Officer Roy nods and makes a note. She does not press — Danica is sixteen, not a child, and nothing in the room suggests otherwise — but Danica wants the record clean, and Officer Roy respects that. It will matter for the DPJ file.

### Beat 6: Orin’s Interview

In the living room, Officer Bouchard takes Orin through it. How they met. How she got to the farm. Who else lives here. Whether anyone touched her. Whether anyone gave her alcohol or anything else.

Orin answers everything honestly and calmly. He explains that Danica left her parents’ house after they made racist remarks about him; that he drove her here because she had nowhere else to go; that she has her own room; that he understands she is sixteen and that he is nineteen and that the law in Quebec is what it is.

> “I’m not trying to be her guardian,” Orin says. “I’m not trying to keep her from her parents. I just wasn’t going to leave her standing on a sidewalk at night.”

Officer Bouchard nods.

> “For what it’s worth — that’s the answer I was hoping to hear. The DPJ is going to look at all of this. Don’t lie to them either.”
>
> “I won’t.”

### Beat 7: The Officers Leave

The three officers confer briefly outside by the cruiser. Officer Roy comes back to the door and speaks to Orin and Danica together.

> “Here’s where we are. Danica, you’re sixteen, you’re safe, and you’ve told me clearly that you don’t want to go home today. I’m not going to force you — I have discretion, and forcing you into the back of a cruiser when you’re safe at a known address doesn’t serve anyone. I’m referring the file to the DPJ. Expect a call Monday.”

She hands Danica a card and a thin pamphlet about emancipation and legal aid.

> “We’re going to tell your parents you’ve been found and that you are unharmed. We are **not** going to tell them where you are — that’s the DPJ’s call, not ours, and they’ll make it based on your safety. If anything changes, if you change your mind, if you need us — call.”

Danica swallows.

> “Thank you.”

The officers leave. The cruiser rolls back down the long drive and the house falls quiet again.

### Beat 8: Breakfast Request

After the police leave, Danica and Orin stand in the kitchen. Danica looks at him and speaks softly.

> “Can you go to Saint-Viateur Bagels and get some bagels with cream cheese? For our breakfast?”

Orin nods without hesitation.

> “Of course. I’ll be back soon.”

He grabs his keys and heads out to buy the bagels. Danica is left alone in the kitchen with the pamphlet in her hand.

## Emotional Notes

- This scene adds **real** external pressure: in Quebec, sixteen is not a free pass. Danica has chosen a path, not escaped a problem.
- The officers are professional, not adversarial. Officer Roy in particular treats Danica as a capable young woman and lays out her actual legal options instead of bullying her.
- Orin acts with maturity: he is honest, he doesn’t claim a role he doesn’t have, and he makes clear he is not Danica’s keeper.
- Danica’s decision not to disclose her location to her parents (yet) is preserved — but here it’s the DPJ, not the police, who will ultimately make that call.
- The pamphlet in Danica’s hand at the end of the scene seeds the next thread: the DPJ visit, the legal aid call, and eventually a Superior Court petition for simple emancipation under art. 168 C.c.Q.
