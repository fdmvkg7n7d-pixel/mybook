# SCENE OUTLINE: CARLOTA TELLS KEITH EVERYTHING — THE TENTH DATE

*Carlota Delicia Key tells Keith Holiday, on their tenth date, the full truth: the kidnapping, the forty-two months, the broken ASL, the bar in Tijuana — and that she is HIV-positive. Carlota is roughly sixteen. Keith is roughly sixteen. They have been dating since the end of grade ten. They are now somewhere in grade eleven.*

---

## SETTING

- **Location:** Carlota's bedroom in **Sabrina Bryna's Vaughan mansion**. Door open. Sabrina and William are downstairs, but Carlota chose her room on purpose — she wanted home around her when she said this, in case Keith left and she had to put herself back together somewhere safe.
- **Time:** A Saturday evening, post-dinner. She has invited him over for what he thinks is the tenth date. She has been planning this conversation for the previous nine.
- **What's already happened on the nine dates:** Movies. The Eaton Centre. A school dance neither of them really danced at. Walks. A hot chocolate place on Yonge. Nothing physical beyond hand-holding. Keith has been content with that. He has not asked for more. He has not even hinted.

---

## IN THE ROOM

- **Carlota Delicia Key** — sixteen. Five years past Tijuana. Still does not sign with her mother. Has rehearsed this conversation seven times alone, twice with her therapist, and once into a mirror. None of the rehearsals went the way the real version is about to.
- **Keith Holiday** — sixteen. Has known for two years that there was *something* about Carlota — a layer she was holding back. Has never asked. Has waited for her to decide.
- **No one else in the room.** The door is open. The hallway light is on. This is on purpose: Carlota has set conditions where she can call for her mother and William if she needs to. Keith does not know the conditions exist, but he understands the open door.

---

## BEAT 1 — BEFORE SHE SAYS IT

Keith is sitting on the floor by her desk because Carlota's bed is the only seat in her room and they both know better than to start there. He has his back against the desk, his knees up, a half-finished can of ginger ale.

Carlota is on the floor too, on the other side of the room, against her dresser. Far enough that she can see his whole face. Close enough that she can hear him breathe.

She says, *"There are things I haven't told you."*

Keith does not say *"like what?"* or *"you don't have to."* He puts the ginger ale down on the carpet and folds his hands.

He says, *"Okay. I'm listening."*

She had practiced for him to interrupt. She had practiced for him to flinch in advance. She had not practiced for *"okay."* For a moment she cannot remember the order she planned to say things in.

---

## BEAT 2 — THE KIDNAPPING

She starts with the simplest, oldest fact: *"My biological father took me when I was nine. He took me to Mexico. I was gone for forty-two months."*

She does not look at Keith while she says it. She looks at the carpet between them, at a spot where the vacuum lines from earlier in the day are still visible.

Keith does not move. He does not say *"oh my god."* He does not say *"I'm so sorry."* He says, *"Okay,"* again, and then, very quietly, *"keep going if you can."*

She keeps going.

---

## BEAT 3 — THE BROKEN ASL

She tells him about ASL. That her mother is deaf, that they used to sign together, that she was *good* at it — that she was the one in grade two whose teacher used her as the example for how a hearing child of a deaf parent could sign fluently both ways.

She tells him that during the forty-two months, her father made her stop. Made her stop in ways she does not want to describe tonight. That when she came back, the signs were locked behind a door inside her, and she has not been able to open the door yet. That she has not signed *to* her mother — not even once — in five years.

She tells him, *"My mother lost me twice. Once when I was taken. And once when I came back and didn't sign."*

This is the first sentence in the conversation that makes Keith look down. Not away — down. He is taking it in. He says nothing.

---

## BEAT 4 — TIJUANA

She tells him about the bar.

She does not give details. She says, *"For most of the time I was gone, my father was selling me to men in a bar in Tijuana. I was ten, eleven, twelve."*

She watches his face for the flinch. She has watched for it in five other people's faces over the years — her aunts, her cousins, a friend at Everest Academy who learned a fragment by accident. Some of them flinch. Some of them try not to and you can see them not trying.

Keith does not flinch. His hands — folded — go very still in a different way. His jaw does something small. His eyes do not move from her face.

After a long moment he says, *"I'm glad you got out."*

It is the first thing anyone outside her family has ever said to her about Tijuana that did not feel like it was about the person saying it.

---

## BEAT 5 — THE LAST ONE

This is the one she has been holding for last on purpose. Because she knows that the first four facts can be received as terrible things that *happened to her*. This one is a terrible thing that *is in her*. People — even good people — sometimes treat those differently.

She says:

> *"I'm HIV-positive."*

She says it flat. She does not soften it. She does not put a comma after it. She wants to see, clean and unhedged, what his face does.

She watches.

Keith's face does — nothing dramatic. His eyebrows do not jump. He does not look at the door, which she had also expected. He looks at her, and she can see him processing it, and she can see — this is the part she did not allow herself to hope for — *that he is processing it as new information about her circumstances, not as new information about her.*

He says, *"Okay. Tell me what I need to know."*

She blinks. She had a paragraph rehearsed about U=U. About the medication. About the fact that with treatment her viral load is undetectable, which means untransmittable, which means — even if they ever, someday — she cannot give it to him.

But he asked *"what do I need to know,"* not *"are you safe to be around."* The difference is enormous. She lets the rehearsed paragraph go and gives him the short version.

> *"I take one pill every day. I have since I came home. My viral load is undetectable — the virus is suppressed below where any test can find it. Undetectable means untransmittable. I can't give it to you. Not by kissing, not by anything. There is no way for you to catch it from me as long as I keep taking the pill, which I will. For the rest of my life."*

He nods slowly. He files it. He says, *"Okay."*

Then, after a beat: *"Is there anything that scares you about it that's not about me? Like — is there a part of this that I should ask about, that's not whether I'm safe?"*

This is the moment, more than any of the others, when Carlota understands that he is going to stay.

---

## BEAT 6 — WHAT HE DOES NOT DO

He does not get up.

He does not move closer (the door is open, her mother is downstairs, and he can read a room).

He does not say *"thank you for telling me"* — which Carlota has come to recognize as a kind, well-meant phrase that nonetheless centers the listener.

He does not ask for any detail she didn't volunteer. Not about the bar. Not about her father. Not about the men. Not about her diagnosis. He waits for her to give him what she wants him to have.

After a long stretch where she lets the silence sit, he says:

> *"None of this is who you are. All of it happened to who you are. I'm not going anywhere."*

She has the urge to tell him not to make a promise he can't keep. She does not. Some promises are made not because they will definitely be kept but because the making of them changes the room. This is one of those.

---

## BEAT 7 — DOWNSTAIRS

Eventually she stands up. She is shaky. He stands up too, slowly, mirroring her — he has been trained at the ballet barre of his own life to match her tempo, even though he has never taken a ballet class.

She walks him downstairs. Sabrina and William are in the kitchen pretending to be doing things. Sabrina looks up. Sabrina has known what tonight was — Carlota told her in writing this morning, *"I'm going to tell him everything."*

Sabrina signs to Carlota, across the kitchen, *"Are you okay?"*

Carlota looks at her mother's hands. She still does not sign back. But she says, in spoken English, loud enough for Sabrina to read on her lips:

> *"Yes. He stayed."*

Sabrina's eyes close for half a second. William, beside her, puts a hand on the small of her back.

Keith says goodnight to both of them at the door. Polite, brief, no performance. He looks at Carlota one more time before he leaves. He does not hug her — he does not assume he can, after a conversation like that. He says, *"Text me when you wake up tomorrow. Just so I know you slept."*

Then he is gone.

---

## BEAT 8 — AFTER HE LEAVES

Carlota goes back to her room. She does not cry until the door closes.

She is crying because he stayed. She is crying because she had genuinely prepared to lose him tonight. She is crying because the four worst sentences in her life are now sentences someone outside her family has heard, and she is still standing.

Downstairs, Sabrina signs to William, *"He stayed."*

William signs back — slow, still learning, ten years in but never as fluent as Mike was — *"Yes. He stayed."*

The house, for the first time since the airport in Tijuana five years ago, has a small new shape in it: the shape of an outside person who knows everything and chose to stay anyway.

---

## WHAT THIS SCENE ESTABLISHES

- The full content of the tenth-date disclosure is now on the page: kidnapping, broken ASL, Tijuana, HIV — in that order, deliberately chosen by Carlota.
- Keith's response is consistent with his profile: steady, non-intrusive, asking *"what do I need to know"* rather than *"am I safe."*
- The **U=U** framework is communicated in-scene, not in narration — which means it is now part of the relationship's working knowledge.
- Carlota's autonomy over disclosure (established at the diagnosis) is honored here — she chooses what to say, when, in what order.
- The first **sign-mediated moment between mother and daughter that includes a *spoken* response** ("Yes. He stayed.") — Carlota still doesn't sign back, but she is now answering her mother's signs aloud instead of nodding or looking away. Small step on the long arc of the Return of the Signs.
- **Mike is not in this scene either** — but Sabrina and William's exchange at the end ("He stayed.") is a kind of family ritual that Mike would have been the natural third voice for. The family is learning to do the rituals with one fewer person.
