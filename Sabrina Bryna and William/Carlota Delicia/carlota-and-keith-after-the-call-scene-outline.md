# SCENE OUTLINE: CARLOTA AND KEITH — THE CALL FROM QUEBEC

*Orin Lorin Junior calls Carlota Delicia from Quebec to tell her he is not coming to Orlando. Carlota and Keith are both twenty-three. Their daughter Madeleine Jaycee Holiday Key is six months old. Carlota is the daughter of Sabrina Bryna and Carlos. Orin is nineteen. They became siblings when she was twenty and he was sixteen. They live in Orlando.*

---

## SETTING

- **Location:** Carlota and Keith's home in Orlando. This is where they live. There are no bags to pack, no travel documents to gather, no garment bags draped over chairs. The house is simply itself — a Saturday, the weekend before Christmas, a life already in place.
- **Time:** Saturday, the weekend before Christmas Day. Minutes after Orin's call to Carlota.
- **What's already happened:** Orin has called Carlota directly from Quebec. He has told her he is not coming to Orlando. He has said a friend needs him because the friend's parents died. The call has just ended. Keith was in the room, or nearby, when it happened. Madeleine Jaycee Holiday Key is six months old and somewhere in the house — in a carrier, in a bassinet, on the floor on a blanket.
- **What this scene is not:** It is not a retelling of the call. The call is over. This scene is what happens after the line goes dead.

---

## IN THE ROOM

- **Carlota Delicia** — twenty-three. Sabrina Bryna's daughter by Carlos. She became Orin's sister when she was twenty and he was sixteen. Five years of Tijuana behind her. HIV-positive, on treatment, undetectable. Mother of Madeleine Jaycee Holiday Key, six months old. She lives in Orlando. She has built a life, a home, a family here. She has known Orin for three years — since she was twenty and he was sixteen, since the moment they became siblings. She has watched him grow, grieve, and become the kind of person who calls from Quebec to say he is not coming to Orlando because a friend needs him.
- **Keith Holiday** — twenty-three. Carlota's partner. The boy who sat on the floor of her bedroom on the tenth date and said *"okay, keep going if you can."* The boy who stayed. Now a father. Still steady, still the kind of person who matches someone else's tempo without being asked. He lives in Orlando with Carlota and Madeleine Jaycee Holiday Key. He is part of the family.
- **Madeleine Jaycee Holiday Key** — six months old. Asleep, or mostly asleep, or intermittently awake in the way six-month-olds are when the energy of the house is different than usual. She does not know anything about Quebec or Orlando or phone calls. She only knows that the adults in her life are breathing differently.

---

## Part 1 — The call ends

Carlota is still holding the phone when the line goes dead. Orin has said what he needed to say. He has not asked for permission. He has not asked for advice. He has told her — directly — that he is not coming to Orlando. That a friend needs him. That the friend's parents died.

She lowers the phone. She does not put it down right away. She holds it in her hand and looks at the screen like the answer is written on it.

Keith is across the room, or in the doorway. He sees Carlota's face and he stops.

He does not ask *"what happened?"* He asks *"what did he say?"* — because he knows the call was Orin, and he knows Orin would not call Carlota on a Saturday before Christmas for small talk.

She says:

> *"He is not coming."*

And then, because Keith deserves the whole sentence:

> *"Orin is not coming to Orlando. He is staying in Quebec. A friend needs him. The friend's parents died."*

She says it flat. She does not soften it. She does not put a comma after it. She wants to say the words out loud and hear how they sound in the room where they live.

---

## Part 2 — Keith listens

Keith does not move right away. He lets the words sit in the air between them. He has learned this from Carlota — from the tenth date, from every conversation since — that the first thing to do with heavy news is to let it land before you try to catch it.

He says:

> *"He called you."*

Not *"he called your mother."* Not *"he called William."* He called Carlota. Directly. She is one of the first people he reached.

Carlota nods. She says:

> *"He called me before he called anyone else. Or one of the first."*

And Keith understands what this means. Orin did not call the parents first. He called Carlota. He called his sister — the one who knows what it means to be a child who does not come home. He called the one who would not panic, would not interrogate, would not demand the whole story. He called the one who would hear *"a friend needs me"* and understand that the missing details were not a lie — they were a boundary.

Keith says:

> *"He knew you would understand."*

And Carlota says:

> *"I do not know if I understand. But I hear him."*

---

## Part 3 — Carlota's body remembers

She was nine when her own father took her. She knows what it feels like to be a child in the hands of an adult who has decided something for you. She knows what it feels like to be forty-two months away from home. She knows what it feels like to come back and not be able to sign to your mother.

Orin is nineteen. Orin was orphaned by a house fire at sixteen. Orin has lived through something that rewired how he sees the world. And now he has called Carlota — not the parents, not William, not the person who could stop him — but Carlota. The person who would listen. The sister he has had for three years.

She does not narrate her own history out loud. She does not need to. Her body remembers. She holds the phone a little tighter. She looks at Madeleine Jaycee Holiday Key — six months old, asleep, safe, here — and something in her chest tightens.

Keith sees it. He does not ask *"are you okay?"* He asks *"what are you thinking?"* — which is the question he learned to ask on the tenth date and has been asking ever since.

She says:

> *"He is nineteen. He is alone up there. And he called me."*

And Keith says:

> *"He is not alone. He called you. That means he has you."*

---

## Part 4 — Madeleine Jaycee Holiday Key

At some point in this conversation, Madeleine Jaycee Holiday Key stirs. She does not cry — she makes that six-month-old sound that is not a cry and not a laugh and not a word, but something in between. Something that says *I am here. I am alive. I am six months old. I do not know anything about Quebec but I know your faces.*

Carlota looks down at her. The weight of Orin's call is still in the room. It is not gone. It will not be gone until Orin comes home, or until Carlota sees him again, or until she hears from someone who has seen him that he is okay.

But Madeleine Jaycee Holiday Key exists. And she is six months old. And she is here. And she is not going anywhere.

Keith picks her up, or Carlota does, or they both reach for her at the same time — the kind of small, wordless choreography that new parents develop without meaning to. The baby settles. The room settles with her.

---

## Part 5 — Orlando is already home

Carlota and Keith live in Orlando. The Orlando house is their home. It is where Madeleine Jaycee Holiday Key sleeps. It is where the family was supposed to gather for Christmas — all of them, including Orin. There are no bags to pack. There is nothing to prepare. They are already where they live.

Carlota says:

> *"He is not coming here. He is not coming to the house."*

And Keith says:

> *"The house will still be here. We will still be here."*

She knows he is right. But the sentence still lands with weight. The Orlando house — the place she has built, the place where Madeleine Jaycee Holiday Key was born, the place that was supposed to hold all of them together for Christmas — will hold one fewer person.

Carlota says:

> *"We were going to take her to see the characters. We were going to let her watch the parade. We were going to take a picture of her with her first Mickey ears."*

And Keith says:

> *"We still will."*

And she says:

> *"Not with him."*

And he says:

> *"Not with him. But with her."*

And they look at Madeleine Jaycee Holiday Key, who is six months old and does not know what a Mickey ear is and does not know that her uncle Orin is not coming. She only knows that she is being held by two people who love her.

And that is enough. Not because it fixes anything. But because it is what is real, right now, in this room, in this house, in this city they call home.

---

## Part 6 — Three years of being siblings

Carlota knows Orin. They became siblings when she was twenty and he was sixteen. Three years. Not a long time in the span of a life, but long enough for her to watch him grow from a sixteen-year-old boy who had just been orphaned into a nineteen-year-old young man who calls from Quebec to say a friend needs him.

She has known him through the hardest three years of his life — the years after the fire, the years of learning to be part of a family that was not the one he was born into, the years of becoming the kind of person who calls his sister before anyone else when he makes a decision that will change the family's plans.

She says something to Keith that only a sister could say — not a biological sister, but a sister by family, by shared history, by the particular way the Key household operates:

> *"He would not say that unless it was real. Orin does not lie about things like this. He might leave things out, but he would not invent a reason."*

Keith nods. He knows this too. Keith has been in this family long enough to understand the difference between Orin's silences and Orin's words. Orin's words are exact. When he speaks, he means it. When he leaves something out, he is not lying — he is protecting.

Carlota says:

> *"Whatever he is not saying, it is because someone else needs him not to say it. That is who he is."*

She is twenty-three and she is a mother and she is speaking from a place that is both younger and older than her age. She knows what it looks like when someone protects another person by not telling the whole truth. She has been on both sides of that.

---

## Part 7 — The decision to trust him

Carlota could call Sabrina. She could call William. She could demand that they call Orin back and ask more questions. She could insist on knowing the full story — who the friend is, what the situation is, whether Orin is safe, whether he needs help.

She does not do any of that.

She looks at Keith. Keith looks at her. They do not need to say the decision out loud. They have both made it.

Carlota says:

> *"We are letting him stay. We are trusting him."*

And Keith says:

> *"He called you. He did not call and run. He called and told you. That is trust, too."*

She knows he is right. Orin could have gone silent. He could have disappeared. He could have let the family find out from someone else, or not at all. Instead, he called Carlota directly. He gave her a reason — not the whole reason, but a true one. He stayed in communication. That is not the same as being taken. That is not the same as being gone for forty-two months with no phone, no call, no choice.

But the overlap is real, and she does not dismiss it by pretending it is not.

---

## Part 8 — Keith's steadiness

Keith does not panic. He never has. He is the person in the family who does not panic.

He says something practical — because that is what he does when the emotional weight is too large for one moment to hold:

> *"Your mother and William are handling it. They are not interrogating him. They are letting him stay. They trust him. We trust him too."*

He says *"we"* — not *"they."* He includes himself and Carlota in the family decision. This is not a small thing. Keith has been in this family long enough that he no longer says *"your mother"* when he means *"our mother."* He says *"your mother and William."* But he says *"we trust him too."* The *we* is deliberate. The *we* is earned.

He adds:

> *"He is at Ferme Clé. He is safe. He has people there. He made a choice. We are letting him make it."*

Carlota nods. She has known all of this since the call ended. But hearing Keith say it — out loud, in the room where they live, in the house that is supposed to hold all of them for Christmas — makes it real in a way that knowing it does not.

---

## Part 9 — The silence

At some point, the conversation runs out of words. Not because there is nothing left to say, but because the thing that needs to be said does not have words.

Carlota sits. Keith sits. Madeleine Jaycee Holiday Key is asleep in one of their arms, or between them, or on the floor on a blanket nearby. The Orlando house is quiet except for the sound of a baby breathing and the distant hum of a city that does not know a nineteen-year-old young man has just changed his family's Christmas plans.

Carlota signs something — not to Keith, not to Madeleine Jaycee Holiday Key, but to herself, in the private way she has learned to sign when no one is watching. A sign she has not used in years. A sign that means *come home safe.*

Keith sees her hands move. He does not ask what she signed. He knows. He has always known.

---

## Part 10 — Closing

The scene ends without resolution about Orin. There is no follow-up call from Quebec. There is no update. There is no moment where Carlota and Keith learn the whole story. That is not what this scene is for.

This scene is for what happens in the minutes after the line goes dead. Two twenty-three-year-olds in their Orlando home, a six-month-old baby, and the knowledge that the family they belong to is big enough to hold a nineteen-year-old boy in Quebec who is making his own decisions and a baby in their arms who is too young to understand any of it.

Carlota puts the phone down. Not on the table. Not on the counter. She puts it face-down on the couch cushion beside her, like a conversation that is not over but is paused. Keith takes Madeleine Jaycee Holiday Key from her arms, or she takes Madeleine Jaycee Holiday Key from his — the small, wordless choreography of parents who know how to hold a family together when the family is bigger than the room.

The Orlando house holds them. The baby holds them. And somewhere in Quebec, a nineteen-year-old young man is exactly where he believes he must be.

---

## WHAT THIS SCENE ESTABLISHES

- Orin calls Carlota directly — not just the parents. She is one of the first people he reaches, which signals how much he trusts her and how much she understands.
- Carlota and Orin became siblings when she was twenty and he was sixteen. They have known each other for three years. She has watched him through the hardest years of his young life.
- Carlota at twenty-three is still shaped by the kidnapping, the forty-two months, the HIV diagnosis — not as active wounds, but as reference points. When Orin's call lands, she does not react as a casual sister. She reacts as someone who knows what it means for a child to be somewhere other than where they are supposed to be.
- Keith remains the steady one — not passive, not dismissive, but grounded in the practical and the emotional truth at the same time. He uses *"we"* when talking about the family decision, showing how fully he belongs.
- Madeleine Jaycee Holiday Key is not a prop. She is a six-month-old with her own presence, her own sounds, her own way of changing the room by existing in it.
- Carlota and Keith are both parents now. The scene shows them parenting Madeleine Jaycee Holiday Key *and* parenting each other through the weight of Orin's news — without solving it, without diminishing it, without turning it into a crisis that is bigger than it is.
- The Orlando house is their home. There is no packing, no preparation. They are already where they live. The scene takes place in the ordinary stillness of a Saturday at home.
- The scene does not resolve Orin's storyline. It exists alongside it. The two scenes — Sabrina/William and Carlota/Keith — are mirrors of each other: one about parents letting go, the other about new parents holding on.

---

## THEMES

- The long tail of childhood trauma on adult decision-making.
- What it means to be a parent — to a six-month-old, to a nineteen-year-old brother, to each other.
- The difference between being taken and choosing to stay — and how both can feel like absence.
- Family rituals that expand to include new people (Keith saying *"we"*).
- The baby as a centering force — not a distraction, but a reminder of what is actually happening in the room.
- The Orlando home as a place of belonging — already lived-in, already theirs, already waiting for a family member who is not coming.
- Trust as an action, not a feeling.
- Three years of siblinghood — not a long time, but long enough to matter.
