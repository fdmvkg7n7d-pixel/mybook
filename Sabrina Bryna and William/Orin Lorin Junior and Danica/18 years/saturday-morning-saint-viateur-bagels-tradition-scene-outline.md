# Saturday Morning — Saint-Viateur Bagels with Cream Cheese Tradition — Scene Outline

## Scene Information

- **Characters:**
  - **Danica (Xochitl)** — 16 years old. The morning after the Friday DPJ inspection. Wearing her Christmas-present shirt and baby blue pyjama bottoms at first, then changing later if needed.
  - **Orin Lorin Junior** — 19 years old. Second-year McGill student, biology and chemistry, pre-medical track. Trying to give Danica a Saturday morning that does not feel like a file, a police report, or an inspection.
  - **Madame Hélène Bélanger** — Ferme Clé chef. Briefly referenced or onstage; she may smile at the bagel tradition but does not intrude.
- **Offstage / referenced:** Claire Desrosiers, Dr. Whitfield, Ms. Renée Lapointe, Geneviève, Chloe, Martin Gagnon, Danica’s parents, Sabrina Bryna and William.
- **Location:** Ferme Clé kitchen, possibly Orin’s car / Montreal errand, Saint-Viateur Bagels, then back to the kitchen.
- **When:** **Saturday morning**, the day after Claire Desrosiers inspected Ferme Clé and accepted it as a temporary safe location for Danica.
- **Tone:** Domestic, ordinary, restorative. This scene begins turning survival into ritual.

---

## Scene Purpose

This scene establishes a new Saturday tradition for Orin Lorin Junior and Danica: **bagels with cream cheese from Saint-Viateur Bagels**.

The tradition matters because it gives them something repeated, harmless, and theirs. After a week of police, school administration, counselling, DPJ inspection, legal uncertainty, and careful transportation plans, Saturday morning becomes the opposite of assessment. It becomes breakfast.

The emotional center is simple:

> They are not solving the future. They are eating warm bagels with cream cheese at the kitchen table.

---

## Continuity to This Point

- Danica first asked for Saint-Viateur bagels after the police visit.
- Orin got them for her because she had requested something specific after a frightening morning.
- Since then, the bagels have become associated with safety, relief, and being listened to.
- On Friday afternoon, Claire Desrosiers inspected Ferme Clé.
- Friday evening, Danica and Orin made pizza and watched a movie in the home theater. Danica wore her Christmas-present shirt and baby blue pyjama bottoms.
- Now it is Saturday morning, and the house has not been inspected. No police are at the door. No one from BCS is calling. No one is asking Danica to prove anything.

# Scene Outline

## Beat 1: Waking Without an Appointment

Saturday morning at Ferme Clé.

Danica wakes later than usual, not because she has slept perfectly, but because there is no school bell waiting for her. No uniform. No pickup point. No office note. No Claire at four o’clock.

She lies still for a moment and listens.

The farm is awake outside:

- distant barn sounds,
- muffled voices of staff,
- a truck door closing somewhere,
- the heating system,
- the winter quiet around the house.

There is no knock at the door.

That matters.

She comes into the kitchen still wearing her **Christmas-present shirt** and **baby blue pyjama bottoms**, hair loose or sleep-messy. Orin is already there, dressed casually, standing by the counter with his phone in his hand.

> “Morning.”
>
> “Morning.”

She looks around the kitchen as if checking whether the day has demands hidden in it.

> “Is someone coming?”
>
> “No.”
>
> “No one?”
>
> “No one official.”

That earns him the smallest look.

> “That should be a category.”
>
> “It is now.”

---

## Beat 2: The Saturday Question

Orin asks it like it is ordinary, but he knows it is not.

> “Bagels?”

Danica looks at him.

> “Saint-Viateur?”
>
> “Saint-Viateur.”
>
> “With cream cheese?”
>
> “Obviously with cream cheese.”

A pause.

> “Is this a special occasion?”
>
> “Saturday.”
>
> “That’s not a special occasion.”
>
> “It can be.”

Danica understands then. He is offering ritual. Not a reward for surviving, exactly. Not pity. Just a way for Saturday to have a shape.

> “Every Saturday?”
>
> “If you want.”

She is quiet.

> “I want.”

---

## Beat 3: They Decide to Go Together

Danica decides she wants to ride with him, not because she especially wants Montreal after such a hard week, but because she wants the errand to belong to both of them from the beginning.

> “Can I come?”
>
> “Of course.”
>
> “I don’t want to talk to people.”
>
> “Then we don’t talk to people except the bagel people.”

She looks down at the Christmas-present shirt and baby blue pyjama bottoms.

> “I should change.”
>
> “Probably.”

The small practical answer helps. He does not make the outing heavy. He does not say this is good for her, or that she needs to get out, or that it will help. He simply lets her come.

Danica goes to the spare bedroom and changes into warm Saturday clothes: jeans, a soft sweater, wool socks, and her winter coat. She puts her boots on at the door only after checking that the laces are dry. The habit of shoes-off-at-the-entrance remains; outside clothes belong outside, house floors stay clean.

Orin gets his keys, wallet, phone, and a reusable bag for the bagels.

Before they leave, he checks in once.

> “Still want to come?”
>
> “Yes.”
>
> “Okay.”

This is the first Saturday tradition: not only eating Saint-Viateur bagels with cream cheese, but going together to get them.

---

## Beat 4: The Drive to Montreal

They drive toward Montreal in the winter morning. The car is warm. Snow lies in gray ridges along the road. The first part of the drive is quiet, but not the frightened kind of quiet.

Danica watches the countryside pass. Ferme Clé disappears behind them. For a moment, leaving the farm makes her uneasy — the farm is the place Claire inspected and allowed for today. But Orin is not taking her away from safety; he is taking her to breakfast and bringing her back.

He says it plainly, as if he knows where her thoughts have gone.

> “We’re coming back after.”
>
> “I know.”
>
> “I’m saying it anyway.”
>
> “Thank you.”

They talk lightly after that:

- whether sesame or poppy seed bagels are better,
- how much cream cheese is too much,
- whether Saturday counts as a special occasion,
- whether Orin has ever tried to study organic chemistry in a bagel shop.

Danica asks if Saint-Viateur is always busy.

> “Usually.”
>
> “Do I have to order?”
>
> “No. I can order. You can stand with me. Or you can tell me what you want before we go in.”
>
> “Sesame. Cream cheese.”
>
> “Obviously.”

The outing remains small and manageable.

---

## Beat 5: At Saint-Viateur Bagels

Montreal winter. The familiar warmth of the bagel shop. The smell of wood fire, sesame, poppy seed, dough, and heat. The line moves quickly. People know what they want. The place is alive without being intimate.

Danica stays close to Orin but not hidden behind him. She looks at everything:

- the bagels coming out hot,
- the workers moving with practiced speed,
- the paper bags,
- the tubs of cream cheese,
- the steam and warmth against the cold outside.

Orin keeps his promise. He handles the ordering unless she chooses to speak.

> “Sesame?” he asks her softly before they reach the counter.
>
> “Sesame.”
>
> “Cream cheese?”
>
> “Plain.”
>
> “Good.”

He buys more than two bagels because he is nineteen and because leftovers matter.

He gets:

- sesame bagels,
- maybe poppy seed bagels,
- plain cream cheese,
- possibly one extra tub because Danica likes enough cream cheese to make the bagel feel soft,
- maybe orange juice or something small if desired.

If the worker asks Danica a simple question, she may answer quietly. Orin does not answer over her unless she looks to him.

The important relationship beat: he makes public space easier without taking her voice away.

A Saturday tradition should be repeatable.

---

## Beat 6: Coming Back to Ferme Clé

They drive back to Ferme Clé with the Saint-Viateur bag between them or on Danica’s lap. The bag is warm enough that she can feel it through the paper.

That warmth matters. It gives her hands something ordinary to hold.

When they turn up the long gravel driveway, Danica looks at the gate and the Ferme Clé sign. Yesterday, Claire inspected this place. Today, Danica left and came back.

That matters too.

Inside the entry, Danica removes her boots again before stepping onto the wooden floor. Orin brings the bagels into the kitchen.

> “They’re still warm.”

Danica’s face changes at that.

Warm bagels are proof not only that he went, but that they went and came back together.

> “You got cream cheese?”
>
> “Danica.”
>
> “That means yes?”
>
> “That means obviously.”

---

## Beat 7: Bagels at the Kitchen Table

They sit at the kitchen table.

The meal is ordinary and specific:

- paper bag opened between them,
- steam still caught in the bag,
- cream cheese tub opened,
- knives clinking softly on plates,
- Orin cutting a bagel carefully,
- Danica spreading cream cheese too thickly or exactly thick enough,
- crumbs on the wooden table.

They do not talk about Claire at first.

They talk about bagels.

Possible small conversation:

> “This one is yours.”
>
> “Why?”
>
> “It has the best sesame coverage.”
>
> “You ranked them?”
>
> “Obviously.”

Or:

> “How much cream cheese is too much?”
>
> “After this week?”
>
> “Yes.”
>
> “There is no too much.”

---

## Beat 8: Naming the Tradition

Halfway through breakfast, Danica says:

> “So this is Saturday?”

Orin looks up.

> “If you want it to be.”
>
> “Saint-Viateur bagels with cream cheese.”
>
> “Every Saturday we can manage it.”
>
> “Even if there’s snow?”
>
> “Especially if there’s snow.”
>
> “Even if I have homework?”
>
> “Bagels before homework.”
>
> “Even if something bad happens during the week?”

That question lands differently.

Orin answers carefully.

> “Maybe especially then.”

Danica nods. She looks down at her plate.

> “Then yes. I want it to be Saturday.”

---

## Beat 9: The Relationship Meaning

The tradition lets them talk about permanence without pretending anything is legally permanent.

Danica might say:

> “Claire said today. Not forever.”

Orin nods.

> “I know.”

> “But Saturday can happen again.”

That is the point.

> “Yes.”
>
> “Not forever. Just again.”
>
> “Again is a good word.”

This gives Danica a gentler unit of time. Not court, not emancipation, not adulthood. Just Saturday, and then another Saturday.

---

## Beat 10: Sabrina Bryna and William, March Break, and the Mistake They Do Not Catch

The conversation turns toward the future carefully, almost by accident.

Danica is spreading cream cheese on the second half of her bagel when she asks:

> “When do Sabrina Bryna and William usually come here?”

Orin looks up.

> “To Ferme Clé?”
>
> “Yes.”
>
> “Not often during term. It’s one of their investment properties. They check on it, but they don’t live here.”

Danica nods, thinking.

> “But Sabrina Bryna has Key Corporation properties in Montreal too, doesn’t she?”
>
> “Yes.”
>
> “So if she comes to Montreal for business, she could come here.”
>
> “She could.”

The answer lands differently than Orin expects. He sees Danica doing calendar math in her head.

> “What?” he asks.

Danica looks at him.

> “March break.”

Orin’s hand stills on his bagel.

> “That’s not for a while.”
>
> “Quebec’s March break is a week earlier than Ontario’s.”

Now Orin understands part of it — but not all of it.

Danica continues, careful but uncertain:

> “BCS will be off before your younger siblings are off. Ontario’s March break comes after ours. If Sabrina Bryna and William are visiting Key Corporation properties in Montreal during the children’s March break, they could come here.”

Orin sets his bagel down.

> “March is coming up very soon.”
>
> “Yes.”

For a moment, the March break visit stops being an abstract future problem and becomes a real approaching date. Sabrina Bryna and William would not be coming primarily to investigate Danica. They would be coming because Ferme Clé is an investment property, because Sabrina Bryna has Key Corporation properties in Montreal, and because the children can accompany them during their Ontario school break.

Danica asks:

> “The children would come with them?”

Orin nods slowly.

> “Most likely. If it’s during their March break, yes. Sabrina and William would bring them.”

That question has weight. Danica knows about Taylor Raven, Sun-Young “Liz,” Roberta Katherine, and William Timothy Junior. She knows Orin has younger siblings whose lives are still arranged around school calendars, family trips, and parents who do not yet know the whole truth.

> “So they could all come here.”
>
> “Yes.”
>
> “To visit the farm.”
>
> “To visit the farm, check the property, maybe stay here, and for Sabrina to visit Key Corporation properties in Montreal.”

Danica looks down at the open cream cheese tub.

> “Would they tell you first?”
>
> “Usually.”

That word — **usually** — should hang in the air, but neither of them lets it become the point. They are young, tired, and grateful for a peaceful Saturday. They see the risk, but they do not yet understand how easily an ordinary family-business visit can turn into a surprise.

Danica asks:

> “Do we tell them before then?”

Orin looks toward the window, toward the snow and the farm Claire inspected yesterday.

> “We should.”

It is not the same as *we will.* That distinction matters.

> “When?”
>
> “After we know more. After Claire’s follow-up. After legal aid. I do not want to tell them pieces and then have to correct everything.”

Danica nods because that sounds reasonable. It also lets the fear move a little farther away.

> “So not today.”
>
> “Not today.”

She picks up her bagel again but does not eat it.

> “If they came with the children, would the children ask why I live here?”
>
> “Probably.”
>
> “What would I say?”

Orin does not answer immediately. He thinks, because children deserve truth too, but not every detail.

> “You say you are staying here because your home situation became unsafe and the adults are helping sort it out.”
>
> “That sounds like Dr. Whitfield.”
>
> “Dr. Whitfield is good at sentences.”

That almost makes Danica smile.

> “Will Sabrina Bryna and William be angry?”
>
> “Maybe.”
>
> “At me?”
>
> “No.”

He says it quickly, then corrects himself because quick reassurance is not enough.

> “I do not think they will be angry at you. If they are angry, it will be at me for not telling them sooner.”

Danica’s voice is small.

> “Will they think I ruined Christmas?”
>
> “No.”
>
> “You don’t know that.”
>
> “I know them.”

A pause.

> “Sabrina will see the frightened part first. William will see the practical risks first. They will both see that I lied by omission. They will both have questions. But they are not cruel people.”

Danica looks around the kitchen — the wooden table, the bagel crumbs, the cream cheese, the snow outside, the house Claire inspected yesterday.

> “I want them to like me.”
>
> “They will.”
>
> “Careful part.”
>
> “Okay. Careful part: they will need time. But liking you will not be the hard part.”
>
> “What will be the hard part?”
>
> “Forgiving me for waiting.”

Danica does not argue with that. She reaches across the table and puts her hand over his for a second.

> “Then maybe you should not wait too long.”
>
> “I know.”
>
> “But not during bagels.”
>
> “Not during bagels.”

The agreement is small and incomplete. Saturday is for bagels. March is coming very soon. Sabrina Bryna and William may arrive with the children as part of a normal family-business trip to Montreal and Ferme Clé. Orin knows he should tell them before then — but he does not set a date, and Danica does not force one.

That is the mistake.

The surprise is already being built by the calendar.

## Beat 11: Closing Image

The scene closes with them still at the kitchen table:

- Danica in her Christmas-present shirt and baby blue pyjama bottoms,
- Orin across from her in his McGill hoodie,
- warm Saint-Viateur bagels between them,
- cream cheese open,
- crumbs on the wooden table,
- snow outside,
- no official appointments on the calendar for that morning.

Closing line option:

> “It was not a legal arrangement. It was not a promise the court could recognize. It was only bagels with cream cheese on a Saturday morning. But for the first time since she left home, Danica understood that ordinary things could be repeated until they became safe.”

# What This Scene Establishes

1. **Saturday bagels become a relationship tradition.** Saint-Viateur bagels with cream cheese are now tied to safety, repetition, and care.
2. **Danica experiences a morning with no official appointment.** This contrasts with police, BCS, counselling, and DPJ scenes.
3. **Orin’s care becomes ordinary rather than heroic.** He does not rescue her in this scene; he gets breakfast.
4. **Danica begins trusting repeatable rituals.** Not forever — just again.
5. **Ferme Clé becomes a lived-in home.** The kitchen table, crumbs, cream cheese, and snow matter as much as the mansion and barn.
6. **The tradition can recur in later scenes.** Saturday bagels can appear during legal stress, family reveal tension, exams, or ordinary domestic mornings.
