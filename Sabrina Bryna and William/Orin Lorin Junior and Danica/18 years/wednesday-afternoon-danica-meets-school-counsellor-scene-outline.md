# Wednesday Afternoon — Danica Meets Ms. Renée Lapointe, the BCS School Counsellor — Scene Outline

## Scene Information

- **Characters:**
  - **Danica (Xochitl)** — 16 years old. Day student at Bishop’s College School, newly back after leaving her parents’ home at the beginning of Christmas break. Still fragile but functioning.
  - **Ms. Renée Lapointe** — BCS school counsellor. Francophone Quebecer, mid-thirties, bilingual, calm, practical, and warm without being sentimental.
- **Offstage / referenced:** Orin Lorin Junior, Dr. Margaret Whitfield, Mme Solange Beaudoin, Geneviève Lavoie, Chloe Mackenzie, the DPJ worker, Danica’s parents, the SQ officers, Ferme Clé staff.
- **Location:** Bishop’s College School, Sherbrooke — hallway outside the student-services office, then Ms. Lapointe’s counselling office.
- **When:** **Wednesday afternoon**, two days after Danica’s Monday return to BCS, one day after the Tuesday morning police nightmare on the couch at Ferme Clé. The DPJ home visit is still scheduled for Friday.
- **Tone:** Quiet, careful, professional, emotionally safe. This is not a dramatic therapy breakthrough; it is the beginning of an adult support relationship at school.

---

## Scene Purpose

This scene gives Danica her first meeting with **Ms. Renée Lapointe**, the school counsellor Dr. Whitfield named during Monday’s office meeting. It should show Danica entering the meeting wary but not blindsided, because Dr. Whitfield already gave her Ms. Lapointe’s name.

The scene should establish:

1. Danica now has one more safe adult at BCS.
2. Ms. Lapointe is not trying to take control away from Danica.
3. The school understands that Danica’s situation is ongoing, not solved because she is back in uniform.
4. Danica admits, carefully, that she had a nightmare about the police coming back.
5. They make a practical school-day safety plan for panic, parent contact, and pickups.

---

## Continuity to This Point

- **Friday before Christmas break:** Danica left her parents’ home with Orin after her parents made racist remarks in Japanese.
- **Saturday morning:** Police came to Ferme Clé because Danica’s parents reported her missing. The officers confirmed she was safe and explained the DPJ / simple emancipation situation.
- **Monday morning:** Danica returned to BCS, met Dr. Whitfield, learned her mother had called the school twice, received emergency replacement uniform and supplies, and was referred to Ms. Renée Lapointe.
- **Monday evening:** Danica fell asleep on the couch at Ferme Clé while studying with Orin. He covered her with a quilt and did not wake her.
- **Tuesday morning at six:** Danica screamed in her sleep from a police nightmare. Orin rushed from his cornflakes to her side, grounded her, and helped her get ready for BCS and McGill. Danica wrote a thank-you note to Madame Hélène for dinner and learned that a Ferme Clé logo truck would pick her up if Orin had class.
- **Wednesday afternoon:** This scene.

# Scene Outline

## Beat 1: The Note from the Office

It is Wednesday afternoon, sometime after lunch but before the final part of the school day. Danica is in class, trying to focus. She has her BCS agenda open and her new notebook in front of her.

A note comes from the office or the teacher quietly tells her:

> “Danica, Ms. Lapointe is ready to see you.”

Because Dr. Whitfield gave her the name on Monday, Danica recognizes it. She still feels her stomach tighten, but it is not the shock of an unknown summons.

She gathers her things slowly. Geneviève or Chloe may catch her eye from across the room. Danica gives the smallest nod: she is okay.

---

## Beat 2: The Walk to Student Services

The hallway feels longer than usual. Danica passes familiar BCS details:

- lockers,
- winter boots by a classroom door,
- a noticeboard with club announcements,
- the smell of floor polish and wet wool,
- other students moving normally around her.

The normality is almost harder than the crisis. Everyone else’s Wednesday is just Wednesday.

Danica stops outside the student-services office and looks at the name plate:

> **Renée Lapointe, M.A. — School Counsellor**

She knocks.

---

## Beat 3: Ms. Lapointe’s Office

Ms. Renée Lapointe opens the door herself.

She is not overly soft, not dramatic, not pitying. She is steady. Her office is warm but not childish: two chairs, a small sofa, a desk, a kettle, a box of tissues, a plant on the windowsill, a basket of fidget objects that looks like it is for any student, not specifically for Danica.

> “Danica? Bonjour. Come in.”

Danica enters.

Ms. Lapointe gestures to the seating without forcing a choice.

> “You can sit wherever you want. Chair, sofa, by the window. Whatever feels easiest.”

Danica chooses the chair nearest the door.

Ms. Lapointe notices, but does not comment.

---

## Beat 4: Consent and Control

Ms. Lapointe starts with boundaries.

> “Dr. Whitfield told me only what I need to know to support you at school. I know there is a DPJ file. I know there was a police visit. I know you are not living at home right now. I do not know every detail, and you do not have to tell me everything today.”

Danica listens carefully.

Ms. Lapointe continues:

> “This office is not a police station. It is not court. It is not your parents’ living room. My job is not to make you say things before you are ready. My job is to help you get through the school day safely and to make sure the adults here do not make things worse.”

Danica’s shoulders lower a fraction.

> “Okay.”

---

## Beat 5: Why Today

Ms. Lapointe explains why she asked to see Danica today rather than waiting.

> “Dr. Whitfield said I would probably reach out by Wednesday. I wanted to keep that promise. When adults say a day and then disappear, it can make things feel less safe.”

That lands with Danica.

She nods.

> “She wrote your name down for me.”
>
> “Good. Then I was not a surprise.”
>
> “No. Not completely.”

---

## Beat 6: The Police Nightmare

Ms. Lapointe does not ask immediately, *What happened?* Instead, she asks:

> “How has your body been since Monday?”

Danica frowns slightly.

> “My body?”
>
> “Sleep. Appetite. Stomach. Headaches. Shaking. Crying when you did not expect to cry. Things like that.”

Danica looks down at her hands.

> “I screamed yesterday morning.”

Ms. Lapointe stays still.

> “In your sleep?”
>
> “Yes.”

A pause.

> “Do you remember the dream?”

Danica nods once.

> “The police came back.”

Ms. Lapointe does not interrupt.

Danica continues, quietly:

> “They came to the farm again. Early. Before school. I was on the couch and I couldn’t get up. They said I had to go back. They said this time I had to go with them.”

Her voice thins at the end.

> “And they took me.”

Ms. Lapointe lets a few seconds pass.

> “That sounds terrifying.”

Danica’s eyes fill, but she does not cry hard.

> “It felt real.”
>
> “Nightmares often do. Especially after police have actually come once.”

That sentence helps because it does not make Danica feel foolish. It connects the dream to something real without saying the dream was true.

---

## Beat 7: Orin’s Response, Carefully Told

Ms. Lapointe asks only enough.

> “What happened when you woke?”

Danica answers carefully, protective of Orin but not lying.

> “Orin was eating breakfast. Cornflakes. He heard me scream and came running.”

Ms. Lapointe notes the name but does not pounce on it.

> “What did he do?”

Danica thinks.

> “He told me where I was. Ferme Clé. Tuesday morning. On the couch. He said nobody was taking me from the couch that morning.”

A pause.

> “He asked before touching me.”

Ms. Lapointe nods once, approving but understated.

> “That was a good thing for him to do.”

Danica looks up quickly, as if she expected suspicion.

Ms. Lapointe keeps her voice even.

> “I am not saying that means everything is simple. It does not. But in that moment, asking before touching you was the right thing.”

Danica nods.

---

## Beat 8: School Safety Plan

Ms. Lapointe shifts gently toward practical help.

> “Let’s make a plan for school. Not a huge plan. Just what you do if fear hits during the day.”

They write it down together.

### Danica’s BCS Safety Plan

1. **If Danica feels panicked in class:**
   - She may ask to go to the washroom or student services.
   - She does not need to explain in front of the class.
   - Teachers will be told discreetly that she has permission to step out.

2. **If Danica worries her parents are at school:**
   - She goes directly to the main office or student services.
   - She does not go outside to meet anyone.
   - Mme Beaudoin, Dr. Whitfield, or Ms. Lapointe handles the adult at the door.

3. **If pickup plans change:**
   - Danica waits inside the school building until the plan is verified.
   - She checks the driver’s name.
   - She checks that the vehicle is expected.
   - If it is a Ferme Clé truck, she checks for the farm logo on the side.
   - If anything feels wrong, she goes back inside and calls Orin or the office.

4. **If she has another nightmare and comes to school shaken:**
   - She can ask to see Ms. Lapointe before first class, at lunch, or after school.
   - She can also ask Mme Beaudoin to send a note.

5. **For Friday’s DPJ home visit:**
   - Danica can come to Ms. Lapointe before or after school if she needs to talk.
   - The school will not surprise her with calls to her parents.

Danica reads the list twice.

> “Can I keep this?”
>
> “Yes. I’ll make you a copy. I’ll keep one here only if you want me to.”
>
> “You can keep one.”

That is a small act of trust.

---

## Beat 9: Parents and the Front Office

Danica asks the question she has been holding.

> “What if my mother comes here?”

Ms. Lapointe answers plainly.

> “Then you do not handle her. Adults handle adults.”

Danica looks at her.

Ms. Lapointe repeats it, slower:

> “Adults handle adults. You are a student here. Your job is to go to class, eat lunch, do your homework, and tell us if you are afraid. It is not your job to manage your mother at the school door.”

Danica almost cries at that, because no one has ever phrased it that way.

> “Okay.”

---

## Beat 10: Orin and the Pickup Boundary

Ms. Lapointe addresses the pickup issue without accusing.

> “Dr. Whitfield told me you came in Monday with the adult friend you are staying with. You have said his name is Orin.”

Danica nods.

> “Is he usually the person driving you?”
>
> “Yes. But yesterday he had class at four, so a staff member from the farm was going to pick me up if he couldn’t. In a truck with the Ferme Clé logo.”

Ms. Lapointe writes that down.

> “Good. Then we make that part formal. If Orin is not picking you up, the office should know who is. Name, vehicle, and pickup point. Not because we distrust you — because after a police nightmare, predictable details help your nervous system.”

Danica nods.

> “I liked knowing about the logo.”
>
> “Of course you did. It gave your eyes something to check.”

---

## Beat 11: The Question of Home

Ms. Lapointe asks gently:

> “Do you want to talk about your parents today?”

Danica is silent for a long time.

> “Not all of it.”
>
> “Then not all of it.”

Danica gives only one piece:

> “They said things in Japanese because they thought Orin would not understand.”

Ms. Lapointe’s expression changes only slightly.

> “And he did understand?”
>
> “Yes.”
>
> “And you understood enough?”
>
> “Enough.”

Ms. Lapointe does not ask her to repeat the words.

> “You do not have to translate cruelty in order for me to believe it happened.”

Danica’s eyes fill again.

> “Thank you.”

---

## Beat 12: Returning to the Day

The meeting has taken maybe thirty to forty minutes. Not too long; enough.

Ms. Lapointe checks the time.

> “Do you want to go back to class, or do you need five minutes here before you go?”

Danica thinks.

> “Five minutes.”
>
> “Take them.”

They sit quietly. Ms. Lapointe does paperwork at her desk without watching Danica too closely. This lets Danica experience the office as a place where she can exist without performing distress.

After five minutes, Danica stands.

> “I can go back.”
>
> “Good. Bring the safety plan with you. If you want to come back tomorrow, tell Mme Beaudoin or send me an email from your school account.”

Danica takes the folded paper.

---

## Beat 13: Hallway Text to Orin

In the hallway, Danica stops near the lockers and sends Orin a brief text.

Possible text:

> Met Ms. Lapointe. She was good. We made a safety plan. I’m okay.

Orin may not answer immediately because he is in class or lab. That is okay. The point is that Danica can report the support without needing him to rescue her from it.

If he does answer later, keep it simple:

> Good. Proud of you. See you after school.

---

## Beat 14: Closing Image

Danica walks back toward class with the folded safety plan in her blazer pocket.

She is still scared. Her parents still exist. The DPJ visit is still coming Friday. Orin still has McGill classes he cannot skip every day. Nothing has magically resolved.

But now, inside BCS, there is another door she can knock on.

---

# What This Scene Establishes

1. **Ms. Renée Lapointe is now onstage** and becomes Danica’s school-based support adult.
2. **Danica discloses the police nightmare** from Tuesday morning, specifically the fear that police would return and take her back.
3. **Orin’s response is seen through Danica’s report**: he rushed from breakfast, grounded her, and asked before touching her.
4. **BCS creates a concrete safety plan** for panic, parent appearances, pickup verification, and Friday DPJ stress.
5. **The Ferme Clé logo truck detail becomes part of school pickup protocol.** Danica is allowed to use visible verification to feel safer.
6. **Ms. Lapointe validates without overstepping.** She does not demand full disclosure or force Danica to translate her parents’ racist remarks.
7. **Danica leaves with a folded safety plan** — a physical object that represents institutional support.
