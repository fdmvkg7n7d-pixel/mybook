# Orin Lorin Junior and Danica — February 15 — The Morning After — Scene Outline

## SCENE INFORMATION

- **Characters:** Orin Lorin Junior (nineteen years old), Danica (seventeen years old — turned seventeen yesterday)
- **Offstage / referenced characters:** the farm chef, Gérard (farm stable hand), Danica's parents (absent, as they have been for fifty-seven days)
- **Location:** Ferme Clé, Quebec — the barn loft (bedroom, bathroom, dining room) and the farmhouse (kitchen)
- **When:** February 15 — a Wednesday — the morning after Valentine's Day / Danica's seventeenth birthday
- **Status:** Revised scene outline
- **Tone:** Tender, raw, emotionally overwhelming — the quiet devastation and strange wonder of waking up in a barn loft bed scattered with crushed rose blossoms, in a body that does not feel the same as it did yesterday. This is not a scene about regret. This is a scene about the weight of an irreversible choice made by a girl who has cried before — she cried when her parents could not accept Orin — but this crying is different. This crying is about her body. About what she gave. About the fact that she is not a virgin anymore and the change is in her cells and she cannot think her way around it.

---

## SCENE PURPOSE

This scene outlines **the morning after Danica and Orin sleep together for the first time** — February 15, a Wednesday, the day after Valentine's Day, the day after her seventeenth birthday.

Danica has cried before. She cried the night her parents looked at Orin and said things in Japanese they thought he wouldn't understand. She cried when she understood that they would not accept him, would not accept her choice, would not accept the man she loved because of what he looked like and where he came from. She cried for the loss of her family's love — not the loss of her family, because they are still alive, still in Sherbrooke, still silent for fifty-seven days — but the loss of their acceptance, which is a different kind of loss, a loss that lives alongside the people who caused it, a loss you can see every time you close your eyes and hear your mother's voice saying words you wish you didn't understand.

But this morning is different. This morning she is not crying about her parents. She is crying about her body. She is crying because she is not a virgin anymore, and the fact is physical and irreversible and it is inside her like a new organ, and she can feel it — the soreness, the tenderness, the specific geography of a body that has been known by another person — and the feeling is so big and so new that it spills out of her, and Orin is there, and his hand finds hers, and they speak in Japanese, the language they share, the language her parents spoke when they rejected him, the language he learned in four years of Japanese across two high schools — one in Atlanta, one in Vaughan — so that he could answer them in their own words, and now it is the language they use when the English is not enough, when the feeling is too big for English, when the words need to be smaller and more precise and more intimate, and Japanese is that for them — not the language of her parents' rejection but the language of their own choosing, the language of the two people who chose each other.

She planned last night. She wrote clues. She asked the chef. She scattered pink rose blossoms on the bed. She brought her uniform to the loft bathroom, and she brought his clothes too, because the loft was hers last night and she wanted it to hold everything — the dinner, the conversation, the bedroom, and the morning after — and in the morning they would go to the house for breakfast, and the going would be part of it too. She planned for the morning. She thought about the morning. She brought her uniform and she brought his clothes and she put them in the bathroom, and the bathroom is through the bedroom door and past the kitchen, and the uniform is hanging on the hook on the back of the door, and his jeans and his shirt are folded on the counter, and she brought them because she knew this night would end in a morning and the morning would require clothes and she did not want to walk across the yard in his shirt to get them. She planned for all of it. She did not plan for the crying. In the morning they would go to the house for breakfast, but the clothes were already here, and the waiting was the plan.

## SCENE ARC / BEATS

### Beat 1: Waking — The Loft — The Blossoms

- She wakes up in the loft bedroom. The room is not any room she has ever woken up in. Not the spare bedroom at the farmhouse. Not her bedroom in Sherbrooke. This is the loft — the room she scattered with pink rose blossoms, the room she led Orin into with her hand around his left hand, the room where she closed the door and did not open it again until morning.

- The blossoms are crushed. The rose petals that were full and soft and scattered across the quilt are now flattened — pressed into the fabric by the weight of two bodies, by the movement of the night, by sleep and by what came before sleep. Some are still pink. Some are bruised, the colour deepened to purple where her shoulder pressed them into the mattress. Some are on the floor, knocked off the bed in the dark. Some are in her hair — she can feel them, small dry petals caught in the strands behind her ear. The room smells like roses and like him and like the faint must of a space that was closed for months before she opened it last night.

- The body knows before the mind catches up. There is a soreness between her legs — not sharp, not terrible, but present, a low steady ache that says *something happened here, something changed, something is not the same as it was seven hours ago.* And there is tenderness in places she did not know could be tender — the inside of her thighs, the small of her back, her lips — and the tenderness is evidence, and the evidence is physical, and the physicality of it is what undoes her, because she has spent eight weeks living in her mind — in crisis, in planning, in survival — and now her body is speaking, and what it is saying is: *you are not the same person you were yesterday.*

- Orin is behind her. His body curved against hers, his arm around her waist, his breath warm and slow on the back of her neck. She does not move, because moving means acknowledging that she is awake, and acknowledging that she is awake means acknowledging where she is, and the where-she-is is in a body that is not the same as it was yesterday, and the not-sameness is not subtle, and it is not abstract, and it is not something she can think her way around, and the knowing of this — the full physical knowing of it — opens something inside her, and what comes out is tears.

### Beat 2: The Tears — Different from Before

- She cries. She has cried before — she cried the night her parents could not accept Orin, she cried when she understood that the people who raised her would not love the man she loved, she cried in a way that was sharp and specific and aimed at a source. This crying is different. This crying has no target. This crying is not about someone else's failure. This crying is about her own body, and the fact that it has changed, and the change is irreversible, and she chose it, and the choosing is the thing that breaks her open, because she has made so many irreversible choices in eight weeks — leaving home, moving to a farm, going back to BCS on her own terms, surviving a police visit, surviving a DPJ inspection — and all of those choices were made under pressure, under crisis, under the urgent necessity of survival. This choice was not made under pressure. This choice was made in a loft bedroom with pink rose blossoms on the bed and a man who waited eight weeks for her to open the door. This choice was made in love. And the love is what makes the crying different, because the love means she cannot blame anyone, cannot point to an outside force, cannot say *they made me do it.* She did this. She wanted this. She led him by the left hand into this room and closed the door. And now it is morning and she is not a virgin anymore and the tears are for the weight of a thing she chose, and the weight is not regret, and the weight is not sadness, and the weight is not small.

- She does not sob. She does not make a sound. The tears come out silently and the bed shakes, just barely, the way a bed shakes when someone in it is crying and trying not to be felt. A crushed rose petal is stuck to her cheek — pressed there by the pillow, damp with tears, the pink bleeding slightly against her skin like watercolour.

- Orin's arm tightens around her. His hand moves — from her waist to her stomach, flat, warm, pressing against her through the sheet. He does not speak. He does not ask what is wrong. He does not say *"Are you okay?"* or *"Don't cry."* He puts his hand on her stomach and he leaves it there, and the hand is warm and still and not asking her to explain.

- She cries and the crying goes on, and the crying is for her body and for the change and for the soreness between her legs and for the small red marks on her collarbone she has not seen yet but can feel, and it is also for last night — the whole night, the clues and the chef and the Japanese dinner and the strawberry shortcake and the moment she took his hand and stood and led him into this room — and it is also for yesterday — the breakfast and the miso soup and the silver chain and the card and the silent phone and her mother not calling — and it is also for December 20, the night she walked out, the night she made the first irreversible choice, and every irreversible choice since, and all of them piling up inside her, one on top of the other, until the pile is so high that the only direction left is out, and out is tears, and the tears are falling onto a pillow that smells like roses and like him.

### Beat 3: What She Says — In Japanese

- When the crying slows — it does not stop, it slows, the way rain slows — she becomes aware of things. The silver chain against her collarbone. The crushed petals everywhere — in the sheets, in her hair, on his arm, one pressed flat against the inside of her wrist. The grey light through the barn window.

- She wipes her face. The petal on her cheek comes away on her fingers — damp, translucent, the pink faded almost to white where her tears soaked through. She looks at it.

> *"Kanashikunai,"* she says. *I'm not sad.*

- Japanese. The language comes out before the English does, because the English feels too blunt for this, too wide, and the Japanese is narrower and more precise, and the Japanese is the language they use with each other when the English is not enough. They speak Japanese together — not always, not exclusively, but at the moments when the feeling outgrows the English, when the words need to be smaller and sharper and closer to the bone. He learned Japanese for four years across two high schools — one in Atlanta, one in Vaughan. She grew up with it in her parents' house. And now it belongs to them — not to her parents, not to the rejection, not to the living room in Sherbrooke where her mother said things she cannot forgive — but to this room, this bed, this morning.

> *"Wakatteru,"* he says. *I know.*

- His Japanese is quiet and careful, the same way his English is quiet and careful, but there is a softness in it that his English does not always have — a tenderness that comes from speaking a language you chose to learn, a language that was not given to you by birth but by study, by discipline, by four years of sitting in classrooms in Atlanta and Vaughan and learning kanji and grammar and the shape of words that would one day let him answer a girl's parents in their own tongue. And now the same tongue is the tongue he uses to say *I know* to that girl in a bed of crushed roses, and the circle of it — from classroom to living room to barn loft — is not lost on either of them.

> *"Chigau kanji ga suru,"* she says. *I feel different.*

> *"Sō da ne,"* he says. *You do. A little. You do.*

- He does not say *"But you're still you"* in either language, because that would diminish what happened, and he will not diminish it. She gave him something last night that she had never given anyone, and the giving changed her, and the change is not bad and it is not nothing, and he will not pretend it away with comfort.

### Beat 4: The Body — Getting Out of the Loft Bed

- She sits up. The quilt falls to her waist. Rose blossoms fall off her — off her shoulders, off her chest, off the sheets — and scatter onto the mattress and the floor. She is not wearing anything. The loft is warm — the heat must have stayed on all night — and the warmth means she did not need to get dressed in the dark.

- The soreness shifts as she sits — moves from a low ache to something more specific, more located, and she winces. Orin sees it. He is sitting up now too, and his hand moves to her hip, not pulling, just resting.

> *"Itai?"* he asks. *Does it hurt?*

> *"Sukoshi,"* she says. *A little.*

> *"Futsū da yo,"* he says. *That's normal.*

- A fact. Not a reassurance. He does not say *gomen* — *sorry* — because he is not sorry, and she is not sorry, and sorry is the wrong word in any language.

- She looks down at herself. The silver chain. The small red marks on her collarbone — his mouth, last night, a place he kissed that left a trace. She did not know that could happen. She touches one with her fingertip.

> *"Kore wa —"*

> *"Kieru yo,"* he says. *It'll fade.*

- She nods. She swings her legs over the side of the bed. Her feet touch the cold hardwood. A rose petal is stuck to the bottom of her foot. She peels it off. She stands. She is steady. The soreness is there but it does not make her unsteady.

### Beat 5: The Shower

- She goes to the bathroom. The loft has a bathroom — small, functional, a shower stall and a toilet and a sink with a mirror above it, the kind of bathroom you put in a finished loft for guests who never came. The door is ajar. She pushes it open.

- Her uniform is hanging on the hook on the back of the bathroom door. The BCS crest, the kilt, the knee socks, the blazer — all there, all hung neatly, the way she arranged them yesterday afternoon when she brought them from the farmhouse. She brought her uniform to the loft. She planned for this morning. She planned for the fact that she would need to get dressed and go to school, and she did not want to walk across the yard in his shirt to get her clothes, and so she carried them across the yard before he came home, before she scattered the roses, and she put them in the bathroom and closed the door and forgot about them until now. She planned for the morning. She did not plan for the crying.

- On the counter, folded neatly: his clothes. His jeans. His shirt. She brought those too — carried them from the farmhouse in the same trip, hung them over her arm while she walked across the yard in winter boots, placed them on the bathroom counter and smoothed the wrinkles out. She brought his clothes because she knew he would need them in the morning, and she knew he would not want to walk across the yard to get them, and the bringing was a kind of care that she did not name at the time, because naming it would have meant acknowledging that she was planning for the morning after, and the morning after was too far ahead to look at, and so she just did it — the way she does most things, quietly, practically, without calling attention to the act.

- She turns on the shower. The water takes a moment to warm — the pipes are old, the loft is not used, the hot water has to travel from wherever the boiler is. While she waits, she looks at herself in the mirror. Her face is blotchy from crying. The faint pink streak on her cheekbone where the rose petal bled. Her hair is a mess — tangled, with dried petals caught in it. The silver chain. The red marks on her collarbone. She looks like someone who spent the night in a bed of flowers with a man, and that is what she is, and the mirror does not lie, and she does not look away.

- The water is warm. She steps in. The shower stall is large — the loft was built for guests, and the bathroom was built to match, and there is room enough for two people to stand under the water without crowding, without pressing against each other just to fit. She is alone for exactly thirty seconds before the door opens and Orin steps in behind her.

- He does not ask if he can join. She does not tell him to leave. He steps in and the water hits both of them and the warmth is everywhere and his hands are on her shoulders — not pulling, not pressing, just there, just resting, and the water runs over her collarbone and over the red marks and over the silver chain, and the chain gets wet and stays, and the water is warm and his hands are warm and the warmth is what she needs, because the morning is cold and grey and the crying has left her hollow and the hollow needs to be filled with something, and the something is this — the water, and his hands, and the spaciousness of the stall, and the closeness.

- She leans back against him. Her head against his chest. The water falls on both of them. His arms wrap around her — not tight, not urgent, just holding. The soreness is still there, between her legs, and the water runs over it and does not erase it, and the not-erasing is honest, and she appreciates the honesty, because nothing about this morning is going to erase what happened last night, and the shower is not a washing-away, it is a being-in, it is a standing-under-water-together, it is the simplest possible intimacy — two people in a small space letting warm water fall on them — and the simplicity is what she needs after the enormity of the night.

- She reaches for the soap. He reaches for the shampoo. They do these things for each other, and for themselves, and the doing is quiet and unhurried, and the shower takes longer than it needs to because neither of them wants to step out of the water and back into the morning, and the water is a border between the night and the day, and they stand in it as long as they can.

- When they turn off the water, she stands in the steam and she breathes, and the breath is deep and full and it fills the hollow that the crying left, and she is not okay — she is overwhelmed and sore and different — but the hollow is not as deep as it was, and the not-as-deep is enough, and the enough is what the morning has to offer, and she takes it.

### Beat 6: Getting Dressed — The Uniform

- She towels off. He towels off. The bathroom is full of steam and the mirror is fogged and the air is warm and close. She reaches for her uniform on the hook — the BCS kilt, the shirt, the knee socks, the blazer. She puts it on the way she puts it on every morning. The kilt first. Then the shirt — she buttons it carefully, and the top button covers the silver chain and the red marks, and the evidence disappears under white cotton, and she looks in the fogged mirror and she looks exactly like she looked yesterday — the same girl, the same uniform — and she is not the same girl, and the not-sameness is invisible, and the invisibility is its own kind of loneliness, because no one at BCS will know, and she does not want to tell anyone, and the not-wanting is not shame — it is privacy, it is the knowledge that some things are so big and so new that telling them makes them smaller, and she does not want this to be smaller.

- Orin gets dressed beside her. His jeans. His shirt. The clothes she brought from the farmhouse and folded on the counter. They dress in the same bathroom, in the steam, the large room giving them space to move around each other without crowding, and the geometry is intimate, and the intimacy is now ordinary, because after last night there is no version of closeness that feels too close, and the bathroom that was made for one guest now holds two, and the holding is what the loft does, and the loft has been doing it since last night, and it does it still.

### Beat 7: The Walk to the House — Breakfast in the Kitchen

- They go down the loft stairs. Her winter boots are at the bottom — she left them there when she changed into the red heels, and they have been sitting here all night, and she steps into them now. Orin pulls on his shoes. They walk across the yard to the house. The February cold is sharp and the sky is white and the snow from last night is thin and patchy on the ground, and the walk is short — they have made it so many times — but this morning the walk is different, because she is in her BCS uniform under her winter coat and the silver chain is under her collar and the soreness is between her legs, and every step reminds her of last night, and the reminding is not cruel, it is just present, the way the cold is present, the way his hand reaching for hers is present.

- The house is cold — the heat is on low, it is always on low at night, and the kitchen is dim and the air is still and it feels like walking into a room that has been waiting. The porch light is still on from last night. She reaches for the switch and turns it off as she passes.

- Orin goes to the stove. The farmhouse kitchen — the same kitchen where he made her miso soup yesterday morning, the same kitchen island where she sat in his shirt and ate every grain of rice, the same counter where she gave him the first clue. He takes out eggs and butter and bread, and she stands beside him and makes toast, and they make breakfast together the way they have made breakfast every morning for eight weeks — reaching around each other, passing things, not speaking, not needing to.

- He cooks eggs. She makes toast. The kitchen fills with the smell of butter and bread and the ordinary everyday sound of a pan on a burner, and the ordinary is what she needs, because last night was not ordinary and this morning is not ordinary and the eggs are ordinary, and the toast is ordinary, and sitting across from him at the kitchen island — the same island where she sat yesterday and ate miso soup and gave him the first clue — is both ordinary and not, because the island is the same and the chairs are the same but the two people sitting in them are not the same as they were seven hours ago.

- She eats half the toast. She eats one egg. She is not hungry. The tears have taken her appetite and the soreness has taken her comfort and the morning has taken the version of herself that could eat a full breakfast without thinking about it.

> *"Motto tabenakya,"* he says. *You have to eat more.*

> *"Onaka ga suitesuki,"* she says. *I'm not hungry.*

> *"Wakatteru. Demo tabenakya."* *I know. But you have to eat.*

- She takes another bite. It is an act of obedience, not appetite. She chews. She swallows. The routine holds.

### Beat 8: Before She Leaves — The Threshold Moment

- She rinses her plate. She puts it in the drying rack. She picks up her school bag from the bench by the front door — she brought it to the loft yesterday afternoon when she was setting up, and brought it to the house when they came over for breakfast, and it is ready, and she is ready, or she is not ready, but she is going anyway, because going is what you do, because the world keeps going, because the morning after is not a place you can stay.

- She stands at the front door. The same front door she walks through every morning. The porch light is off now — she turned it off. The February morning is grey outside.

- She turns around. Orin is leaning against the kitchen doorframe, watching her. His face is open in a way she has never seen it — unguarded, the holding released, the quiet not a wall but a window.

> *"Kono mama dekinai,"* she says. *I don't know how to do this.*

> *"Gakkō ni ikeba ii,"* he says. *Just go to school. Kaette kure. Watashi wa koko ni iru.* *Come home. I'll be here.*

- *Watashi wa koko ni iru.* I'll be here. In Japanese it sounds different than in English — the words are simpler, the promise is the same. I will be here when you come back. The loft will be here. The house will be here. I will be here.

> *"Suiyōbi da yo,"* she says. *It's Wednesday.* *"Mukae ni konakute mo ii."* *You don't have to pick me up.*

> *"Mukae ni iku,"* he says. *I'm picking you up.*

- She does not argue. She knows why. The morning after is not a morning for Gérard's truck and the feed receipts on the passenger seat and the silence of a man who never asks questions. The morning after is a morning for Orin's car and Orin's hand on the gear shift and Orin's quiet presence in the driver's seat, and she is grateful that he does not give her the option of anything else.

- She opens the door. The cold hits her — the February cold, the same cold that hit them last night when they walked between the barn and the house. She steps onto the porch and turns back. And she says:

> *"Orin."*

> *"Nani?"* *Yeah?*

> *"Kinō no yoru —"* *Last night —*

- She stops. What is she going to say? *Last night was the most important night of my life?* Too big. *Last night I gave you something I can never get back and I'm not sorry?* Too dramatic. *Last night I stopped being a virgin and now I'm standing in a barn in a school uniform and I feel like my body is a house that someone just moved into and I'm still learning where the furniture is?* Too much.

> *"Arigatō,"* she says. *Thank you.*

- It is not enough. It is the wrong word in either language. But it is the word she has, and she says it, and he hears it, and he does not say *"Arawanakute mo ii"* — *You don't have to thank me* — because he has said that before and she has not believed him and this morning is not the morning for that argument. He comes to the door and puts his hand on the side of her face — his palm against her cheek, where the rose petal left its streak, where the tears dried — and he holds it there for a moment, and then he lets go, and she walks down the porch steps into the February morning.

### Beat 9: Closing Image — The Loft After She's Gone

- The loft is quiet. The bedroom door is open. The quilt is tangled and the sheets are pulled and the bed is a map of the night — the crushed rose blossoms everywhere, pressed flat where bodies lay, scattered where arms reached, a few still pink, most bruised to purple, some brown at the edges, already dying, already becoming what flowers become when they are not in water. One petal is on the nightstand where she placed it — the one she peeled off her cheek, the one that was damp with her tears.

- The bathroom is full of steam. The mirror is fogged. The shower is off but the air is warm and wet, and the hook on the back of the door is empty — her uniform is gone, his clothes are gone, and the only thing left is the damp towel hung over the rod and the faint scent of soap in the humid air.

- The dining room. The two plates are stacked. The green tea cups are cold. The strawberry — the single strawberry she did not eat — is still on the plate, wrinkled now, beginning to darken. The two cards are not on the table anymore — she took them when she dressed, and they are in her bag now, going to school with her, two white cards with red hearts in a BCS bookbag, riding in the passenger seat of Orin's car, crossing the fields toward Sherbrooke.

- Orin stands at the kitchen window and watches her walk to the car. She is in her uniform and her winter boots, carrying her school bag, and the house is behind her and the road is ahead, and she does not look back — not because looking back makes the leaving harder, but because this morning she does not need to. She is going to school. She is coming home. *Watashi wa koko ni iru,* he said. I'll be here.

- The loft holds the evidence. The crushed roses. The damp towel. The stacked plates. The dying strawberry. The shape of two people in a space made for guests who never came — a space that was empty and clean and waiting for a purpose until a seventeen-year-old girl gave it one, and the purpose was this: a dinner, a bed of flowers, a night, a morning, and a shower in a stall made for guests that held two.

- The farmhouse kitchen holds its own evidence. Two plates in the drying rack. The stovetop still warm. The kitchen island where she sat in his shirt yesterday and where she sat in her uniform this morning, and the island does not know the difference — it is just an island, and the girl sitting at it is the same girl, except that she is not.

- Outside, the snow has stopped. The fields are white. The sky is white. The footprints in the yard are from this morning — two sets going from the barn to the house, the walk they made together in the cold to have breakfast where they always have breakfast. The footprints are fresh, not yet frozen, and they will melt by noon. The morning after does not last. It just is, and then it is over, and then it is the rest of the day, and the rest of the day is waiting.

It precedes whatever comes next — Danica at school on February 15, the evening at Ferme Clé, the ongoing question of where she sleeps. The spare bedroom at the farmhouse still has her things; she has not moved out of it, she simply did not sleep there last night. The question of sleeping arrangements is unresolved and not urgent.
