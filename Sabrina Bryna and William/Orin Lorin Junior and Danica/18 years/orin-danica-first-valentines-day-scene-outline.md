# Orin Lorin Junior and Danica — First Valentine's Day at Ferme Clé — Scene Outline

## SCENE INFORMATION

- **Characters:** Orin Lorin Junior (nineteen years old), Danica (turning seventeen years old today)
- **Offstage / referenced characters:** Sabrina Bryna Key, William, Danica's parents, the BCS school counsellor, the farm staff (Gérard, the stable hand), Geneviève and Chloe (Danica's BCS friends)
- **Location:** Ferme Clé, Quebec — the horse farm between Montreal and Sherbrooke
- **When:** February 14 — Valentine's Day / Danica's seventeenth birthday — roughly eight weeks after Danica left home
- **Status:** Revised scene outline
- **Tone:** Quiet, tender, tentative — two people who are still learning how to be a couple inside a crisis, finding a single evening that belongs only to them. And underneath it: the ache of a birthday spent away from the people who raised you, even when leaving them was the right thing to do.

---

## SCENE PURPOSE

This scene outlines **Orin and Danica's first Valentine's Day together at Ferme Clé** — which is also **Danica's seventeenth birthday**.

Roughly eight weeks after Danica walked out of her parents' house in Sherbrooke on a Friday night in December — after her parents looked at Orin and said things in Japanese they thought he wouldn't understand, and he answered in flawless Japanese, and she left without packing a bag — she turns seventeen in a farmhouse she did not know existed two months ago, with a man she met in a food court in November.

Eight weeks. In eight weeks Danica has: left her parents' home, moved into a stranger's farmhouse, survived a police visit, opened a DPJ file, walked into the BCS office in civilian clothes and convinced the Dean to keep her, bought a new uniform at the campus bookstore, started seeing a school counsellor, survived a DPJ home inspection, and settled into a life that did not exist before December 20.

And now it is her birthday. Her first birthday away from her parents. Her first birthday without her mother's call, without her father's gruff *"Happy birthday,"* without the Japanese cake her mother always ordered from the bakery in Sherbrooke — the strawberry shortcake with the whipped cream and the candles and the photograph her mother took every year, every single year since she was born, standing in the same spot in the kitchen with the same expression on her face. There will be no photograph this year. There will be no strawberry shortcake. There will be no call from her mother, because her mother said things in Japanese that December night that Danica cannot forgive, and the not-calling is its own wound, and the wound is fresh, and it is her birthday, and she is seventeen, and she is not at home.

Her grandparents are dead. There is no one in Japan to notice her absence, no FaceTime call from Tokyo, no grandmother's voice saying *"Otanjōbi omedetō"* across an ocean. The silence from her parents is total. There is no one else to call.

Orin knows it is her birthday. He has known since the second week, when she mentioned it casually — the way you mention a fact about yourself when you are still learning someone, when the fact is just a fact and not yet a feeling. February fourteenth. Valentine's Day. She said it like it was an inconvenience — *"My birthday's on Valentine's Day, it's the worst"* — and Orin heard the casualness and understood that the casualness was a cover, because no one who grew up with birthday cakes and photographs says their birthday is the worst without also meaning: *I loved it once and I don't know how to love it now.*

Valentine's Day is not a holiday Orin has ever cared about. But a birthday is different. A birthday is the one day that belongs entirely to you. And this is her first one here. And he is going to make sure it is hers.

## SCENE ARC / BEATS

### Beat 1: February 14 — A Tuesday — Her Birthday — Morning
- It is Tuesday. It is a school day. It is also Danica's seventeenth birthday. And it is Valentine's Day. The three things land on top of each other like plates — birthday, Valentine's, Tuesday — and Danica does not want to ignore any of them.

- She has been back at BCS for six weeks now, commuting from Ferme Clé every morning. The routine is shared: some days Orin drives her — on the days his first class is later, he eats breakfast at the farm and takes her on the way to McGill, and she sits in his car instead of the farm truck and they drive in silence or they talk, depending on the day. On Tuesdays, his first class is at ten, so he almost always drives her. On the other days, one of the farm workers takes her — usually Gérard, the stable hand, who is always awake anyway, who drives the truck with the feed receipts still on the passenger seat and who has never once asked her a personal question. On the days Gérard isn't available, one of the other hands takes her. The pickup at 3:30 is the same: Orin when he can, Gérard when Orin can't. On Tuesdays, Orin's last class is at four, which means he can sometimes pick her up himself. The routine holds.

- She comes downstairs in his shirt — the one he gave her for Christmas, the one she sleeps in now, the one that is too big and smells like him and is the most comfortable thing she has ever worn. She is not wearing her uniform yet. Her hair is down. Her feet are bare on the hardwood floor. She has not brushed her teeth. She is seventeen and she is not ready for the day and she is coming downstairs anyway because she smells something impossible from the kitchen.

- This morning, Danica comes downstairs in his shirt and the kitchen is not dark. The light is on. The stove is on. And Orin is standing at the counter in his pyjamas — the grey ones, the ones she has seen him wear every morning for eight weeks because she is always awake before he leaves for McGill and he is always in those pyjamas, and today he is still in them, which means he has not dressed yet, which means he is not leaving yet, which means his first class is not until ten and he has time, and he is using the time to stand at the stove and cook.

- She stops in the doorway. She sees the stove. She sees the pot. She smells it before she sees it — rice. Miso soup. The soft, warm, impossible smell of a Japanese breakfast, the smell of her mother's kitchen on a February morning, the smell of the thing she did not think anyone in this house could make.

- He heard her on the stairs. He does not turn around. He says, without looking up from the pot:

> *"Happy birthday."*

- Two words. Simple. He does not sing. He does not make a fuss. He says it the way he says most things — quietly, directly, like it is a fact and not a performance. But he is here. He is still in his pyjamas. His first class on Tuesdays is not until ten, and he chose to spend the hours before it in this kitchen, at this stove, making her breakfast instead of driving to Montreal early. And he made rice and miso soup and a soft-cooked egg — the way Japanese mothers do, the way her mother did, the way no one in this house has ever done before — and he learned how, which means he looked it up, which means he thought about it, which means he wanted to get it right, and the wanting is the thing she cannot look at directly, because looking at it directly would mean acknowledging what it costs a nineteen-year-old man to learn how to make a stranger's mother's breakfast, and the cost is love, and the word is too big for the kitchen, and she is not ready to say it, and he is not asking her to.

- She sits down at the kitchen island. He puts the bowl in front of her. Rice. Miso soup. Soft-cooked egg. The same breakfast. The same three things. She looks at it and her throat closes and she does not cry — she is not a crier — but her hands are not steady when she picks up the chopsticks.

> *"You learned this,"* she says.

> *"Yeah."*

> *"For me."*

> *"Yeah."*

- She eats. It tastes like home. It tastes like the thing she left. It tastes like the thing her mother will not make for her today, because her mother is not calling, and the breakfast is here instead, on a kitchen island in a farmhouse in Quebec, made by a man who looked up a recipe so that a girl he met in November could have rice and miso soup on her birthday. She eats all of it. Every grain of rice. Every sip of soup. The egg is soft and warm and perfect and she does not tell him that, because telling him would be too much, and the eating is the telling, and he watches her eat and he knows.

- And then she reaches into the pocket of his shirt — the one he gave her for Christmas, the one she slept in, the one she's still wearing — and she takes out a folded piece of paper, and she slides it across the kitchen island counter to him.

> *"First clue,"* she says.

- He looks at her. He looks at the paper. He picks it up. He unfolds it. In her handwriting — neat, careful, the handwriting of someone who learned kanji before cursive — it says:

> *"Don't follow this until tonight. But when you do, start here. This is where you made me miso soup. Check where we had our first dinner."*

- He reads it. He reads it again. He looks up at her. She is sitting across the kitchen island in his Christmas shirt, her hair down, her feet bare, and she has just given him a clue on her birthday, and he does not know what it means yet, and she is not going to tell him, because the clue is not for now. The clue is for later. The clue is the first step of something she has planned, and she is giving it to him at breakfast because the giving is the start, and the start belongs here, at this island, in this kitchen, where he made her miso soup and she ate every grain.

> *"Tonight,"* she says. *"After your last class. After you drive home. Follow them then."*

- He folds the note. He puts it in his pocket. He does not ask what the other clues are. He does not ask where they lead. He looks at her, and she looks at him, and something passes between them — not a secret, not a promise, just the knowledge that tonight is not over, and the morning is only the beginning.

- On the kitchen island counter, next to her empty bowl: a small wrapped box — dark paper, no bow, no ribbon, just a small square of dark paper with a piece of tape. Next to it, a white envelope. Her name on the front, in his handwriting — small, careful, the handwriting of someone who thinks before he writes.

- She picks up the envelope. She opens it. Inside, a card — white, with a red heart on the front. No glitter. No elaborate design. Just a red heart on white paper. She opens it.

> *Happy birthday. Happy Valentine's Day. You are the best thing that has happened at this farm. — O.*

- She reads it twice. Three times. *Happy birthday. Happy Valentine's Day.* Both. He named both. He did not choose one over the other. The *"Happy birthday"* is first because it is hers, because the day is hers before it is anyone else's. And then *the best thing that has happened at this farm* — not the best thing that has happened to him, the best thing that has happened at this farm, Ferme Clé, the place that was empty before she arrived, the place where he lived alone for a year, the place that was an investment property to his aunt and uncle and a sanctuary to him and nothing to anyone else until a sixteen-year-old girl walked in and filled it with her clothes in the closet and her books on the shelf and her presence in the room down the hall.

- She puts the card down. She picks up the box. She unwraps it — carefully, slowly, while he stands at the counter and does not watch. Inside the box: a thin silver chain. Simple. No pendant. No charm. Just a chain — fine, delicate, the kind of thing you forget you're wearing until you touch your collarbone and feel it there. She lifts it out of the box. She holds it up to the kitchen light. It catches the overhead bulb and throws one small spark across the ceiling.

- She puts it on. The clasp is small and her fingers are clumsy and she has to try twice, but it fastens, and the chain settles against her collarbone, and it is cold for a moment and then it is warm, and it is the first piece of jewelry anyone has given her that was not from her parents, and it is sitting against her skin where no one can see it, and she will wear it under her uniform all day — under the BCS crest and the blazer and the responsibility of being a day student who looks like she belongs — and no one will know it is there except her.

- She goes upstairs. She puts on her uniform — the BCS crest, the kilt, the knee socks, the blazer. The spare bedroom is her room now. Has been since the second week. She sleeps there, in the bed that was made up for her the first night, the bed Orin showed her to and then stepped back from, saying he'd be down the hall if she needed anything. He is down the hall. He has always been down the hall. He has never come into her room uninvited, and she has never gone into his, and the hallway between their bedrooms is seven feet of hardwood floor and silence, and the silence is respectful and the silence is kind but the silence is also a wall, and the wall has stood for eight weeks. She takes off his shirt — carefully, the way you take off something that belongs to someone else — and she folds it and sets it on the pillow, and she puts on her uniform, and the silver chain goes under the collar of her shirt, and she comes back downstairs ready for school.

- Orin drives her to school today. He made her breakfast. He gave her a present. He gave her a card. And now he is driving her to BCS in his car, not the farm truck, and the silver chain is against her collarbone, and the taste of miso soup is still on her tongue, and at the door of the car, she pauses. She always pauses. She is not good at the moment of separation, even though it happens every day, even though the routine is solid. Her hand on the door. Her eyes on Orin. He says what he always says:

> *"3:30. I'll be here."*

- She nods. She gets out. She walks through the front door of BCS. She does not look back, because she has learned not to look back, because looking back makes the leaving harder, and the leaving is already hard enough.

### Beat 2: At School — The Valentine's Day Atmosphere / Her Birthday
- BCS is a school full of teenagers on Valentine's Day. The hallways are loud with it. Girls carrying roses from boyfriends at other schools. Girls carrying roses from each other. The dining hall decorated in red and white by the social committee. A Valentine's Day bake sale at lunch. Candygrams being delivered to homerooms by student council reps with stacks of pink envelopes. The whole day is saturated in a kind of forced, cheerful romance that makes Danica feel like she is watching a movie about someone else's life.

- Geneviève and Chloe know it is her birthday. They have known since grade seven — the three of them in the same homeroom, the same English class, Danica's birthday always falling on Valentine's Day, always the girl with the birthday on the holiday, always the one who said *"My birthday's on Valentine's Day, it's the worst"* and meant it and didn't mean it at all. Four years of knowing. Four years of construction-paper cards and stolen candles and whispered singing in hallways. And this morning, before homeroom, they gave her a card — a folded piece of pink construction paper with a heart drawn in marker and both their names inside, and a cupcake from the dining hall with a single candle that Geneviève produced from her blazer pocket and lit with a lighter she wasn't supposed to have. They sang happy birthday in whispered harmonies in the hallway outside the bathroom, and Danica blew out the candle and made a wish she will not tell anyone, and for thirty seconds it felt like a birthday — a real one, the kind where people know and care and show up with construction paper and stolen fire. The same as every year. The same as grade seven.

- She is not alone. She knows she is not alone. Orin made her breakfast this morning. He gave her a chain and a card. Geneviève and Chloe sang to her in the hallway. The silver chain is against her collarbone under her blazer. But the people who are supposed to call — the people whose blood she carries — have said nothing, and before December 20, her birthday was a room full of people — her parents, her friends from school, the strawberry shortcake with the candles, the photograph her mother took every year. And today is the first birthday without any of that, and the absence sits on her chest like a weight, and she carries it through the hallways in her BCS kilt and her knee socks and her blazer, and nobody sees it, because she looks like a day student who is having a normal Tuesday.

- At lunch, Geneviève and Chloe are talking about their Valentine's plans. Geneviève's boyfriend is taking her to dinner in Sherbrooke. Chloe's mother is making a special dinner at home. They ask Danica if Orin is doing anything tonight. Danica shrugs. She does not know. She has not asked. He made her breakfast and gave her a present and a card and drove her to school, and she did not ask about tonight because asking means expecting something more, and expecting something more from Orin feels wrong — not because he wouldn't give it, but because he already gave her so much this morning that asking for more feels like taking.

- Geneviève says, *"You're allowed to want things, Danica."* Danica does not answer. She touches her collarbone under her blazer. The chain is there. She eats her lunch.

- What she does not say: *My mother hasn't called.*

- Her phone is silent all day. No call. No text. No voice message. She left her parents' house on December 20. It is February 14. That is fifty-six days. In fifty-six days, her parents have not reached out once. Not through the school. Not through the police. Not through the DPJ. And today is her birthday and the silence is so complete that it has its own sound — a low, constant hum in her ears, the sound of people who should love you choosing not to speak to you. Her grandparents are dead. There is no one in Japan. There is no one anywhere except this school and this farm and this man, and the man is in Montreal now, and the farm is an hour away, and she is standing in front of a pillar at 3:30 waiting.

- Gérard pulls up at 3:35. Orin's last class on Tuesdays doesn't end until four, and the drive from Montreal is an hour and a half, so he can't pick her up today. She gets in the truck. It smells like hay and coffee and the cold that comes in through the floorboards. He does not ask how her day was. He never does. She is grateful for this.

### Beat 3: Home — Late Afternoon — The Decision
- It is 4:15 when Danica walks through the front door of Ferme Clé. The house is empty — it is always empty in the late afternoon, Orin at McGill, the farm workers outside. She drops her schoolbag on the bench by the door. She takes off her blazer. She stands in the kitchen in her uniform shirt and kilt, and she looks at the kitchen island.

- The card is still there. The wrapping paper is still there. The empty bowl from breakfast is still there. Orin made her breakfast this morning and drove her to school, and she has been carrying his chain and his words all day, and in fifty-six days he has given her a home and a bedroom and a ride to school and a uniform and a Japanese breakfast and a silver chain and a card that says *you are the best thing that has happened at this farm*, and she has given him nothing. She has been the one who receives. She has been the one who is taken care of. She has been the crisis that moved into his house and ate his food and took up his time and his hallway and his life, and he has never once made her feel like a burden, and she knows she is not a burden, and she also knows she has not given him a single thing that he did not give first.

- She picks up the card. She reads it again. *Happy birthday. Happy Valentine's Day. You are the best thing that has happened at this farm. — O.*

- And something shifts. Not a decision exactly — more like a door opening in her chest. She has been carrying the weight of this birthday all day — the silence from her parents, the strawberry shortcake that doesn't exist, the photograph that won't be taken, the fifty-six days of nothing. And she has been letting the weight push her down. She has been letting herself be the girl who lost something, the girl who is missing something, the girl whose birthday is a wound.

- But she is also the girl who walked out of her parents' house on a Friday night in December. She is the girl who sat in a stranger's truck and drove to a farm she'd never seen. She is the girl who walked into a school office in borrowed clothes and said *I'm staying.* She is the girl who survived a police visit and a DPJ file and a counsellor and a new life in eight weeks. She has been so busy surviving that she forgot: she is someone who can do things. She is someone who can make things happen. She is someone who can surprise a person who has been surprising her since November.

- She puts the card down. She goes upstairs. She changes out of her uniform — carefully, the kilt and the blazer and the knee socks folded over the chair — and she opens the closet. In the back, behind the winter coat and the borrowed clothes, there is a garment bag. She unzips it. Inside: the red dress. Backless. She bought it on the Montreal shopping trip three weeks ago, on the second floor of a department store while Orin waited downstairs in the men's section, and she did not show it to him, and she did not tell him about it, and she carried it home in a shopping bag and hung it in the closet and zipped the garment bag over it, and she has been waiting for a reason to wear it ever since.

- Tonight is the reason.

- She puts on the dress. It fits — it fit in the store and it fits now, against her collarbone and down her back and open at the spine, the fabric heavier than she expected, the red deeper than she remembered. She puts on nude nylons — the only pair she owns, bought at a pharmacy in Saint-Hyacinthe last week, a purchase she made with her face hot and her eyes down. She puts on the red heels. Four inches. She has never walked in four-inch heels on a farm. She has never walked in four-inch heels anywhere. She stands in front of the mirror in the spare bedroom and she does not look like herself, and she also looks more like herself than she ever has — a seventeen-year-old in a red backless dress and nude nylons and red heels, standing in a farmhouse bedroom with the silver chain against her collarbone, on her birthday, about to set a trail of clues for a man to find her. This is who she is when she is not surviving. This is who she is when she chooses.

### Beat 4: Danica Prepares Her Surprise
- The idea was born before she went to sleep last night. She lay in bed in the spare bedroom in his Christmas shirt, staring at the ceiling, and she thought: *He is going to make me breakfast tomorrow. He is going to give me something. And I am going to give him something back.* The first clue she wrote then — in the dark, on a page torn from the notepad she keeps on her nightstand, by the light of her phone screen on the lowest brightness. She folded it and put it in the pocket of his shirt and went to sleep. This morning, she gave it to him. Tonight, he will follow it.

- Now she has the rest of the afternoon. Orin is at McGill. His last class ends at four. The drive is an hour and a half. He will walk through the door around five-thirty, and he will have the first clue in his pocket, and he will start here, at this kitchen island, and she needs the rest of the trail to be waiting.

- She sits at the kitchen table with a pen and the notepad they use for grocery lists and farm messages — the one with Gérard's handwriting on half the pages and Orin's on the rest. She tears off five sheets — five more, the first was already given at breakfast. Six clues in total. She writes on each one. Her handwriting is neat and careful — the handwriting of someone who learned kanji before cursive, who was taught that the shape of a letter carries weight.

- The first clue is already done — already in his pocket, already read, already folded. It told him to check where they had their first dinner. So the second note goes on the kitchen table, taped to the bottom of the bowl that sits in the center — the one that holds fruit they never eat. It says: *"The second night, I couldn't sleep. I came downstairs and sat in the dark. Check the window."*

- The second night. She couldn't sleep. She came downstairs and sat by the window in the living room and looked out at the snow, and Orin came down twenty minutes later — not because he heard her, but because he couldn't sleep either — and he sat on the other end of the couch, and they sat in the dark together without talking, and after a while she went back upstairs, and that was the first night she slept through till morning.

- The third note goes on the windowsill in the living room, next to the legal aid pamphlet that is still there even though she doesn't need it anymore. It says: *"You taught me to make coffee here on the first Saturday. Check the mugs."*

- The first Saturday. He taught her how to use the French press. She had never made coffee before — her mother drinks tea, her father drinks black coffee from a machine, and Danica drank neither. Orin showed her the ratio, the water temperature, the timing. She burned the first batch. He drank it anyway.

- The fourth note goes inside the mug she uses — the one she picked out of the cabinet the second week, the one with the chip on the rim that she chose because it was imperfect and she liked it. It says: *"You're running out of house. Check the front porch."*

- The front porch. The first morning she walked out the front door and stood on the porch and looked at the snow and the fields and the long driveway and realized that she was standing on a porch in rural Quebec and her parents were four hundred kilometres away and she was not going back. She stood there for three minutes. Orin was in the kitchen. He did not follow her out. He let her stand there. She came back inside and closed the door and that was the first time she understood that this house would not trap her the way her parents' house had.

- The fifth note goes on the front porch railing, taped to the underside where she has to reach to find it. It says: *"The chef is making dinner for two. He's bringing it to the barn. Check the barn."*

- She asked the chef this afternoon. She walked to the farmhouse kitchen and found him there — the farm's cook, the man who feeds the hands lunch every day, the man who has never spoken to her directly in eight weeks — and she asked him if he would make dinner for two and bring it up to the loft. He looked at her the way Gérard looks at her — a long, steady look, no questions — and he said, *"What kind of dinner?"* and she said, *"Japanese. A Japanese dinner. My birthday,"* and he looked at her for a long time, and then he said, *"I studied in Osaka. Two years. I can do Japanese,"* and she did not know this — she did not know the farm's cook had spent two years in a kitchen in Osaka, and the knowing is its own kind of wonder, that a man who feeds farm hands lunch every day once stood in a kitchen in Japan and learned to make the food she grew up eating — and he said, *"Six o'clock,"* and she said, *"Thank you,"* and that was the whole conversation.

- The sixth and final note goes on the barn door. She walks across the yard in the cold — the black sweater over her arm, the red dress already on, her winter boots on her feet because she cannot walk across the packed snow in four inches of red, and the heels are in her hand, carried, waiting — and she tapes the note to the door at eye level, where he can't miss it. It says: *"Come in. I'm waiting. — D."*

- She is waiting. That is the point of the clues — not the dinner, not the barn, not the chef. The clues are so he can find her. She has never been the one who is found. She has been the one who left, the one who walked away, the one who sat in a truck and drove to a place she did not know. Tonight she is the one who stays in one place and waits for someone to come to her. And she is wearing a red backless dress and nude nylons and red heels that are four inches high, and she has never worn anything like this in this house, and she bought the dress on the Montreal shopping trip three weeks ago and did not tell him about it, and it hung in the closet in the spare bedroom in a garment bag and he never asked what was in it, and tonight she is taking it out and putting it on and standing in a barn in rural Quebec in a red dress on her seventeenth birthday, and she wants him to find her.

### Beat 5: Orin Comes Home from McGill
- It is 5:35. Orin's car turns into the driveway of Ferme Clé. His last class ended at four. He drove straight from campus — an hour and a half on the highway, the February dark settling over the fields, the radio off, the cab quiet. He is carrying something he could not leave at home — the knowledge that this morning he made her breakfast in his pyjamas and watched her eat rice and miso soup and gave her a chain and a card and drove her to school, and the words on the card said what he could not say out loud, and he does not know if the words landed, and the not-knowing is its own kind of weight.

- He parks the car. He turns off the engine. He sits for a moment. The farmhouse is lit — the kitchen light is on, the porch light is on. But something is different. Something he can't name. The house feels — not empty exactly, but waiting. The way a stage feels before the actors come on. Like the house is holding its breath.

- He goes inside. The kitchen is warm. The light is on. The kitchen island counter — where he made her breakfast this morning, where she sat in his Christmas shirt and ate miso soup and gave him the first clue — is cleared. The empty bowl is gone. The wrapping paper is gone. The card is gone. He takes the first clue out of his pocket. He reads it again: *"Start here. This is where you made me miso soup. Check where we had our first dinner."*

- He starts here. He stands at the kitchen island where he stood in his pyjamas this morning, where she sat across from him in his shirt, and he looks toward the kitchen table — where they had their first dinner, grilled cheese and tomato soup, the first night she was in this house. And there it is. Under the bowl. The second note.

- Something moves through him — not surprise exactly, not confusion, but a kind of breathlessness, as if the air in the room has shifted and he is standing in a different house than the one he left this morning.

- Danica. This is Danica. She has never done anything like this. She gave him the first clue at breakfast. She looked him in the eye across the kitchen island and said *"First clue"* and she was wearing his shirt and she was barefoot and she was seventeen years old and she was planning something, and he has been thinking about it all day — through his ten o'clock class, through lunch, through his afternoon seminar, through the drive — and now he is home, and the trail is waiting, and she is not here, and the not-here is its own kind of answer: she is already at the end.

- He moves through the house the way she moved through it hours ago — room to room, note to note, each one a small bright thing that lands in his chest like a stone in water, rippling outward. The kitchen table. The window. The mug with the chip on the rim. Each clue is a memory that he did not know she was keeping, and the keeping of them is a gift he did not expect, and by the time he finds the fourth note — *"You're running out of house. Check the front porch"* — his hands are not steady, and he has to stand in the kitchen for a moment before he goes outside, because the alternative is walking to the front porch too fast, and Orin does not do things too fast, and he is not going to start now.

### Beat 6: The Barn — The Last Clue
- He walks out the front door and onto the porch. The February cold hits him the moment he steps outside — his coat is inside, he didn't grab it, he didn't think about it, he was following a trail left by a seventeen-year-old and he forgot his coat. He checks the railing. Underneath. The fifth note.

- He reads it: *"The chef is making dinner for two. He's bringing it to the barn. Check the barn."*

- *The chef. The barn.*

- She asked the chef. She asked the chef to make dinner and bring it to the loft. And she is up there, in the loft. He knows she is up there because the clues have been leading him to her, and the last clue is on the barn door, and behind that door there are stairs and above those stairs she will be waiting, and the knowing of this — that she is in a barn loft on a February night waiting for him to find her — is a kind of light in his chest that he does not have a word for.

- He walks across the yard in the dark. The snow crunches under his shoes. The barn is lit — the main light is on, the one they never use, the one that stays off unless someone is working late — and the door has a piece of paper taped to it at eye level. Her handwriting.

- The sixth and final note. He reads it: *"Come in. I'm waiting. — D."*

- *I'm waiting.*

- She is waiting. Not the dinner. Not the loft. Not the chef. She is waiting. She is upstairs in the loft, and she wants him to find her, and he has been following her handwriting through this house for ten minutes, and every clue has been a memory that he did not know she was keeping, and the keeping is a gift, and the trail is a gift, and the waiting is a gift, and the fact that it's her birthday and she is the one who planned all of this — the breakfast clue, the afternoon trail, the chef, the loft, the waiting — is the most Danica thing she has ever done, and he did not know she had this in her, and now he knows.

- He opens the barn door. The ground floor is dark. The loft above is lit — the warm yellow of a real room, not a barn light, the kind of light that means someone is home. He sees the stairs. He sees her winter boots at the bottom, the ones she wore across the yard. He goes up.

### Beat 7: The Barn — She's Waiting
- The barn is warm. Warmer than he expected. She has set it up in the loft — the barn has a loft, finished years ago, with a kitchen and a dining room and a bedroom, a space that was renovated for farm guests who never came, and it has been sitting empty and clean and waiting for a purpose, and tonight Danica gave it one. Her winter boots are at the bottom of the stairs. She changed into the heels inside — climbed the loft stairs in the red dress and unzipped the boots and stepped out of them and into the four inches of red, and the loft floor is hardwood, not packed dirt, and she is standing on it in heels like it is a marble floor, like it is a ballroom, like this is where she was always meant to be found. The loft smells like wood and the faint must of a room that has been closed for months, and underneath those smells, something else — food. The chef has already come and gone. In the dining room, two plates on the table, covered, still warm. A basket of bread. A bottle of something — not wine, Orin doesn't drink, maybe sparkling cider or just water in a wine bottle because she wanted the shape of it, the look of it, the idea of a bottle on a table for two.

- And she is there. She is sitting at the dining room table of the barn loft, and she is not in the black sweater. She is in a red dress — backless, the fabric catching the light from the fixture overhead, the color of it warm and impossible against the white walls of the loft. Nude nylons. Red heels, four inches, planted on the hardwood floor like they belong there, like she belongs here, like a girl in a red dress in a barn loft on a February night is the most natural thing in the world. Her hair is down — not in the ponytail she wears to school, but down, the way she wears it at the farmhouse, the way he sees it in the evenings when she's doing homework and the light is low and she doesn't know he's looking. She is sitting with her hands around a glass of sparkling water, and she is looking at the top of the stairs, and when he comes up and sees her, she sees him, and she does not wave, and she does not stand, and she does not call his name. She just looks at him, and the look says: *You found me.*

- He sits across from her at the loft's dining room table. And for a moment they just look at each other, because the scene is the reversal of everything it should be. He should be the one who planned the dinner. He should be the one who set up the table and lit the candle. He should be the one who arranged the evening. But she did. On her birthday. She planned the evening for him, here, in the barn where she has never set foot before tonight, in a building that smells like the animals she does not tend and the work she does not do — and she carried two kitchen chairs across the yard in the snow and asked the chef for dinner and lit a candle that no one has ever lit, and the barn is warm and the plates are hot and she is sitting here, and he is standing in the door without his coat, and this is the most unexpected thing that has happened to him since she walked into his life.

> *"You asked the chef,"* he says.

> *"Yes."*

> *"On your birthday."*

> *"Yes."*

> *"You asked the chef to make dinner in a barn loft. And you wore a red dress."*

> *"I wanted you to find me."*

> *"In a barn loft. In February. In a backless dress."*

> *"You took me in when I had nowhere to go. A red dress seemed like the least I could do."*

- He does not argue. He does not say she doesn't owe him anything. He has said that before and she has not believed him and tonight is not the night to have that argument. Tonight is the night she planned, and he is going to let her have it.

- They uncover the plates. The chef made a Japanese dinner — rice, miso soup with wakame, grilled fish with a ginger-soy glaze, pickled vegetables, edamame, green tea — and it is perfect. Not close to perfect. Perfect. The rice is the right texture, the miso soup is the right warmth, the fish is done the way her mother did it, and the pickled vegetables taste like the ones that sat in the jar in her parents' kitchen, and Danica stares at the plate and then at Orin, and Orin says:

> *"The chef. He studied in Osaka. Two years."*

- And she understands. She asked the farm's cook for a Japanese dinner and he said *I can do Japanese* because he spent two years in a kitchen in Osaka learning to make exactly this — rice and fish and miso and pickles for people who are not farm hands, for people who sit at a table and eat with chopsticks and drink green tea, and the dinner is perfect because the man who made it learned how in the country where her parents were born, and now the food is sitting on a plate in a barn loft in Quebec on her seventeenth birthday, and it tastes like home, it tastes exactly like home, and the tasting is its own kind of grief and its own kind of gift, and she eats, and Orin eats, and they do not talk while they eat, because the food is too good to talk over.

- After the main plates, the chef has left dessert — a small Japanese strawberry shortcake, the same kind her mother ordered from the bakery in Sherbrooke every year. Sponge cake. Whipped cream. Strawberries. The same three layers. The same white and red. Danica looks at it and her throat closes and she does not cry — she is not a crier — but her hand is not steady when she picks up the fork, and Orin does not say anything, because there is nothing to say. The cake is here. The photograph is not. The cake is here and her mother is not and the chef made it because he studied in Osaka and Danica asked and this is what she got: a strawberry shortcake in a barn loft with a man who gave her a chain and a card and learned to make miso soup for her breakfast, and the cake tastes like every birthday before this one, and it also tastes like the first birthday that is only hers.

- It is the most normal thing they have ever done together, sitting in a barn loft eating a Japanese dinner and a strawberry shortcake, and the normalcy of it is almost disorienting, because they have spent eight weeks inside a crisis and tonight the crisis is below them, on the ground floor, in the dark, and they are in here, eating rice and fish and cake and drinking green tea, and it is possible — just for tonight — to be two people having dinner instead of a nineteen-year-old and a seventeen-year-old surviving something.

### Beat 8: What They Talk About
- They talk about things they have never talked about, because they have never had the space. At the farm, every conversation exists in the shadow of what happened. At school, Danica is the girl who left home, even if nobody says it. On the phone, the calls are short — Orin calling from between classes, Danica texting from the hallway, the distance between Montreal and Sherbrooke compressed into fragments and check-ins and *"How was your day?" / Fine."*

- But here, with the light and the loft and the snow visible through the window, there is room.

- Danica asks Orin what he was like before the fire. She has never asked. She has never wanted to push, because the fire is the thing he does not talk about, the way her parents' living room is the room she does not enter. But tonight she asks, because tonight feels like a night when you can ask things.

- Orin does not flinch. He does not shut down. He considers the question. And then he tells her: he was loud. He was the loud kid. He was the one who made his uncle laugh, the one who made his cousins laugh, the one who filled a room with noise and movement. And then the house burned, and the noise stopped, and the silence came, and the silence was so complete and so heavy that he did not know how to make noise again. So he learned to be quiet. He learned to be observant. He learned to listen the way Sabrina listens — with his whole body, with his eyes, with his hands. And the quiet became who he is. He does not miss being loud. But sometimes he remembers the loud boy and he feels like that boy is someone he used to know, not someone he is.

- Danica listens. She does not say *"I'm sorry"* because sorry is too small and she knows it. She says:

> *"You're still loud. You're just loud on the inside."*

- Orin looks at her. Nobody has ever said that to him. Nobody has ever noticed that the quiet is not the absence of noise but the containment of it. He does not say thank you. He puts his hand on the table, palm up, and she puts her hand in it, and they sit like that in the quiet of the loft.

- Danica tells him about Valentine's Day when she was twelve. Her mother made her give chocolates to every girl in her class — not because it was romantic, but because it was Japanese obligation chocolate, *giri choco*, and her mother insisted on the tradition even though they were in Quebec and nobody else did it. Danica thought it was embarrassing. She thought it was the most embarrassing thing her mother had ever made her do. Now she thinks about it and it is so small — so impossibly, laughably small — compared to what her mother actually did, which was look at Orin and say things in Japanese that she thought he wouldn't understand.

- She does not say this with anger. She says it with the flatness of someone who has been through eight weeks of counselling and has started to separate the small things from the large things. The chocolate was small. The living room was large.

- After the giri choco story, they sit for a while. The plates are empty. The strawberry shortcake is gone — she ate every bite, and Orin watched her eat it, and he did not ask if she wanted more, because there was no more, and the eating of it was the thing, and the thing was finished. The green tea is cold. The snow is falling outside the window. The loft is warm and still and full of the smell of miso and fish and cake, and the silence between them is not awkward, it is the silence of two people who have just spent an evening together and do not want it to end.

- Danica stands. She does not say anything. She walks around the table to his chair, and she takes his left hand — his left hand, the one that is resting on the table, the one with the long fingers and the calluses from four years of high school Japanese textbooks and weight on a steering wheel and a winter of driving her to school — and she holds it, and she looks at him, and he looks up at her, and she does not let go.

- She leads him. Across the loft. Past the kitchen with its empty plates and its cold tea. Past the dining room where they ate a Japanese dinner made by a man who studied in Osaka. To the bedroom door. She opens it.

- The bedroom is full of pink rose blossoms. On the floor. On the bed. Scattered across the quilt like snow, like the snow that is falling outside the window, except these are pink and soft and they smell like flowers, and they are everywhere — on the pillow, on the sheets, on the floor by the bed, on the floor by the door, a path of pink from the threshold to the bed, and the bed is covered in them, and the room is dark except for the light from the hallway falling across the blossoms, and the effect is impossible — pink on white, flowers on a quilt, in a barn loft in rural Quebec, on a February night, on her birthday, and she made this. She did this. She scattered pink rose blossoms on a bed in a barn loft because she is seventeen and she is alive and she is standing in a red dress in a doorway holding a man's left hand, and she wanted the room to look like this, and it does.

- Orin stops. He stands in the doorway. He looks at the blossoms and he looks at her and he does not breathe for a moment, because the bedroom is the thing he did not expect, and the not-expecting is its own kind of undoing, because he spent the whole evening being undone by her — the clues, the red dress, the chef, the dinner, the strawberry shortcake — and now there is a bed covered in pink rose blossoms and she is still holding his hand and she is not letting go.

> *"Danica."*

> *"I know."*

> *"There are roses."*

> *"I know."*

> *"On the bed."*

> *"I know."*

> *"In a barn."*

> *"In a barn loft. With a kitchen and a dining room and a bedroom. You said you'd be down the hall if I needed anything. You didn't say how far down the hall."*

- He almost laughs. Almost. The sound that comes out is not quite a laugh and not quite a breath — it is the sound of a man who has been holding himself together for eight weeks and has just been handed a room full of pink rose blossoms by a seventeen-year-old in a red dress.

- She leads him inside. She closes the door.

### Beat 9: The Walk Back to the House / The Card
- After, they do not go straight to the house. They stand outside the barn in the snow — the loft is dark behind them, the blossoms still on the bed, the plates still on the table — and Orin looks at her, and she looks at him, and she says:

> *"I planned this far. I didn't plan past this."*

- He almost smiles. Almost. *"Walk back with me?"*

- They walk across the yard toward the house. The snow is still falling. The porch light is on. The farmhouse windows are dark. Their breath comes out in clouds. She changed back into her winter boots at the bottom of the loft stairs before going outside — the heels are beautiful but they are not made for packed snow, and the walk is long enough that she does not want to fall, and the boots are practical and the dress is not, and she is wearing both at the same time, and the combination is its own kind of truth: a girl in a red backless dress and winter boots walking across a February yard, holding the heels in one hand and Orin's hand in the other. The barn is behind them, lit for a moment and then dark as they move away. The house is ahead, the porch light a small yellow square in the white.

- He stops halfway across the yard. He lets go of her hand. He reaches into his coat — he grabbed it on the way out of the house, finally, after the barn — and he takes something out. A small envelope. White. A red heart on the front.

- She looks at it. She looks at him.

> *"When did you —"*

> *"On the way home. There's a gas station on the highway. They had three cards. This was the best one."*

- A gas station on the highway. He bought a card at a gas station. On the drive home from McGill. While he was thinking about the clue in his pocket and the breakfast he made her and the trail she was leaving for him, he stopped at a gas station and stood in the fluorescent light and picked a card from a spinning wire rack next to the beef jerky and the lottery tickets, and it was the best one they had, and the best one was a white card with a red heart, and he bought it and wrote in it, and he has been carrying it since the highway, and now he is standing in the snow halfway between the barn and the house and he is handing it to her.

- She opens it. Inside, in his handwriting — the same small, careful handwriting as this morning — he has written:

> *I already said this once today. I'm saying it again because you asked the chef to make dinner in a barn loft on your own birthday and I am never going to get over that. Happy birthday. Happy Valentine's Day. — O.*

- She stares at it. She reads it again. *I already said this once today. I'm saying it again because you asked the chef to make dinner in a barn loft on your own birthday and I am never going to get over that.* He is saying it twice. Once at the kitchen island, across the breakfast he made her. Once here, in the snow, halfway between the barn and the house. Once when she was eating rice and miso soup. Once when she was standing in a red backless dress with snow on her eyelashes. The first card was a gift. The second card is a response. The first card said *you are the best thing that has happened at this farm.* The second card says *I see what you did, and I am undone by it.*

> *"Orin."*

> *"Yeah."*

> *"You bought a card. At a gas station. On the highway."*

> *"You left clues. All over my house. While I was at McGill."*

> *"I had more time than you."*

> *"You had more courage than me."*

- She laughs. It is a real laugh — short, surprised, the kind of laugh that comes out when you are not expecting it. And Orin hears it and something in his shoulders releases, because he has been holding his breath since he walked into the barn and saw her at the table, and the laugh is the sound of the evening landing the way she wanted it to land — the way they both wanted it to land.

- She holds both cards — one in each hand. The first from the kitchen island, the second from the gas station. The morning and the night. The gift and the response. She puts the second card in her pocket. She will not lose it. She will put it on the nightstand in the spare bedroom, next to the first card, and they will stay there, together, the two things he wrote on the day she turned seventeen.

### Beat 10: The Walk to the House — What She Says
- They walk the rest of the way across the yard. The snow is still falling. The porch light is waiting. Danica's hand finds his again, and their fingers lace, and the barn is dark behind them and the house is ahead.

- Halfway to the porch, Danica says:

> *"Next year, I'm getting you a card. And I'm asking the chef."*

- Orin says: *"You already asked the chef."*

> *"Then I'm asking him again."*

> *"You don't have to."*

> *"I know. That's why I'm telling you now. So you can't say I didn't warn you."*

- He does not smile. Orin does not smile the way other people smile — his smiles are small, private, visible only if you are looking for them. But Danica is always looking for them, and she sees the one that flickers at the corner of his mouth, and it is enough. It is more than enough.

- At the porch steps, Danica says, quietly:

> *"My mother didn't call."*

- It is the first time she has said it out loud. All day she has been carrying it — through the morning, through the Japanese breakfast and the silver chain and the card, through the cupcake and the candle and Geneviève's lighter, through the silent phone, through the clues and the chef and the barn and the Japanese dinner and the strawberry shortcake and the walk and the snow and the second card — and now, standing at the porch steps in the dark, she says it. Not as an accusation. Not as a wound. Just as a fact. The fact of the day. The thing that happened — or rather, the thing that didn't happen — on her seventeenth birthday.

- Orin does not say *"I'm sorry"* because sorry is not big enough and he knows it. He does not say *"She should have called"* because should is not helpful. He does not say *"You're better off without her"* because he does not know that and neither does Danica and neither of them is ready to say it. He tightens his fingers around hers. And after a moment, Danica leans her head against his shoulder, and the snow falls on them, and they stand like that at the porch steps for a long time, and the silence is not empty — the silence is full of the thing she said and the thing he didn't need to say because his hand said it for him.

### Beat 11: Home — The Hallway
- They go up the porch steps. The house is dark — they left the porch light on, that's all. Orin opens the door. The warmth hits them.

- Danica looks at the house. The dark windows. The porch light. The front door she walked through eight weeks ago in a dinner outfit and ivory tights and no bag. The house that was empty before she made it full. The house that is now hers — not on paper, not legally, not in any way that a court or a DPJ worker would recognize, but hers in the way that a place becomes yours when you sleep in it every night and eat in it every morning and cry in it and laugh in it and leave clues in it for the man who lives down the hall. The barn loft where she scattered pink rose blossoms and ate a Japanese dinner and led him by the left hand into a bedroom.

- She turns to Orin. She does not say thank you. He does not say thank you. They have been saying thank you all night — at the loft, at the porch steps. They are done with thank you. She takes his left hand, the same way she took it in the loft, and she leads him upstairs.

- The hallway. Seven feet of hardwood floor. Two closed doors. The boundary that has stood for eight weeks — seven feet and a closed door — and she does not stop at her door, the spare bedroom, the room where she has slept alone for fifty-six nights. She walks past it. She opens his door.

- She goes inside. He follows.

- She puts both cards on his nightstand — the first from the kitchen island, the second from the gas station. The morning and the night. The gift and the response. They lean against each other, two white cards with red hearts, on the nightstand of a bedroom she has never been in, in a house where she has never crossed this hallway, on a night when she has already crossed every other line — the clues, the chef, the red dress, the loft, the rose blossoms — and this is the last one, and it is the quietest, and it is the most simple, and she does not need to say *"I don't want to sleep alone tonight"* because she is already inside, and he is already behind her, and the door is already closing, and the boundary that stood for eight weeks is not broken — it is dissolved. There is a difference. Broken means something was destroyed. Dissolved means something was no longer needed.

- She leans into him. He puts his arm around her. She puts her head on his shoulder. His shirt smells like the barn — like hay and miso and the cold air from the walk and the faint sweet ghost of pink rose blossoms — and she breathes it in, and his arm tightens, and they stand like that in the dark, and the house is quiet around them, and the snow falls on Ferme Clé, and somewhere in the distance a horse shifts in the barn, and the two cards are on the nightstand, and the silver chain is against her collarbone, and her head is on his shoulder, and his arm is around her, and this is the first night she has been in his room, and she is not reaching for him with desperation, she is not reaching for him with crisis, she is reaching for him because she wants to be held, and he is holding her, and the holding is enough.

### Beat 12: Closing Image — The House in the Snow
- Later. The lamp is off. The house is dark. The snow is falling on Ferme Clé — on the barn, on the fields, on the long driveway, on the porch light they left on. The barn is dark too, but upstairs in the loft there are two chairs at a dining room table and the plates are stacked and the mugs are empty and the bedroom door is closed and inside it the pink rose blossoms are still on the bed, still on the floor, still scattered across the quilt where she led him by the left hand, and the evidence of dinner and the evidence of the evening are there, waiting for tomorrow.

- Inside, the house is warm. The kitchen is dark. The two mugs are in the drying rack. The two pairs of slippers are by the door. The legal aid pamphlet is on the windowsill. The notepad is on the table, missing five pages. The bowl on the kitchen table still has the second clue taped to the bottom. The first clue is in Orin's coat pocket. The others are scattered through the house like small, bright evidence of a girl who decided, on her birthday, to give something back.

- Upstairs, in Orin's bedroom, the two cards are on the nightstand. Two white cards with red hearts. The first one says *Happy birthday. Happy Valentine's Day. You are the best thing that has happened at this farm.* The second one says *I already said this once today. I'm saying it again because you asked the chef to make dinner in a barn loft on your own birthday and I am never going to get over that.* Both in his handwriting. Both in the same small, careful script. One written and placed on the kitchen island counter next to the breakfast he made her. One bought at a gas station on the highway and written in a parked car.

- Danica is asleep beside him. Her head is on his shoulder. Her breath is slow and even. The silver chain is on her collarbone, catching the faint light from the window. His arm is around her. He is not asleep. He is watching the snow fall through the window, and he is thinking about the notes — the clues she left, the first one given at breakfast from the pocket of his shirt, the rest placed through the house and the porch while he was at McGill, six clues that led him from the kitchen island to the loft to her — and he is thinking about the fact that in eight weeks she has never initiated anything, she has never been the one to act, she has never been the one to reach, and tonight she did all three, and the three things together — the chef, the clues, the doorway — are the most courageous thing he has ever seen anyone do, and he has seen people survive a house fire.

- The spare bedroom down the hall is empty. The bed is made. The first card is not on the nightstand anymore — she brought it with her. The room is waiting, the way it has been waiting for eight weeks, but tonight no one is in it. Tonight she is here.

- Outside, the snow falls on Ferme Clé. Inside, the house holds them. The cards hold the hearts. The night holds the first time she crossed the hallway.

- **The Barn Loft:** The barn has a finished loft with a kitchen, dining room, and bedroom. It was renovated years ago for farm guests who never came, and has been sitting empty and clean, waiting for a purpose. Danica gives it one on Valentine's Day — she sets the dining room table, asks the chef to bring dinner up, and waits for Orin to find her there. The loft has hardwood floors, its own fixtures, its own furniture. It is not a converted space — it is a real living space that simply has not been lived in.
- **The Chef and Osaka:** The farm's cook studied culinary skills in Osaka, Japan for two years. When Danica asks for a Japanese dinner, he says *"I studied in Osaka. Two years. I can do Japanese"* — and the dinner is perfect. The rice, the miso soup, the grilled fish, the pickled vegetables — all of it tastes like home. The chef's time in Japan is the reason a barn loft in rural Quebec can have a perfect Japanese meal on a February night. The chef cooks the dinner at the farmhouse kitchen and brings it up to the loft.
- **The Strawberry Shortcake:** The chef also makes dessert — a Japanese strawberry shortcake, the same kind Danica's mother ordered from the bakery in Sherbrooke every year on her birthday. Sponge cake, whipped cream, strawberries. This is the cake that the scene purpose section says would not exist this year — and then it does, but not from her mother, not from the bakery, from a francophone chef who learned to make it in Osaka. The cake connects to the "Strawberry Shortcake" continuity entry: this year there was supposed to be no strawberry shortcake and no photograph. But the cake is here. The photograph is not.
- **The Bedroom and the Rose Blossoms:** After dinner, Danica takes Orin's left hand and leads him into the loft's bedroom. The bed and the floor are covered in pink rose blossoms — she scattered them before he arrived, before the chef came, before she sat down to wait. The blossoms are on the quilt, on the pillow, on the floor by the bed, on the floor by the door — a path of pink from the threshold to the bed. She did this because she wanted the room to look like this. She did this because she is seventeen and she is alive and she wanted him to find her in a room full of flowers.
- **The Red Dress:** Danica wears a red backless dress, nude nylons, and four-inch red heels. She bought the dress on the Montreal shopping trip three weeks ago, on the second floor of a department store while Orin waited downstairs. She did not show it to him. She hung it in the closet in the spare bedroom in a garment bag and has been waiting for a reason to wear it. Tonight is the reason. The dress is the first thing she has owned since leaving home that is not practical, not borrowed, not a school uniform or a farm sweater or Orin's clothes. It is hers and it is beautiful and it serves no purpose except this: she wanted to be found in it.
- **The Reversal:** The core of this scene is reversal. Orin has been the one who acts — he gives her a home, a bedroom, a ride to school, a uniform, a present, a card. Danica has been the one who receives. On her birthday, she reverses the dynamic. She is the one who asks the chef. She is the one who writes the clues. She is the one who carries chairs across a yard in the snow and waits in a barn. She takes him to dinner on her birthday, and the act of giving is its own kind of claiming — not of him, but of herself. She is not just the girl who lost her family. She is the girl who asked the chef.
- **Danica's New Life at BCS:** Danica has been back at BCS for six weeks. She walks through the front door in a uniform she bought herself at the campus bookstore the same morning as her office meeting with Dr. Whitfield. She sees a school counsellor regularly. Her friends Geneviève and Chloe know she left home; the details are not discussed. They have known her birthday is February 14 since grade seven — four years of shared homerooms and the same joke about the worst birthday date. This morning, before homeroom, they gave her a construction-paper card and a cupcake with a candle. They sang. It was small and real and thirty seconds long. Same as every year. Same as grade seven. She looks like a normal day student. She is not.
- **Danica's Birthday — February 14:** Her birthday falls on Valentine's Day. She has always had a complicated relationship with this — *"My birthday's on Valentine's Day, it's the worst"* — which she said casually to Orin in the second week. The casualness was a cover. She grew up with strawberry shortcake from the bakery in Sherbrooke, her mother's photograph every year, her father's *"Otanjōbi omedetō."* This year: none of that. Her parents have not reached out in fifty-six days. Not through the school, not through the police, not through the DPJ. Her grandparents are dead — there is no one in Japan to notice her absence. The silence is total. Standing at the porch steps, Danica says *"My mother didn't call"* — the first time she says it out loud.
- **The Strawberry Shortcake:** Every year, Danica's mother ordered a Japanese-style strawberry shortcake from a bakery in Sherbrooke — whipped cream, sponge cake, strawberries, candles. Her mother photographed her in the same spot in the kitchen every year. There is no strawberry shortcake this year. There is no photograph. Danica does not mention this. Orin does not know about this. It is a private grief that sits underneath the evening like a stone under a tablecloth.
- **Orin's Background — The Fire:** Danica asks Orin what he was like before the house fire. He tells her he was loud. The quiet came after. The silence was so complete he didn't know how to make noise again. Danica's response — *"You're still loud. You're just loud on the inside"* — is the most perceptive thing anyone has ever said to Orin, and it breaks something open in him that he does not have words for.
- **Danica's Background — Giri Choco:** Danica tells the story of obligation chocolate — *giri choco* — that her mother made her give to every girl in her class when she was twelve. She thought it was the most embarrassing thing her mother ever made her do. Now she knows it was impossibly small compared to what her mother actually did. The story is told with the flatness of someone who has been in counselling and is learning to separate the small things from the large things.
- **No Flowers:** There is no flower shop. There is no restaurant. Orin buys her a card at a gas station. She asks the chef to make dinner in the loft. These are exactly right for who they are — not grand gestures, not public declarations, just a quiet man at a gas station card rack and a quiet girl with a notepad, each of them choosing the right words.
- **Connection to Other Scenes:** This scene follows the Sunday evening scene (the night before the BCS office meeting), the Monday morning BCS office meeting, the Wednesday counsellor meeting, the Tuesday nightmare, the DPJ Friday inspection, and the Saturday Saint-Viateur bagels tradition. It is the first scene in the arc that is not about surviving the crisis. It is about living inside the life the crisis created.
