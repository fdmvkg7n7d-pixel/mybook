**POV:** Primarily Andrea Jennifer (with brief shifts to Emerald Ginger)
**Tone:** Steel. Controlled. The fiercest love a mother has, expressed as action.
**Location:** [TBD — Andrea Jennifer's home; or wherever she takes the call from Las Vegas. Could be split: the call from Brandi's house, then Andrea Jennifer acting from her own.]

## CONTENT NOTE

**The abuse is never depicted.** What Andrea Jennifer learns has already been disclosed off-page (see `emerald-ginger-discloses-to-brandi-jessica-scene-outline.md`). This scene is about the **response** — belief, protection, and the machinery of justice set in motion. The drama is entirely in Andrea Jennifer's choices: how fast she believes, how completely she acts, and how she shields her daughter from carrying any of it.

---

## BEFORE THE SCENE — WHAT ANDREA JENNIFER WALKS INTO

*Establish the gut-punch of dramatic irony.*

- Andrea Jennifer has spent weeks frantic since the runaway note, then weeks relieved-but-uneasy since Brandi's "she's alive and safe" message. She does not yet know **why** her eldest ran.
- She loves Fachnan. She built a life with him — three children (Racquel Rahiq, Simeon Quade, Quetzalxochitl Saima). He is, this morning, still the man she trusts. **The reader knows what's coming and she does not.** That gap is the engine of the scene's opening.
- The call comes from Brandi's house in Las Vegas — Emerald Ginger and Brandi, together, as promised.

---

## BEAT 1 — THE DISCLOSURE LANDS (OFF-PAGE CORE)

*She hears it. We do not need the words.*

- On the phone (or in person if she's flown to Vegas), Emerald Ginger tells her — with Brandi beside her. **Rendered with restraint:** we may stay in Andrea Jennifer's POV and watch the information arrive on her face/body rather than transcribe it. A held breath. A hand gripping the counter. The world reorganizing.
- The two facts land: Emerald Ginger is pregnant; **the father is Fachnan.**
- **Crucial — the speed of belief:** Andrea Jennifer does **not** say "are you sure," does not ask what Emerald Ginger did, does not say "that can't be true," does not reach to protect Fachnan or the family's image. She believes her daughter **immediately.** That single fact is the emotional core of the entire arc.

**Thematic note:** This is the inverse of Emma's father Randy, who disowned his child for who she loved. Andrea Jennifer is the anti-Randy: she chooses her child instantly and without condition. The Key family "does nothing alone" — and it does not sacrifice its children to protect its men.

---

## BEAT 2 — EMERALD GINGER'S FEAR OF THE FALLOUT

*The daughter braces for the cost.*

- Emerald Ginger, even now, tries to manage it — the kids, the family, the explosion:
- **Dialogue cue (Emerald Ginger):** "Mom, please — the kids, everyone, if you do this it all—"
- Andrea Jennifer stops her. She takes the weight entirely off her daughter:
- **Dialogue cue (Andrea Jennifer):** "Look at me. None of that is yours to hold. Not one piece of it. You did nothing wrong. *Nothing.* This is mine now. I've got it."
- **Brief brace against her own guilt:** Andrea Jennifer brought Fachnan into the house. She names it once — *"I should have seen it. I didn't. I see it now."* — and then puts it down, because this moment is not about her grief. (Save her private collapse for a later beat.)

---

## BEAT 3 — THE CALL

*The action that is the love.*

- Andrea Jennifer does not hesitate, does not call a lawyer first, does not call Fachnan to "hear his side," does not phone her parents to manage it quietly. She calls the police.
- **On the page:** the ordinary, terrible procedure of it — dialing, the operator's calm questions, Andrea Jennifer's voice level and clear as she reports the sexual abuse of her child by her partner. The flatness of the official language against the enormity of what it means.
- **Dialogue cue (Andrea Jennifer, steady):** "I need to report a crime against my daughter. She's a minor. The man who did it is my partner. His name is Fachnan."
- Possible logistics to weave in (kept procedural, not graphic): cross-jurisdiction (Emerald Ginger in Nevada, the family/Fachnan elsewhere), the involvement of child protective services, the fact that Emerald Ginger will need to be interviewed by professionals — and Andrea Jennifer promising she will be beside her for all of it.

---

## BEAT 4 — THE TWO PHONES / THE TWO TRUTHS

*Protecting the daughter while the machine starts to move.*

- Intercut, if useful: Emerald Ginger in Las Vegas, hearing her mother on the line a thousand miles away, finally understanding she is **believed and defended** — that she did not burn the family down; the man who hurt her did.
- Andrea Jennifer, between calls, sends word to the family's protective network (Mike, Terri Anne, the sisters) — not to debate, but to mobilize. *Echo:* the family that took in Emma; the family that fostered Klára. They close ranks around the child, not the accused.
- **The three children:** a quiet, hard awareness lands — Racquel Rahiq, Simeon Quade, and Quetzalxochitl Saima are about to lose their father to this. Andrea Jennifer will have to hold them, too. [How and when she tells them — later beat, TBD.]

---

## BEAT 5 — THE AFTERMATH BEGINS

*End on protection, not resolution.*

- Fachnan's arrest/removal happens **off-page or reported** — we don't need the spectacle. What we need is the image of a daughter who is finally safe and a mother who put herself between her child and the world.
- **End image:** Andrea Jennifer's hands finally shaking once the calls are done — the steel allowed to crack now that the work is done. Or: the two of them on the phone in silence, the line open, neither hanging up, a mother and daughter just breathing together across the distance.
- **Forward hooks:**
  - Andrea Jennifer's private collapse / grief over Fachnan and her own guilt.
  - Telling and holding the three younger children.
  - The legal process, with Emerald Ginger choosing her level of participation.
  - The baby — later **Jenna Istra** — born and loved; raised by Emerald Ginger and **Staci** as her two mothers, never treated as shame.
  - The wider Key-family reckoning and the saga's intergenerational question: *does the family repeat the pattern, or break the chain on purpose?* Here, decisively, it breaks the chain.
