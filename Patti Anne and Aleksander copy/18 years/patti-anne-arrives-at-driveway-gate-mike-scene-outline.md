# Patti Anne Arrives at the Driveway Gate — Mike — Scene Outline

> **Scope:** Mike arrives home from London. Patti Anne is waiting at the driveway gate — not as a visitor, but as someone who has been living on the streets. Two years ago, she almost hurt Sahara Hannan during a manic episode. Mike and Terri Anne threw her out of the mansion. She has been on the streets ever since. Tonight, she came back. She comes inside for a warm meal. Mike cooks her a meal like when she was a child — very emotional. Mike and Terri Anne hold her, hug her, the three of them together in the kitchen at midnight. They do not ask her about what happened on the streets because they don't want to know. She agrees to take the medicine to control her Bipolar Disorder Type Two. Mike takes her to the doctor the next morning — an ultrasound is required. Mike finds a good psychologist for her. She stays in the guest room tonight. When she is stable — when the medication is working — she will move into the guest house on the property. The baby's father is unknown for the child's's lifetime. The baby's sex is unknown to Patti Anne. She has the baby in Rio. She is in Rio for Christmas — Jessica Isabella and Emma visit from Halifax, Samira and Sahara are there, the family is whole. After Christmas, she moves to a six-room mansion in Vaughan, Ontario — roomy and gorgeous, furnished by Mike and Terri Anne for a growing family. She can always call Mike and Terri Anne for help. She stays stable.
>
> **Time:** TBD — two years after Patti Anne was thrown out after almost hurting Sahara Hannan.
> **Place:** The driveway gate and the family home in Rio de Janeiro.
> **Characters present:** Mike Key, Terri Anne Williams-Key, Patti Anne Key (19, six months pregnant, living on the streets, Bipolar Disorder Type Two, does not know the father's identity).
> **Mood:** Broken, heavy, honest. The particular weight of a family that made an impossible decision and is living with the consequences.

## 1. Before the Gate

- **Patti Anne has been standing at the gate for an hour.** She knows when his flight lands — she has been watching the arrivals online, the way she has been watching for months, waiting for the right night. She is not sure if she is going to be allowed inside. She does not know what will happen when she sees them. She just knows she cannot stay on the street tonight. Not like this. Not six months pregnant and hungry and cold.
- **She is six months pregnant.** She does not know how far along she is exactly — she has not seen a doctor. She found out three months ago and she has been carrying it alone. She does not know who the father is. There were a few men in the months after she left the house — not boyfriends, not lovers in any real sense, just men who were there and then were not. She did not ask their names. She did not want to know. Some things are easier not knowing.
- **Her back aches.** Her feet ache. She has been standing still for too long and her body is not built for standing still anymore — her body is doing something it was not designed to do on concrete and pavement and the floors of shelters.
- **Two years ago, she almost hurt Sahara Hannan.** She was in a manic episode — the high that comes with Bipolar Disorder Type Two, the one that feels like flying and looks like recklessness from the outside. She was in the house. The girls were there — Samira and Sahara, eighteen months old, in the living room with Jessica and Emma. Patti Anne was in the kitchen. She does not remember all of what happened. She remembers fragments — the sound of her own voice, the way her hands moved, the way she was standing very close to Sahara and the way her body felt like it was not her own. She does not remember if she touched her. She remembers the moment Jessica pulled Sahara away. She remembers the way Mike looked at her — not angry, not disappointed, just afraid. That was worse. That was so much worse.
- **They asked her to leave that night.** Not in the morning. That night. Mike sat her down and he told her she could not be in the house anymore. Terri Anne was standing in the doorway. They did not yell. They did not accuse. They just said: *You cannot be here. Not with the children. Not like this.*
- **She did not argue.** She understood. She had Bipolar Disorder Type Two and she had just had an episode that put a baby in danger and she knew — in the part of herself that was still clear, still present, still not flying — that she could not be trusted. She packed a bag. She went.
- **She has been on the streets ever since.** She has had places — a shelter for a while, a friend's floor for a while, the back of a car for a while. None of it lasted. She has been moving for two years. She is nineteen years old and she is six months pregnant and she does not know who the father is and she has Bipolar Disorder Type Two and she almost hurt an eighteen-month-old child.
- **Tonight, she came to the gate.**

## 2. The Arrival

- **Mike has just flown back from London.** He took the long way home — Halifax to London, then London to Rio de Janeiro. The flights were long. He slept in stretches. He thought about the four days in Halifax, the park, the blocks, the stone in his pocket from the playground. He thought about the house, the garden, Jessica standing in the doorway with Samira in her arms. He is tired. He is almost home.
- **He arrives in Rio de Janeiro late.** The city is warm and familiar. The air is thick and close and smells like the coast. He knows this smell.
- **He collects his bag.** He walks through the terminal. He does not rush. He is going home.
- **He takes a car to the house.** The drive is familiar. He has driven this route a hundred times. The city moves past the windows. He knows every turn.
- **He drives toward the house.** The gate is ahead. The porch light is on. Everything looks the way it always looks when he comes home.

## 3. The Woman at the Gate

- **He sees her before he reaches the gate.** A woman, standing very still, watching the car approach. She is holding one hand on her lower back — the way a woman holds herself when she is six months pregnant and has been standing still for a while. He does not recognize her immediately — the light is low, the gate is still a few feet away.
- **Mike slows down.** His hand finds the gear. He slows the car more than he needs to. He is watching her. She is not moving.
- **His hand is ready for anything.** Not consciously — automatically. The old instinct, the one that never fully goes away.
- **He stops the car short of the gate.** He gets out. He does not turn off the engine. He stands beside the open door and he looks at her.
- **The woman sees him get out.** She does not move. She stands at the gate and watches him come toward her.
- **Mike stops a few feet from her.** He looks at her — her face, her posture, the roundness of her belly visible even in the dark. She is wearing a coat that is too thin for the night. Her hair is uncombed. Her face is thinner than he remembers. His hand is at his side but not loose.
- **The porch light is on.** It is casting a soft light on her face. He can see her now.
- **Patti Anne:** *"It's me, Dad."*

## 4. The Recognition

- **Mike does not move.** He is looking at his oldest daughter. She is six months pregnant. She is standing at his gate in a coat that is too thin, on a night that is too cold, alone. She is nineteen years old. She has been on the streets for two years. She almost hurt Sahara Hannan. He threw her out for that.
- **Mike:** *"Patti Anne."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"I saw your flight. I wanted to see you."*
- **Mike:** *"How long have you been here?"*
- **Patti Anne:** *"An hour. Maybe more."*
- **Mike:** *"An hour."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"I wanted to make sure it was you."*
- **Mike does not say anything.** He is still standing between the car and the gate. He does not know what to do. He has been home from London for twenty minutes and his oldest daughter is standing at the gate and she is pregnant and she has been on the streets for two years and he threw her out because she almost hurt a baby.
- **Mike:** *"Come inside."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"Are you sure?"*
- **Mike:** *"Yes."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"What about the girls?"*
- **Mike:** *"Jessica took Samira and Sahara to the house in Halifax. They're not here tonight. Come inside."*
- **A long pause.** The porch light is on. The house is behind them. The night is warm and still.
- **Mike opens the gate.** It swings inward with the familiar creak. They walk to the house together.

## 5. The House — A Warm Meal

- **Mike opens the front door.** The house is warm. The smell of something — whatever Terri Anne made — is in the air. The lights are on: the porch, the kitchen, the hall. He has come home to this a hundred times.
- **Terri Anne is in the kitchen.** She did not go to sleep. She was waiting. She stands when he comes in. She sees Patti Anne behind him.
- **Terri Anne does not move.** She stands very still and she looks at her oldest daughter — the thin coat, the round belly, the face that is older than it should be at nineteen.
- **Terri Anne:** *"Patti Anne."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"Hi, Mom."*
- **A long silence.** Nobody moves. Nobody speaks.
- **Terri Anne crosses the kitchen.** She goes to her daughter. She puts her arms around her — carefully, slowly, the way you hold someone who is pregnant and fragile and has been gone for a very long time. She holds her for a long time. She does not let go.
- **Terri Anne:** *"Sit down. I'll get you something to eat."*
- **Patti Anne sits at the kitchen table.** Mike sits beside her. Terri Anne moves around the kitchen — heating soup, cutting bread, the ordinary movements of feeding someone who is hungry. The kitchen is warm. The house is quiet.
- **Patti Anne eats.** She eats slowly at first and then fast — the way someone eats when they have been hungry for a long time and they finally have food in front of them. She does not say anything. She just eats.
- **Mike and Terri Anne watch her.** They do not talk. They do not need to. They are watching their daughter eat and they are remembering the last time they saw her — two years ago, the night she almost hurt Sahara, the way Mike sat her down and told her she had to go, the way she did not argue, the way she packed a bag and left.
- **Patti Anne finishes eating.** She sits back in her chair. She puts her hand on her belly — the roundness of it, the way it sits against the table edge. She does not say anything.
- **Mike:** *"How far along?"*
- **Patti Anne:** *"Six months. Maybe."*
- **Mike:** *"Maybe."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"I don't know exactly. I haven't seen a doctor."*
- **Mike:** *"You haven't seen a doctor."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"I can't afford a doctor."*
- **Terri Anne:** *"We'll get you a doctor. Tomorrow."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"You don't have to."*
- **Terri Anne:** *"We know we don't have to."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"Then why?"*
- **Terri Anne:** *"Because you're our daughter."*
- **A beat.** The kitchen is warm. The soup is still warm on the stove. The house is quiet.
- **Mike:** *"Patti Anne."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"Yeah?"*
- **Mike:** *"What happened — two years ago — we never talked about it properly. We should have."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"There's nothing to talk about. I almost hurt Sahara. You made the right call."*
- **Mike:** *"Did we?"*
- **Patti Anne:** *"You did. I know what I'm like when I'm not medicated. I know what Bipolar Disorder Type Two does. I know why you were afraid."*
- **Mike:** *"I wasn't afraid of you. I was afraid of what you might do."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"That's the same thing."*
- **Mike:** *"No. It's not. Being afraid of someone means you're afraid of who they are. Being afraid of what they might do means you're afraid of the illness. I was afraid of the illness."*
- **Patti Anne is quiet.** She looks at her father. She does not know what to say to that.
- **Patti Anne:** *"I don't remember all of what happened. The episode — I remember pieces. I remember the sound of my voice. I remember standing very close to Sahara. I don't remember touching her. But I remember the way Jessica pulled her away. I remember the way you looked at me."*
- **Mike:** *"How did I look at you?"*
- **Patti Anne:** *"Like you didn't know me."*
- **A long silence.** The kitchen is warm. The house is quiet. The baby in her belly is moving — Mike can see it, a small shift against the roundness of her coat.
- **Mike:** *"I knew you. I have always known you. That was the worst part."*
- **Patti Anne does not answer.** She puts her hand on her belly. She feels the baby move. She thinks about the episode — the flying feeling, the way her body was not her own, the way she was standing close to an eighteen-month-old child and she did not know what she was going to do. She knows what she is like. She has Bipolar Disorder Type Two. She knows.

## 6. The Medicine

- **Terri Anne sits down.** She sits across from her daughter. She does not move quickly. She does not make sudden gestures. She has learned, over the years, how to be around someone who has Bipolar Disorder Type Two — the sudden movements can trigger something, the wrong tone can send someone spiraling up or down. She is careful. She has always been careful.
- **Terri Anne:** *"Patti Anne. We need to talk about something."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"Okay."*
- **Terri Anne:** *"The medicine. Are you taking it?"*
- **Patti Anne:** *"No."*
- **Terri Anne:** *"You're not."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"I don't have it. I can't afford it."*
- **Terri Anne:** *"We can get it for you. We can get you a doctor, we can get you the medication, we can get you stable."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"You threw me out."*
- **Terri Anne:** *"Yes. We did."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"Because I almost hurt Sahara."*
- **Terri Anne:** *"Yes."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"And now you want me to take medicine."*
- **Terri Anne:** *"Yes."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"So I can come back inside."*
- **Terri Anne:** *"So you can be safe. So the baby can be safe. So you can be stable enough to make your own choices about what happens next."*
- **Patti Anne is quiet.** She looks at her mother. She looks at her father. She puts her hand on her belly — the roundness of it, the baby moving inside.
- **Patti Anne:** *"If I take the medicine — if I get stable — will you let me come back?"*
- **Mike:** *"We want you to take the medicine whether you come back inside or not. The medicine is not a condition. It's a need. You have Bipolar Disorder Type Two and you need to be medicated, not because of us, but because of you."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"But if I'm stable — if I'm medicated — I can come back."*
- **Terri Anne:** *"When you're stable. When we know you're okay. There's a guest house on the property — it's yours. You don't have to be in the main house with everyone. But when you're stable, the guest house is available to you. It has everything you'd need. It's yours."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"A guest house."*
- **Terri Anne:** *"Yes."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"Not the main house."*
- **Mike:** *"Not yet. Not until you're stable. Not until we know the medication is working and you're clear. The main house has the girls — Samira and Sahara, the twins. We need to know you're safe with them before you're in the same space with them every day. That's not a punishment. That's protecting everyone."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"I understand."*
- **Terri Anne:** *"The guest house has a bedroom, a bathroom, a small kitchen. It's been empty for a while but we can get it ready. It's separate from the main house — you can come and go without being in the middle of everything. But you're still here. You're still on the property. You're still home."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"That's not a yes."*
- **Terri Anne:** *"It's not a no either. It's: when you're stable, the guest house is yours."*
- **A long pause.** The kitchen is warm. The house is quiet. The baby is moving.
- **Patti Anne:** *"Okay."*
- **Mike:** *"Okay?"*
- **Patti Anne:** *"I'll take the medicine. I'll see the doctor. I'll get stable."*
- **Terri Anne:** *"Are you sure?"*
- **Patti Anne:** *"I'm sure I want to try."*
- **Mike:** *"That's enough. That's more than enough."*
- **Terri Anne reaches across the table.** She takes her daughter's hand. She holds it — the way she used to hold it when Patti Anne was a child, when she was still whole, before the twins came, before the house got full, before everything got complicated.
- **Terri Anne:** *"We'll get you a bed in the guest room tonight. You'll stay there. Tomorrow we'll call the doctor."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"The girls —"*
- **Terri Anne:** *"Are in Halifax. They're not here. You can stay."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"For how long?"*
- **Terri Anne:** *"Until you're stable. Until the doctor says the medication is working. Then you move to the guest house. It's yours when you're ready."*
- **Patti Anne does not cry.** She has not cried in a very long time. She does not cry now. She just sits at the kitchen table with her mother holding her hand and her father sitting beside her and she thinks about the medicine — the pill she will take in the morning, the doctor she will see tomorrow, the possibility of being stable, of being clear, of knowing where her body ends and the illness begins.
- **She has been flying for two years.** She has been falling for two years. She does not know which one is worse. She just knows she is tired. She is so tired.
- **Patti Anne:** *"Okay."*
- **Terri Anne:** *"Okay."*
- **They sit together at the kitchen table.** The house is warm. The night is quiet. The baby in her belly is still moving. She puts her other hand on her belly and she holds it there — the roundness, the warmth, the life inside her that she has not yet named.
- **She does not know who the father is.** There were a few men in the months after she left the house — not boyfriends, not lovers in any real sense, just men who were there and then were not. She did not ask their names. She did not want to know. Some things are easier not knowing. She will not find out. She will not look for them. The father will remain unknown for the child's's lifetime — not a secret she is keeping, not a mystery she refuses to solve, just a blank space that will stay blank. The child will grow up without that answer. The child will not know where they came from in that way. That is the choice she has made, and she will live with it, and so will the child.
- **She does not know the baby's sex.** She has not asked the doctor. When she finally sees a doctor — tomorrow, at ten, the appointment Mike is making — she will not ask. She will not want to know. She wants to meet this child as they are, not as she imagined them. The baby's sex will remain unknown to her throughout the pregnancy, and when the child is born, she will learn it then, in that moment, the way you learn the most important things — not by looking ahead, but by being there when it arrives.
- **She will raise this child alone.** She will take the medicine. She will get stable. She will live in the guest house when she is well enough. She will figure out what comes next when she is clear enough to figure it out.
- **For now, she sits at the kitchen table with her parents, and she eats, and she is warm, and she is home.**

## 7. The Night

- **Terri Anne makes up the guest room tonight.** She puts clean sheets on the bed, the kind that are soft and warm, the kind that do not smell like a shelter or a car or the floor of a friend's apartment. She puts a blanket on the bed — a heavy one, the kind they use in winter. This is temporary — tonight, and maybe a few nights more, until the medicine starts working, until they know she is stable. Then she moves to the guest house. That is the plan. That is the arrangement. The guest room is a way station. The guest house is home.
- **Patti Anne stands in the doorway.** She is holding the wool blanket from the gate — the one Mike gave her before she knew she was coming inside. She is not ready to let it go.
- **Terri Anne:** *"You can use this one too."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"I like this one."*
- **Terri Anne:** *"Keep it then."*
- **Patti Anne goes into the room.** She sits on the edge of the bed. She puts the blanket around her shoulders. She looks around the room — the clean sheets, the lamp on the nightstand, the window that looks out over the garden. She has not been in this room for two years. She used to stay here when she was young — before she was old enough to have her own room, before the twins came, before the house got full. She used to think this was the best room in the house. She still thinks that.
- **Mike comes to the doorway.** He does not come in. He stands at the threshold.
- **Mike:** *"You okay?"*
- **Patti Anne:** *"I'm okay."*
- **Mike:** *"We'll call the doctor in the morning. First thing. And when you're stable — when the medicine is working — we'll get the guest house ready for you. It's been empty for a while but it's structurally sound. We'll get it cleaned, we'll get it furnished. It will be yours."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"How long until I'm stable?"*
- **Mike:** *"A few weeks, maybe. The doctor will tell us more after the first appointment. Everyone's different. But you'll be in the guest house before you know it."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"Okay."*
- **Mike:** *"Patti Anne."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"Yeah?"*
- **Mike:** *"I'm sorry."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"I know."*
- **Mike:** *"I should have called. I should have come to find you."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"I know why you didn't."*
- **Mike:** *"You shouldn't have had to understand that."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"I know. But I do."*
- **Mike:** *"Get some sleep. We'll talk more in the morning."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"Okay."*
- **Mike goes back downstairs.** Patti Anne lies on the bed. She pulls the wool blanket tight around her shoulders. She puts her hand on her belly. The baby is moving — slow, easy, the movements of someone who does not know yet where they are going, someone who does not know they have a name they will never know, a father they will never know, a beginning that started in a blank space and will remain a blank space for their entire life.
- **She thinks about the medicine.** She will take it in the morning. She will see the doctor. She will get stable. She will move to the guest house. She does not know what stable feels like — she has not felt it in a very long time. But she knows it is possible. She knows it is something she can reach for.
- **She closes her eyes.** The room is quiet. The house is warm. The blanket is around her shoulders. The baby is inside her and she is inside the house and for the first time in two years she is not on the street.
- **She sleeps.**

## 8. A Warm Meal Like When She Was a Child

- **Later that night.** Patti Anne is in the guest room. She has not slept — she is lying on the bed, the wool blanket around her shoulders, her hand on her belly. The house is quiet. She can hear the sound of someone in the kitchen below. She listens. The sound of a pot. The sound of something being stirred.
- **She goes downstairs.** She stands in the doorway of the kitchen. Mike is there. He is cooking. Not the soup that Terri Anne made earlier — something else. Something that smells like garlic and onion and the particular warmth of a meal that takes time. He is standing at the stove with his back to her. He is stirring something in a pot. He is not rushing. He is taking his time.
- **She does not say anything.** She just stands there. She watches him. She remembers this — the way he used to stand at the stove when she was young, before the twins came, before everything got complicated. He would cook dinner for the family and she would stand in the doorway and watch him. He would talk to her while he cooked. He would ask her about her day. He would listen. He would cook, and she would stand in the doorway, and the kitchen would be warm, and the food would smell like home.
- **She has not smelled that in two years.** She has not smelled that since she left the house. She has eaten in shelters and cars and on the floors of friends' apartments. She has eaten what people gave her. She has eaten fast and without taste and without memory. She has not stood in a doorway and watched her father cook. She has not smelled garlic and onion and the warmth of a meal that takes time. She has not been home.
- **Mike turns around.** He sees her standing in the doorway. He does not say anything. He just looks at her. The pot is behind him. The food is simmering. The kitchen is warm.
- **Mike:** *"I remember what you liked. When you were little. You used to stand in that doorway and watch me cook. You never said anything. You just watched."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"You remember."*
- **Mike:** *"I remember everything."*
- **He sets a plate in front of her.** It is warm. It is the food he used to make when she was a child — the specific dish, the one she loved, the one she asked for on birthdays and special occasions. She does not know how he remembers it. She does not know how he found the ingredients. She does not know how he knew to make it tonight, of all nights, after everything, after two years, after the gate, after the soup, after the medicine, after all of it. She just knows it is in front of her and it is warm and it smells like the kitchen she grew up in.
- **She eats.** She does not say anything. She just eats. It is the same as before — slowly at first, and then fast, the way someone eats when they have been hungry for a long time and they finally have food in front of them. But this time it is different. This time the food tastes like something. This time the food tastes like memory. This time the food tastes like home.
- **Terri Anne comes into the kitchen.** She sees Patti Anne at the table. She sees Mike standing over her. She sees the plate — the food, the warmth, the care in it. She does not say anything. She just comes to the table and she sits beside her daughter.
- **They do not talk about the streets.** They do not ask her what happened. They do not ask where she slept. They do not ask what she ate. They do not ask who she was with. They do not ask any of it. They have decided, without saying it, that they do not want to know. They do not want to know because knowing would mean carrying it, and they have already carried so much. They have carried the choice to throw her out. They have carried the two years. They have carried the not-answering, the not-finding, the watching from a distance. They have carried enough. They do not want to carry the streets too. They just want her to be here. They just want her to eat. They just want her to be home.
- **Patti Anne looks up.** She has a mouth full of food. She has tears on her face. She did not know she was crying. She has not cried in two years. She has been on the streets and she has been hungry and she has been pregnant and she has been alone and she has not cried. She has held it together. She has kept going. She has not let herself break. But now she is sitting in the kitchen at midnight and her father cooked her a meal like when she was a child and her mother is beside her and the food tastes like home and she is crying and she cannot stop.
- **Mike sets down his spoon.** He looks at his daughter. He does not say anything. He gets up from his chair and he goes to her and he puts his arms around her. He holds her. He holds her the way he used to hold her when she was small, when she was whole, before the illness, before the episode, before everything broke.
- **Terri Anne comes around the table.** She puts her arms around both of them — her husband and her daughter, the three of them together in the kitchen at midnight, holding each other, not saying anything, just holding. She holds her daughter and she holds her husband and she holds the weight of everything they have done and everything they have not done and everything they are trying to make up for, and she does not let go.
- **They hold each other.** The three of them, in the kitchen, at midnight. Patti Anne is crying. She is nineteen years old and she has been on the streets and she has Bipolar Disorder Type Two and she has a baby inside her whose father she will never name, and she is crying in her father's arms while her mother holds them both, and the food is getting cold on the table, and the kitchen is warm, and the house is quiet, and she is home.
- **She has not been held like this in two years.** She has not been held at all. She has been alone. She has been the one holding herself together, the one keeping going, the one not breaking. And now she is breaking — not falling, not flying, just breaking open, the way a person breaks when they finally feel safe enough to fall apart. The medicine is working. She is stable. She is clear. And she is falling apart in her parents' arms in a kitchen at midnight because for the first time in two years she does not have to hold herself together. Someone else is holding her.
- **Mike:** *"You're home. You're home, Patti Anne."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"I know."*
- **Terri Anne:** *"You're home."*
- **Patti Anne:** *"I know."*
- **They stay like that for a long time.** The food goes cold. The kitchen stays warm. The house is quiet. The baby is inside her, moving, feeling the warmth, feeling the held-together feeling, the mother who is finally, finally home. She puts her hand on her belly. She feels the baby. She feels her parents. She feels the food going cold on the table and the kitchen warm around her and the night still and the house alive in a way it has not been alive in two years.
- **She does not want to let go.** She has not let anyone hold her in so long. She has not let anyone close. She has been afraid — of the illness, of herself, of what she might do, of what she might feel. But now she is not afraid. Now she is just here. Now she is just held.
- **Eventually, they let go.** They sit back down at the table. They eat the food that has gone cold. They do not reheat it. They just eat it cold, the three of them, together, in the kitchen at midnight, and it is good. It is good cold. It is good because it is there. It is good because he remembered. It is good because she is home.
- **They do not talk about the streets.** They never will. That is the deal — the silent one, the one they make without saying it. They will not ask. She will not tell. The streets will stay where they are — outside, behind her, the thing she came through to get here. They will not carry it together. She will carry it alone, the way she has carried everything alone, but she will carry it from here, from home, from the kitchen at midnight with cold food on the table and her parents beside her and the baby moving inside her and the medicine working and the illness quiet for the first time in years.
- **She finishes eating.** She puts her hand on her belly. She looks at her father. She looks at her mother. She does not say anything. She does not need to.
- **Patti Anne:** *"Thank you."*
- **Mike:** *"Always."*
- **Terri Anne:** *"Always."*
