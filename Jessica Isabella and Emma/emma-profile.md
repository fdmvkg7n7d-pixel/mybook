## BACKGROUND — WHERE EMMA COMES FROM

### The Father Who Left — and Came Back Without Love

Emma's father, **Randy**, left when she was five.

Not in the dramatic way — no shouting, no slammed doors, no single catastrophic night that she can point to and say *there, that's when it broke*. He left the way a tide goes out. Gradually, then all at once. First the late nights. Then the missed weekends. Then the phone calls that became shorter, then sporadic, then silent. Then he was gone.

Emma does not remember the exact moment she understood he was not coming back. She remembers the moment she stopped asking.

Randy was not, in the beginning, a cruel man. This is the hardest thing to write about him, and the most important. He was warm, once. He was present, for a while. He held his daughter. He made Catherine laugh — really laugh, the kind of laugh that Catherine barely remembered she was capable of.

And then he couldn't sustain it.

There was no single moment. No argument that broke the marriage. No betrayal. Instead, there was erosion. The apartment, the baby, the demands of a life that required him to be present in specific ways at specific times — he began to experience these as a weight. Not because the weight was unusual. Because something inside him could not bear weight. He did not name this. He could not have named it. He just... thinned. He became less present until his absence was louder than his presence had ever been, and then he was gone.

**His own father left too.** The intergenerational pattern. Randy inherited leaving — the pattern repeated through him, as it may have repeated through his father before him. Emma is the one who breaks the chain by staying. But breaking a pattern does not mean being free of it. It means carrying it consciously — knowing that the tendency lives in you, that the gravity of leaving is real, and that staying is a daily choice, not a natural state.

**He is Muslim.** Same tradition as Catherine. They were a couple at the mosque, known together. When he left, the community that had watched them build a life looked at Catherine as half of something broken. She could not bear the questions, the pity, the silence louder than words.

> **[Canon note — Randy's timeline reconciled.]** Randy did **not** work for Mike Key when Catherine died. The order of Randy's life: marries Catherine (both Fritz) → leaves when Emma is 5 → Catherine dies (Emma 8) → Randy returns to take custody, not yet connected to the Keys → *later* becomes a Key Porn Studios employee (the job tied to Patti's parentage and the Historican backstory) → disowns Emma at 16, and Mike fires him.

**Then Catherine died.** When Emma was eight, breast cancer took the one parent who had stayed. And Randy — the man who left — came back. Not because he had changed. Not because he had grown. But because there was no one else, and a child cannot be left in an empty apartment. He returned out of **obligation, not love.**

This is the cruelty that is worse than cruelty: he came back, and it changed nothing. He gave Emma a roof, food, the gymnastics fees, the machinery of a managed life. He did not give her warmth. He did not hold her while she grieved her mother. He was present in body and absent in every way that mattered — the same absence that had defined him when he left, now living under the same roof. Emma learned that a parent can be in the next room and still be gone.

**And then he abandoned her again.** When Emma was sixteen and finally told the truth about who she loved, Randy threw her out of his life. (See the Key family profiles: he told Mike Key to take the twins away from Jessica "before she turns them into lesbians"; Mike threw Randy out of his house and fired him.) The man who left her at five, who returned at eight without love, abandoned her a final time at sixteen — this time on purpose, with full intention, choosing it.

**Emma is abandoned twice by the same man.** Once by his leaving. Once by his rejection. The first time, he could not help it. The second time, he chose it. That is the shape of Emma's wound: not the question *why did you leave?* but the harder one — *why, when you came back, could you still not love me?*

**He loved Emma once.** This is not in dispute. He held her as a baby. He was the first person to make her laugh — a fact Catherine never told her, because it would have meant admitting that the man who left was also the man who stayed, once. Catherine took that fact to her grave. Emma will never know it.

### Catherine (Mother) — Who She Was, and What She Left in Emma

*Catherine is gone before the saga's present. She lives in it as memory, as the shape of Emma's habits, and as the grief of everything that was never said.*

**Who she was before:** Catherine was not always tired. Before the apartment with one income and the gymnastics fees she could barely afford, before the prayer rug in the corner and the halal meals she made for one — Catherine had a life with texture.

She grew up in [another city — TBD] with a full community — a mosque she walked to on Fridays, an auntie who taught her to cook, cousins who filled the apartment with noise, a father who came home every evening and was *there*, reliably. Catherine grew up knowing what a full house felt like. She grew up knowing that love was not something you earned — it was something you came home to.

She was bright. She was funny — genuinely funny, the kind that surprised people. She had opinions. She said them. She was not the quiet woman Emma remembers. She became that woman later.

Then she met Randy. She chose him. They built a life. Emma was born. He was present. And then he wasn't.

**What the leaving cost Catherine:** A partner. A second income. Her community (most of it left behind in another city). And — the loss she felt most — *the version of herself that was not tired.* The woman who had opinions and said them. The woman who was funny. The woman who had plans. That woman did not die when Randy left; she was buried under the exhaustion of doing everything alone. And then Catherine herself died, before she could ever dig that woman back out.

**Her work:** Administrative assistant. The invisible worker. She organized everyone else's schedules, fixed everyone else's problems, and came home too tired to fix anything for herself. The job was a mirror of her mothering.

**Her love for Emma:** Expressed through action, not words. The rent paid. The dinner in the oven. The gymnastics fees covered. She did not say *I'm proud of you* or *I see you*. She said it through the structure she maintained. Emma learned to translate. *The rent is paid = I love you. The dinner is in the oven = I love you. I am too tired to hold you but I made sure you have a roof = I love you, I love you, I love you.*

This was not inadequate. It was real. But it was incomplete. And then it ended.

**Catherine's faith — the Islam she carried:** Not the Islam of public declaration. Domestic Islam. The prayer rug she unfolded at dawn, before Emma woke. The *bismillah* before meals. *Sabr* — patience, endurance — made into the architecture of her whole life, until endurance became indistinguishable from self-erasure. *Tawakkul* — trust in God's plan. She practiced alone. She prayed alone. The nearest mosque was a drive, not a walk. God was everywhere and Catherine was lonely, and those two truths existed in the same space without resolving each other. This private faith is what she passed to Emma: that faith is a private thing, kept in the corners, not the center of the room.

**What Catherine left in Emma (her arc, now an inheritance):** Catherine's arc was the inverse of Emma's. *Emma must learn to be seen. Catherine never learned to speak.* For her whole short life Catherine held everything in silence — her grief, her anger, her exhaustion, her love — and expressed all of it only through action. She never once said the thing she felt most deeply: *I am tired. I am scared. I love you so much it terrifies me because the people I love have a habit of leaving.*

She did not get the chance to learn better and pass it on. She died with the words unsaid. And so Emma inherited the silence whole, unbroken, with no one to teach her the other half. This is the tragedy at the root of Emma: the lesson she most needs to unlearn was sealed into her by the death of the person who taught it. **What Catherine could not give Emma — permission to need, to ask, to be seen — Jessica must give her instead (see Thread #10).**

**What Emma carries about Catherine and Jessica:** Catherine never met Jessica. She died when Emma was eight; Emma met Jessica at ten. The great love of Emma's life is someone her mother never knew existed. Emma carries this as a specific grief: she will never get to watch her mother see her light up at Jessica's name. She will never get to introduce them. She will never know whether Catherine — a Muslim mother — would have blessed her daughter loving a Catholic girl, raising children between two faiths, building a family the world did not recognize. Emma carries an *imagined* Catherine: a mother she consults in her head, whose verdict she will never actually hear. *Would you have stayed? Would you have understood? Would you have loved me through this, the way you loved me through everything quiet?* The question, like the question of Randy, is a room with no one in it.

### The Mike Key Mirror

Mike Key is the father who stays. Randy is the one who left, came back without love, and left again. When Emma encounters Mike, she encounters the father she never had — the proof that staying was possible, that a parent could show up in hospitals, protect his children, choose them without condition. This is not resentment toward Mike. It is grief aimed at an absence — two absences, a father's and a mother's. And when Mike and Terri Anne take Emma into their home after Randy disowns her, Emma receives, at sixteen, the unconditional welcome she lost when Catherine died at eight.

### The Move to Burlington

When Emma was nine — the year after Catherine died — she moved to Burlington, into Randy's custody. Not because Burlington was a dream. Because it was where her father was, and her father was, now, all she had. A smaller city. A place where Randy could drop her at a gymnastics club after school and not have to think about her in the hours between. Gymnastics was not a passion anyone chose for Emma out of love. It was childcare — a way to occupy a grieving child a reluctant father did not know how to hold. He would not have put it that way. He would not have put it any way at all. But it gave Emma the one thing that was hers.

### What Emma Carries (The Terrain)

**She has learned to need less.**

Not to want less. She wants. She wants badly. She wants to be seen, to be held, to be told *you matter to me in a way that has nothing to do with whether you're being good.* She wants a family that fills a room. She wants a hand to hold in public. She wants a mother who is alive. She wants a father who came back and meant it. She wants to stop translating love from silence.

But she has taught herself that wanting is dangerous. That asking for things makes you a burden. That the people who love you either leave, or die, or come back without love — and to ask for more is to risk the little you have.

So she stays. She stays and she does not ask. She stays and she does not reach. She stays and she loves in the way her mother taught her: through presence, through reliability, through the act of simply being there when no one expects her to be.

This is beautiful. It is also a prison.

Jessica Isabella Key is the first person who sees the prison. Not because Emma shows her — Emma does not show anyone. But Jessica is the girl who reaches across. It is her nature. She reaches across the room to a new girl at gymnastics. She reaches across the kitchen to push her sister out of the way of boiling water. And when she reaches across to Emma, she sees what no one else has seen: **a girl who is holding so tightly to the bar that she doesn't know she can let go.**

Jessica does not teach Emma to let go. That would be the wrong metaphor. She teaches Emma that she can hold on with one hand and reach with the other. That staying does not mean disappearing. That being the one who stays and being the one who is seen are not opposites.

This is the terrain. It is not dramatic in the way a fire is. There is no burn ward. There is only the slow, invisible erosion of a child who lost her mother, was raised coldly by the father who'd left, and learned that love is what people do when they can't say it — and the girl who finally says it.

But she stays in the margins. She stays when no one is looking. She stays in the places where love can exist without being named.

What Emma does at sixteen is the thing that defines her: she stays when staying is invisible. She stays when the world expects her to walk away. She stays when the question is not whether she will stay, but whether she can be seen doing it. And the answer, for Emma, has always been yes. Even if no one knows.

This is also the year her father throws her away. (See the Key family profiles.) The same year she is most fully a partner and a parent in secret is the year Randy, the man who left and came back without love, leaves her a final time — and the Keys take her in.

## THREAD #10 — JESSICA GUIDES EMMA

### What This Thread Is

Thread #10 is not a single event. It is a series of moments — small, specific, largely invisible to the outside world — in which Jessica Isabella Key actively, deliberately pulls Emma out of the habit of disappearing. It is the story of how a girl who reaches teaches a girl who stays that she is allowed to be seen. It is Jessica giving Emma the second half of a lesson Catherine died before she could teach.

It is not a rescue. Emma does not need rescuing. She needs *witnessing*. And witnessing is what Jessica gives her — not all at once, but in increments, in gestures, in the slow accumulation of small moments that say: *I see you. You are not too much. You are not a burden. You are here, and that is enough, and I am telling you that because you will never ask.*

The thread has five movements:

### Movement 1 — Jessica Sees the Wound

**When:** After the pregnancy becomes visible. After the family knows. After Emma is holding Jessica's hand in the hospital stairwell and no one knows she's there.

**What happens:**

They are in the stairwell. Jessica is sitting on a step. Emma is beside her. This is their place — the one corner of the hospital where no one comes. Jessica has been crying. Emma has been holding her hand. This is what Emma does. She holds. She stays.

But this time, Jessica looks at her. Really looks. And she sees something she has seen before but never named: Emma is exhausted. Not from the pregnancy — that's Jessica's body. Emma is exhausted from holding. From staying. From being the one who absorbs everything and gives everything and asks for nothing in return. She is exhausted from the particular loneliness of being the person no one knows is there.

Jessica says: *You're always here for me.*

Emma says: *Where else would I be?*

And Jessica hears what Emma doesn't say. She hears: *I don't know how to be anywhere else. I don't know how to leave. I don't know how to be the one who needs something instead of the one who provides it.*

This is the moment Jessica understands that Emma's love — beautiful, steadfast, invisible — is also a cage. And she built it herself, out of a dead mother and an absent father and a lifetime of learning to need less.

Jessica does not say any of this. She files it away. She reaches across and takes Emma's other hand — both hands now — and she doesn't let go until Emma stops shaking.

Emma didn't know she was shaking.

### Movement 2 — The First Reach

**When:** Sometime after the stairwell. A quiet evening, in one of their hidden places.

**What happens:**

Jessica asks Emma a question she has never asked before: *What do you want?*

Not *what do you want for dinner.* The question. The big one. The one Emma has never been asked by anyone, including herself.

Emma doesn't have an answer. She is sixteen and she has spent her entire life learning to translate her wants into something smaller, quieter, more acceptable. She has learned to want things that don't inconvenience anyone. Gymnastics — because someone was paying for it anyway. Good grades — because they don't cost anything.

Jessica sees her struggling. She waits. This is what Jessica does for Emma that no one else does: she waits without filling the silence.

Emma finally says: *I don't know. No one's asked me that before.*

Jessica says: *I'm asking now.*

This is the first reach. Not a rescue. Not a solution. Just a question. The beginning of a door.

### Movement 3 — Emma Resists

**When:** Over weeks or months. The pregnancy progresses. Emma is in the room sometimes — as the friend, the gymnastics partner — and she is invisible. She helps. She carries bags. She is useful. Being useful is how she justifies being there.

**What happens:**

Jessica starts doing small things that are unlike what Emma expects:

- She saves Emma a seat — in the quiet places — and looks at Emma until Emma sits down. *You don't have to earn your place here. I saved it for you.*
- She asks Emma's opinion about things that don't involve Jessica, as if Emma's interior life matters, as if Emma is a person with a self that exists outside of what she does for other people. Emma is startled every time.
- She tells Emma things she doesn't tell anyone else — not secrets, truths — modeling what she wants Emma to do.

Emma resists. Not deliberately. She resists by deflection, by turning every gift into a debt, by responding to Jessica's vulnerability with care and competence but never with her own. She never lets the scales tip in her direction. Because if she takes more than she gives, she becomes a burden. And she learned early — when a father left, when a mother died, when a father came back without love — that burdens are the first things people leave behind.

Jessica sees this. She does not push. She keeps reaching. She is patient with Emma the way Emma has been patient with everyone else, and this reversal is the thing Emma doesn't know how to receive.

### Movement 4 — The Names

**When:** Before the twins are born. The question of names is real, present, urgent. Everyone has opinions and everyone is saying them out loud. Jessica and Emma are alone.

**What happens:**

Jessica says: *I want to name them after you.*

Emma says: *What do you mean?*

Jessica says: *Not after you. From you. From your world. From the things you grew up with that no one in my family knows about. I want their names to be yours.*

Emma doesn't understand. Or she understands and she can't believe it. No one has ever given her a piece of something permanent. Randy left, came back without love, and would leave again. Catherine gave what she could — and then she was gone, when Emma was eight, and there was no one left to give Emma anything that lasted. Her love for Jessica exists in the margins, in the dark. Nothing about Emma is permanent. Nothing about her is written down.

And now Jessica wants to write her down. In the names of her children. In the language of Emma's dead mother.

They talk about it. Maybe they argue — not because Emma disagrees, but because she is afraid. A name is a public thing, on the birth certificate. If Jessica names her daughters Samira and Sahara, someone will ask where those names came from.

Emma says: *What if someone figures it out?*

Jessica says: *Then they figure it out.*

Emma says: *Your family—*

Jessica says: *My family will love them. And the names are theirs. Not my family's. Ours.*

**Samira.** Companion in evening conversation. The one who stays up with you when the rest of the house is asleep.

**Sahara Hannan.** The desert and the compassion. The waiting for water and the tenderness that comes when it arrives.

Emma cries when they decide on the names. Not the quiet, controlled tears she usually cries when she's alone. The messy kind. The kind that come from being given something she didn't ask for — and from hearing her mother's language spoken back to her, out loud, chosen, kept, made into the names of children who will live.

This is the moment Emma begins to understand that Jessica is not just the person she stays for. Jessica is the person who refuses to let her disappear. The names are the proof.

### Movement 5 — Emma Reaches Back

**When:** After the twins are born. When the family has gone home. When it is just Jessica and Emma and two small babies in a room.

**What happens:**

Jessica is holding Sahara. Emma is holding Samira. They are sitting side by side, close enough to touch.

Emma looks at Samira — at the tiny face, the absolute trust of a newborn who does not yet know the world will ask her to prove she belongs in it. Emma looks at this child and she sees herself: the child who learned to need less, the girl who learned to stay without being seen, the girl who lost her mother at eight.

And something breaks. Not dramatically. Something inside Emma — a wall she built so long ago she forgot it was there — shifts.

She says: *Jessica.*

Jessica looks at her.

Emma says: *I need you.*

Three words. The first three words Emma has ever spoken that are purely, entirely about what *she* needs.

She says: *I need you to know that I'm scared. I'm scared that I'm going to do to them what my father did to me. I'm scared that I'm going to love them in the margins the way I love you, and they deserve more than the margins. I'm scared I don't know how to be seen. I'm scared I'm going to disappear into this family without anyone knowing I was here. I'm scared that I need too much and that needing too much is the thing that makes people leave. Or die.*

She says all of this without looking up. She says it to Samira. Because saying it to Jessica is too much.

Jessica takes Samira from her, gently, and puts the baby in the bassinet beside Sahara. Then she takes both of Emma's hands. Both of them. The way she did in the stairwell.

Jessica says: *You are not going to disappear.*

Emma says: *How do you know?*

Jessica says: *Because I won't let you. Because you are here. Because you have always been here. Because you are in their names. Because you are in my life. Because the only person who can't see you is you, and I will spend the rest of my life holding a mirror up to you until you can.*

Emma reaches.

For the first time in her life, she does not stay. She reaches. She lets Jessica hold her. She lets herself be held. She lets herself need — the thing Catherine never taught her was allowed, because Catherine died before she could.

This is what Jessica guides Emma through. Not a single moment of revelation, but a slow, patient, daily practice of being seen. Of learning that wanting is not dangerous. Of learning that the people who love you are not tolerating you — they are choosing you. Of learning that *sabr* — patience, steadfastness, her mother's last gift — is not just a way of being. It is a way of loving. And it is enough. More than enough. But it doesn't have to be the only thing.

## ARC AND CORE THEME

Emma is the one who stays. Not because she is passive — because she has decided that staying is what love looks like. It is not dramatic. It is not announced. It is simply: she is at the door. She has always been at the door.

Her wounds are quiet ones. A father who left when she was five. A mother — the parent who stayed, who loved in silence, who taught Emma that love is what you do when you can't say it — dead of breast cancer when Emma was eight. And then that same father, returned out of duty, raising her without warmth, and finally throwing her away at sixteen for loving a girl. Abandoned twice by the man, orphaned once by the woman. Emma learns, young and completely, that the people you love leave, or die, or come back without love — and so she makes herself small, makes herself useful, learns to need less.

She meets Jessica Isabella Key when they are both ten. Jessica is the one who reaches across. Emma is the one who stays. Between them, they build something neither could build alone. When Jessica is burned and spends six months in hospital, Emma waits. When Jessica comes home, Emma is there. At sixteen, the friendship becomes love — and the pregnancy comes, and Emma stays again, in secret, in the spaces between what the family sees and what the girls know.

Her wound is invisible — Randy who left and came back hollow, Catherine who loved quietly and died too soon, the childhood where she learned to need less. Jessica's wound is visible — the burns, the hospital, the body marked by sacrifice. They are each other's blind spot made flesh. Jessica teaches Emma to be seen. Emma teaches Jessica to rest. And in the great absence at Emma's center — the mother who died before she could finish teaching her daughter how to be loved — Jessica steps in, not to replace Catherine, but to finish her sentence.

The names — Samira, Sahara, Hannan — are the proof that Emma exists in the permanent record, even when no one knows to look for her there. They are also the proof that Catherine exists there: a grandmother the twins will never meet, alive in the Arabic of their names. They are Jessica's love letter written in a language only they share — the language of a dead woman, kept alive.

The person who waits six months and is at the door when you come home — that is not a minor character. That is the centre of the story. Even when no one knows she's there.

*Especially* when no one knows she's there.
