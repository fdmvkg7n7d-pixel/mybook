# SCENE OUTLINE: THE DONOR — FINDING HIM
## Donation Drive + Sperm Bank Visit (Combined)

**Part of the ~2054 second child arc.**
**Characters:** Jessica Isabella Key (30), Emma Fritz (30), clinic liaison Maricel, and the donor — known in the outline as **Donor 7-Asha** (full profile below).
**Time in saga:** ~2054, after the decision conversation and before the Russia vacation where they tell the twins. This scene sits between those two beats.
**Tone:** Quiet, deliberate, hopeful. Clinical details stay high-level. The scene is about two people choosing — not passively receiving — the next chapter of their family's story. Saga restraint throughout.

---

## THE DONOR — FULL PROFILE

### Donor Code: 7-Asha

**Why this code:** The clinic uses numbered codes paired with an optional personal identifier chosen by the donor. He chose *Asha* — meaning *hope* or *life* in several traditions (Sanskrit, Swahili, Arabic). He didn't explain it in the paperwork. He explained it in his essay, which is the first thing Jessica and Emma read.

---

### Background

- **Full name:** Not disclosed. He is enrolled in the identity-release program — the sons may contact him when they are 18 if they choose.
- **Age at time of donation:** 29.
- **Ethnicity / Background:** Mixed heritage. Father's family from coastal West Africa (Ghana). Mother's family from the United Kingdom (Scottish, with some Irish). He grew up in Toronto. Dual citizenship (Canadian and British).
- **Build / Physical:** Tall, lean, dark-skinned. Brown eyes. Quiet in his face — the kind of person who looks like he is always about to say something careful.
- **Education:** Undergraduate degree in Music (theory and composition), University of Toronto. Currently completing a master's in Music Education at Dalhousie University. He is in Halifax at the time of donation. The clinic does not tell Jessica and Emma this. They do not need to know.
- **Occupation:** Teaching assistant in the Faculty of Music at Dalhousie. Gives private piano and composition lessons on the side.
- **Religion / Spiritual life:** Not actively practicing. Mother raised him in a broadly Christian household (Church of Scotland, irregularly attended). Father's family holds traditional Ghanaian spiritual practices — he describes them with respect but says he no longer observes any formal tradition. He writes: *I believe in responsibility. I believe in what you leave behind. I believe that music is the closest thing I have to prayer.*
- **Health:** Excellent. No hereditary conditions flagged on either side. No history of cancer, heart disease, diabetes, or neurological disorders. Full genetic carrier screening completed, all results negative. Blood type O positive.
- **Physical family history:** Father and mother both living, both healthy in their 50s. One older brother (31), one younger sister (25). No family history of hereditary illness across three generations.
- **Children of his own:** None.
- **Relationship status:** Not in a relationship at the time of donation.
- **Donation history:** Two prior donations; two known successful pregnancies. He knows this. He says it matters to him that the families are real, not abstractions.
- **Why he donates:** He has one close friend — a woman he has known since secondary school — who could not conceive. He watched her go through three failed IVF cycles. He donated for the first time because of her (she was already matched with another donor by then, but he went through with it anyway). He has not stopped.

---

### The Donor Essay (Read by Jessica and Emma at the Clinic)

*They give me a page. I'll try to use it well.*

*My name in this file is 7-Asha. Asha means hope. My mother's name is Margaret. My father's name is Kwame. Between them I have two languages, two landscapes, two ways of understanding what it means to belong somewhere. I grew up in Toronto, which is its own third thing.*

*I am a musician. Not a famous one — a real one. I spend most of my days listening. To music, to students, to the spaces between notes. I believe you can hear a great deal about a person in how they handle silence.*

*I donate because I believe that families are not accidental. I have watched people fight hard — harder than I can describe — to build a family. I have seen what it costs. I want to be useful to that fight, in the small way available to me.*

*I am not asking to be part of your family. I understand what this is and what it isn't. But I want the child — if there is one — to know someday that their beginning was not anonymous in the way that word usually means. I was not indifferent. I was deliberate. I chose this.*

*I am enrolled in an identity-release program. When your child is eighteen, if they want to find me, I will be there. I will answer their questions honestly. I will not make it complicated.*

*That is all I can offer. I think it is enough.*

*— 7-Asha*

---

### Why This Donor Fits This Family

- **The interfaith household:** He holds no rigid faith of his own but speaks of both parents' traditions with respect. He will not complicate the Catholic-and-Islamic-heritage household. He will add nothing that needs to be explained away.
- **Emma's register:** His essay is written in the same careful, restrained, precise cadence that Emma lives in. She will read it and recognize something — not herself exactly, but the shape of it.
- **Jessica's instinct:** The musician. Jessica is the gymnast, the mover, the reacher. When she reads that he *listens* — that music, for him, is about the spaces between notes — something clicks. The older twins (Samira and Sahara) are already ballet dancers. The new sons may carry something else. Music. Or at least its possibility.
- **Identity-release:** The family does not keep secrets about origins. Having a donor already enrolled in identity-release — who has already decided the child deserves to find him if they want to — is not a policy checkbox. It is a value. Their value, handed back to them by a stranger.
- **Heritage:** The sons will be born into a family where one mother is Irish-English Canadian (Jessica, Key family), one mother is of Muslim heritage (Emma, born in Burlington), and the older sisters carry unknown-father origins from Brazil. The donor's mixed West African and British heritage adds another layer to an already layered family. The sons will carry multiple inheritances, none of which cancel each other out. This is correct for this family.
- **The Dalhousie proximity (never known to them):** He is completing his master's in Halifax. He may have sat at a table near them in the library. He may have held a door. The clinic does not tell them. They do not need to know. But the proximity is a quiet grace note the saga can decide whether to use.

## THE COMBINED SCENE

### Part One — The Donation Drive

**Setting:** A private vetted event held by the clinic. Not a public open house. A curated gathering where the Keys are given a private room, a liaison, and access to a shortlist of pre-selected profiles. The Keys' profile makes a standard open event impossible; privacy is the clinic's primary concern. They are given ten profiles chosen against criteria they submitted in advance.

---

**Before they arrive:**

Jessica and Emma submitted their criteria to the clinic's liaison — Maricel — three weeks earlier. The list is detailed: health history, genetic screening, identity-release willingness, values, educational and professional background. And one line that Emma added, almost as an afterthought, and then did not remove:

*We would like the donor to have thought, at least once, about what it means to leave something good behind.*

Maricel reads it. She puts it in the file. She selects ten profiles. Donor 7-Asha is number seven. In the middle. Not highlighted.

---

**Arrival:**

Late morning. Halifax, mid-week. The clinic is in a low building near the harbour — chosen for discretion, not visibility. Maricel meets them at the side entrance. She is a small, efficient woman in her forties. Fourteen years in this work. She can tell, within the first five minutes of meeting a family, which ones are certain and which ones are still deciding whether they are allowed to want this.

She meets Jessica and Emma and thinks: *certain.* Then: *scared.* Then: *both of those things at once.*

They are taken to a private consultation room. Soft chairs, low table, water, a box of tissues nobody will touch yet. On the table: a tablet and a binder. Ten profiles. Maricel explains the process. Jessica listens with her hands folded in her lap, the way she holds herself when she is paying serious attention. Emma has a pen. She doesn't plan to take notes. She doesn't know why she has the pen. She keeps it.

---

**Reading the profiles:**

They go through the binder together, side by side.

The first six profiles are fine. Well-screened, clearly decent people. Jessica says of one: *He sounds kind.* Emma says: *He does.* They set it aside. Three more. One has a multi-generational history of hypertension; Jessica, given her own medical past, is quietly uneasy. She doesn't call it a no. She puts it at the bottom.

And then profile number seven.

Emma picks it up. She reads the first line — *My name in this file is 7-Asha* — and something shifts almost imperceptibly in her posture. Not a reaction. A recognition. She reads it through without saying anything. When she finishes, she sets it on the table between them.

She says: *Read this one.*

Jessica reads it slowly. She reaches the line about listening — *I spend most of my days listening. To music, to students, to the spaces between notes. I believe you can hear a great deal about a person in how they handle silence* — and stops. She reads it again.

She says: *He's a musician.*

Emma says: *He's at Dalhousie.*

A pause. They look at each other.

Jessica says: *We've probably sat in the same library.*

Neither of them knows if that's true. But neither dismisses it. The world is not large. This family has always known that. Rachel Darcy walked into a bathroom. Emma's father and Mike worked together. Patti Anne is Emma's half-sister and neither knew it until sixteen. The world folds back on itself. That is not a reason to say yes. But it is not nothing.

Emma says: *Read the last paragraph.*

Jessica reads it. *When your child is eighteen, if they want to find me, I will be there. I will answer their questions honestly. I will not make it complicated.*

She sets the profile down. Quiet for a moment.

Then: *That's what we'd want.*

Emma: *Yes.*

---

**Conversation with Maricel:**

They ask for more on 7-Asha. Maricel answers what she can: health and genetic profile, educational background, two prior donations, two known successful pregnancies. He is currently enrolled in identity-release and confirmed willingness again at his most recent screening. He has placed no preferences or conditions on the type of family.

Jessica asks: *Has he ever met a recipient family?*

Maricel: *Not this family. But he has exchanged letters through the clinic with one of the two prior families. At their request.*

Emma: *He agreed?*

Maricel: *He answered the same day.*

Emma writes something on the notepad in front of her. She crosses it out.

Jessica: *What did he write to them?*

Maricel: *I'm not able to share that. But I can tell you the family kept the letter.*

Emma looks at the profile again. She is looking at: *I was not indifferent. I was deliberate. I chose this.*

She says, quietly, to Jessica — not to Maricel: *That's us.*

Jessica looks at her.

Emma: *That's us. We're not just — we're choosing this. Like he chose it. We chose each other. We're choosing who he's going to be in their story. We're —* She stops. She picks up the pen again. Doesn't write. *I'm not explaining this well.*

Jessica takes her hand under the table.

*You are.*

---

**End of the drive session:**

They do not decide today. This is the rule they set before they came: no decisions on the day. They take the profile home — all ten, but they both know which one they will come back to.

They leave the clinic side entrance the way they came. Early afternoon. Halifax harbour visible from the street. The light is bright.

Emma is quiet on the walk to the car.

Then: *The twins will ask about him someday.*

Jessica: *Yes.*

Emma: *And we'll be able to say: he was a musician. He believed in what you leave behind. He chose this.*

Jessica: *That's a good story to give them.*

Emma: *It's not a story. It's true.*

Jessica: *I know. That's what makes it good.*

---

### Part Two — The Sperm Bank Visit (Final Selection)

**One week later. Same clinic, different room.**

They have read 7-Asha's essay twice more, separately, without telling each other. They both circle the same lines on their own copies. Emma circles: *I was not indifferent. I was deliberate.* Jessica circles: *I believe that music is the closest thing I have to prayer.*

They sit down with Maricel.

Maricel: *Have you made a decision, or would you like to discuss more options?*

Emma: *We've made a decision.*

She looks at Jessica. Jessica nods once.

Emma: *7-Asha.*

---

**What follows (kept high-level):**

Maricel walks them through the process. The mechanics are not the point. What matters:

- **Identity-release confirmed.** They sign paperwork. If and when the sons are ready, at eighteen, the path exists. The clinic holds the information. The family holds the key.
- **Medical coordination.** Maricel connects them with Jessica's fertility specialist. Medical steps to follow: egg retrieval, fertilization, embryo creation, transfer to Jessica. Timeline set with the fertility team.
- **One document:** A printed summary card — donor code, health profile summary, donation history, identity-release status. Emma puts it in her bag. She won't look at it again for a while. She has already memorized what matters.

---

**The moment:**

After the paperwork, Maricel steps out to make copies.

Jessica and Emma are alone.

Jessica: *Are we good?*

Emma looks at the window. Then at Jessica.

*I keep thinking about Catherine.*

A pause.

*She would have wanted to know. She would have had opinions — about his health history, whether he seemed kind. She would have asked if he was funny. She always said you can tell everything you need to know about a person from whether they're funny.*

She looks at her hands.

*I don't know if he's funny. The essay doesn't say.*

Jessica: *The essay says he listens. I think funny people listen well.*

Emma looks at her.

Jessica: *That's a guess.*

Emma: *It's a good guess.*

A pause.

*She would have liked that he chose Asha. She would have liked that it means hope. She would have said —* Emma stops. Exhales slowly. *She would have said: that's the right name for a man who does this.*

Jessica doesn't answer. She takes both of Emma's hands — the way she does when it matters — and she holds them until Maricel comes back.

---

**Leaving:**

They leave together. Emma's bag. The decision made. What comes next is medical and months away — the egg retrieval, the fertilization, the transfer, the pregnancy, the Russia vacation where they will tell the twins before any of that begins.

But for now, outside on the Halifax street, the light bright near the water —

Jessica says the names out loud for the first time. Not as a question. As a fact that does not yet exist but will.

*Eliot David and Kalvin LaMarcus.*

Emma says them back.

*They're going to be so loved.*

Jessica: *They already are.*

## WHAT THE SAGA DOES WITH THE DONOR

### What the sons will be told (when they are old enough)
Per the family's truth-telling rule:
- They have two mothers: Jessica, who carried them, and Emma, who has always been there.
- They were made with the help of a donor — a man who chose to help families, who left something good behind on purpose.
- His name in the record is 7-Asha. Asha means hope. He was a musician.
- When they are eighteen, if they want to find him, they can. He said he would be there.

### Whether the sons contact him
TBD — left open. The clinic holds the path. Whether Eliot David or Kalvin LaMarcus chooses to walk it is their story, when they are old enough to tell it.

### Whether Jessica and Emma ever know he was in Halifax
TBD — left open. A quiet grace note available to the saga if it chooses to use it.

### His structural role
He is not a father. He is not a stranger. He is the deliberate person in the background of the origin story — the one who chose this, who enrolled in identity-release, who wrote an essay that two women read in a room near Halifax harbour and recognized something in. He does not know their names. They know his essay by heart. That asymmetry is correct. It is also, in its way, tender.
