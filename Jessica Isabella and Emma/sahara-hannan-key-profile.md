## MEDICAL — TYPE 1 DIABETES

When Sahara Hannan is **four years old**, she is diagnosed with **Type 1 diabetes** — what older generations call "sugar diabetes." It is the autoimmune form that typically appears in childhood: her body stops producing insulin, and so insulin must be supplied from outside, every day, for the rest of her life. It is no one's fault. It is not caused by anything she ate or anything her mothers did. It simply arrives, the way it arrives for so many small children, and the family meets it the way the Key family meets everything: practically, completely, without panic and without pity.

**What it means, day to day:**
- **Blood-sugar monitoring** — finger-pricks and/or a continuous glucose monitor (CGM) worn on the body, tracked through the day and night.
- **Insulin** — delivered by an **insulin pump**: a small device worn on the body that supplies insulin continuously (basal) plus dosed amounts at meals (bolus). Paired with her CGM, the pump can run in a hybrid closed-loop ("automated") mode that adjusts delivery automatically as her glucose rises and falls — particularly valuable for an active child whose levels swing with training. No multiple daily injections; the pump's infusion site is changed every few days.
- **Carbohydrate counting** at meals; balancing food, insulin, and exertion.
- **Vigilance against highs and lows** — hyperglycemia and, more urgently, hypoglycemia (low blood sugar), which can come on fast and must be treated immediately with fast sugar.
- **Sick-day and exercise planning** — illness and hard physical activity both change insulin needs.

**Why it matters for *this* child, in *this* family:**
- **The gymnast-and-dancer who must read her own body.** Sahara already lives in her body — gymnastics, ballet, the Vaganova spotting. Diabetes means she also has to *manage* that body with a precision most children never learn: how a hard training session drops her blood sugar, how to fuel before the barre, how to listen for the early signs of a low. Her athleticism and her condition are not in opposition; they make her, young, an expert in the very thing the rest of the family studies. (Her mother Jessica is pre-med; her co-mother Emma too. Sahara grows up in a house where the body is already the central text.) The **pump** is part of how she does both: many pumps can be set to a temporary lower rate or briefly disconnected for a gymnastics or ballet session and reconnected after, so she can train hard and still keep her levels steady. She learns early where to wear the site so it stays put through tumbling and turns.
- **"Your body is yours" — extended.** The family's founding principle takes on another layer with Sahara. Her body is hers — including the daily, unglamorous work of keeping it running. Her mothers carry the management while she is small and hand it to her, piece by piece, as she grows, so that autonomy includes responsibility. During the ~2054 vacation, this extends to swimming (undressed per family comfort with bodies) while managing her pump/CGM in water activities.
- **Two mothers, divided labour.** Emma — the one who stays, who counts heads on instinct — is built for the steady vigilance diabetes demands: the 3 a.m. glucose check, the snacks in every bag, the quiet competence. Jessica brings the medical understanding and the fierce advocacy. Between them, the condition is held without ever becoming the whole story.

## VAGANOVA BALLET ACADEMY

Sahara is the twin who **says yes.** Spotted at ten on the family cruise, she chooses to pursue ballet at the highest level and enters the **Vaganova Ballet Academy** in St. Petersburg — the nearly-300-year-old school that trains for the Mariinsky, the most rigorous classical training in the world.

**The resonance with her diabetes.** There is something fitting, even pointed, in *Sahara* being the one who goes — the twin who has lived since age four inside a body that must be **read, measured, and governed** every single day. Type 1 diabetes made her, young, an expert in exactly the kind of bodily discipline elite ballet demands: precision, vigilance, knowing her own limits and pushing them anyway. Where another dancer learns to listen to their body in training, Sahara has been doing it her whole life. Her insulin pump and CGM go with her into the studio (see Medical section); managing blood sugar through the punishing hours of Vaganova training is a real, ongoing factor — but it is also the thing she is, uniquely, built to handle.

**The cost.** Vaganova means **St. Petersburg** — far from Halifax, far from her mothers, far from Samira for the first time in their lives. For a family built by people who were left, sending a beloved child across the world *by her own choice* is its own profound test of the saga's principle: *your life is yours.* They do not hold her back. They let her go — and stay reachable, the way Emma always stays. [How the distance is managed; how Sahara's diabetes care works abroad; whether a parent relocates with her for a time — TBD.]

**The twin separation.** Sahara going to Vaganova (St. Petersburg) while Samira trains at the Bolshoi (Moscow) is the **first true divergence** of two girls who shared a face, a language, and every choice until now. It is not a rupture — it is two people who love each other completely choosing different lives. (See Samira's profile for her Bolshoi path. This mirrors, a generation down, Jessica and Rachel: twins who began as one and had to become separate.)

## ARC AND CORE THEME

Sahara Hannan was not chosen in the ordinary way, but she was carried, was born, was loved. Her origin is heavy. Her life is not. She is growing up in a house where love is not conditional, where family is not defined by biology alone, and where the people who raised her did everything they could to make sure she knew one thing: she belongs.

She has a mother who reaches across and a mother who stays. She has a grandmother who holds the family together and a grandfather who throws out the people who would take her away. She has a twin who walks beside her — and dances beside her. She has aunts who love her in different ways, one of whose art she has made her own.

She is Sahara Hannan. She is not her mother's past. She is not a secret. She is not a mistake. She is a girl in a Catholic school uniform, walking down a hallway in Halifax, learning who she is — and a girl in a studio, moving the way her mother and her aunt taught the family to move, who an old master of ballet looked at once and saw a future.
