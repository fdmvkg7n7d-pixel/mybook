# HOW JESSICA ISABELLA GETS PREGNANT — FIRST TWINS ARC

**Arc:** Jessica Isabella Key becomes pregnant with daughters **Samira Chaka** and **Sahara Hannan**.

**Jessica’s age:** 15 when the Pitangueiras Beach incident happens; 16 when the pregnancy and birth become part of family life.

**Important canon clarification:** Jessica, Emma, Terri Anne, and the doctors know there are **two babies** after the first appointment/ultrasound, but they **do not know yet that Samira Chaka and Sahara Hannan are identical twins**. That knowledge comes later, after further medical confirmation or after birth.

**Tone rule:** The harm itself is **never shown**. This arc must remain survivor-centered, non-graphic, and focused on Jessica’s agency, faith, medical care, Mike’s protection in Brazil, Emma’s quiet staying, Terri Anne’s mothering, and the family’s refusal to let trauma define the children.

---

## CANON ANSWER — WHAT HAPPENS

Jessica Isabella gets pregnant after the **Pitangueiras Beach incident** in Guarujá, Brazil, while she is on a business trip with Mike.

She goes to the beach bathroom. Two men follow her in.

The scene cuts away.

The story never describes the assault. The reader only learns what matters afterward: Jessica survives, Mike comes immediately, she is taken to hospital, and she is offered emergency contraception. Jessica refuses the morning-after pill because of her Catholic faith and because, even in the aftermath of violence, she wants her body and conscience respected.

Mike backs her completely.

When Jessica returns home, she tells Emma what happened. Emma does not have to guess. Jessica chooses to tell her. Emma receives the truth without demanding details and without making Jessica repeat more than she can bear.

Weeks later, Jessica realizes she may be pregnant. **Emma is with her in the bathroom when Jessica takes the pregnancy test.** Emma is physically present for the result. This is one of the earliest private moments where Emma becomes part of the pregnancy, even though the world does not yet know she is Jessica’s partner.

At the first doctor appointment, **Mike does not come**. The room is women only: Jessica Isabella, Terri Anne, and Emma. Emma holds Jessica’s **right hand**. Terri Anne holds Jessica’s **left hand**. The doctor confirms the pregnancy and then confirms there are **two babies**.

No one in that room knows yet that the girls are identical twins.

The pregnancy becomes the beginning of Samira Chaka and Sahara Hannan’s lives — not a definition of who they are.

---

## MEDICAL / TIMELINE BEATS

1. **Brazil trip:** Jessica travels with Mike to São Paulo for his business trip.
2. **Pitangueiras Beach:** Jessica spends time at the beach in Guarujá.
3. **The bathroom incident:** Two men follow her into the bathroom. The scene cuts away.
4. **Hospital in Brazil:** Mike arrives, stays with her, and protects her. The doctors discuss care and emergency contraception.
5. **Jessica’s refusal:** Jessica refuses the morning-after pill on religious grounds. This is her choice. Mike supports her without hesitation.
6. **Return home to Canada:** Jessica comes home changed.
7. **Jessica tells Emma:** Jessica tells Emma what happened. Emma listens, stays, and does not ask Jessica to perform the pain for her.
8. **Missed period / symptoms:** A few weeks later, Jessica realizes pregnancy is possible.
9. **Pregnancy test in the bathroom:** Emma is in the bathroom with Jessica Isabella when Jessica takes the test. They see the result together.
10. **First doctor appointment:** Mike does not attend. Terri Anne and Emma go with Jessica.
11. **Hand-holding canon:** Emma holds Jessica’s right hand; Terri Anne holds Jessica’s left hand.
12. **Medical confirmation:** The doctor confirms that Jessica is pregnant.
13. **Two babies:** The first appointment/ultrasound reveals two babies/two heartbeats.
14. **Not yet identical:** They do not know yet that Samira Chaka and Sahara Hannan are identical twins.
15. **Emma stays:** Emma remains in the margins publicly — not named as Jessica’s partner yet — but privately she is present from the beginning.
16. **Family response:** Terri Anne protects and mothers Jessica; Mike protects and supports from outside the appointment; the Key family treats the babies as wanted, loved, and innocent.

## SCENE STRUCTURE OPTION

### Scene 1 — Brazil, Before

Show Jessica being fifteen: curious, athletic, alive, ordinary. She is not a symbol yet. She is a girl at a beach, wet hair, sand on her feet, thinking about what she will tell Emma when she gets home.

This matters because the story must not begin with harm. It begins with Jessica as a whole person.

### Scene 2 — The Door

Jessica enters the beach bathroom.

She notices the sound of footsteps behind her.

Cut.

No details. No depiction. No lingering.

### Scene 3 — Mike Arrives

Mike is pulled out of a meeting. He comes immediately.

His anger is present, but he does not make Jessica carry it. He keeps his voice low. He asks permission before touching her. He stays close enough that she can reach for him but not so close that she feels trapped.

Key line possibility:

> “I’m here,” Mike said. “I’m not leaving unless you ask me to.”

### Scene 4 — The Hospital Choice

Doctors explain care options, including the morning-after pill.

Jessica is exhausted and frightened, but she is still Jessica: the girl who once negotiated skin graft surgery with ice cream, questions, and terms. She asks what it does. She asks when it has to be taken. She asks if she has to.

When she says no, the room pauses.

Mike does not correct her. He does not argue. He does not decide for her.

He says:

> “You heard her.”

This is the scene’s moral center: the violence took choice from her; the hospital scene gives choice back.

### Scene 5 — Jessica Tells Emma

Jessica returns to Canada. Emma is waiting.

Emma knows something is wrong, but she does not force the truth out of Jessica. Jessica chooses the moment. Maybe it happens in Jessica’s bedroom, a stairwell, or a quiet bathroom with the fan on so no one else can hear.

Jessica tells Emma what happened. Not every detail. Not the part the story refuses to show. But enough for Emma to understand.

Emma does not collapse. She does not make Jessica comfort her. She does not ask why, or how, or anything that would make Jessica feel trapped in the memory.

She stays.

Possible line:

> “You don’t have to tell me anything else,” Emma said. “I believe you. I’m here.”

### Scene 6 — The Pregnancy Test

Weeks later, Jessica knows before anyone says it aloud.

Emma is with her in the bathroom.

This is not Emma finding out afterward. Emma is there when Jessica takes the test. She stands close enough to be reached, but not so close that Jessica feels crowded. Jessica is the one holding the test. Emma is the one watching Jessica’s face before she looks at the result.

The result is positive.

This scene should be quiet, not melodramatic. The shock is deep enough without raised voices. Jessica may sit on the edge of the bathtub or on the closed toilet lid, holding the test like it belongs to someone else.

Emma stays in the room.

Possible line:

> Jessica looked at the line until it blurred.
>
> Emma did not ask if she was sure.
>
> She only sat down beside her and offered her hand.

### Scene 7 — The First Doctor Appointment

Mike does **not** come to the first doctor appointment.

This is important. Mike protected Jessica in Brazil, but this appointment belongs to Jessica, Terri Anne, and Emma. The room is quieter without him. Not safer — Mike is safe — but different. It becomes a circle of female care around Jessica.

Jessica sits on the exam table.

Emma stands or sits on Jessica’s right side and holds her **right hand**.

Terri Anne stands or sits on Jessica’s left side and holds her **left hand**.

The doctor confirms the pregnancy.

Then comes the second heartbeat.

They know there are two babies.

They do **not** know the girls are identical.

Possible line:

> “There are two,” the doctor said gently.
>
> Jessica’s right hand tightened around Emma’s.
>
> Her left hand tightened around Terri Anne’s.
>
> For a moment nobody spoke.
>
> Two babies. Not one.
>
> Two lives arriving out of something no one in that room would ever call their fault.

### Scene 8 — Naming

Later, Jessica chooses names that write Emma into the story before anyone knows Emma belongs there.

- **Samira** — companion in evening conversation; the one who stays up with you in the dark.
- **Sahara Hannan** — desert and tenderness/compassion; the waiting and the mercy.

This is Jessica’s quiet declaration: the twins’ origin may have begun in violence, but their lives will be named by love.

## SAMPLE PROSE DRAFT — NON-GRAPHIC

Jessica remembered the tile afterward.

Not everything. Not the order of things. Not every word. Her mind refused some of it, folded it away with a mercy she did not understand yet. But she remembered the tile in the beach bathroom at Pitangueiras, pale and wet under her bare feet, and the sound of the door closing behind her.

Then she remembered Mike.

He came out of a meeting still wearing his suit jacket, his tie pulled loose, his face so white with rage that for one second she was afraid of the anger before she understood it was not aimed at her. Never at her.

He stopped at the hospital room door.

“Jessica,” he said, and his voice broke on her name.

She looked at him.

He did not rush in. He did not grab her. He stood where she could see him and held both hands open.

“I’m here,” he said. “I’m not leaving unless you ask me to.”

She did not ask him to leave.

Later, a doctor spoke carefully about medicine and timing and possibilities. The words came through cotton. Jessica heard enough. She understood enough. The morning-after pill. A choice. A window of time.

She was fifteen years old, and she had already learned that her body was hers in hospital rooms. She had learned it with burns on her back and doctors asking to take skin from her chest. She had learned it with Mike and Terri Anne beside her, telling her no one got to touch her without permission, not even people trying to help.

So she asked questions.

The doctor answered them.

Mike stood beside the bed and let her ask every one.

When the room went quiet, Jessica looked down at her hands. They were shaking. She folded them together until they stopped.

“No,” she said.

The doctor glanced at Mike, perhaps because adults often expected fathers to become the final voice in a room like that.

Mike did not become the final voice.

He became the wall beside her.

“You heard her,” he said.

And that was the end of it.

When Jessica came home to Canada, Emma was waiting.

Emma saw it before Jessica said it. She saw the way Jessica moved carefully through doorways. She saw the way her eyes went to the floor when footsteps came too fast behind her. She saw that Jessica had returned from Brazil with something heavy inside her, even before either of them knew how literal that would become.

But Emma did not ask in front of anyone.

She waited until they were alone.

Jessica chose the bathroom because the fan made a soft noise and the lock worked and because sometimes a small room felt safer than a large one. She sat on the edge of the bathtub. Emma sat on the closed toilet lid, knees together, hands folded so tightly she looked almost formal.

Jessica told her.

Not all of it. Not the part no one should ever ask her to make into words. But enough.

Emma’s face changed. Only once. Only for a second. Then she put the change away, because this was not the moment for Jessica to take care of her.

“I believe you,” Emma said.

Jessica closed her eyes.

Emma moved slowly, giving Jessica every chance to say no. When Jessica did not say no, Emma sat beside her on the edge of the tub and took her hand.

“You don’t have to tell me anything else,” Emma said. “I’m here.”

Weeks later, Jessica was in the bathroom again.

This time Emma was with her from the beginning.

The pregnancy test lay on the counter between the sink and the soap dish. Jessica had set it down and then stepped back as if distance could change what was happening. Emma stood beside her, close enough that their shoulders almost touched.

Neither of them spoke while they waited.

When the line appeared, Jessica stared at it until it blurred.

Emma looked at Jessica first. Not at the test. At Jessica.

Jessica sat down on the edge of the bathtub.

Emma sat beside her.

“It’s positive,” Jessica said, though Emma could see that it was.

“I know,” Emma said.

Jessica’s hands were cold. Emma took the right one between both of hers.

For a while that was all they did. Sit in the locked bathroom with the fan running and the small white test on the counter, while the future changed shape in front of them.

At the first doctor appointment, Mike did not come.

He wanted to. Jessica knew he wanted to. But Terri Anne went, and Emma went, and Mike stayed back because this was what Jessica asked for. He had been the wall in Brazil. He did not need to be the wall in every room after.

Jessica sat on the exam table with paper crinkling beneath her legs.

Emma stood on her right and held her right hand.

Terri Anne stood on her left and held her left hand.

The doctor spoke gently. Pregnancy confirmed. Dates discussed. Questions asked and answered.

Then the doctor looked again.

A pause.

Jessica felt Emma’s fingers tighten.

“What?” Terri Anne asked, because mothers asked the questions when daughters could not.

The doctor turned the screen slightly.

“There are two,” she said. “Two babies.”

Jessica did not understand it at first. The words reached her before their meaning did.

Two.

Her right hand closed hard around Emma’s.

Her left hand closed hard around Terri Anne’s.

Emma made one small sound and then swallowed it. Terri Anne began to cry silently, her thumb moving over Jessica’s knuckles again and again.

No one said identical. No one knew that yet.

They only knew there were two heartbeats.

Two lives.

Two daughters, though they did not know that either.

Later, when Jessica began to think of them not as proof of what had happened but as people coming toward her, she chose names the world would not understand.

Samira.

Sahara Hannan.

A companion in the evening. A desert full of hidden life. The Tender. The Compassionate.

Names from Emma’s language. Names from Catherine’s memory. Names that made a place for love in the official record before anyone knew to look for it there.

The beginning was heavy.

Jessica would never pretend otherwise.

But her daughters were not the heaviness.

They were Samira and Sahara.

And they were loved before they were born.

## WHAT TO AVOID

- Do not depict the assault.
- Do not eroticize, dramatize, or linger on the perpetrators.
- Do not make Mike’s anger louder than Jessica’s needs.
- Do not put Mike in the first doctor appointment; Terri Anne and Emma accompany Jessica.
- Do not reveal at the first appointment that Samira Chaka and Sahara Hannan are identical twins; the family only learns there are two babies.
- Do not have Emma find out after the pregnancy test; Emma is in the bathroom with Jessica when Jessica takes it.
- Do not make Jessica hide the Brazil truth from Emma indefinitely; Jessica tells Emma what happened.
- Do not frame the babies as a punishment, mistake, or burden.
- Do not make Jessica’s faith decision a debate scene where others pressure her.
- Do not make Emma demand excessive details. Emma receives Jessica’s truth without requiring Jessica to narrate trauma.

---

## CORE THEME

The violence took choice from Jessica for a moment. The story gives choice back to her everywhere it can: in the hospital, in faith, in telling Emma, in choosing who stands beside her, in the bathroom with the pregnancy test, in the doctor’s office with Emma holding her right hand and Terri Anne holding her left, in naming, and in how her daughters’ lives are understood.

Samira Chaka and Sahara Hannan are born from a terrible origin, but they are not terrible. They are loved. They are named by tenderness. They belong to Jessica, to Emma, and to the family that chooses them without hesitation.
