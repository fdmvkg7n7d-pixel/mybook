# Jessica Isabella’s First Gymnastics Competition in Ottawa — Scene Outline

## Purpose of This File
This is a planning / scene-outline file for **Jessica Isabella Key’s first gymnastics competition**.

Jessica Isabella is **seven years old**. The competition is in **Ottawa**. **Mike Key** takes her. She wins **second place**. On Sunday, Mike takes her to the **Canadian Museum of Nature**. On Monday, before they go back to **Carlisle**, Mike and Jessica Isabella tour **Parliament Hill**.

## Core Premise
At seven, Jessica Isabella enters her first gymnastics competition. It is not a huge national event; it is the first real moment where she learns what it feels like to perform in front of judges, wait for a score, and discover that effort can be rewarded without needing to be perfect.

Mike is the parent who takes her. **Terri Anne stays home at the Carlisle mansion with the rest of their children, including Kiley Rose, who is two years old and too young to go to these competitions.** The weekend becomes an early father-daughter memory: competition, nerves, second place, and then a quiet Sunday museum visit that teaches Jessica Isabella that being loved is not dependent on winning first.

## Suggested Timeline Placement
- Jessica Isabella is **seven years old**.
- This occurs before she meets Emma at Burlington Gymnastics Club at age ten.
- This occurs before the kitchen accident involving Kiley Rose.
- Jessica Isabella is already involved in gymnastics, but this is her **first competition**.

## Main Characters
- **Jessica Isabella Key** — seven years old; serious, brave, eager to do well, proud but sensitive.
- **Mike Key** — her father; patient, protective, proud, determined not to make the weekend only about scores.
- **Terri Anne Williams-Key** — stays home at the Carlisle mansion with the rest of the children, including two-year-old Kiley Rose, because Kiley is too young to go to the competitions.

## Location
- **Ottawa, Ontario** — gymnastics competition weekend.
- **Canadian Museum of Nature** — Sunday father-daughter outing after the competition.
- **Parliament Hill** — Monday tour before Mike and Jessica Isabella go back to Carlisle.

## Core Emotional Question
Can Jessica Isabella learn that second place can still be something beautiful?

## Beat Sheet

### 1. Traveling to Ottawa
- Mike takes Jessica Isabella to Ottawa for her first gymnastics competition.
- Terri Anne stays home at the Carlisle mansion with the rest of their children, including **Kiley Rose**, who is **two years old** and too young to go to these competitions.
- Jessica is excited but nervous.
- She may bring her leotard carefully folded in her bag.
- Mike checks the schedule, directions, hotel details, and meal plans.
- Jessica asks practical questions that reveal anxiety:
  - What if she forgets the routine?
  - What if everyone else is better?
  - What if she falls?
- Mike does not promise she will win. He promises he will be there.

### 2. The Night Before
- At the hotel, Jessica lays out her competition things.
- Mike helps her make sure nothing is missing.
- She practices small movements in the room until Mike gently tells her her body needs rest too.
- He reminds her that preparation matters, but sleep is part of preparation.
- Jessica falls asleep thinking about the apparatus, the judges, and the other girls.

### 3. Competition Morning
- Jessica wakes early.
- Mike gets her breakfast and makes sure she has enough time to get ready.
- The gym feels bigger and louder than practice.
- Jessica sees other gymnasts stretching, warming up, and talking with their coaches.
- She becomes quiet.
- Mike notices but does not embarrass her by making too much of it.
- He tells her, simply: “You belong here.”

### 4. First Events
- Jessica performs with effort and concentration.
- She is not perfect.
- There may be a small wobble, a short pause, or a landing that is not exactly what she wanted.
- She keeps going.
- This matters more than a flawless score: she learns she can make a mistake and continue.

### 5. Waiting for Scores
- Jessica waits with the other girls.
- She tries not to look too anxious.
- Mike watches from the parent area, proud and tense in the quiet way parents get when they cannot do the hard thing for their child.
- Jessica keeps glancing toward him.
- Each time, Mike is still there.

### 6. Second Place
- Jessica Isabella wins **second place**.
- For a moment, she is thrilled.
- Then she sees first place and has a complicated feeling: pride mixed with disappointment.
- Mike sees both emotions cross her face.
- He does not erase the disappointment.
- He tells her second place in a first competition is extraordinary.
- He lets her feel proud without forcing her to pretend she did not want first.

### 7. The Ribbon / Medal Motif
- Jessica keeps touching her second-place ribbon or medal.
- She may carry it in her coat pocket, bag, or hand.
- It becomes the physical anchor for the rest of the weekend.
- Mike notices every time she checks that it is still there.

### 8. Sunday Morning Decision
- On Sunday, Mike does not rush them straight home.
- He tells Jessica they are going somewhere special before they leave Ottawa.
- Jessica may think it is another gymnastics-related place, or a treat shop.
- Instead, Mike takes her to the **Canadian Museum of Nature**.

### 9. Arrival at the Canadian Museum of Nature
- The building impresses Jessica immediately.
- It feels castle-like and important.
- After the noise and pressure of the gym, the museum feels spacious and steady.
- Mike lets her slow down.
- The day is not about being judged.
- Nothing here gives her a score.

### 10. Dinosaurs and Wonder
- Jessica is drawn to the dinosaur exhibits.
- The size of the skeletons makes her feel small in a good way.
- Mike watches her become a child again after a weekend of trying very hard to be composed.
- Jessica touches the ribbon or medal in her pocket while looking up at something ancient and enormous.
- The contrast matters: yesterday’s competition felt like the whole world; today the world is much bigger.

### 11. Mike’s Lesson Without a Lecture
- Mike does not turn the outing into a speech.
- He lets the museum do most of the teaching.
- Possible quiet line from Mike:
  - “Second place still comes home with you.”
  - “You did something brave yesterday.”
  - “I was proud before they called your name.”
- Jessica hears him because he says it gently.

### 12. Jessica Processes Second Place
- Jessica admits she wanted first.
- Mike says wanting first is not wrong.
- He tells her that wanting to improve is good, but it cannot be the same thing as believing second is failure.
- Jessica thinks about this.
- She may not fully understand it yet, but the memory stays.

### 13. A Small Souvenir
- Mike lets Jessica choose a small museum souvenir if appropriate:
  - dinosaur postcard,
  - small mineral,
  - pencil,
  - bookmark,
  - tiny animal figure.
- The souvenir pairs with the second-place ribbon as a memory of the weekend.
- The ribbon means achievement.
- The museum object means being loved after the achievement.

### 14. Monday at Parliament Hill
- Mike extends the Ottawa weekend long enough for one more important stop.
- On Monday, he takes Jessica Isabella to tour **Parliament Hill**.
- After the child-centered wonder of the Canadian Museum of Nature, Parliament Hill gives the trip a more civic, grown-up feeling.
- Jessica notices the stone buildings, the formal rooms, the flags, the history, and the sense that important decisions happen there.
- Mike explains Parliament in simple terms: people argue, vote, make laws, and are supposed to serve the country.
- Jessica listens seriously, still carrying the memory of her second-place finish.
- The visit plants an early idea that public places belong to ordinary children too — even a seven-year-old gymnast from a large family.
- Mike does not make the day heavy. He lets her ask questions.
- Possible Jessica questions:
  - “Do kids get to vote?”
  - “Do they ever get nervous speaking in front of everyone?”
  - “Do they get second place?”
- Mike can answer gently that grown-ups also have to learn how to lose, keep going, and do better next time.

### 15. Going Back to Carlisle
- On the way back to **Carlisle**, Jessica is tired.
- She may fall asleep in the car with the ribbon or medal nearby.
- Mike drives quietly.
- He understands that the weekend mattered.
- Jessica has learned that competition is not only about winning; it is also about continuing, being seen, and being held steady afterward.

### 16. Back at the Mansion
- When Mike and Jessica Isabella return to the family mansion in **Carlisle**, the trip finally settles into home.
- Jessica may still be tired from the competition, the museum, Parliament Hill, and the drive back.
- She has her second-place ribbon or medal with her.
- Before the weekend fully becomes ordinary life again, Jessica thanks Mike for taking her to her **first gymnastics competition**.
- The thank-you is simple but important.
- Mike understands that she is not only thanking him for the drive or the hotel or the competition fee. She is thanking him for being there.
- Possible line from Jessica:
  - “Thank you for taking me, Daddy.”
- Possible line from Mike:
  - “I’d take you again tomorrow.”
- This closes the weekend with gratitude rather than victory, reinforcing that the true memory is father-daughter presence.

## Key Emotional Beats
- Jessica is nervous before performing.
- Mike does not promise victory; he promises presence.
- Jessica wins second place and feels proud and disappointed at the same time.
- Mike validates both feelings.
- The Canadian Museum of Nature becomes the reward that is not dependent on first place.
- The Monday Parliament Hill tour adds a civic, grown-up memory to the weekend.
- Back at the Carlisle mansion, Jessica thanks Mike for taking her to her first gymnastics competition.
- Jessica learns early that Mike’s pride is not conditional.

## Recurring Motifs
- **The ribbon / medal:** proof of effort, pride, and complicated feelings.
- **The museum building:** steadiness after pressure.
- **Dinosaurs / ancient scale:** the world is bigger than one competition.
- **Mike waiting in the parent area:** presence without control.
- **Parliament Hill:** public life, grown-up rooms, and the idea that important places can still include children.
- **The car ride back to Carlisle:** exhaustion, safety, memory settling into the body.
- **The mansion:** the return to family life, where Jessica thanks Mike and the weekend becomes a lasting memory.
