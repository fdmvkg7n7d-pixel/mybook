## SHARED ANATOMY — JESSICA ISABELLA AND RACHEL DARCY

Jessica Isabella and Rachel Darcy are born sharing two structures:

**One liver:**
The liver is the most commonly shared organ in conjoined twins who are successfully separated. Surgeons can divide the liver along its natural midline — each twin receives a functioning half. The liver's remarkable regenerative capacity means both halves grow to full function over time. Liver separation, while complex, is one of the more surgically achievable splits; it does not require donation or transplant, only precise division.

At separation, the liver is divided. Each twin — Jessica Isabella and Rachel Darcy — receives one half. Both halves regenerate. Both women live with full liver function.

**One reproductive system:**
Where one reproductive system exists between two twins, the surgical decision at separation is that it must go entirely to one twin. It cannot be divided. The Key family, the medical team, and — as they grow old enough — the twins themselves navigate this decision.

**Jessica Isabella receives the reproductive system.**

This means:
- Jessica Isabella has the capacity to conceive and carry children.
- Rachel Darcy does not.

## THE KITCHEN ACCIDENT — KILEY ROSE IS SEVEN

**What happens:**
Jessica Isabella and Kiley Rose are playing near the stove. They accidentally knock a pot of boiling water. In the moment it tips, Jessica Isabella pushes Kiley Rose out of the way. The boiling water spills entirely onto **Jessica Isabella's bare back**.

She suffers **second and third degree burns.**

- **Second degree burns** damage the outer layer of skin and the layer beneath it. They are extremely painful, cause blistering, and require careful wound care. Healing can take weeks and may leave scarring.
- **Third degree burns** destroy all layers of skin and can damage deeper tissue. They require more intensive treatment — skin grafting — and leave significant permanent scarring. The nerve damage in third degree burns can mean that the burned area itself feels less pain than the surrounding second degree areas, though the overall experience is severe.

**Shriners Children's Hospital — six months:**
Jessica Isabella is treated at **Shriners Children's Hospital** for **six months**. Shriners is one of North America's leading paediatric burn treatment centres, providing care at no cost to the family. Treatment for second and third degree burns of this severity involves:
- Emergency stabilisation and wound assessment
- Wound cleaning and dressing
- **Skin grafting** for the third degree areas — skin is taken from Jessica Isabella's chest to repair her back
- During surgery, Jessica Isabella must lie **face down on her stomach**. Her head is held completely still in a **head brace** to keep her steady throughout the procedure.
- Pain management
- Infection prevention
- Lengthy recovery and rehabilitation — **six months in hospital**
- **Scarring** — the burns on Jessica Isabella's back leave permanent marks. She carries them for the rest of her life.

**What this moment means:**
Jessica Isabella does not think. She pushes Kiley Rose. The instinct is immediate and complete — the same instinct that will later make a teacher look at her and think: *she is the one to send.* The same instinct that will one day make her offer Rachel Darcy her eggs. She puts herself between harm and the people she loves. She has always done this. The scars on her back are the most visible proof.

## THE NEGOTIATION — A FUNNY MOMENT

Before the skin graft surgery can proceed, the doctors explain to Jessica Isabella that they need to take skin from her **chest** to repair her **back**. They also explain that she will need to lie face down for the surgery, and that her head will be placed in a **brace** to keep it completely still.

Mike and Terri Anne have already told her — clearly, seriously, and with full intention — that **no one has the right to touch her body unless she gives her permission.** They mean it. They want her to know it even here, even in a hospital, even when the people asking are doctors trying to help her.

Jessica Isabella takes all of this information in. She considers it. And then she negotiates.

With Mike and Terri Anne in the room, she sets her terms with the doctors — in full, with great seriousness, and with the confidence of someone who has been told her body is hers and has decided to act accordingly:

- **Ice cream.** Specifically agreed upon before she signs off on anything.
- **Which doctor.** Not just any doctor. She has opinions. She makes them known.
- **Exactly how and when.** She wants the rules understood before she gives her permission. She asks questions. She waits for the answers. She follows up.

The doctors — who have performed many skin grafts and fitted many head braces — have likely never had quite this experience. Mike and Terri Anne are somewhere between deeply proud and desperately trying not to laugh.

In the end, she gives her permission. On her terms. The surgery proceeds. She lies on her stomach, her head steady in the brace, having negotiated every condition of the encounter.

The funny moment is this: Mike and Terri Anne told her her body was hers and no one could touch it without permission. They were right. They were also perhaps not fully prepared for the fact that she would take that lesson and run it like a business meeting.

## EMMA'S FATHER — THE RECKONING

Emma's father comes over to pick her up from a sleepover. He is a man who hates lesbians. He has made this known. He has always made this known. Emma has known, for a long time, that this conversation would end the way it ends.

Just like that. No negotiation. No ice cream. No rules. Just a door closing on a child who told him the truth about who she loves.

Mike does not negotiate. Mike does not waver. Mike does not ask Emma's father to reconsider.

Mike throws Emma's father out of his house. And he fires him.

He backs the love that was never wrong and never needed to be hidden. He closes the door on the man who would throw his child away for loving someone, and he fires him, and he does it without a single doubt.

And then he turns to Emma and he welcomes her into his home.

Not as a guest. Not as a friend of Jessica's. As family. As the girl who has been at the door for six years, who waited through six months of hospital, who stayed through the pregnancy, who stayed through the birth, who stayed through the nights that were too long and the mornings that came too fast. Mike sees her. Terri Anne sees her. Rachel Darcy sees her. The sisters see her. She is no longer in the margins. She is in the room.

## ARC AND CORE THEME

Jessica Isabella Key begins her life joined — literally, physically, completely — to her sister. She is also the twin who, at separation, receives what cannot be shared: the reproductive system. She did not ask for it. It simply came to her by anatomy and surgical necessity.

When a pot of boiling water is about to fall on her little sister, she does not hesitate. She pushes Kiley Rose away and takes it herself. The burns on her back are second and third degree. The scars are permanent. She spends six months at Shriners — face down, head in a brace, having first negotiated the terms of her own surgery with a composure that makes the doctors blink and makes Mike and Terri Anne bite the insides of their cheeks to keep from laughing.

When she comes home after six months, Emma is waiting at the door. The family sees a friend. Jessica sees the beginning of everything.

At fifteen, in a beach bathroom in Guarujá, something happens that she did not choose and did not deserve. She is taken to hospital. Her father is furious — and he stays. He helps her walk to the bathroom because she trusts him without any doubt. When the hospital offers her the morning after pill and she says no — because she is Catholic and that is her faith and her decision — Mike stands beside her and tells the hospital it will be respected. He backed her at the skin graft negotiation. He backs her now. He has always backed her. Emma is there too. In the stairwell. In the silence. No one knows.

At sixteen, the girl who met her on the gymnasium floor six years earlier becomes the woman she loves. And the pregnancy comes, and Emma stays, and Jessica carries. Neither of them asked for this. Neither of them asked for the kitchen accident, or the separation, or the beach, or the hospital, or the life that keeps putting things in their hands that they did not reach for. But they are here. They are sixteen. And they are together. In secret. In the margins. In the spaces between what the world sees and what the girls know.

Four months after the twins are born, Rachel Darcy walks into a bathroom. She sees them kissing in a bathtub. She turns around. She tells her mother. And the secret is over — not because it was exposed, but because it was no longer needed.

Mike and Terri Anne support Jessica Isabella and Emma without any single doubt. The same man who stood in a hospital in Brazil and backed his daughter's right to refuse the morning after pill on religious grounds stands in his own living room and backs his daughter's right to her own heart. When Emma's father throws her out of his life and tells Mike to take the babies away "before she turns them into lesbians," Mike throws Emma's father out of his house and fires him. Without hesitation. Without a single doubt. He opens the door to Emma instead.

She was born joined to another person. She has never not known what it is to be bound to someone. The separation ends the physical join. The instinct does not end. She still reaches across. And the people who love her reach back. Emma reaches back — quietly, invisibly, completely — until the morning in the bathroom when she no longer has to be invisible. Rachel Darcy reaches back. Mike reaches back. Terri Anne reaches back. Everyone Jessica Isabella loves, she reaches toward. And they reach back.

Now, at Dalhousie University, Jessica Isabella studies the body she was born with, the body she carries, the body she loves. Medicine is not just a career; it is an answer to the question of how to live inside a skin that has been burned, separated, and chosen. Emma is there. Samira Chaka and Sahara Hannan are there. The family holds. The map stretches. But the instinct remains: *she reaches across, and they reach back.*

Somewhere in the Shriners recovery ward, there is a doctor who negotiated ice cream flavours with a child before being granted permission to perform a skin graft — and who remembers it for the rest of their career. Somewhere in a doorway in Burlington, there is a girl who waited six months and was still there when the door opened — and who never left, who was still there at sixteen, who was still there in a bathtub four months after the twins were born, who is still there now, in the room, in the light, in the family that finally sees her.
