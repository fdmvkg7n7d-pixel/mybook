# Jessica Isabella, Emma, Samira Chaka, and Sahara Hannan Visit Vancouver — Christmas and Catherine's Grave — Scene Outline

> **Scope:** Jessica Isabella, Emma, and their daughters **Samira Chaka** and **Sahara Hannan** visit **Vancouver** for the first time since Emma's mother, **Catherine Fritz**, died. The trip holds two emotional poles: the joyful, ordinary chaos of Christmas morning with the girls jumping on their mothers' bed, and the deeply emotional Boxing Day visit to Catherine's grave — the first time Emma brings her wife and daughters to the place where her mother is buried.
>
> **Time:** Christmas season, year TBD. Samira Chaka and Sahara Hannan are old enough to understand that Catherine is Emma's mother and their grandmother, but still young enough for Christmas-morning bed-jumping.
> **Place:** Vancouver, British Columbia. Hotel/guest room on Christmas morning; cemetery/graveyard on Boxing Day. The Whistler Christmas-present aftermath is handled in a separate sequel file.
> **Characters present:** Jessica Isabella Key, Emma Fritz, Samira Chaka Key-Fritz, Sahara Hannan Key-Fritz.
> **Mood:** Christmas joy → old grief rising → family witness → quiet healing.

---

## 1. Why Vancouver Matters

- **The return:** This is Emma's first time back in Vancouver since Catherine died. Vancouver is not simply a city; it is the last place where Emma had a mother.
- **Catherine's presence:** Catherine Fritz is buried there. Emma has carried her mother for years as memory, faith, silence, and the names of the twins — but she has not stood at the grave with the family she built.
- **Jessica's role:** Jessica understands that this trip is not tourism. It is pilgrimage. She does not push Emma to explain everything. She simply stays close.
- **The girls' role:** Samira and Sahara carry Catherine in their names and family story, but this is the first time they physically enter Catherine's geography.

---

## 2. Christmas Eve — The Weight Under the Holiday

- **The room:** They are staying in a Vancouver hotel or guest suite. The girls are excited — travel, Christmas, stockings, a different city, maybe snow or rain against the windows.
- **Emma's quietness:** Emma participates. She helps set out the girls' gifts. She folds pyjamas. She checks that the stockings are ready. But Jessica sees the old quietness in her — not withdrawal exactly, but the way Emma becomes useful when she is hurting.
- **Jessica sees her:** After the girls fall asleep, Jessica comes up behind Emma and wraps her arms around her.

  *"You don't have to make Christmas perfect to earn tomorrow,"* Jessica says.

- **Emma:**

  *"I know."*

  But she says it like someone who is trying to know it.

- **The unspoken truth:** Tomorrow is Christmas, but the next day is the grave. Emma is already there in her mind.

---

## 3. Christmas Morning — The Girls Jump on the Bed

- **The wake-up:** Early Christmas morning, Samira Chaka and Sahara Hannan wake before their mothers. They whisper at first, trying and failing to be quiet.
- **The burst:** The bedroom door opens. The girls run in and jump onto Jessica Isabella and Emma's bed.
- **The line:** Together, or nearly together, they shout:

  *"Merry Christmas!"*

- **The physical comedy:** The girls climb over the blankets, knees and elbows everywhere. Jessica groans dramatically and pretends to be crushed. Emma laughs despite herself — a real laugh, sudden and unguarded, the kind Jessica treasures because Emma did not manufacture it for anyone else's comfort.
- **Samira:** Bold and immediate, she throws herself across Jessica first and then reaches for Emma.
- **Sahara:** More careful but no less intense, she tucks herself against Emma's side and says Merry Christmas again, quieter, right into Emma's shoulder.
- **Emma's emotional flicker:** For one second Emma almost cries. Not because she is sad, exactly — because the bed is full. Because Christmas morning did not stay in the small apartment of memory with Catherine gone and Randy absent. It is here, loud and warm, with Jessica and two daughters who know how to reach for her.
- **Jessica notices:** Jessica does not point it out. She simply puts one hand over Emma's under the blankets.
- **The girls' gifts:** Stockings, small presents, hotel-room Christmas mess. Samira and Sahara are delighted by simple things. Their joy gives Emma a place to stand before the grief of Boxing Day.

---

## 4. Christmas Meals at the Hotel Restaurant

- **The plan for the day:** Because this Vancouver trip is emotionally heavy, Jessica and Emma decide not to overcomplicate Christmas Day. They will eat **breakfast, lunch, and dinner at the hotel restaurant**. No rushing around the city. No complicated reservations. No pretending this is a normal vacation. The hotel becomes their soft landing place.

### Christmas Breakfast

- **Getting dressed:** After the bed-jumping and presents, Jessica and Emma get the girls dressed for breakfast. The morning is still full of hotel-room chaos — misplaced socks, hair brushing, Samira asking if she can bring one of her gifts downstairs, Sahara checking the window to see what Vancouver looks like on Christmas morning.
- **The restaurant:** They go down to the hotel restaurant for Christmas breakfast. The room is warm and decorated — garland, small lights, perhaps a Christmas tree near the entrance. Other families are there too, but the atmosphere is quieter than a normal morning, softened by the holiday.
- **The table:** Jessica and Emma sit side by side or across from the girls, depending on the visual desired. Samira and Sahara study the menu like it is a major event. Pancakes, fruit, hot chocolate, toast — the ordinary abundance of a hotel breakfast becomes part of the Christmas memory.
- **Emma's emotional double vision:** Emma watches her daughters in the restaurant and feels the ache of contrast. As a child after Catherine died, Christmas became something smaller, quieter, managed rather than celebrated. Now there are two girls in front of her arguing gently over syrup and whipped cream, and Jessica beside her, and no one is asking Emma to make herself small.
- **Jessica notices:** Jessica sees Emma watching the girls and quietly reaches for her hand under the table.

  *"You okay?"*

- **Emma's answer:** Emma looks at Samira and Sahara, then at Jessica.

  *"I think so. I just keep thinking she would have liked this."*

- **Who "she" is:** Jessica does not need to ask. Catherine is already at the table in the space between them.
- **The girls include Catherine:** If the moment feels right, Sahara asks whether Grandma Catherine liked pancakes, or Samira announces that they should have breakfast for Grandma Catherine too. This lets Catherine enter Christmas Day gently before the graveyard visit on Boxing Day.

### Christmas Lunch

- **Returning downstairs:** After a slow morning in the room — gifts, phone calls, maybe a short walk through the lobby — they return to the same hotel restaurant for lunch. The repetition matters: same elevator, same hallway, same decorated room, the same staff beginning to recognize them.
- **The girls' familiarity:** Samira and Sahara are more comfortable now. They remember which table they liked. They wave at a server. They argue gently over what to order. The hotel restaurant begins to feel, for one day, like a temporary family kitchen.
- **Emma softening:** Lunch is easier than breakfast. Emma has already survived the first wave of memory. Jessica notices her laughing more naturally when Samira says something bold or Sahara asks a precise question about Vancouver.
- **Catherine in ordinary conversation:** Catherine is mentioned lightly, not only with grief. Emma may tell the girls one small thing about her mother — something simple, like what she liked to eat, how she took tea, or how she used to make sure Emma had breakfast before school. This lets Catherine become a person at the table, not only a grave they will visit tomorrow.

### Christmas Dinner

- **The evening meal:** They return to the hotel restaurant again for Christmas dinner. By now the day has a rhythm: the restaurant has held them three times. Breakfast was joy with grief underneath; lunch was gentler; dinner becomes the quiet closing of the day before Boxing Day.
- **The holiday atmosphere:** The restaurant is warmer and dimmer in the evening — candles or small table lights, Christmas music low, families lingering over dessert. Outside, Vancouver is dark and wintery.
- **Emma's anticipation of Boxing Day:** As dinner goes on, Emma becomes quieter again. Tomorrow is getting closer. Jessica sees the shift and does not force cheer.
- **Jessica's promise:** Under the table, Jessica takes Emma's hand again.

  *"Tomorrow, we go together."*

- **Emma's answer:**

  *"I know."*

  A pause.

  *"I'm glad it's not just me this time."*

- **The girls' closing beat:** Samira and Sahara are tired from Christmas and travel. One or both lean against their mothers at the table or in the elevator afterward. Their sleepy weight becomes the final gift of the day: Emma is not alone in Vancouver anymore.
- **The emotional function:** Eating all three Christmas meals in the hotel restaurant gives the day a safe, repetitive structure. Vancouver begins to hold warmth before it asks Emma to face grief at Catherine's grave on Boxing Day.

## 5. Christmas Day — Catherine in the Background

- **The city:** They spend Christmas Day gently. No overpacked schedule. Maybe a walk, a simple meal, time in the hotel room, and a call to the wider Key family.
- **The Terri Anne call:** Jessica Isabella and Emma call **Terri Anne** on Christmas Day, with **Samira Chaka** and **Sahara Hannan** each wanting a turn to wish their grandmother a **Merry Christmas**.
- **The call's emotional function:** It lets Vancouver fold into the wider family holiday while keeping the day gentle. Terri Anne hears her daughter, daughter-in-law, and granddaughters from one branch of the family before or around hearing from Kiley Rose and her girls in Natal.
- **Possible texture on the call:**
  - Samira says Merry Christmas loudly and immediately.
  - Sahara waits for her turn and says it more softly, but with intense seriousness.
  - Terri Anne asks after all four of them.
  - **Rachel Darcy and Antoine have called Terri Anne too**, so Terri Anne may pass along their love or mention that another branch of the family has already checked in.
- **The girls ask:** At some point, one of the twins asks about visiting Grandma Catherine tomorrow.
- **Emma's reaction:** The word *Grandma* catches in Emma. Catherine never got to be a grandmother in life. Hearing Samira and Sahara use the word makes the loss and the love arrive together.
- **Emma answers:**

  *"Yes. Tomorrow. Boxing Day."*

- **Jessica's hand:** Jessica reaches for Emma's hand under the table or beside the girls' coats. Emma lets her.

### Sample phone-call snippet

> **Terri Anne:** "Merry Christmas, my loves. Let me hear my girls."
>
> **Samira:** "Merry Christmas, Grandma! We're in Vancouver!"
>
> **Terri Anne:** "I can hear that in your voice. Are you having a beautiful day?"
>
> **Samira:** "Yes, and we jumped on the bed and Mama pretended we crushed her."
>
> **Jessica, laughing in the background:** "I did not pretend very much."
>
> **Sahara:** "Grandma? Merry Christmas."
>
> **Terri Anne:** "Merry Christmas, sweetheart. I was waiting for your turn."
>
> **Sahara:** "We want to see Grandma Catherine tomorrow."
>
> **Terri Anne:** "I know, baby. She would be very proud of all of you."
>
> **Emma:** "Merry Christmas, Terri Anne."
>
> **Terri Anne:** "Merry Christmas, darling. How are you holding up?"
>
> **Emma:** "I'm okay. Better because they're excited."
>
> **Terri Anne:** "Rachel and Antoine rang too. They wanted me to tell all of you Merry Christmas."
>
> **Samira:** "Tell Auntie Rachel and Antoine we said it back!"
>
> **Terri Anne:** "I will. We'll talk again tomorrow if you want. Just be together today."

## 6. Boxing Day Morning — Going to the Graveyard

- **The preparation:** The next morning is quieter. The girls understand, without fully understanding, that today is different.
- **What they bring:**
  - Flowers for Catherine.
  - A small written note or drawing from Samira and Sahara.
  - Optional: a small stone, a ribbon, or something connected to Catherine's Muslim faith/family tradition if canon supports it.
- **Emma's body language:** Emma becomes very still as they approach the cemetery. She is not crying yet. Jessica knows this stillness. It is the stillness Emma learned as a child: if you do not move, maybe the grief will not spill.
- **Jessica's support:** Jessica does not fill the silence. She walks beside Emma and lets the girls walk slightly ahead or between them, depending on the emotional tone desired.

---

## 7. At Catherine's Grave

- **The first sight:** Emma sees the grave marker. The years collapse. She is no longer only a wife and mother; she is eight years old again, motherless in the place where the world changed.
- **Emma stops:** She cannot move for a moment.
- **Jessica waits:** Jessica does not tug her forward. She stands beside her.
- **Samira and Sahara:** The girls become quiet. They understand this is sacred, even if they do not understand every layer.
- **Emma approaches:** She kneels or stands at the grave. She touches the stone with her fingers.
- **The first words:** Emma speaks to Catherine softly:

  *"Hi, Mum."*

- **The emotional break:** That small greeting undoes her. Not dramatically at first. Her face changes, then her breath catches, then the tears come.
- **Jessica moves:** Jessica comes close, one arm around Emma, but leaves Emma enough room to face the grave.

---

## 8. Introducing Jessica and the Girls to Catherine

- **Emma's introduction:** This is the heart of the scene. Emma finally introduces the people Catherine never got to meet.

  *"This is Jessica,"* Emma says, voice shaking. *"This is my wife."*

  A pause.

  *"You would have loved her. I think you would have. I hope you would have."*

- **Jessica's response:** Jessica's eyes fill, but she keeps steady for Emma. She may say softly:

  *"Thank you for Emma."*

  This is not a performance. It is a direct address to the woman who raised the person Jessica loves.

- **The girls:** Emma brings Samira and Sahara closer.

  *"And these are our daughters. Samira Chaka and Sahara Hannan."*

- **The names:** Emma explains, if she can:

  *"Their names come from your world. From the language you gave me. Jessica chose them so they would carry you, too."*

- **Samira:** Bold but tender, she places her drawing/flower at the grave.

  *"Merry Christmas, Grandma Catherine."*

- **Sahara:** Quieter, perhaps asks if Grandma Catherine can hear them. Emma struggles to answer; Jessica helps.

  Jessica: *"I think love hears love."*

---

## 9. Emma's Grief Fully Surfaces

- **What Emma says:** Emma finally speaks the grief she has carried silently:

  *"I wanted you to meet them."*

  A beat.

  *"I wanted you to know I was okay. I wanted you to know I found someone who stayed."*

- **Jessica's line:** Jessica answers not for Catherine, but beside Emma:

  *"You did. And you are not standing here alone."*

- **Emma allows herself to need:** This is a major emotional milestone. Emma does not only stand and endure. She leans into Jessica. She lets Samira and Sahara wrap their arms around her. She lets herself be visibly grieved.
- **The girls' embrace:** Samira hugs hard. Sahara presses in gently. They do not fix it. They simply become the family around Emma's grief.

---

## 10. Faith Beat — Quiet and Respectful

- **Emma's Muslim inheritance:** If used, Emma may whisper **Al-Fatiha** or a simple prayer she remembers from Catherine. She may not perform it perfectly. The imperfection matters: this is the faith she carried alone as a child.
- **Jessica's Catholic presence:** Jessica does not overwrite the moment with her own tradition. She stands respectfully, perhaps silently crossing herself only if Emma welcomes it or if that is established between them.
- **The interfaith family:** The girls witness both traditions as part of their household — Catherine's Islam, Jessica's Catholicism, and the shared center of love and remembrance.

---

## 11. Leaving the Graveyard

- **The leaving:** Leaving is hard. Emma has spent years away from this grave; now leaving it again feels like abandoning Catherine a second time.
- **Jessica names the truth gently:**

  *"Leaving the grave isn't leaving her."*

- **Emma:**

  *"I know."*

  This time, she almost does.

- **The girls:** Samira and Sahara each say goodbye. The gesture is small but enormous: Catherine has been acknowledged by the grandchildren she never met.
- **Closing image:** Emma walks out of the graveyard holding Jessica's hand, with Samira and Sahara close. She is crying openly now, but she is walking. Vancouver is no longer only the city where her mother died. It is also the city where Emma brought her family back to meet her.

## 12. Character Work

### Emma Fritz
- **The return:** Emma's first return to Vancouver since Catherine's death forces her to stop carrying her mother only privately.
- **The breakthrough:** She lets Jessica and the girls witness her grief. This is the opposite of disappearing.
- **The mother/daughter mirror:** Emma arrives as Catherine's daughter and as Samira and Sahara's mother. Both truths stand together at the grave.

### Jessica Isabella Key
- **The witness:** Jessica's role is not to manage Emma's grief, but to witness it without fear.
- **The bridge:** By naming the girls with Arabic names, Jessica already wrote Catherine into the family. At the grave, she helps make that act visible.

### Samira Chaka and Sahara Hannan
- **The living legacy:** The twins are Catherine's granddaughters through love, naming, and family belonging.
- **Different responses:** Samira is more physically demonstrative; Sahara is quieter and more contemplative. Both reach Emma in their own way.

### Catherine Fritz
- **Absent but central:** Catherine does not appear, but the whole scene is built around what she gave Emma and what she never got to see.

## 13. Thematic Motifs

- **The bed full of daughters:** Christmas morning rewrites Emma's childhood loneliness with warmth, noise, and children reaching for her.
- **The grave as introduction:** Boxing Day is not only mourning; it is Emma introducing Catherine to the life she built.
- **Names as resurrection:** Samira Chaka and Sahara Hannan carry Emma and Catherine's world into the future.
- **Staying and being seen:** Emma does not stay quietly in the background here. She is seen grieving, needing, and being held.
- **Vancouver transformed:** The city of loss becomes, if not healed, then widened — it now contains Jessica and the girls too.
