# Comfort and Confession in Halifax

**Time:** Winter ~2060
**Location:** The Key-Fritz Mansion, Halifax

The Halifax mansion was large enough to rattle. Inherited through the Key family holdings, the twelve-room stone-and-clapboard estate had been renovated with the expectation of a sprawling, unconventionally enormous family. But in the winter of ~2060, approximately three weeks after Samira’s disappearance into the Siberian training facility, the empty rooms echoed. 

It was a Tuesday evening, 6:15 PM, the one night of the week when both Jessica and Emma were home before seven. Jessica's white coat was abandoned on the hook by the door, her pager blessedly silent. In the kitchen, Emma stood at the stove stirring a pot of chicken soup—the kind her mother, Catherine, used to make, the kind that was supposed to heal everything. 

At the long oak farmhouse table, five-year-old identical twin brothers Eliot David and Kalvin LaMarcus Key-Fritz sat in their booster seats. They wore matching pajamas, at Eliot’s insistence for symmetry, and the table was set with the blue plates they had chosen when they were three. Napkins were folded into precise triangles by Kalvin’s small, careful hands.

But the kitchen, normally the loud, chaotic center of the family, was sick with silence.

Eliot stared at his soup. He had not picked up his spoon. Kalvin had eaten two bites before stopping entirely, his hand resting on his brother’s thigh under the table—a gesture of comfort invisible to the adults.

Jessica stood near the sideboard, her hands shaking slightly as she poured tea. She forced a bright, determined smile onto her face, a survival instinct she had inherited from her mother, Terri Anne. "Eat up, boys," she said, her voice cheerfully strained. "Emma made your favorite. And after dinner, we can call Auntie Rachel. She wants to hear about your kindergarten concert."

Eliot did not move. "I don't want to call Auntie Rachel," he said.

"Why not? You love Auntie Rachel."

"I want to call Samira."

The silence that followed was physical. Emma stopped stirring the soup. Jessica’s smile froze. Under the table, Kalvin squeezed Eliot’s leg harder.

"Samira is very busy with her ballet teaching, sweetheart," Jessica said, speaking a little too quickly. "You know how busy she is. She’s in Russia. The time difference is tricky. We’ll call her soon."

Eliot looked up. His eyes, dark like Jessica’s, held the same fierce intensity she possessed at sixteen. "You said that last week," he stated flatly. "And the week before. And the week before that. She is not busy. She is gone."

Beside him, Kalvin’s face crumpled. Tears began to fall, silent and continuous, rolling down his cheeks and dropping into his untouched soup. He didn't make a sound. He was crying the exact way Emma cried—invisibly, so as not to disturb the room.

Jessica put down her tea. She stepped forward and reached for Eliot’s hand. 

He pulled it away. "Don't touch me," he snapped. "You are lying. I know you are lying. Your mouth goes small."

Emma turned from the stove, her heart lurching. She looked at Jessica and saw it instantly: Jessica’s mouth was small, pursed—a nervous tell Emma had recognized for years but had never named aloud. The boys had named it. Eliot had been studying them both like a surgeon studying scans.

"I am not lying, Eliot," Jessica’s voice cracked, the cheerful facade shattering. "I am trying to keep you safe. There are things that grown-ups know and children don’t need to know."

"That is a lie too!" Eliot stood up in his booster seat, small but radiating fury. "That is the lie grown-ups tell when the truth is too scary." He glared down at his mother, his chest heaving. "Kalvin wets the bed. Every night. He hides it. I know because I smell the pee in the hamper. He thinks you don't know, but you do, because Emma washes the pajamas." 

Kalvin squeezed his eyes shut, his shoulders shaking. 

"And he doesn't eat at school," Eliot pressed on mercilessly. "Mrs. Patterson sent a note. I saw it in your coat pocket. We are not babies. We know things. And we know Samira is not busy. Something bad happened to her."

Kalvin was crying harder now, his small hand still clutching Eliot's leg. He wasn't crying because his secrets were revealed, but because the truth was finally in the room, and it was simply too heavy for him to hold alone anymore.

Emma set down the wooden spoon. She walked to the table and knelt so she was at eye level with the boys. "Kalvin, baby," she said softly. "Come here."

Kalvin looked up at Eliot. Eliot gave a sharp, permissive nod. Unbuckling his booster seat, Kalvin slid down, walked straight to Emma, and climbed into her lap. He buried his wet face in her neck, smelling of strawberry shampoo, child sweat, and profound fear. Emma wrapped her arms tightly around him and looked up at Jessica. Her eyes communicated everything: *We cannot do this anymore. We have to tell them something.*

Jessica stood up. She walked to the kitchen window and stared out at the Halifax snow falling beneath the streetlights—the ordinary world where daughters did not disappear into Russian facilities. "I don't know how," she whispered to the glass.

"I do," Emma replied gently.

Jessica turned around and walked back to the table. She pulled Eliot’s booster seat closer to her own chair so they were side by side. She reached for his hand again. This time, he didn't pull away, though he didn't squeeze back either. 

"Eliot. Kalvin," Jessica began, her voice dropping to a low, serious register. "I need to tell you something. And it is going to be scary. And I am scared too. And Emma is scared. And you are allowed to be scared. You are allowed to cry. You are allowed to be angry. You are allowed to feel whatever you feel. Because your feelings are yours. Do you understand? Your feelings belong to you."

Eliot gave a small, sharp nod. Kalvin turned his head slightly from Emma’s neck, his face red and hopeful.

"Samira is not busy with ballet," Jessica continued, the words tasting like ash. "She is far away, in a very cold place in Russia. She went there because she thought she was doing something important for her work. But the people who took her there are not good people. They are not letting her call home. They are not letting her write letters. They are keeping her in a place where she cannot talk to us. And we do not know when she will come back."

"Is she dead?" Eliot asked. The word landed like a heavy stone on the table.

Jessica flinched physically. "We do not think so. We think she is alive. Your sister Sahara thinks she is alive. We are all working very hard to find her. But we do not know for sure. And that is the truth. That is the scary truth."

Kalvin let out a loud sob—a sound he had been holding in his small chest for weeks. It was half relief and half pure terror. He clutched the fabric of Emma’s shirt with his tiny fists, sobbing openly into her shoulder. 

Emma rocked him back and forth. *"Sabr, ya habibi. Sabr,"* she murmured in Arabic. Then, in English: "Patience, my love. Patience. We are coming for her."

Eliot wasn't crying. He was thinking, his brow furrowed. "Why did she go with the bad people?"

"Because she did not know they were bad people when she went," Emma answered. "She thought she was doing something brave. And she was brave. But sometimes brave people need help. And we are going to help her."

"How?" Eliot demanded.

"We are talking to people in Russia," Jessica said. "We are talking to the government. We are talking to Sahara. We are all working together."

"Can we go?" 

"No, baby. Not yet. It is too dangerous. But when we can, we will."

"I want to go now," Eliot’s voice broke. "I want to hit the bad people." He finally started to cry, his anger dissolving into grief, his small face screwing up like a fist opening. 

Jessica pulled him into her lap. He was heavy for a five-year-old, all dense muscle and bone, but she lifted him easily, holding him the way she had held Samira when she was born, the way she had held Sahara, the way she had held every child who came from her body. "I want to hit them too," Jessica whispered into his hair. "But hitting is not how we get her back. We get her back by being smart, and by being patient, and by not giving up. And we need you to help us not give up."

Kalvin hiccupped, his breath catching as his sobs began to subside. He lifted his head from Emma's shoulder. "Can we draw her a picture?"

Emma looked at Jessica, who nodded. "Yes," Emma said. "We can draw her a picture. We can draw her a hundred pictures. And when we find her, we will give them all to her."

Eliot wiped his wet face on Jessica’s sleeve. "I want to draw a map. So she can find her way home."

Jessica's heart broke in a clean, surgical line. "That is a very good idea."

Kalvin slid down from Emma's lap and ran up the stairs to their bedroom. A minute later, he returned clutching *Mira*, the stuffed rabbit Samira had given him on her last visit home. Its ears were chewed, and the fur was worn away in a patch shaped like a sad moon where he rubbed it against his cheek. 

"I want to send Mira," Kalvin said, holding the rabbit out. "So she has something to hold."

It was a profound offering—his most reliable source of comfort, surrendered to the sister who needed it more.

Emma took the rabbit gently. "We cannot send Mira to Russia, baby. But we can keep Mira here, and every night when you hold Mira, you can think about Samira, and your thinking will be like a hug that travels across the world. Do you understand?"

Kalvin didn't fully grasp the metaphysics, but he trusted Emma implicitly. He nodded.

"I understand," Eliot chimed in. "It is like the phone. But in your head."

Emma smiled—a real, genuine smile, the first in weeks. "Yes. Exactly like that."

Jessica stood up, still carrying Eliot, and walked to the kitchen drawer. She pulled out the wall calendar Eliot had been crossing days off of in red crayon. Setting it on the table, she said, "Eliot, you have been counting the days. That is very observant. But from now on, we are going to count the days together. Not because we are waiting for Samira to break a promise. But because we are counting the days until we bring her home. And every day we count, we do one thing to help. A picture. A prayer. A phone call. A kind word. One thing. Do you understand?"

"Can we start tonight?" Eliot asked.

"Yes. We can start tonight."

***

After dinner, the family moved to the living room. At the coffee table, Eliot drew his map: a house labeled *HOME*, a long wavy line across the paper, a building labeled *COLD PLACE*, and a stick figure with long hair labeled *SAMIRA*. He drew a path from the cold place to the home, covered in stars. "They are the hugs from everyone who loves her," he explained.

Kalvin drew four people holding hands: himself, Eliot, Jessica, and Emma. In the corner, smaller and watching, he drew a fifth person. "That is Sahara," Kalvin said softly. "Sahara is sad too. She is holding Samira's hand in her mind."

Emma looked at the drawing, seeing the twin geometry clear as day. The family was a constellation of pairs, and the missing star was still part of the shape. 

Later, Jessica put the boys to bed. She read them *The Runaway Bunny*, a story they had heard a hundred times before, about a mother who always finds her child. Eliot listened with his eyes open, staring at the ceiling. Kalvin listened with *Mira* pressed against his cheek. 

When the book was closed, Jessica leaned in and kissed them both. "I will always find you," she whispered into the dark. "No matter how far you run. No matter how far someone takes you. I will always find you." She was saying it to the boys, but she was also vowing it to Samira, and to the universe. She turned off the light.

In the quiet darkness, Eliot whispered, "Kalvin?"

"Yes?"

"If someone took me, would you find me?"

Kalvin rolled over and draped his arm securely around Eliot’s waist. "Yes. I would find you. Even in the cold place. Even in the dark. I would find you."

"How?"

"Because I would feel you. Like Sahara feels Samira. Like the stars feel the sky. I would feel you. And I would come."

Eliot was quiet for a long moment. "I would feel you too. And I would come. And if I could not come, I would send you Mira."

"You don't have a Mira," Kalvin pointed out.

"I would send you me. I would send you my voice in your head."

They fell asleep like that, curled around each other back-to-chest—the exact physical grammar of the older twins, a bond that predated language. Standing in the doorway, Jessica watched them, her hand over her mouth, her body shaking with the memory of her own separated twin in Virginia, who would know without a phone call if Jessica were ever in a cold place.

***

Jessica closed the bedroom door softly and returned to the kitchen. Emma was sitting at the table, the boys' drawings spread out in front of her beside a cup of cold tea. Emma was crying—not the silent, contained crying of her childhood, but open, audible, exhausted tears.

Jessica sat down beside her, moved the teacup away, and put her arms around her wife. They held each other without speaking. They had crossed a threshold. There was no going back to the performance of normalcy. 

After a long time, Emma spoke. "I told them about the bad people. I told them she might be alive. I did not tell them about the KGB. I did not tell them about the facility. I did not tell them about the tests."

"That is enough," Jessica assured her. "That is the right amount."

"Is it? What if they tell their teachers? What if Mrs. Patterson asks questions? What if someone calls child protective services because we told our children that their sister is being held by bad people?"

"Then we tell the truth to them too," Jessica said fiercely. "We tell them that we are a family that does not hide. We are a family that believes children are people. And we deal with the consequences."

"I am afraid."

"I am too. I am afraid that I have broken them. I am afraid that I have told them too much and not enough. I am afraid that Samira is dead and they will never forgive us for giving them hope. I am afraid of everything. But I am more afraid of lying to them. Because I was lied to. And you were lied to. And Samira was lied to by the state. And I will not be the next liar in the chain."

Emma nodded slowly. She picked up Kalvin's drawing, her finger tracing the little figure of Sahara holding Samira's hand in the corner. "They understand more than we think. They understand twinship. They understand distance. They understand love. We have not broken them. We have given them a language for what they already knew."

Jessica took Eliot's map with its trail of stars. "We need to send this to Andrei. And to Sahara. We need to let them know that the boys are holding them too. That the whole family is counting."

Emma wiped her eyes and pulled out her phone. She took a photograph of the two drawings, sending the image to Andrei in Moscow, Sahara in St. Petersburg, Rachel Darcy in Virginia, and Terri Anne and Mike back in Vaughan. She typed the caption: *"The boys know. They are with us. They are counting."*

A minute later, the phone buzzed on the oak table. It was a reply from Sahara, sent from Samira's apartment in Moscow where she was sleeping in her missing sister's bed. 

*"Tell them I am counting too. Tell them Samira feels them. I know she does."*

Jessica read the message aloud. She and Emma reached for each other again, the phone glowing steadily between them. Upstairs, the boys breathed in tandem. Outside, the Halifax snow continued to fall, the world continuing its indifferent rotation. And inside, the family—fractured but fiercely unbroken—held its ground.
