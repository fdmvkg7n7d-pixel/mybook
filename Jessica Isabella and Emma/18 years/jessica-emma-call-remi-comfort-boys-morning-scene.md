# The Architecture of Help

**Time:** Winter ~2060, 7:30 AM (The morning after)
**Location:** The Key-Fritz Mansion and Armbrae Academy, Halifax

It was 7:30 AM, and the Halifax sky was the bruised grey-pink of an Atlantic winter morning. Snow was still falling. Inside the Key-Fritz mansion, Jessica and Emma had been awake for twenty-six hours. 

The kitchen was littered with the wreckage of the night's despair. A half-empty bottle of whiskey stood on the sideboard beside Emma’s untouched cardamom tea. The boys’ drawings from the previous evening—Eliot’s map with its trail of stars, Kalvin’s rendering of Sahara holding Samira’s hand across the world—lay scattered across the oak farmhouse table next to the calendar with its brutal red *X*s. 

Jessica stood by the island in yesterday’s scrubs, smelling of surgical soap and stale whiskey. At 6:45 AM, she had texted her chief resident: *"Family emergency. Delaying to 9 if possible. Will page if cannot make 9."* It was a professional surrender she had never made before. She could feel her own delayed reaction time, her profound emotional lability, the slight, terrifying tremor in her hands. She was a danger to patients today, and she knew it. 

Across the kitchen, Emma sat in her pajamas and an oversized sweater, typing and retyping an email on her laptop to her department chair. She was supposed to deliver a lecture on pediatric neuroanatomy at 8:00 AM. *Family emergency involving our eldest daughter overseas. I will need to reschedule the lecture and may need to take personal leave.* She hit send, feeling the nausea of the incomplete explanation.

Then, Emma stopped typing. She listened. 

She was attuned to the sounds of the house the way she was attuned to a patient's vital signs. Upstairs, the boys were awake. But they were not crying. They were talking in the low, urgent whisper that twins use when they are afraid. The whispered conversation was a diagnostic signal: they were not calling for their mothers, which meant they had decided that their mothers were not safe to call. Emma recognized this decision; she had made it herself at age eight after her own mother died. 

"We cannot do this today," Jessica said, her voice hollow.

"We have to. We are their mothers," Emma replied softly, though she didn't move. 

"We are their mothers, and we are broken, and we are dangerous today," Jessica said, staring at her shaking hands. "We will say the wrong thing. We will cry in front of them. We will frighten them more than they are already frightened. We need help."

Emma looked up. In twenty years—through teenage pregnancy, conjoined twin separation, medical school, residency, parenting, the Bolshoi departure, the Vaganova departure—Jessica had never said the words *we need help*.

"Who?" Emma asked.

"Rémi."

The name hung in the kitchen. It was the only name that made sense. Dr. Rémi Arsenault was the biological father of their sons, a neurosurgeon colleague, and a steadfast, deeply respectful presence in their lives. He was at the QEII hospital right now. He was steady. He was safe.

"Are we sure?" Emma asked carefully. "If we call him in, we cannot call him out again. The boys will know that he came when we could not. That changes his place."

"I know," Jessica said, her jaw tight. "But we are not protecting the map right now. We are protecting the boys."

Jessica picked up the landline. She called his office. He answered on the second ring.

Jessica did not tell him everything. She did not say she had drunk whiskey or that Emma was dissociating. She spoke the shorthand language of surgeons in triage. "Samira is missing. We are in crisis. We need you to take the boys today. We need you to be steady for them. We cannot be."

On the other end of the line, Rémi, who had a complex tumor resection scheduled for 10:00 AM, did not hesitate. "I am on my way," he said.

***

Rémi Arsenault arrived at 7:45 AM. He let himself in through the mudroom, shaking the snow from his wool coat. He walked into the kitchen and assessed the room in three seconds: the whiskey, the drawings, the trembling women. 

He didn't ask how they were. He knew how they were. 

"I have transferred my surgeries to Dr. Hayes," he said calmly, taking his medical bag off his shoulder. "I am clear for the day."

He walked past them and headed upstairs. On the landing, he noticed the hamper outside the bathroom. The lid was slightly ajar. Inside lay Kalvin's wet pajama bottoms. Rémi paused. He took the pajamas, walked quietly into the bathroom, started a cold water soak in the sink, and washed his hands. He handled the physical evidence of Kalvin’s terror without comment, absorbing the child's shame silently so Kalvin wouldn't have to carry it.

He knocked softly on the boys' bedroom door and opened it. 

The two beds had been pushed together. Eliot and Kalvin were curled around each other, back-to-chest, forming an impenetrable twin fortress. Kalvin was clutching *Mira*, his worn stuffed rabbit. They looked up at him, their dark eyes wide and guarded. 

"Good morning," Rémi said. He didn't rush them. He walked over to the wall and looked at the world map hanging there, the pins marking Russia and Virginia. "I see you have a map. I like maps. They help me know where I am." He pointed to a spot on the Canadian coast. "Do you know where I am from?"

"Halifax," Eliot said suspiciously.

"Yes. But before that, I am from a small town in New Brunswick. Very small. Smaller than this house." He pointed correctly on the map. "It is near the ocean. Like Halifax. I grew up with the ocean. Do you like the ocean?"

"Samira took us to the beach," Eliot said. "In Brazil."

A micro-expression of grief flickered across Rémi's face, instantly suppressed. "I remember. I was there too. We built sandcastles. Samira helped you build a very tall one. And then a wave knocked it down. And she helped you build another one."

"She is good at building," Eliot whispered.

"She is. And she is good at coming back. She has always come back."

"This time she is not coming back," Eliot stated flatly.

Rémi sat on the edge of the bed. He did not lie. "This time is harder. This time she is farther away. And there are people who are not letting her come back. But your mothers are working very hard. And Sahara is working very hard. And Andrei is working very hard. And I am going to work hard too."

"Is she dead?" 

The question Eliot had been holding all night finally dropped. Kalvin went completely still.

"I do not think so," Rémi said softly but firmly. "I am a doctor. I know about bodies. And I know about your sister. She is very strong. She is very stubborn. She is very loved. And people who are strong and stubborn and loved do not die easily. They fight. And I believe she is fighting."

"Fighting who?"

"People who think they can take what is not theirs," Rémi answered.

Eliot’s face hardened. "They are bad people."

"Yes," Rémi agreed. "They are bad people. But there are more good people. Now." He reached into the pockets of his coat, which he had draped over his arm. He pulled out two small, impeccably crafted stuffed animals from the hospital gift shop. He handed a moose wearing a tiny stethoscope to Eliot. "This is Doctor Moose. He is very good at listening." He handed a beaver to Kalvin. "This is Beaver. He is very good at building things that don't fall down."

Kalvin looked at Beaver, then at *Mira*, the rabbit he was supposed to think of to send hugs to Samira. He reached out and tucked Beaver under his other arm, holding both. 

"Are you taking us to school?" Eliot asked.

"I am," Rémi said. "If you are ready."

***

Downstairs, Rémi found Jessica and Emma standing exactly where he had left them. 

"The boys are dressing," Rémi told them. "I will make them toast, and then I will take them to Armbrae. You two are going to sleep."

Jessica shook her head. "I have to call the embassy. I have to call the RCMP liaison. I have to—"

"You are going to sleep," Rémi interrupted gently, stepping into her space. "You cannot operate on this patient with shaking hands. Sleep. I have the boys."

Emma reached out and took Jessica’s hand. "Come on," Emma whispered. "He's right."

They shuffled to the stairs like elderly women, leaning heavily on each other. As they passed the boys' open door, they heard Rémi’s warm, steady voice reading *The Runaway Bunny*. They heard Eliot correct him on a word. Then, they heard Kalvin laugh—a small, rusty sound, but the first laugh in days. Jessica covered her mouth, her eyes welling with tears, and let Emma guide her into their bedroom. 

***

At 8:45 AM, Rémi drove the boys to Armbrae Academy on Oxford Street. Eliot held Doctor Moose; Kalvin held Beaver. 

When they arrived, Rémi walked them inside and introduced himself to Mrs. Patterson. "I am Dr. Arsenault. I am a family friend. The boys' mothers are unwell today. I will be their pickup contact." He handed her his business card. 

Mrs. Patterson looked at the neurosurgeon, then at the unusually quiet twins holding their stuffed animals like shields. She didn't press. "Of course, Doctor. We'll take good care of them."

Rémi picked the boys up at 12:15 PM and brought them back to the mansion. He made them grilled cheese and tomato soup for lunch. The afternoon passed in a gentle, constructed bubble of normalcy. The boys drew pictures at the coffee table—Eliot drew a helicopter flying over the 'cold place,' and Kalvin drew the family, with Samira smiling in the corner. Rémi put on a familiar movie and sat in the armchair, simply existing as the steady frame around their fractured picture.

At 2:45 PM, Jessica emerged from the bedroom. She had slept for nearly six hours. She was in sweatpants, unshowered, but her hands had stopped shaking. She leaned against the doorframe, watching her sons safely engrossed in the movie, and watched Rémi sitting guard. The profound humility of gratitude washed over her, mixing with lingering shame, but the gratitude won.

Rémi stood up and walked over to her in the hallway, keeping his voice low. 

"You look better," he said.

"I can think clearly again," Jessica murmured. "Thank you, Rémi. I don't know what we would have—"

"Jessica," Rémi interrupted gently. "I know what you need to do this afternoon and tonight. You have the RCMP to coordinate with, the Russian embassy, the family in Vaughan and Virginia. You and Emma need unbroken focus. A few hours of sleep didn't fix the logistics."

Jessica looked at the floor. "I know."

"Let me take them," Rémi offered. "I will take Eliot and Kalvin to my apartment for a sleepover tonight. I've already cleared my schedule for tomorrow morning. They can bring Doctor Moose, Beaver, and *Mira*. They will be safe, they will be fed, and they will be distracted."

Jessica’s breath hitched. Eliot and Kalvin had never spent the night away from the mansion without her or Emma. It was another massive boundary crossed, another piece of their two-mother fortress dismantled. But she looked at Rémi—at his quiet, unshakeable decency, at the man who was her sons' father finally stepping fully into the center of the storm—and realized she wasn't dismantling a fortress. She was expanding it.

"Okay," Jessica whispered, tears finally slipping down her cheeks. "Okay. Take them for the sleepover."

Rémi nodded, his expression softening with deep, paternal resolve. "I'll go help them pack their bags."
