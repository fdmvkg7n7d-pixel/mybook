# Jessica Isabella and Emma’s First Day of Second-Year Residency / Samira Chaka’s First Day of Grade Five — Scene Outline

## Purpose of This File

This is a **planning / scene-outline file** for a parallel first-day scene in Halifax:

- **Jessica Isabella Key** and **Emma Fritz** begin the active rhythm of their **second year of neurosurgery residency**.
- **Samira Chaka Key** begins **Grade Five** at the Sacred Heart School of Halifax.
- **Sahara Hannan Key** is absent because she has recently begun living at the **Vaganova Ballet Academy** in St. Petersburg.

The emotional point is that everyone is moving forward, but the family shape has changed. This is Samira’s first first-day-of-school without Sahara beside her.

---

## Timeline Placement

- **Time:** Early September **~2051**, shortly after the August handoff to Vaganova.
- **Ages:**
  - Jessica Isabella: **27**
  - Emma: **27**
  - Samira Chaka: about **11**
  - Sahara Hannan: about **11**, newly at Vaganova
- **Jessica and Emma’s status:** **Second-year neurosurgery residents** / **PGY-2** doctors in Halifax.
- **Samira’s school:** **Grade Five** at the Sacred Heart School of Halifax.
- **Location:** Key-Fritz mansion in Halifax; Sacred Heart School of Halifax; QEII / Dalhousie hospital setting.

### Terminology Note

Jessica and Emma are already **doctors** because they have finished medical school. They are not medical students anymore. They are in **residency**, meaning supervised specialty training.

Best wording:

> Jessica Isabella and Emma are second-year neurosurgery residents.

Or:

> Jessica Isabella and Emma are in PGY-2 of neurosurgery.

## Characters Present

### Jessica Isabella Key

- Twenty-seven.
- Biological mother of Samira and Sahara.
- Second-year neurosurgery resident.
- Protective, precise, and emotionally raw underneath her competence.
- Feels the split between hospital responsibility and motherhood sharply today.

### Emma Fritz

- Twenty-seven.
- Co-mother of Samira and Sahara.
- Second-year neurosurgery resident.
- Practical, steady, deeply attentive to logistics.
- Carries Sahara’s Vaganova safety plan in her mind even while preparing for hospital rounds.

### Samira Chaka Key

- About eleven.
- First day of Grade Five.
- Beginning school without Sahara beside her for the first time.
- Trying to act normal because Sahara’s Vaganova life is supposed to be a triumph.
- Proud of Sahara, lonely without her, and embarrassed by how visible the loneliness feels.

### Sahara Hannan Key

- Absent physically.
- Present through a message / note / time-zone awareness.
- Newly living at Vaganova in St. Petersburg.
- Her absence shapes the morning: one lunch not packed, one uniform not worn, one school drop-off changed forever.

---

## Core Emotional Question

How does this household survive a morning where everyone has a first day, but one of the twins is no longer there to have hers with them?

---

## Emotional Core

This is a promotion day for everyone:

- Jessica and Emma are not new doctors anymore. They are PGY-2 residents, expected to carry more responsibility.
- Samira is not little anymore. She is a Grade Five student.
- Sahara is not a Halifax schoolgirl this year. She is a Vaganova student in Russia.

But the morning does not feel only like growth. It feels like subtraction.

The house has learned how to prepare two girls for school. Now it must prepare one.

---

## Scene Structure

This outline can be written as a braided scene with three movements:

1. **Morning at the mansion** — everyone prepares for the day.
2. **Samira’s school drop-off** — Grade Five begins without Sahara.
3. **Jessica and Emma at the hospital** — PGY-2 begins, with motherhood still running under the scrubs.

---

## Beat Sheet

### Beat 1 — Pre-Dawn / Early Morning at the Mansion

- The Halifax mansion wakes before sunrise.
- Jessica and Emma are already up because hospital time starts early.
- The kitchen is lit before the rest of the house.
- Items on the counter show the three lives being managed at once:
  - two hospital ID badges,
  - Jessica’s and Emma’s own iPhones,
  - coffee,
  - lunch for Samira,
  - school forms,
  - a Grade Five cardigan / uniform piece,
  - a Russia folder or Vaganova contact sheet,
  - Emma’s notes about Sahara’s patch supplies and emergency contacts.
- The absence is visible because there is no second Sacred Heart uniform laid out for Sahara.

### Beat 2 — Jessica Notices the Missing Uniform

- Jessica looks toward the place where Sahara’s uniform would have been.
- She does not say anything at first.
- Emma sees Jessica seeing it.
- They share a quiet look: not dramatic, but deep.
- This is the first first-day morning where only Samira needs a Halifax uniform.

Possible line from Jessica:

> “I almost ironed two.”

Emma answers gently:

> “I almost packed two lunches.”

- Neither mistake is foolish. It is muscle memory.

### Beat 3 — Samira Comes Downstairs

- Samira enters in her Grade Five uniform.
- She looks older than they expected.
- Jessica’s first instinct is pride.
- Emma’s first instinct is to check practical details:
  - shoes,
  - lunch,
  - schedule,
  - emergency contact card,
  - whether Samira has everything.
- Samira notices both reactions and tries to smile.

Possible line:

> “You’re both looking at me like I’m going to Russia too.”

- It is meant as a joke.
- It lands with more truth than she intended.

### Beat 4 — Sahara’s iPhone Message

- An iPhone message from Sahara arrives because of the time difference, or was sent earlier from Vaganova.
- It may be short and twin-specific:

> Good luck in Grade Five. Do not let my side of the classroom be dramatic.

Or:

> If anyone asks where I am, say I am being corrected in Russian.

Or:

> Wear the cardigan. You get cold and pretend you don’t.

- Samira reads it and almost laughs.
- The almost-laugh is painful because Sahara still knows her exactly.
- Jessica and Emma let Samira have the iPhone message privately for a moment.

### Beat 5 — Breakfast With One Twin at the Table

- Breakfast happens as normally as possible.
- Jessica and Emma are in partial hospital mode already: checking times, service assignments, their own iPhone volumes, commute.
- Samira is in school mode: backpack, lunch, first-day nerves.
- But there is an empty chair / empty place in the rhythm.
- No one has to say Sahara’s name for the room to be full of it.

Possible small actions:

- Samira reaches for the cereal and almost slides it toward Sahara’s usual place.
- Emma cuts fruit and makes too much.
- Jessica asks, “Do you have your ballet things?” then realizes Samira’s after-school plan is different without Sahara.

### Beat 6 — Jessica and Emma Explain Their Second Year

- Samira asks what “second year” means for them.
- This is a chance to clarify the doctor timeline naturally.

Possible dialogue:

> Samira: “So are you students or doctors today?”

> Emma: “Doctors.”

> Jessica: “Doctors who still get told what to do.”

> Emma: “Second-year residents. PGY-2.”

> Samira: “That sounds like a robot name.”

> Jessica: “It feels like one before coffee.”

- Emma explains simply:

> “We finished medical school. Residency is the part where we train in neurosurgery. More responsibility this year. More patients. More pages. More people expecting us to know things.”

- Samira hears the pressure but also the pride.

### Beat 7 — The First-Day Photograph

- Jessica wants a first-day photo.
- Samira resists because she is eleven and because Sahara is not there.
- Emma suggests one photo for Samira, and one later to send Sahara.
- Samira agrees only if they do not make it too sentimental.
- They take a photo on the mansion steps / by the front door / in the kitchen.
- The frame is visibly missing Sahara.

Possible line from Samira:

> “Don’t leave space beside me on purpose.”

Jessica answers:

> “Okay.”

- Jessica obeys. That matters.
- She does not make Samira pose inside absence.

### Beat 8 — The Drive to Sacred Heart

- Jessica and Emma both insist on taking Samira if schedules allow.
- If only one can go, the other walks them to the car and makes the goodbye count.
- Recommended version: both go to drop-off, then go straight to hospital.
- The drive is quiet at first.
- Samira looks out the window.
- The city is ordinary: school traffic, uniforms, parents, backpacks.
- Ordinary life feels strange because the family has just crossed an ocean emotionally.

Possible dialogue:

> Samira: “Everyone will ask where she is.”

> Emma: “What do you want to say?”

> Samira: “The truth.”

> Jessica: “Then say the truth.”

> Samira: “She’s at Vaganova. In Russia.”

> Emma: “That is a very good truth.”

### Beat 9 — Arrival at Sacred Heart / The Questions Begin

- Sacred Heart School of Halifax is familiar.
- The uniformed students gather in first-day clusters.
- The other Grade Five students notice immediately that Sahara is not beside Samira.
- At first, one or two girls ask. Then the questions multiply because Sahara’s absence is too large and too unusual to ignore.
- Samira answers with practiced pride:

> “She’s at Vaganova Ballet Academy in St. Petersburg.”

- The classmates react with awe, confusion, curiosity, maybe envy.
- They have many questions:
  - “Is she in Russia now?”
  - “Does she live at the ballet school?”
  - “Does she sleep there?”
  - “Is it like boarding school?”
  - “Does she speak Russian?”
  - “Can she come home on weekends?”
  - “Is she going to be famous?”
  - “Are you going there too?”
  - “Do you miss her?”
  - “Is it weird being here without your twin?”
  - “Who is your partner now?”
  - “Can you FaceTime her on your iPhone?”
- The questions are not cruel, but there are too many of them.
- Samira feels proud and overwhelmed at the same time.
- She answers the factual ones first:

> “Yes, Russia.”
>
> “St. Petersburg.”
>
> “Yes, she sleeps there.”
>
> “No, she can’t come home on weekends. It’s across the ocean.”

- When someone asks whether Samira misses her, Samira pauses. This question is true but too public.
- She chooses a careful answer:

> “She wanted to go. I’m proud of her.”

- That is true, but not all of it.
- Jessica and Emma watch from a respectful distance, seeing how much Samira is carrying before the bell even rings.

### Beat 10 — Samira’s First Moment Alone

- Samira walks toward the Grade Five line / classroom without Sahara beside her.
- This is the emotional center of the school half.
- She may instinctively turn her head to check where Sahara is.
- No one is there.
- She straightens.
- She keeps walking.

Possible internal / narration beat:

> It was not that Samira did not know how to walk into school. It was that every first step had always had an echo.

### Beat 11 — Teacher Recognition / Containing the Questions

- The Grade Five teacher greets Samira with warmth.
- The teacher knows Sahara is away.
- The teacher also sees the other students clustering with questions.
- She does not embarrass Samira by making a speech about Sahara, but she does gently contain the room.

Possible teacher line:

> “Good morning, Samira. We’re very glad to have you.”

Then, to the class:

> “I know many of you have questions about Sahara. Samira can share what she wants to share. She does not have to answer everything today.”

- This matters because Samira is allowed to be proud without becoming the class spokesperson for Sahara’s entire life.
- The teacher may allow one simple factual explanation:

> “Sahara is attending a ballet academy in St. Petersburg, Russia. That is exciting, and it is also a big change for Samira and for our class.”

- The teacher does not ask, “Are you okay without your sister?” in front of everyone.
- She gives Samira a normal welcome and a boundary at the same time.
- This steadiness helps Samira.

### Beat 12 — Goodbye at School

- Jessica and Emma say goodbye.
- Samira is older now; they do not hug her in a way that embarrasses her unless she initiates it.
- She does initiate something small:
  - takes Emma’s hand for one second,
  - lets Jessica adjust her collar,
  - leans in quickly,
  - or says, “Text me if Sahara texts.”
- Emma promises:

> “If Sahara sends something for you, you’ll see it after school.”

Jessica adds:

> “And if she sends something urgent, we’ll find you.”

Samira says:

> “She won’t. She’ll be busy being corrected in Russian.”

- This is her bravery returning through humor.

### Beat 13 — Jessica and Emma Return to the Car

- After Samira disappears inside, Jessica and Emma get back into the car.
- For a moment neither moves.
- Their hospital day is waiting.
- Their daughter is inside Grade Five.
- Their other daughter is across the ocean.
- The silence is full.

Possible line from Jessica:

> “We have one in Grade Five and one in Russia.”

Emma:

> “And we are supposed to operate on brains today.”

Jessica:

> “Second year.”

Emma:

> “Second year.”

- They laugh once, not because it is funny, but because otherwise they may cry.

### Beat 14 — Hospital Arrival / PGY-2 Begins

- They arrive at the hospital / QEII / Dalhousie service.
- The tone shifts: fluorescent lights, scrubs, white coats, their own iPhones, OR boards, consult lists.
- They are no longer first-year residents.
- People expect more from them immediately.
- A senior resident or attending greets them with a workload instead of ceremony.

Possible line:

> “Welcome back. You’re PGY-2 now. Consults are already piling up.”

- Jessica and Emma exchange a look: no sentimental transition, just responsibility.

### Beat 15 — The Difference Between PGY-1 and PGY-2

Show what has changed:

- They know where to stand during rounds.
- They know which forms matter.
- They know which pages are urgent and which are noise.
- They can answer more questions than last year.
- Junior learners may look to them now.
- They are still supervised, but no longer protected by being brand-new.

Possible internal contrast:

> Last year, they were terrified of not knowing. This year, they were terrified because they knew enough to understand what could go wrong.

### Beat 16 — First Patient / First Page

- Jessica gets a page or consult early.
- Emma is pulled toward rounds / OR prep / clinic.
- The day separates them professionally, mirroring the way the family has been separated geographically.
- They squeeze hands quickly before splitting.

Possible line from Emma:

> “Text me when you can.”

Jessica:

> “You too.”

- This echoes what they told Samira about Sahara.

### Beat 17 — Samira in the Grade Five Classroom

Cut back to Samira.

- She enters the Grade Five classroom.
- There may be paired desks or assigned seats.
- Sahara’s absence is practical now:
  - no twin beside her,
  - no shared pencil case,
  - no whispered commentary,
  - no matching answer to a teacher’s question.
- The teacher asks students to write something about their summer.
- Samira freezes because summer means Russia, Vaganova, goodbye.

Possible prompt:

> What is one thing that changed this summer?

- Samira writes:

> My sister moved to Russia for ballet.

Then she adds:

> I am proud of her.

After a pause:

> I miss her.

- This echoes the pool scene: both things are true.

### Beat 18 — Jessica in the Hospital

Cut to Jessica.

- Jessica scrubs / rounds / answers a question correctly.
- She functions well.
- Her body knows the hospital.
- But when she sees two pediatric patients / siblings / twins in a hallway, she thinks of Samira and Sahara.
- She does not collapse. She keeps moving.
- PGY-2 requires movement.

### Beat 19 — Emma in the Hospital

Cut to Emma.

- Emma checks a chart, speaks to a nurse, or prepares for a consult.
- She is calm and exact.
- Emma’s own iPhone remains on her person because Vaganova could contact her.
- She does not check it obsessively in front of patients.
- But between tasks she looks once.
- No message.
- No message is good.
- No message is also hard.

### Beat 20 — Lunch / Midday Echo

- Samira opens her lunch at school.
- Emma packed one lunch, not two.
- Maybe there is a note inside from Jessica and Emma:

> Grade Five. First day. We love you. Both things are true.

- Or a small line:

> You do not have to be the easy one.

- Samira reads it privately.
- She does not cry.
- Or she almost does and then eats.

Parallel hospital beat:

- Jessica and Emma barely have lunch.
- One texts the other on their own iPhones:

> She got inside okay.

The other replies:

> So did we.

### Beat 21 — Afternoon / Everyone Survives the First Day

- Samira gets through class.
- Jessica and Emma get through hospital responsibilities.
- Sahara remains away at Vaganova, but the family has made it through the first ordinary day after the extraordinary change.
- Nothing is fixed.
- The new structure has begun.

### Beat 22 — After-School Pickup / Reunion

- One or both mothers pick Samira up, depending on the hospital schedule.
- If neither can because of residency, a trusted driver / family support picks her up, but Jessica and Emma call as soon as possible.
- Recommended version: Emma manages pickup; Jessica is delayed but calls from the hospital.
- Samira gives a minimal first report:

> “It was fine.”

- Emma knows “fine” means too much to unpack in the car.
- She does not force it.

### Beat 23 — Sahara’s iPhone Message After School

- An iPhone message from Sahara arrives after Samira’s school day.
- It can be short:

> Did you survive Grade Five?

Samira replies:

> Did you survive Russian corrections?

Sahara:

> Barely.

Samira:

> Same.

- This exchange is small but essential. They are apart, but still in conversation.

### Beat 24 — Evening at the Mansion

- The family reunites in the mansion at night.
- Jessica and Emma are exhausted from PGY-2.
- Samira is exhausted from being a twin alone at school.
- They eat something simple.
- No one performs triumph.
- They tell the truth at the table:
  - one good thing,
  - one hard thing,
  - one thing about Sahara.

Possible table exchange:

> Jessica: “Good thing: I answered an attending correctly.”

> Emma: “Hard thing: my own iPhone made me hate sound.”

> Samira: “Good thing: nobody sat in Sahara’s chair.”

> Jessica: “Hard thing?”

> Samira: “Nobody sat in Sahara’s chair.”

- The same fact can be good and hard.
- Jessica and Emma understand.

### Beat 25 — Closing Image

- The day ends with three people in the mansion and one across the ocean.
- Jessica and Emma place their hospital badges on the counter.
- Samira places her Grade Five bag beside them.
- The objects make a new family map:
  - hospital badges,
  - school bag,
  - Samira’s own iPhone with Sahara’s message,
  - Vaganova contact sheet.
- They survived the first day.
- Tomorrow will not be first anymore, but it will still be new.

## Thematic Work

- **First days as thresholds:** Grade Five and PGY-2 both mark growth, but growth is not painless.
- **Doctors and mothers:** Jessica and Emma are competent physicians, but motherhood remains present under every hospital task.
- **One twin at school:** Samira’s first day without Sahara makes the separation public.
- **Classroom curiosity:** The rest of the Grade Five students have many questions about Sahara; Samira has to learn the difference between sharing truth and being put on display.
- **Sahara’s absence as presence:** She is not in the scene physically, but her iPhone message and missing chair shape everything.
- **Two truths:** Pride and grief coexist. Sahara’s success does not erase Samira’s loneliness.
- **Residency clarity:** The scene can gently explain that Jessica and Emma are already doctors, now training as neurosurgery residents.

---

## Continuity Notes

- This scene should follow the August pool conversation and Sahara’s early Vaganova dorm scenes.
- Jessica Isabella and Emma are **twenty-seven** and in **second-year neurosurgery residency / PGY-2**.
- Samira Chaka is beginning **Grade Five** at Sacred Heart School of Halifax.
- Sahara Hannan is newly living at **Vaganova Ballet Academy** in St. Petersburg.
- Avoid making Samira’s sadness into pressure on Sahara. The adults protect both truths.
- The hospital material does not need heavy technical detail; the important thing is the increased responsibility and the emotional split between doctoring and parenting.

## Final Image

On the kitchen counter at the Halifax mansion, Jessica and Emma’s hospital badges lie beside Samira’s Grade Five bag. Emma’s Vaganova contact sheet is tucked under a mug. Jessica’s own iPhone is still on. Samira’s own iPhone lights once with a message from Sahara:

> Barely survived Russian corrections. Your turn.

Samira types back:

> Barely survived Grade Five. Same tomorrow?

Across the ocean, Sahara will read it when she can.

In Halifax, Jessica turns off the kitchen light. Emma checks that her own iPhone volume is on. Samira carries her school bag upstairs.

The first day is over.

The new year has begun.
