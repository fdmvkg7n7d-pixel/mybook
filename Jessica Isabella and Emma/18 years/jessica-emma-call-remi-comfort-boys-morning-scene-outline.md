# Jessica Isabella and Emma Call Rémi to Comfort the Boys — The Morning After — Age Thirty-Six (~2060) — Scene Outline

*Jessica Isabella & Emma's branch. A structured, emotionally grounded, and psychologically precise scene outline set in the Halifax mansion the morning after the 2 AM selfishness conversation, approximately five hours after Jessica and Emma finally stop talking. It is 7:30 AM. Jessica has a 7 AM surgical shift she has already texted to delay. Emma has an 8 AM lecture she is already drafting an apology email for. Neither has slept. The boys are awake, frightened, confused by the previous night's confession, and clinging to each other in a way that mirrors the older twins' bond. Jessica and Emma, depleted beyond the point of competent parenting, make the call they have never made before: they ask Rémi Arsenault to come not as a babysitter, not as a biological donor, but as a third parent who is steady enough to hold what they cannot. The scene explores the radical humility of asking for help, the architecture of chosen family under crisis, and the moment when a man who has always been careful to stay in his lane is asked to drive into the center of the storm.*

---

## Canon Placement

- **Time:** Winter **~2060**, the morning immediately following the all-night conversation in the kitchen. The sky is grey-pink, the snow still falling. It is 7:30 AM. Jessica and Emma have been awake for twenty-six hours. They have been crying, shouting, holding each other, and deciding on a new strategy. The adrenaline has left their bodies. They are in the crash phase of exhaustion.
- **Location:** The **Halifax mansion**, specifically the kitchen and the second-floor boys' bedroom. The kitchen is littered with the remains of the night: cold tea, the whiskey bottle on the sideboard, drawings on the oak table, the calendar with red X's. The boys' bedroom is a twin room with dormer windows, paper stars, the world map with pins, and the two small beds pushed together because Eliot has been refusing to sleep alone. The house smells of coffee that Jessica started and forgot, and the faint sulfur of the radiator kicking on after a cold night.
- **Core Thematic Tension:** The Key family principle *"your body is yours"* and the corollary they forged last night—*"your family is yours, your consequence is yours"*—requires a third axiom that they have never needed before: *"your help is allowed."* Jessica and Emma are women who have built their lives on self-sufficiency. They carried teenage twins at sixteen while finishing school. They became neurosurgeons while parenting. They bought a mansion and filled it with love and did not ask for help because asking felt like admitting the architecture was flawed. Now, with a daughter missing in Siberia and two sons who need steadiness they cannot provide, they must learn to ask. And Rémi—who has spent five years as a donor, a colleague, a vacation companion, an uncle, but never a daily parent—must decide whether to accept the invitation into the inner circle, knowing that once he enters, he cannot step back without damaging the boys.
- **The Rémi Paradox:** Rémi Arsenault is the biological father of Eliot and Kalvin. He has been scrupulous about not overstepping. He babysat in Natal, he attends birthdays, he is present but not possessive. He has never been asked to parent in a crisis because Jessica and Emma have never had a crisis they could not manage together. Now they are asking him to do what he has always been biologically capable of and emotionally restrained from: to be a father. The scene tests whether chosen family can expand under pressure without collapsing the original structure.

## Core Premise

Jessica is supposed to be in surgery at 7 AM. At 6:45, she texts her chief resident: *"Family emergency. Delaying to 9 if possible. Will page if cannot make 9."* She knows she is not capable of operating. She knows she is not capable of driving safely. She stands in the kitchen in yesterday's scrubs, smelling of surgical soap and whiskey, and she looks at Emma, who is in her pajamas and a sweater, typing and retyping an email to her department chair explaining that she will miss her 8 AM lecture on pediatric neuroanatomy.

Upstairs, the boys are awake. They are not crying. They are talking in the low, urgent whisper that twins use when they are afraid. Eliot is explaining something to Kalvin. Kalvin is making small sounds of agreement or fear. They have not called for their mothers. This is worse than crying. They have withdrawn into each other, the twin bond becoming a fortress, and the fortress is locked from the inside.

Jessica says: *"We cannot do this today."* Emma says: *"We have to. We are their mothers."* Jessica: *"We are their mothers and we are broken and we are dangerous today. We will say the wrong thing. We will cry in front of them. We will frighten them more than they are already frightened. We need help."* Emma stops typing. She looks at Jessica. She has never heard Jessica say those words. In twenty years, through teenage pregnancy, conjoined twin separation, medical school, residency, parenting, the Bolshoi departure, the Vaganova departure, the second pregnancy, the Brazil trip, the Russia trips, every challenge, Jessica has never said *we need help.* Emma says: *"Who?"* Jessica says: *"Rémi."* The name hangs in the kitchen. It is the only name that makes sense. He is in Halifax. He is at QEII, probably already in his office. He knows the boys. He loves them. He is steady. He is safe. And he is theirs to call—he has always been theirs, waiting at the edge of the family, respectful, present, ready. Emma says: *"Are we sure?"* Jessica: *"We are sure that we are not sure of anything else."* She picks up the phone. She calls his office. He answers on the second ring.

## Characters

### Jessica Isabella Key (36)
- She has not slept. She has not changed. She has drunk three fingers of whiskey on an empty stomach and her stomach is burning. She is experiencing the particular disorientation of a surgeon who knows her own impairment: she can feel her own delayed reaction time, her own emotional lability, the slight tremor in her hands that would make her disqualify herself from any OR. She is a danger to patients today and she knows it. The text to her chief resident was a professional surrender she has never made before.
- She is also a mother who is terrified of her own children. Not of their needs, but of her inability to meet them. She remembers her own mother, Terri Anne, holding the bowl while everyone else cried. She remembers thinking Terri Anne was invincible. She now understands that Terri Anne was probably exhausted, probably terrified, probably wanted to scream, and held the bowl because someone had to and she was the one who had learned how. Jessica does not have the bowl today. She has shattered it. She needs someone else to bring a bowl.
- Her call to Rémi is a triple betrayal of her self-image: she is asking a man for help, she is asking her colleague to parent her children, and she is admitting that the two-mother fortress she built with Emma is not impermeable. She hates the necessity and she is relieved by it in equal measure, and the conflict makes her irritable, sharp, and vulnerable.
- She will not tell Rémi everything on the phone. She will tell him enough: *"Samira is missing. We are in crisis. We need you to take the boys today. We need you to be steady for them. We cannot be."* She will not say *we are angry at her* or *we are falling apart* or *I drank whiskey and I am not fit to drive.* She will say what a surgeon says: *"We need support."* And Rémi, who speaks the language of surgeons, will hear what she is not saying.

### Emma Fritz (36)
- She has also not slept. She has not prayed. She has not eaten. She is experiencing the dissociation that comes from prolonged maternal performance: she has been translating her own emotions for so long that she is no longer certain what her original language is. She is drafting an email to her chair that she knows is inadequate, deleting it, drafting again, knowing that no explanation is sufficient without telling the truth, and the truth is not hers to tell to a department chair. She settles on: *"Family emergency involving our eldest daughter overseas. I will need to reschedule the lecture and may need to take personal leave. I will update you as soon as possible."* She sends it. She feels the shame of the incomplete explanation.
- She is the one who hears the boys first. She is attuned to their sounds the way she is attuned to a patient's vital signs. The whispered conversation upstairs is a signal: they are not calling for help, which means they have decided that help is not available. She recognizes this decision because she made it herself at age eight, after her mother died, when she stopped calling for her father because he was not safe to call. The recognition makes her nauseous. She wants to run upstairs and break through the fortress, but she knows that if she runs, she will break it destructively, not rescue them. She needs someone who can enter without force.
- She is more comfortable with the Rémi call than Jessica is, but in a different way. Emma has always known that they would need Rémi eventually. She has watched him with the boys at Natal, on birthdays, at the hospital when one of them had a febrile seizure at age two. She has seen that he is a natural father, that his restraint is a choice, that his love is disciplined and deep. She has wondered, in the quiet moments, whether they have done him a disservice by keeping him at the perimeter. Now, in crisis, she is glad the perimeter is there to be crossed. She is also afraid that crossing it will change the map forever. She says to Jessica, while the phone is ringing: *"If we call him in, we cannot call him out again. The boys will know that he came when we could not. That changes his place."* Jessica says: *"I know. But we are not protecting the map. We are protecting the boys."* Emma nods. She agrees. But she files the worry away for later, when the crisis has passed and the new geography must be negotiated.
- She makes coffee while Jessica calls. Real coffee, not the forgotten pot. She makes it with the mechanical precision of someone who needs to perform a task in order to remain upright. She puts two slices of bread in the toaster. She will force Jessica to eat. She will force herself to eat. They are surgeons. They know that a body without fuel is a body that makes mistakes.

### Dr. Rémi Arsenault (mid-forties)
- A neurosurgeon, Jessica and Emma's mentor and colleague at QEII Health Sciences Centre. The biological donor of Eliot and Kalvin. He is a warm, steady, methodically kind man who has spent the five years since the boys' birth carefully calibrating his presence: close enough to be known, distant enough to be unobtrusive, loving enough to be felt, restrained enough to be safe. He has never asked for more access. He has never complained about less. He has built a life that includes the boys on the edges: birthdays, a beach vacation, occasional babysitting, hospital visits where he stops by the pediatric wing with stickers and patience.
- He is in his office at 7:15 AM when the call comes. He has already reviewed three scans, scheduled two consults, and is preparing for a tumor resection at 10 AM. He is a man who likes his routines. The call shatters the routine. He hears Jessica's voice—flat, exhausted, stripped of the performative confidence he has always known her to carry—and he knows before she says the words that something is catastrophically wrong. He has never heard Jessica ask for help. He has heard her demand, argue, charm, and command. He has never heard her need.
- His response is immediate and total. He cancels the 10 AM resection. He reschedules the consults. He tells his PA: *"Family emergency. I am unavailable until tomorrow."* He does not know yet what the emergency is. He does not need to know. Jessica said *family*, and he is, however peripherally, family. He drives to the mansion in the snow, his car equipped with winter tires and the steady rhythm of a man who has been waiting for this call without knowing he was waiting.
- When he arrives, he must assess the situation with the speed and precision of a physician assessing a trauma: the mothers' condition, the boys' condition, the immediate needs, the longer-term plan. He must be steady enough to hold the boys without being so steady that he seems indifferent to the crisis. He must ask the questions that need asking without demanding answers the mothers are not ready to give. And he must decide, in the first hour, whether his role today is temporary support or permanent recalibration. He is a man who has always been careful. Today, care is a luxury. Today, he must be present.
- He brings his medical bag, not because he expects to use it, but because it is a physical anchor—a symbol of competence that he can hold when words fail. He also brings two small stuffed animals he keeps in his office for pediatric patients: a moose and a beaver, Canadian, unthreatening. He has never given them to Eliot and Kalvin before because he did not want to compete with *Mira* or the boys' existing attachments. Today, he brings them because the boys may need new anchors, and he is willing to be one.

### Eliot David Key-Fritz (5)
- He woke at 6:30 AM, before the sun was fully up, because he heard the radiator click and it sounded different from usual. He lay in bed listening to the house. He did not hear his mothers moving in the kitchen. He heard low voices, but they were not the morning voices—cheerful, coffee-making, toast-burning. They were the night voices, the voices from after the talking, from the confession, from the crying. He recognized the tone. He did not know what to do with the recognition.
- He turned to Kalvin. His brother was already awake, eyes open, staring at the ceiling. They did not speak for a few minutes. They listened. Then Eliot whispered: *"They are still sad."* Kalvin nodded. Eliot: *"We should not make noise."* Kalvin: *"I am hungry."* Eliot: *"We can wait."* They waited. They whispered about Samira, about the map Eliot drew, about whether the bad people would also take Sahara, about whether they could hide in the closet if someone came. Eliot told Kalvin that he would protect him. Kalvin said he knew. They held hands under the blankets.
- When they hear the front door open downstairs—a sound that is not their mothers' footsteps—they stiffen. Eliot sits up. He listens. He hears a man's voice, low, familiar. He recognizes it as Rémi's. He feels a complicated emotion: relief, because Rémi is safe, and confusion, because Rémi is not a morning person in their house. Rémi comes for birthdays and dinners, not for ordinary mornings. Eliot says to Kalvin: *"Rémi is here. Something is really wrong."* Kalvin pulls the covers over his head. Eliot does not. He sits up straighter. He is the twin who faces things. He will face this too. But his hands are shaking, and he hides them under the blanket so Kalvin will not see.
- He will ask Rémi, directly, whether Samira is dead. He has been preparing this question since last night. He will not ask his mothers because he knows they will cry, and he cannot bear to make them cry again. He will ask Rémi because Rémi is a doctor, and doctors know the truth, and Rémi is not his mother, so Rémi's crying is not his responsibility. This is a five-year-old's calculus of survival, and it is precise and heartbreaking.

### Kalvin LaMarcus Key-Fritz (5)
- He woke when Eliot woke, or perhaps he never fully slept. He has been holding *Mira* the rabbit against his chest, his thumb in his mouth—a habit he had stopped at four but has resumed in the past month. He is wet again. He wet the bed in the night, and he knows it, and he is ashamed, and he is afraid that if he gets up, Eliot will smell it, or the mothers will, and they will be disappointed in him for backsliding. He is lying in his own urine, cold and uncomfortable, but he will not move. He will not announce it. He will endure it because announcing it would require attention, and attention would require his mothers to come upstairs, and he does not want them to come upstairs because they are broken and he cannot fix them.
- When Rémi arrives, Kalvin does not feel the same relief as Eliot. Rémi is kind, but he is not the person Kalvin wants. Kalvin wants Samira. He wants the warm presence of his big sister, who used to read him stories in a funny voice, who used to carry him on her shoulders even when he was too big, who used to call him *Kalvin the Brave* when he was afraid of the dark. Rémi is not Samira. No one is Samira. The realization that Samira is not coming, that the person who could make this okay is the person who is missing, is a grief that Kalvin does not have words for. He only knows that he wants to be smaller, to curl up inside *Mira*, to disappear.
- He will not ask Rémi anything. He will let Eliot ask. He will listen. He will absorb. He will hold *Mira* and he will wet himself again, perhaps, and he will not say anything, because silence is the only gift he has left to give his mothers. He is the twin who stays. He is the twin who holds. And today, he is holding so tightly that he is afraid he will break.

## Detailed Scene Beats

### 1. The Call (7:15 AM)
- Jessica stands in the kitchen. She pulls out her iPhone from the pocket of yesterday's scrubs. She finds Rémi's office number in her contacts and taps it. The screen is cracked in the corner, a casualty of a rushed locker-room change three weeks ago, but the call connects. She holds it to her ear, the thin rectangle feeling absurdly light for the weight of what she is about to ask. She has never called him at work for a personal reason. She has called him to consult on scans, to ask about cases, to coordinate schedules. Never for this.
- He answers on the second ring. *"Arsenault."* She says: *"Rémi. It's Jessica."* He hears her voice and stops. He does not say *hello* or *how are you* because he knows she is not calling to be asked how she is. He says: *"What do you need?"* The directness is a relief. She says: *"Samira is missing. She is in Russia. She was taken by a state program. We are in crisis. Emma and I have not slept. We cannot parent today. We need you to take the boys. We need you to be steady. We need you to be..."* She stops. She does not know what word to use. She cannot say *their father*. She has never said it. She says: *"We need you to be what you have always been. Only more."* There is a silence on the line. Then Rémi says: *"I am leaving now. I will be there in twenty minutes. I will cancel my day. Text me the door code. I will let myself in. Do not wait up. Go to bed."* Jessica says: *"We cannot go to bed. We have to—"* Rémi interrupts, gently but firmly. *"Jessica. You called me because you cannot do it. Let me do it. Go to bed. Or sit. Or cry. Or whatever you need. I will handle the boys. When you are ready, you will tell me what you need me to tell them. Until then, I will hold the space."* He hangs up. Jessica stands with the phone in her hand. She puts it down. She looks at Emma. She says: *"He is coming."* Emma says: *"Good."* She is making toast. She is crying. She does not know she is crying. Jessica walks to her and takes the butter knife from her hand before she cuts herself.

### 2. The Preparation (7:20–7:40 AM)
- Emma goes upstairs. She stands outside the boys' door. She listens. She hears the whispering. She does not knock. She goes to her own bedroom, changes out of her pajamas into leggings and a sweater, brushes her teeth, splashes water on her face. She looks in the mirror. She looks like a woman who has been beaten. She does not care. She applies no makeup. She pulls her hair back. She is preparing to be seen in her worst state by a man who has never seen her unwashed, uncombed, unguarded. She is too tired to be embarrassed.
- Jessica changes into clean scrubs, not because she is going to work, but because she cannot be in yesterday's clothes anymore. The smell of whiskey and surgical soap and sweat is making her nauseous. She brushes her teeth, drinks three glasses of water, takes two acetaminophen for the headache. She looks at the whiskey bottle on the sideboard. She puts it in the cabinet. She does not pour the rest down the drain because she may need it again tonight. She hates that she thinks this. She sits at the kitchen table and waits.
- At 7:35, she texts Rémi the door code. At 7:40, she hears his car in the driveway. She hears the door open. She does not stand. She is too tired to stand. Emma comes downstairs. They sit together at the table, side by side, holding hands, waiting for him to find them.

### 3. The Arrival (7:45 AM)
- Rémi enters the kitchen. He is wearing his winter coat, his medical bag over his shoulder, snow on his boots. He is carrying a paper bag from a bakery. He looks at the two women. He takes in the scene: the whiskey bottle gone from the sideboard but the cup still on the table, the drawings, the calendar, the red X's, the exhaustion, the hand-holding, the way they look at him with a mixture of desperation and gratitude and shame.
- He does not ask for details. He puts the bakery bag on the table. He takes off his coat. He hangs it on the hook by the door—the hook where Samira's red coat hangs in Moscow, the hook where the boys' snowsuits hang, the hook where Terri Anne's coat hung at Christmas. He puts his medical bag on the floor. He pulls out a chair. He sits across from them. He opens the bakery bag. He takes out two croissants and two coffees. He puts them in front of Jessica and Emma. He says: *"Eat. Drink. Then tell me what I need to know."* Jessica looks at the croissant. She has not eaten since lunch yesterday. She picks it up. She takes a bite. It is cold, but the butter is real and the pastry is flaky and the act of chewing is so normal that it makes her cry. She cries while eating the croissant, and Rémi does not look away, and he does not comfort her with words, because he knows that looking away would be shame, and words would be intrusion. He simply sits.
- Emma drinks the coffee. It is black, strong, exactly what she needs. She says: *"The boys are upstairs. They know something is wrong. We told them last night that Samira is in a cold place with bad people and we are trying to find her. We did not use the words KGB or spy or Siberia. They are five. They know she is missing. They know we are scared. Kalvin has been wetting the bed. Eliot has been angry. They are whispering to each other. They have not called for us. That is worse than crying."* Rémi nods. He says: *"I will go up. I will not tell them anything unless you have told me what you want them to hear. But I will go up and be present. I will sit with them. I will eat breakfast with them. I will read to them. I will be the person they can be normal with, because I am not carrying what you are carrying. That is my role today. Normalcy."* Jessica says: *"You cannot give them normalcy. Their sister is missing."* Rémi says: *"Normalcy is not denial. Normalcy is the frame around the picture. They need to know the picture is real. They also need to know that the frame is still holding it. That is what I can give. The frame."* Emma reaches across the table. She touches his hand. It is the first time she has ever touched him in a gesture of need. She says: *"Thank you."* He says: *"Do not thank me yet. Wait until I have managed a morning with two frightened five-year-olds. Then thank me if they are still sane."* He smiles, a small, tired smile. It is the first smile in the kitchen since yesterday. It does not fix anything. But it reminds them that smiling is still possible.

### 4. Upstairs (8:00 AM)
- Rémi climbs the stairs. He knows the way. He has been in the mansion before, but usually for parties, for dinners, for occasions. He has never gone upstairs uninvited. Today, he is invited. He knocks on the boys' door. He says, in the gentle voice he uses with pediatric patients: *"Eliot. Kalvin. It's Rémi. May I come in?"* There is a silence. Then Eliot's voice, small but firm: *"Come in."* Rémi opens the door.
- The boys are sitting up in their pushed-together beds, still in their pajamas. Eliot is wearing a superhero shirt. Kalvin is wearing a shirt with a whale on it. They look at him. Eliot's eyes are defiant. Kalvin's eyes are hidden behind *Mira*. The room smells faintly of urine. Rémi notices. He does not comment. He walks in and sits on the floor between their beds, cross-legged, a posture that makes him smaller, less threatening, more accessible. He says: *"Your mothers are very tired. They had a long night talking about Samira. They asked me to come and have breakfast with you. I brought croissants. But I ate one downstairs. I hope you do not mind. I was hungry."* Eliot says: *"You are not supposed to eat our croissants."* Rémi says: *"You are right. I apologize. I will bring more tomorrow."* Eliot nods, accepting the apology. This small exchange—violation, apology, acceptance—establishes a normal rhythm. It is the first normal thing that has happened in the room in twelve hours. Kalvin lowers *Mira* slightly. His eyes are visible.
- Rémi says: *"I also brought something."* He opens his medical bag. He takes out the moose and the beaver. He holds them up. *"These are for patients at the hospital. But I thought you might need them more today. They are very good at listening. They do not talk back. They do not ask hard questions. They just sit with you. Would you like to meet them?"* Eliot considers. He says: *"What are their names?"* Rémi: *"They do not have names yet. They are waiting for you to name them."* Eliot takes the moose. He examines it. He says: *"His name is Doctor. Because he is a doctor, like you."* Rémi smiles. *"Doctor Moose. I like it."* Kalvin reaches for the beaver. He holds it against his chest, beside *Mira*. He whispers: *"Beaver."* Rémi says: *"Just Beaver?"* Kalvin nods. He does not elaborate. Rémi accepts it. *"Beaver it is."
- Rémi looks at the world map on the wall. He sees the pins. He says: *"I see you have a map. I like maps. They help me know where I am. Do you know where I am from?"* Eliot: *"Halifax."* Rémi: *"Yes. But before that, I am from a small town in New Brunswick. Very small. Smaller than this house. Do you know where New Brunswick is?"* Eliot points to the map. He points to the wrong spot, near Ontario. Rémi says: *"Close. It is here."* He points correctly. Eliot nods. Rémi: *"It is near the ocean. Like Halifax. I grew up with the ocean. Do you like the ocean?"* Eliot: *"Samira took us to the beach. In Brazil."* Rémi's face changes, barely, a microexpression of grief that he suppresses. He says: *"I remember. I was there too. We built sandcastles. Samira helped you build a very tall one. And then a wave knocked it down. And she helped you build another one."* Eliot: *"She is good at building."* Rémi: *"She is. And she is good at coming back. She has always come back. From Russia. From camp. From school. She always comes back."* Eliot says: *"This time she is not coming back."* The words are flat. They are not a question. They are a statement. Rémi looks at him. He does not lie. He says: *"This time is harder. This time she is farther away. And there are people who are not letting her come back. But your mothers are working very hard. And Sahara is working very hard. And Andrei is working very hard. And I am going to work hard too. We are all working to help her come back."* Eliot: *"Is she dead?"* The question is the one he has been holding. Rémi looks at Kalvin. Kalvin is very still, listening, his eyes wide. Rémi says: *"I do not think so. I am a doctor. I know about bodies. And I know about your sister. She is very strong. She is very stubborn. She is very loved. And people who are strong and stubborn and loved do not die easily. They fight. And I believe she is fighting."* Eliot: *"Fighting who?"* Rémi: *"People who want to keep her. People who do not understand that she belongs to her family. People who think they can take what is not theirs."* Eliot: *"They are bad people."* Rémi: *"Yes. They are bad people. And bad people do not always win. Good people win too. But it takes longer. And it is harder. And it requires good people to help each other. That is why I am here. That is why your mothers are talking to everyone they know. That is why we are all helping."* Eliot nods. He accepts this. It is not a complete answer, but it is an honest one. He says: *"I want to help too."* Rémi: *"You are helping. You are being strong for Kalvin. You are being a good brother. That is the most important job right now. And Kalvin is helping too. He is being strong for you. That is what brothers do. That is what twins do."* Kalvin whispers, his first words since Rémi entered: *"Samira is a twin."* Rémi: *"She is. And Sahara is her twin. And they are helping each other too. Even though they are far apart. Just like you and Eliot."* Kalvin nods. He pulls Beaver closer. He looks at Rémi with something that is not trust, not yet, but the possibility of trust. And in a child's world, the possibility of trust is a lifeline.

### 5. The Mothers Collapse (8:30 AM, Downstairs)
- While Rémi is upstairs, Jessica and Emma sit at the kitchen table. They have finished the croissants. They have finished the coffee. They are not talking. They are listening. They can hear the low murmur of Rémi's voice, Eliot's responses, Kalvin's occasional whispers. They cannot hear the words, but they can hear the tone. The tone is steady. The tone is safe.
- Jessica says: *"He is good."* Emma says: *"He is better than good. He is present."* Jessica: *"I should have called him sooner."* Emma: *"You called him when we needed him. That is what matters."* Jessica: *"Do you think the boys will be okay?"* Emma: *"I think they will be okay because he is with them. And I think they will be angry at us for not being with them. And I think they will forgive us because they are our children and they love us. And I think we will need to forgive ourselves, which is harder."* Jessica puts her head on the table. She is too tired to hold it up. Emma strokes her hair. They sit like this for ten minutes, a tableau of exhaustion, two surgeons in their prime reduced to a kitchen-table collapse by the simple weight of being human.
- At 8:45, Emma says: *"I am going to bed. I cannot function. I will sleep for two hours and then I will wake up and I will call Rachel. And then I will call Mike and Terri Anne. And then I will make a plan. But first, I will sleep."* Jessica lifts her head. She says: *"I will sleep too. In the guest room. I do not want to disturb you."* Emma: *"No. We sleep together. In our bed. We hold each other. We sleep. We wake up together. We do this together."* Jessica nods. They stand. They hold each other as they walk to the stairs. They move like elderly women, shuffling, leaning on each other. They pass the boys' door. They hear Rémi reading *The Runaway Bunny* in his warm, steady voice. They hear Eliot correcting him on a word. They hear Kalvin laughing, a small, rusty sound, the first laugh in days. They pause. They listen. Then they continue to their bedroom. They close the door. They lie down. They hold each other. They sleep. It is not peaceful sleep. It is the sleep of the exhausted, the sleep of the grieving, the sleep of the angry. But it is sleep. And sleep is mercy.

### 6. The School Run (8:45 AM)
- After the mothers have gone upstairs, Rémi helps the boys finish dressing. He finds their backpacks by the mudroom door—Eliot's decorated with superhero stickers, Kalvin's with whale stickers, both packed the night before by Emma with lunches, water bottles, and a change of clothes. He remembers, then, that it is a school day. The boys attend a morning kindergarten program at a nearby school. He asks them: *"Do you want to go to school today?"* Eliot straightens his shoulders and says: *"Yes. I want to be normal."* Kalvin clutches Beaver and *Mira* and says: *"I don't want to leave Mira."* Rémi kneels to his level. *"You can leave Mira here. She will guard your bed. ## Thematic Notes

- **The frame vs. the picture:** Rémi's metaphor of normalcy as the frame around the picture is central to the scene's philosophy. Children in crisis need to know the crisis is real (the picture), but they also need to know that the structure of their lives is intact (the frame). The frame does not deny the picture. It holds it.
- **Asking as strength:** Jessica and Emma's call to Rémi is framed as a moral and emotional breakthrough, not a failure. The scene argues that the inability to ask for help is a form of pride that can become a form of neglect, and that asking is an act of love for the children who need the help more than the parents need to appear self-sufficient.
- **The donor father's role:** The scene deepens Rémi's position in the family from peripheral donor to active caregiver without erasing the mothers' primacy. He is explicitly not a co-parent. He is a "node" in the network. The language of networks—nodes, connections, frames—allows the family structure to expand without collapsing into traditional hierarchy.
- **The urine as unspoken grief:** Kalvin's bedwetting is handled without comment by Rémi, which is a crucial choice. The scene avoids making the child feel shame by having the adult absorb the shame silently. This is a model of how to handle children's regression in crisis: acknowledge it practically, do not dramatize it, and allow the child to retain dignity.
- **The stuffed animals as transitional bridges:** The moose and beaver are new transitional objects, offered by Rémi, which allow the boys to form a new attachment without abandoning the old one (*Mira*). This models the idea that grief can be accompanied by growth, and that new anchors do not replace old ones but support them.
- **The whispering as warning:** The boys' whispered twin conversation at the start of the scene is a diagnostic sign of their emotional state. They have retreated into the twin bond as a fortress, which is both protective and isolating. Rémi's entry into the room is a gentle breach of that fortress, not a destruction of it. He respects the bond while offering an outside connection.
- **The exhaustion as authenticity:** Jessica and Emma's physical collapse—sleeping in scrubs and sweatpants, shuffling like elderly women, holding each other to walk—is a visual representation of the cost of the crisis. The scene does not glamorize their resilience. It shows the limits of resilience, and argues that reaching those limits is not a failure but a necessary phase of a long struggle.
