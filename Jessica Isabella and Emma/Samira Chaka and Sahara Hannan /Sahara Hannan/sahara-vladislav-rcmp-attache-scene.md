# The Embassy Back-Channel

**Time:** Winter ~2060, late evening
**Location:** St. Petersburg, Russia (Sahara Hannan’s apartment)

The knock on Sahara Hannan’s apartment door was not the heavy, authoritative pounding of the state, nor was it the frantic, staccato rap of an emergency. It was a precise, measured knock—three distinct taps that signaled calculation.

Sahara froze. She was standing by the small radiator, a mug of chamomile tea warming her hands. She checked her continuous glucose monitor. It read 182. Elevated. It had been elevated for weeks. 

Across the room, Vladislav looked up from his laptop. For the past hour, he had been deep in the supply-chain matrices, tracking the anomalies of "Candidate Four" to a military freight contractor servicing a blank spot seventy kilometers north of Krasnoyarsk. 

Vladislav stood up. He walked to the door, placing his body firmly between the entrance and Sahara. He checked the peephole.

"It is a man," Vladislav murmured over his shoulder, his Russian quiet and tight. "Civilian clothes. Suit and overcoat. He is holding a Canadian passport up to the lens."

Sahara’s heart hammered against her ribs. *A Canadian passport.* 

She set her tea down on the radiator cover. "Let him in."

Vladislav hesitated, his hand hovering over the deadbolt. "Sahara—"

"If he wanted to hurt us, he wouldn't be showing his passport," she said, her voice steady. "Let him in."

Vladislav unlocked the door and pulled it open. 

The man standing in the hallway was in his late forties, with a weathered face and sharp, perceptive eyes. He stepped inside quickly, closing the door behind him and locking it with a fluidity that suggested he was accustomed to securing perimeters. He did not immediately speak. He swept his gaze across the room—taking in the cramped layout, Vladislav’s defensive posture, the open laptop displaying freight manifests, and finally, Sahara. 

"Sahara Hannan Key," the man said in English. His accent was distinctly Canadian, carrying the flat, pragmatic vowels of the Prairies. 

"Yes," Sahara replied, stepping out from behind Vladislav. 

The man reached into his breast pocket. He produced a leather wallet and opened it, displaying a badge. "I am Inspector David Laurent. I am the Royal Canadian Mounted Police attaché stationed at the Canadian Embassy in Moscow. I apologize for the late hour and the unannounced arrival, but my instructions were to make contact off the books."

Sahara stared at the badge. "Instructions from who?"

"Your mothers, Jessica Isabella Key and Emma Fritz, have been in extensive contact with our liaison office in Halifax," Inspector Laurent said softly. "And your grandfather, Mike Key, established channels with our directorate long before he passed. When a Key daughter goes missing overseas, the RCMP listens."

Vladislav remained tense. "If you are from the Moscow Embassy, why are you in St. Petersburg?"

"Because Moscow is currently a surveillance panopticon regarding the Bolshoi Academy," Laurent answered, switching flawlessly to Russian to address Vladislav. "Your brother Andrei is already being watched. If I walked into Samira's apartment, or approached him at work, I would burn him instantly. You two are slightly further from the epicenter. And according to Halifax, you are the ones generating the intelligence." 

He looked back at the laptop. "I understand you've been tracking supply lines to Facility 12."

Vladislav’s jaw tightened. "I don't know what you're talking about."

Laurent offered a small, grim smile. "It’s alright, Vladislav. You are doing exactly what you should be doing. I am not here to shut you down. I am here to tell you that you are not operating in a vacuum." He turned his attention entirely to Sahara. "Sahara. The Canadian government is aware of the 'Candidate Four' anomaly. We know your twin sister was taken to a classified state facility under the guise of a cultural seminar. We know it is a violation of international law."

"Then do something," Sahara said, her voice trembling with sudden, fierce hope. "Go get her. Send the military. Send diplomats. Go to Krasnoyarsk."

"We can't," Laurent said gently. "Not overtly. If Canada lodges a formal, public diplomatic protest right now, the Russian state-security apparatus will initiate an immediate lockdown. Facility 12 is off the grid. If we force their hand publicly before we know her exact condition and exact physical location inside that compound, they could move her. Or worse, they could decide she is a liability."

The phantom cold Sahara had been carrying in her left hand for weeks suddenly spiked, crawling up to her elbow. She wrapped her arms around herself. "So you are doing nothing."

"No," Laurent corrected. "We are establishing a back-channel. Through you."

He reached into his pocket again and pulled out a small, encrypted burner phone. He set it on the small kitchen table. 

"The intelligence you and Vladislav are gathering—the freight manifests, the guard rotations, the caloric intake numbers you're pulling from the medical shipments—is vital. It is local, it is civilian, and it flies under the radar of counter-intelligence. My office is analyzing satellite imagery of the Krasnoyarsk region, but we cannot see inside the buildings. You are mapping the inside from the outside." 

Laurent pointed to the phone. "That line is secure. It connects directly to my desk at the Embassy, and it bridges to Halifax. When you find something, you send it. When we find a crack in their security—a guard who can be bought, a transport truck we can intercept—we will tell you. You are no longer two kids with a laptop. You are part of a coordinated intelligence effort."

Sahara stared at the phone. It looked remarkably unremarkable. Just a piece of black plastic. 

"My mothers," Sahara whispered. "Are they okay?"

Laurent’s expression softened. "They are exhausted. They are terrified. But they are formidable. They asked me to tell you something, to prove I was real."

Sahara looked up. "What?"

"They said to tell you that Eliot drew a map, and Kalvin sent Mira."

The breath caught in Sahara’s throat. Tears, hot and sudden, spilled over her eyelashes. She knew exactly what it meant. The little boys. The map of stars. The stuffed rabbit. The family was holding the line.

"Okay," Sahara said, swiping the tears away with the back of her hand. She stepped forward and picked up the encrypted phone. "Okay. We have the supply manifests for the winter gear shipped to Facility 12 last week. We know how many women's coats they ordered."

Inspector Laurent nodded approvingly. "Good. Send it to me." He turned toward the door. "I will not come back here unless it is an absolute emergency. Keep your heads down. Do not do anything heroic. Just do the math."

"We will find her," Vladislav said quietly from the door.

"I know you will," Laurent said. "And when you do, we will be the ones who open the door."

He stepped out into the hallway, and the door clicked shut behind him, leaving Sahara and Vladislav alone again. But the room no longer felt like a desperate, isolated island. The phone sat on the table, heavy with the weight of an entire nation's resources, and the unbreakable will of the Key family.
