# THE FIRST NURSING

The mid-January afternoon was bright with the kind of sharp, cold sunlight that only exists in an Ontario winter. Inside Room Four at McMaster Children’s Hospital, the heat was turned up, making the air thick and still. The nasogastric tube had been removed an hour ago, leaving Lÿsa Mandy’s face clear for the first time in six weeks. 

Fallon Glenda sat in the heavy nursing rocker. She had spent the last month and a half as a creature of the industrial milking machine, her life measured in collection bottles and sterilized syringes. Today, the machine was silent in the corner.

"Take your time, Fallon," the nurse whispered, helping her adjust the pillow. "She’s strong enough now. She just needs to find her rhythm."

Fallon brought Lÿsa Mandy close. The baby was heavier now—over five pounds—and her dark eyes were open, looking up at her mother with a quiet, unfocused intensity. Fallon’s hands were steady, a confidence born of forty-five days in this ward. 

When Lÿsa Mandy finally latched, the world seemed to contract until it was only the size of the two of them. 

The sound was small—a tiny, rhythmic *pull-swallow-breath*. It was the sound Fallon had been imagining since Rio, the sound she had fought for through the judgment of the doctors and the hum of the pumps. There was no plastic tube between them. No syringe. Just skin on skin.

As the first minute passed, Fallon felt a sudden, sharp prickling behind her eyes. 

She didn't try to stop it. The tears began to spill over, hot and silent, dripping onto the white blanket wrapped around her daughter. They weren't tears of grief or pain; they were a massive, tectonic release of everything she had been carrying. 

She cried for the twelve-year-old girl in Rio who thought her life was over. She cried for the fear of the last two months, for the nights spent pumping milk in the quiet of the Carlisle mansion, and for the sheer, overwhelming weight of the love she felt for the tiny person in her arms.

"She's doing it," Fallon whispered, her voice breaking. "She's really doing it."

Mike was standing by the window, his reflection caught in the glass against the bright Hamilton sky. He didn't move toward her. He knew better than to break the circle they had finally made. He watched his thirteen-year-old daughter, her shoulders shaking with quiet sobs as she nursed her child, and he felt a profound, aching pride. 

Fallon leaned her forehead against Lÿsa Mandy’s soft, dark hair. The smell of the baby—uncomplicated by the antiseptic scent of the hospital—filled her senses. 

"I've got you," Fallon whispered through her tears, her voice finally finding its strength. "I've got you, Lÿsa. I'm never letting go."

In that quiet NICU room, the machine was gone, the tube was a memory, and the "liquid gold" was finally flowing where it was meant to go. Fallon Glenda wasn't just a provider anymore. She was a mother, whole and anchored, and for the first time since Rio, she felt as if she could finally breathe.
