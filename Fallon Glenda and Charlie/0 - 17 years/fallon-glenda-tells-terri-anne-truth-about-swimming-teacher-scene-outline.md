# Fallon Glenda Tells Terri Anne the Truth About Her Swimming Teacher — Scene Outline

> **Scope:** The morning after the patio — after Kiley Rose's bisexual disclosure and the Brazilian barbecue. Fallon Glenda, twelve years old, finds Terri Anne alone and tells her the truth about what her swimming teacher has been doing to her. She has been carrying this alone. Watching her sixteen-year-old sister tell a terrifying truth and be met with nothing but love gave her something she did not have the night before: the belief that she can survive saying it out loud.
>
> **Time:** The morning of May 2, 2045. The family is living in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil. Fallon Glenda is twelve years old. She is not yet visibly pregnant — or barely so, enough to have noticed something is wrong in her own body but not enough for others to have seen it yet.
> **Place:** The Key family residence in Rio de Janeiro. Mike and Terri Anne's bedroom — the most private room in the house, the one place that belongs entirely to her parents. Fallon knocks on the door before the rest of the household is awake.
> **Characters present:** Fallon Glenda, Terri Anne Williams-Key. Mike Key enters partway through.
> **Mood:** Dread → confession → the moment the weight transfers from child to parents → the beginning of justice.

---

## 1. The Night Before — What Fallon Witnessed

- **What she saw:** From the patio, Fallon Glenda watched Kiley Rose tell Mike and Terri Anne that she is bisexual. She heard Kiley Rose ask — in the most frightened voice Fallon has ever heard her use — *"Do you still love us?"* And she watched Mike crouch in front of Kiley Rose and say *nothing you are changes what you are to us.* She watched Terri Anne take Kiley Rose's hand and not let go.
- **What it did to her:** Fallon Glenda has been carrying her secret in her body for weeks — maybe longer. She has watched her swimming teacher's behaviour become something she does not have words for. She has felt things change in her body that she does not fully understand. She has been hiding in oversized clothes and staying quiet and telling herself that if she does not say it out loud, it is not fully real.
- **The crack:** Kiley Rose said it out loud. And the sky did not fall. The family did not shatter. Mike and Terri Anne did not turn away. They moved *closer.* That is the thing Fallon could not stop thinking about as she lay in bed last night, unable to sleep: they moved closer.
- **The decision:** She does not decide to tell Terri Anne in the way you decide to do something you want to do. She decides the way you decide to pull out a splinter — because leaving it is worse than the moment of pain. She gets up before the others. She finds Terri Anne.

## 2. Finding Terri Anne — The Bedroom

- **The setting:** Early morning. The Rio house is quiet. Light comes in through the bedroom window — the particular Rio morning light, warm and directional, falling across the bed. The city is already audible outside: traffic, birds, the distant sound of something that is always happening in this city.
- **Fallon's knock:** She stands at the bedroom door for a long moment before she knocks. Long enough that she almost turns back. Long enough that she has to remind herself of what she saw on the patio last night — Mike crouching in front of Kiley Rose, Terri Anne's hand not letting go — before her knuckles find the wood.
- **The knock itself:** Soft. Almost too soft. But Terri Anne hears it.
- **Terri Anne:** She is awake — she always sleeps lightly after one of her children has carried something, and last night she carried a great deal. She hears the knock and is on her feet before she has fully decided to be. She opens the door.
- **What she sees:** Fallon in the hallway. Small in her oversized clothes. Her face already gone — already crumpled, already wet, already past the point of holding it together. She has been crying since before she knocked. Maybe since before she got out of bed. She is not trying to hide it. She has run out of the capacity to hide anything.
- **No words yet:** Fallon does not say anything. She cannot. She opens her mouth and what comes out is not a sentence — it is a sound, the kind that comes from somewhere below language, from the place where children keep the things they have been carrying alone for too long. Her whole body shakes with it.
- **Terri Anne does not ask a single question.** She opens the door wider and pulls Fallon in — not gently, not carefully, but completely, the way you hold someone who is falling. She wraps both arms around her and brings her fully into the room, into the warmth, away from the hallway. She holds her the way she held her when she was small enough to be carried. She does not let go.
- **The crying:** Fallon cries the way she has not let herself cry in weeks — fully, without management, without the careful self-containment she has been practising every day since this began. It is loud and it is ugly and it is the most honest thing she has done in a very long time. Terri Anne holds her through all of it. She does not tell her to breathe. She does not tell her it is going to be okay. She just holds her and lets the crying be as large as it needs to be.
- **Mike:** He wakes to the sound of it. He is on his feet within seconds — a father who has been jolted from sleep by a child's distress more than once and whose body responds before his mind catches up. He sees Terri Anne holding Fallon in the middle of the room, Fallon shaking, and he closes the bedroom door behind him quietly and sits on the edge of the bed nearby. He does not try to take Fallon from Terri Anne's arms. He simply puts one hand on Fallon's back — warm, steady, there — and stays.
- **The three of them:** For a long time — longer than feels comfortable, longer than the crying seems like it should last — they stay like this. Terri Anne holding Fallon upright. Mike's hand on her back. The Rio morning light coming through the window. The city outside, indifferent. In here, nothing moves until Fallon is ready.
- **When the crying begins to ease:** Not because it is finished. But because the body runs out of the immediate fuel for it. Fallon's breathing becomes ragged and then slower. She is still shaking slightly. Terri Anne moves her to the edge of the bed and sits beside her, one arm still around her shoulders. Mike stays close. Nobody rushes her.
- **The first words:** When Fallon finally speaks, it is not the thing she came to say. It is the thing underneath the thing — the part that has been pressing on her chest since the middle of the night:

  *"I couldn't sleep. I didn't sleep at all. I was awake the whole night worrying."*

  Her voice is wrecked — hoarse from crying, thin from exhaustion. She is not looking at Terri Anne when she says it. She is looking at her own hands in her lap.

  *"I kept thinking about — I kept thinking I had to tell you. And then I kept thinking I couldn't. And then I'd think I had to again. All night. I didn't sleep at all."*

  She says it twice — *I didn't sleep at all* — the way you say something twice when you need someone to understand not just the fact of it but the weight of it. The whole night. Alone in her room. Lying in the dark with something enormous pressing on her from the inside, going back and forth between *I have to say it* and *I can't say it* until the Rio morning came through her window and she got up because lying there any longer was unbearable.

- **Terri Anne's response to this:** She does not address the sleeplessness as a symptom. She receives it as a confession — because that is what it is. Fallon is not telling her she is tired. She is telling her how long she has been carrying this alone. How many hours. How much it cost her just to get to this door.

  Terri Anne tightens her arm around Fallon's shoulders. She says:

  *"I know. I know, sweetheart. You're here now."*

  And then, after a moment, very quietly:

  *"You don't have to carry it past this room."*

- **Mike, from the bed:** He has heard it. He says nothing yet. But something happens in his face that he cannot entirely control — not anger, not shock, but the particular expression of a parent whose heart is breaking and who is not going to let his child see it break. His jaw tightens once. His eyes go bright for a moment — just a moment — and then he looks down at his hands and breathes, slowly and deliberately, until he has it back.

  His heart is breaking. It is breaking because she is twelve years old and she lay awake all night alone worrying, going back and forth in the dark, too frightened to sleep, too frightened to speak, carrying something no child should ever have to carry — and she did all of that alone in a room down the hall from him while he slept. He was right there. He was right there the whole night and he did not know. She was twelve years old and alone and worrying and he did not know.

  He will not let her see that. He makes the choice, the same choice he always makes in these rooms: she needs his steadiness more than she needs his grief. His grief is real and enormous and he will feel it fully later, somewhere she cannot see him. Right now he lifts his eyes back to her face and keeps them there and keeps his voice even.

  He files the image away — his daughter awake all night, worrying herself hollow — and it goes into the same place the rage goes. The place he will open later, in the other room, when he makes the call. Not now. Now he stays.
- **Fallon comes in fully now.** She sits on the edge of the bed, Terri Anne's arm still around her. She wraps her own arms around herself beneath Terri Anne's. She looks at the floor. She is trying to find the beginning of the sentence.

## 3. The Telling

- **How it starts:** Not with the worst of it. The way these things always begin — at the edge of the truth, approaching it obliquely:

  *"Mum. My swimming teacher — "*

  She stops. Starts again.

  *"Something happened. With my swimming teacher. Some things happened."*

- **Terri Anne's stillness:** She does not move. She does not gasp. She does not fill the pause with a question. She becomes the particular kind of still that Terri Anne becomes when she is receiving something that requires all of her — the nurse's stillness, the mother's stillness, the stillness of a woman who has learned that the most important thing she can do right now is not react in a way that makes this harder to say.

  She says, very quietly:

  *"I'm listening, Fallon."*

- **What Fallon tells her:** She tells it unevenly, the way children tell things they have never said before — in pieces, not in order, some parts louder and some parts barely audible. She tells Terri Anne what the swimming teacher did. Not everything at once. But enough. The things he said. The things he did when they were alone. The way he made her feel like it was a secret that belonged to both of them. The way he made her feel like it was her fault somehow, like she had done something to cause it, like saying it out loud would make everyone see something wrong in her.

- **The body part:** Somewhere in the telling, Fallon's hands move to her abdomen — not deliberately, just the way a body moves toward what it is trying to say when words are not sufficient. She does not say the word *pregnant* yet. But her hands say it. And Terri Anne, who is a nurse and a mother and has been watching Fallon for weeks, understands.

  She does not say it either. Not yet. She lets Fallon finish.

- **Fallon's guilt, arriving:** As it always does — the guilt comes in after the telling, not before. She has been carrying guilt in silence, but saying it out loud makes it louder somehow:

  *"I should have — I don't know. I should have told someone sooner. Or I should have — "*

  Her voice breaks.

  *"I don't know why I didn't just —"*

- **Terri Anne moves:** She comes around the table or the counter — she does not stay across it. She puts her hands on Fallon's hands and waits until Fallon looks up at her.

  *"You are telling someone now. You are telling me now. That is what matters."*

  A beat.

  *"None of what he did is your fault. None of it. Do you hear me? You are twelve years old."*

## 4. Mike — Already There

- **He is already in the room:** Mike woke to the sound of Fallon crying in the hallway — a father whose body responds to a child's distress before his mind catches up. He closed the bedroom door behind him and put his hand on Fallon's back and stayed. He has been here from the beginning.
- **His role in the crying:** He does not try to take Fallon from Terri Anne. He does not ask questions. He sits on the edge of the bed with one hand steady on her back and he waits. He has held space for a crying child before. He knows the only thing to do is stay.
- **When the crying eases and the telling begins:** Mike does not move closer when Fallon starts to speak. He stays where he is — near, but not pressing. He waits for Terri Anne to look at him. She does. A single look between them — the look of two parents who have been here for each other through everything — and then he looks at Fallon.

  *"Hey."*

  Just that. Quiet and entirely present.

  *"Take your time."*

- **Fallon telling it with both of them there:** Her voice is uneven and she stops and starts. But Mike's presence — steady on the bed beside them, his hand no longer on her back but close, available — means she does not have to say it twice. He hears it the first time, along with Terri Anne. His face does not change. He has made a decision about what his face will show her.
- **Mike's internal state:** Beneath the absolute stillness of his face, he is experiencing something enormous — the same protective fury that will later become the phone call to his OPP contacts, the international coordination, the arrest. He is already, in the back of his mind, dismantling the swimming teacher's life. But Fallon cannot see any of that. What she sees is her father, steady and present and not angry at her. He has made a decision about what his face will show her, and he keeps it.

## 5. The Word Neither of Them Has Said Yet

- **The moment:** After the abuse is named, after Terri Anne has said *none of this is your fault* and Mike has said *I hear you and I believe you* — there is a pause in which something else is present in the room, unnamed.
- **Fallon's hands go to her abdomen again.** This time deliberately. She looks down at them.

  *"There's something else,"* she says.

  Terri Anne and Mike wait.

  *"I think — I think I might be —"*

  She cannot finish it. But she does not need to. Terri Anne is already beside her, and Mike is already leaning forward, and neither of them look at her with anything except the same complete, absolute attention they have held for this entire conversation.

  *"I think something is happening to my body. I don't know how to say it."*

- **Terri Anne's response:** Steady. Clinical and maternal at once.

  *"We will find out together. Today. Whatever it is, we will know, and we will handle it together. Not you alone. Us."*

- **Mike:** He reaches across and covers Fallon's hands — both of them — with one of his. He does not say many words. He says:

  *"You are not in trouble. You did nothing wrong. We are going to take care of you."*

  He says it the way he says things he means with his whole body.

## 6. After — The Room Holds Them

- **The immediate aftermath:** The three of them stay in the bedroom for a while. The Rio morning continues outside — traffic, birds, the city indifferent to what has just happened in this room.
- **Fallon's body:** She is exhausted in the way people are exhausted after they have been holding something too long and have finally put it down. Not better yet — not healed, not resolved — but lighter in the particular way that comes from not being alone with a thing anymore.
- **Terri Anne's first practical step:** She tells Fallon quietly that they will make an appointment today. That she will be with her for every part of it. That nothing will happen without Fallon knowing exactly what is happening and why.
- **Mike's first step:** He does not say what he is going to do about the swimming teacher. Not yet — not in this room, not in front of Fallon, because this moment belongs to her and he will not fill it with his anger. But when he stands, he puts his hand briefly on the top of Fallon's head — the gentlest gesture, the one that says *I am here, you are safe, I am going to take care of this* — and then he walks to the other room. And the call he makes is not gentle at all.
- **The sisters:** They are not awake yet, most of them. The household has not started its day. Kiley Rose is still asleep, five-month-old Brandi Jessica and Pandora Paige beside her. The younger ones — Sabrina Bryna, Andrea Jennifer, Ashley Erin — will wake to a morning that looks ordinary, not knowing that two things have shifted in this family in the space of twelve hours.
- **Closing image:** Terri Anne and Fallon Glenda on the edge of the bed. The Rio light through the bedroom window. Fallon's hands in her lap, Terri Anne's hands over hers. Outside: the city, the water, the particular sound of Rio in the morning. Inside: a twelve-year-old girl who has finally put down something she was never supposed to carry. And a mother who is already making sure she never has to carry it alone again.

## 7. The Connection to the Night Before

- **What Kiley Rose gave Fallon:** Not a plan. Not courage in any grand sense. Just proof. Proof that you can say the most frightening thing you have ever said in this kitchen, in this family, and the people you love will move toward you instead of away. Fallon watched it happen from the patio. She lay awake thinking about it. And in the morning she got up and she found Terri Anne.
- **The parallel:** Kiley Rose asked *do you still love us?* Fallon Glenda does not ask the question in words. But she is asking the same thing — and the answer is the same. It is always the same in this family. It has always been the same.
- **What Fallon does not know yet:** She does not know that Mike is already on the phone. She does not know the swimming teacher's life is already ending. She does not know that the Key family's full weight — financial, legal, international — is already moving on her behalf. She only knows that she told the truth and the world did not end.

  That is enough for this morning.

## 8. Character Work

### Fallon Glenda (12)
- **What she carries in:** The abuse. The fear. The guilt the teacher placed in her. The physical changes she does not yet have words for. The belief — placed in her by him — that saying it will make things worse.
- **What changes it:** Kiley Rose's courage the night before. Not because Kiley Rose said anything to Fallon. She did not. She did not even know Fallon was watching. But the image of Mike crouching in front of Kiley Rose — *nothing you are changes what you are to us* — is the thing that got Fallon out of bed this morning.
- **Her voice in this scene:** Uneven, quiet, sometimes barely there. She does not have the vocabulary for what has been done to her. She tells it in pieces. That is enough.
- **What she does not yet know:** That she is pregnant. That the family will rally around her completely. That Charlie will one day choose her and Lÿsa Mandy both. She only knows that she is twelve and she is terrified and she told the truth.

### Terri Anne Williams-Key
- **Her mode:** The nurse's stillness deployed as the most radical form of love. She does not fill Fallon's silences. She does not rush the telling. She moves from across the counter to beside Fallon at exactly the right moment — close enough to hold, far enough to let Fallon breathe.
- **What she does not do:** She does not cry in front of Fallon. Not yet. She will cry later — when she is alone, or with Mike, when Fallon cannot see it. Right now she is the place Fallon is putting the weight down, and she stays solid for that.
- **Her key line:** *"You are telling someone now. You are telling me now. That is what matters."*

### Mike Key
- **His entrance:** He does not interrupt. He reads the room from the doorway and sits down quietly and says *take your time.* This is who he is — the man who crouched on the beach, at the Capoeira studio door, on the patio the night before. He comes down to the level of whoever needs him.
- **His breaking heart:** When Fallon says she could not sleep — that she was awake the whole night worrying, alone in her room, going back and forth — Mike's heart breaks. Not quietly, not neatly. It breaks the way a parent's heart breaks when they understand that their child spent the night suffering in a room down the hall and they did not know. He was right there. The whole night. And she was alone with it.
  He does not let Fallon see it break. His jaw tightens. His eyes go bright for one unguarded moment. Then he looks down at his hands and breathes and chooses — the same choice he always makes — to give her his steadiness instead of his grief. His grief is real and it is enormous and he will feel it fully later. Not here. Not in front of her. She needs him solid. He stays solid.
- **The internal/external split:** Inside, two things are happening at once: his heart is breaking for his daughter, and his mind is already in motion — already dismantling, already calling, already making sure this man will never be near a child again. Outside, he is entirely still and entirely Fallon's. He has made a choice about which one she sees. He gives her the steadiness. He saves everything else for the other room.
- **His hand on her head:** The gentlest gesture in the scene. One hand, briefly, on the top of Fallon's head before he goes to make the call. It is the only place his grief surfaces in this room — compressed into that one touch, that one second of his palm against her hair. Everything he cannot say. Everything his heart is doing that she cannot see. In that gesture, and then he goes.

## 9. Thematic Motifs

- **One sister's truth making room for another's:** Kiley Rose did not know she was giving Fallon anything. She was just telling her own truth and asking to be loved. But the truth she told made a space in the family's air — a space that said: *you can say the thing. You will be held.* Fallon walked into that space the next morning.
- **The hands:** Fallon's hands on her abdomen — the body knowing before the mouth can say it. Terri Anne's hands over Fallon's — receiving without pulling away. Mike's hand on Fallon's head — protection expressed as tenderness.
- **The bedroom door:** Fallon could have waited for a more ordinary place — the kitchen, the hallway, a moment when Terri Anne was already up and moving. She knocked on the bedroom door instead. That choice matters. The bedroom is the most private room in the house. Bringing her truth there says: *I need you fully. Not the version of you that is already dressed and moving. The version that is just you.*
- **The call in the other room:** Mike's phone call to his OPP contacts is not shown. It does not need to be. The scene ends before it — just the gesture of his hand on Fallon's head and then his departure. The reader knows what follows. Fallon will learn it later. Right now she does not need to know. Right now she only needs the kitchen and Terri Anne's hands.
- **Rio outside:** The city goes on outside the window. Indifferent and alive. The same city where the abuse happened. The same water Fallon swam in. One day, much later, the family will figure out how to give her the water back. But that is not today. Today is the kitchen. Today is the truth being told.
