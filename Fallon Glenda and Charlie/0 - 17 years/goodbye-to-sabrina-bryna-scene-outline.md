# SCENE OUTLINE: GOODBYE TO SABRINA BRYNA

**Characters:** Fallon Glenda (17), Charlie (18), Lÿsa Mandy (4–5), Sabrina Bryna (19–20).
**Setting:** Sabrina Bryna’s accessible penthouse near the University of Toronto — a full-floor residence in a mid-rise building in the Annex or Yorkville, with a private elevator, roll-under workspaces, and wide sightlines across the city toward campus.
**Timeframe:** A quiet afternoon in June, one month before Fallon, Charlie, and Lÿsa Mandy leave for Stanford in July.

## THE CONTEXT
Fallon Glenda (17), Charlie (18), and Lÿsa Mandy (4–5) are making a deliberate round of farewells before the July move to California. Sabrina Bryna (19–20), Fallon’s older sister by two and a half years, is now living in Toronto and attending the University of Toronto. She is not yet with William; she is fully independent for the first time, navigating U of T, her Vaughan mansion still waiting for her, and a life that has not yet given her Carlota Delicia or the years of trial ahead.

This goodbye is a sister-to-sister farewell between two people who once stood together in the hardest season of Fallon’s life — the Rio disclosure, the pregnancy, the flight home — and who now must accept that Fallon is the one leaving the fortress first.

## THE SCENE

**Arrival at Sabrina’s Penthouse:**
*   Fallon, Charlie, and Lÿsa Mandy arrive at Sabrina’s building on a bright June afternoon. This is not their first visit; they have been to the penthouse before for dinners and Sunday afternoons, and the staff at the private elevator know them by sight. The elevator rises quietly to the top floor, opening directly into Sabrina’s foyer.
*   Charlie carries a small canvas tote bag containing Lÿsa Mandy’s wooden building blocks — the familiar set she will not travel without on a long visit. They are not a toy for the occasion; they are a regulation. If Lÿsa Mandy has her blocks, she can tolerate new emotional weather.
*   Sabrina is waiting for them — not because she has been watching, but because she knows their habits. She is wearing a U of T sweatshirt, her hair pulled back, and she stands with the slight asymmetry of her CP gait. She does not try to hide it. She signs *Come in* with the sharp, fluid grace that ASL gives her hands.
*   Charlie knows the penthouse now. He does not need to study the accommodations; he already knows the floating desk, the ergonomic chair, the wide doorways, the visual doorbell signal, the closed-captioning always on the television. He also knows the small leather-bound notepad and pen that live on the kitchen counter — the tools Sabrina uses when she wants precision with a hearing person who is not yet fluent in her language. He understands, without comment, that this is a space engineered for Sabrina’s full agency.

**Lÿsa Mandy and Sabrina:**
*   Lÿsa Mandy enters with her usual sensory caution, taking in the room’s geometry before stepping fully inside. She has known Sabrina all her life and is comfortable with her signing, but she is also four years old and easily overwhelmed by new spaces.
*   Charlie sets the canvas tote on the floor near the sofa and lets Lÿsa unpack the blocks herself. She does this methodically, sorting them by colour before she will build anything. The ritual is visible to Sabrina, who recognizes the same need for order she has learned to build into her own life.
*   Sabrina does not demand a hug. She crouches carefully — a physical effort she does not hide — and signs *Hello, Lÿsa* at Lÿsa’s eye level. Lÿsa Mandy studies her hands, then responds with a small, approximate sign of her own: *Hello, Aunt Sabrina.*
*   Sabrina smiles. She does not correct Lÿsa’s signing. She reaches into her pocket and pulls out a small, smooth stone she has kept on her desk — a polished river rock from the Humber River — and places it in Lÿsa’s palm. Lÿsa examines it, turns it twice, compares it to the smallest wooden block in her set, and then slips it into the tiny purse she has insisted on bringing. It is accepted.

**The Penthouse — A Familiar Space:**
*   The penthouse is not new to them, but today the rooms feel different because the purpose has changed. They move through the open-plan living area toward the bank of windows that looks south toward campus. Sabrina signs as she goes, pointing out the desk where she does her U of T business-management coursework, the kitchen with its roll-under workspace, the same maple tree visible in the courtyard below.
*   On the wall beside the fireplace is a framed photo of the three youngest Key sisters on the Carlisle mansion lawn: Andrea Jennifer, Fallon Glenda, and Ashley Erin. It was taken the summer before the Rio move, before Fallon’s body began to change, before the disclosure, before the family learned how to hold what she was carrying. Sabrina is not in the frame. She was away that weekend, or behind the camera, or simply already living in a different orbit; the photo belongs to the three girls who were still children together in the same way.
*   Fallon pauses at the photo. She is twelve in it, standing between Andrea Jennifer and Ashley Erin, already wearing a loose shirt even though the day is warm. Her younger sisters are laughing at something off-frame. Fallon is not. She is looking down at her own hands, as if she is already trying to hide something.
*   Sabrina signs, standing beside her: *You were carrying it even then.*
*   Fallon signs back, not looking away from the photo: *Yes.*
*   Sabrina: *I did not see it in time. I am sorry.*
*   Fallon turns to look at her. She signs: *You saw it when it mattered.*
*   They do not need to say more. The Rio years hang in the room for a moment, then settle.

**Charlie and Sabrina Alone in the Kitchen:**
*   Fallon takes Lÿsa Mandy to the bathroom, leaving Charlie and Sabrina alone in the kitchen for a beat. Sabrina is pouring iced tea into glasses at the counter, her body braced against the cabinetry for stability.
*   Charlie knows his ASL is not strong enough for a conversation of any depth, and Sabrina prefers precision over charitable interpretation. On the counter beside her sits a small leather-bound notepad and a pen she keeps there for exactly this purpose. Charlie picks up the pen and writes: *Thank you for having us. We did not mean to take your whole afternoon.*
*   Sabrina reads it, sets down the glass pitcher, and writes back: *Family is not an afternoon. It is the whole calendar.* She passes him a glass of iced tea and takes the pen again: *Your ASL is improving. But paper is faster for what I need to say.*
*   Charlie nods, grateful not to be patronized. They write back and forth in the compact, deliberate silence Sabrina prefers for serious things.
*   Sabrina writes: *Fallon is fragile in places you cannot see. She will not tell you. She will try to carry everything so you do not have to.*
*   Charlie writes: *I know. I am not going anywhere.*
*   Sabrina studies him with the same analytical gaze she uses in boardrooms. She writes: *I am not asking you to stay. I am asking you to be safe. That is harder.*
*   Charlie writes: *I will be safe for her. I will be patient. I will learn.*
*   Sabrina holds his gaze for a long moment. Then she nods — the same slow, serious nod their father recognizes — and writes one last line: *Then you are already family.* She passes him the glass.

**The Real Conversation (Living Room):**
*   The four of them sit in the living room. Lÿsa Mandy sits on the floor among her building blocks, arranging the smooth stone and a small set of keys she has taken from her purse into a line, perfectly parallel. She stacks two red blocks on either side of the stone like gateposts. The block structure is small, precise, and entirely private — a four-year-old’s way of securing the room while the adults talk.
*   Sabrina signs to Fallon: *You are scared to leave.* It is not a question.
*   Fallon signs back: *Yes. But I want to go.*
*   Sabrina: *You think you are leaving us behind.*
*   Fallon: *I am.*
*   Sabrina shakes her head. She signs firmly: *You are carrying us forward. That is different. We do not stay behind. We stay *here* — and you take the family with you. That is how it works.*
*   Fallon feels the words land. Sabrina has always spoken in facts. There is no sentimentality in what she signs, which is why it feels true.
*   Sabrina continues: *Lÿsa is your first gift. Charlie is your second. California is your third. Do not apologize for any of them.*
*   Then she adds, with a wryness that only Fallon recognizes: *Besides, you are not the first to go. Jessica Isabella is in Halifax. Rachel Darcy is in Philadelphia. Kiley Rose is in New York City. We are already scattered. You are just the next one to make the map bigger.*
*   Fallon looks at the framed photo again — the three youngest sisters on the lawn, all of them still in one place. Then she looks back at Sabrina and understands: the family has not stopped being a family; it has simply stopped being a single address.

**The Gift:**
*   Sabrina reaches into the desk drawer and pulls out a small leather-bound notebook, plain and unmarked. She signs: *For you. For medical school. For hard days.*
*   Fallon opens it. Inside, on the first page, Sabrina has written — in her careful, slightly uneven handwriting — a single line: *The truth is always told in love. Remember that when the truth is about yourself.*
*   Fallon closes the notebook and holds it against her chest. She does not cry. Sabrina does not cry. They do not need to.
*   Sabrina signs: *When you are a doctor, you will see people who are carrying what you carried. You will know what to say because you lived it.*
*   Fallon signs: *I am not a doctor yet.*
*   Sabrina smiles: *You have always been one. You just did not have the words.*

**The Goodbye:**
*   They stand at the penthouse door. The private elevator is waiting. The afternoon light is gold through the maple tree visible in the courtyard below. Lÿsa Mandy has finished dismantling her block structure and has returned the stone to her purse. She stands holding Charlie’s hand, ready to go.
*   Fallon hugs Sabrina carefully, aware of her sister’s CP and the way her body braces for balance. Sabrina hugs back with the strength of her upper body, her arms tight and deliberate.
*   Sabrina signs against Fallon’s shoulder: *Go build it. We will be here when you come back.*
*   Fallon pulls back and signs: *I love you.*
*   Sabrina signs back: *I know. Same.* Then she steps back slightly, holding Fallon’s gaze, and signs the phrase the sisters have adopted for these leavings — the one they have already used for Jessica Isabella in Halifax, for Rachel Darcy in Philadelphia, for Kiley Rose in New York City, and now, finally, for Fallon Glenda: *Farewell and good luck, Fallon Glenda.*
*   Charlie signs a simple, careful *Goodbye, Sabrina* — the one sign he has made sure is clean — and then gives her a small, respectful nod. She returns the nod and an approving smile.
*   Lÿsa Mandy, unprompted, signs *Goodbye* with both hands and offers the stone back to Sabrina — a reciprocation, a four-year-old’s understanding that gifts can flow both ways.
*   Sabrina takes the stone, crouches again, and places it back in Lÿsa’s palm. She signs: *Keep it. For California.* Lÿsa Mandy nods and puts it away.
*   Charlie picks up the empty canvas tote — the blocks have already been packed away by Lÿsa in the exact order she unpacked them — and slings it over his shoulder.

**The Departure:**
*   Charlie buckles Lÿsa Mandy into her car seat. Fallon stands on the sidewalk for a moment, looking up at the penthouse windows.
*   Sabrina is visible behind the glass, her hand raised in the still, clear shape of *I love you* in ASL. Then she shifts her hand into the signed phrase the sisters have adopted since the family began spreading across cities — the phrase they say to whichever sister is leaving home: *Farewell and good luck, Fallon Glenda.* Fallon raises her own hand and signs it back to her.
*   As Charlie pulls the car into Toronto traffic, Fallon opens the notebook to the first page again and reads Sabrina’s words. She understands, for the first time, that leaving is not the same as being left behind — and that Sabrina, who once stood beside her in Rio, is now standing still so that Fallon can walk forward.

## WHY THIS SCENE MATTERS

*   **The Rio Shadow Resurfaces and Is Released:** The photograph and the brief exchange about Sabrina knowing first recall the trauma without requiring the scene to reenact it. This goodbye becomes a way to acknowledge what Sabrina witnessed and to let it rest.
*   **Sabrina’s Communication Authority:** The scene foregrounds Sabrina’s fluency and physicality in ASL while also respecting her choice of medium. With Fallon and Lÿsa she signs; with Charlie she shifts to paper and pen because precision matters more than performance. Charlie’s willingness to meet her on her terms — without pretending to be fluent — is the test he quietly passes.
*   **Lÿsa Mandy’s Inclusion:** Lÿsa is not a prop in the scene; she brings her blocks, builds a small private structure, receives a gift, and reciprocates it. The neurodivergent child and the deaf aunt understand each other through order and touch rather than spoken language.
*   **Fallon’s Permission to Become a Doctor:** Sabrina names Fallon’s future medical vocation not as an ambition but as a continuation of her survival. This reframes the Stanford move as a healing trajectory.
*   **The Sisterhood Shift:** Sabrina is the older sister, but Fallon is the one leaving the family orbit first. The scene honors that reversal — Sabrina staying, Fallon going — without making either role feel like a loss.
*   **Farewell and Good Luck:** The phrase is what the sisters say to whichever sister is leaving home. Jessica Isabella in Halifax, Rachel Darcy in Philadelphia, and Kiley Rose in New York City have already heard it; now it is Fallon Glenda’s turn. The phrase is not cold — it is the Key family’s way of blessing the one who is walking forward, without pretending that distance is easy.
