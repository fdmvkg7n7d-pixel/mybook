# THE FIRST HOLD — CHRISTMAS AT MCMASTER

The drive from the Carlisle mansion to Hamilton was a blur of grey sky and snow-dusted fields. Inside the SUV, the atmosphere was quiet but charged with an electric tension. It was Christmas morning, 2046, but the holiday felt secondary to the singular promise the doctors at McMaster had made the night before: today, thirteen-year-old Fallon Glenda would finally be allowed to hold her daughter.

Mike pulled the car up to the main entrance of McMaster Children’s Hospital. He looked over at Fallon, who was sitting on the edge of her seat, her small hands gripped tightly around the strap of her diaper bag.

"Go on ahead, Fal," Mike said, his voice warm and steady. "I’ll find a spot in the garage and meet you at the ward doors. Don't wait for me."

Fallon didn't hesitate. She climbed out of the car and hurried through the sliding glass doors. The hospital lobby was uncharacteristically empty, the silence of Christmas Day hanging heavy in the air. She moved through the hallways with a determined stride, her heavy winter coat swishing against her legs, her mind focused entirely on Room Four of the Neonatal Intensive Care Unit.

She reached the heavy, restricted double doors of the NICU. Her heart was pounding, a frantic, hopeful rhythm. She reached for the handle, her hand trembling with twenty-four days of anticipation.

"Excuse me. You can't be back here."

The voice was sharp and clinical. Fallon stopped, her hand frozen. A doctor in a white coat was stepping out of the nursing station, a clipboard tucked under his arm. He looked at Fallon—at her youth, her height, and the way she looked in her oversized coat—and his expression immediately hardened into one of professional dismissiveness.

"Children aren't allowed on the restricted wards, miss," the doctor said, his voice final. "The waiting area is back toward the elevators."

Fallon felt a hot flush of embarrassment and panic. "I'm... I'm here for my daughter," she said, her voice shaking. "Lÿsa Mandy Key. The nurses told me I could hold her today."

The doctor let out a short, incredulous breath. He stepped into the hallway, physically blocking her path to the door. "Your daughter? Miss, you look like you’re barely thirteen. You’re far too young to be a mother. I don't know who told you that you could be back here, but you’ve clearly misunderstood. You need to return to the waiting room and wait for an adult."

"I am the mother!" Fallon snapped, her eyes filling with angry tears. "I’ve been here every single day! I know she's in Room Four! Please, just let me in!"

"I'm not going to argue with you," the doctor replied, his tone growing cold. "It’s a restricted area for a reason. Now, please move before I have to call security."

Fallon backed against the wall, her breath coming in shallow, jagged gasps. She felt small, exposed, and utterly defeated by the weight of the doctor’s judgment. 

The chime of the elevator echoed down the hall. The doors slid open, and Mike Key stepped out. He saw the scene instantly—his daughter cornered against the wall, her face red and wet with tears, and the doctor standing over her with a posture of superior dismissal.

Mike didn't run. He walked. But the sound of his heavy boots on the linoleum was like the approach of a storm. 

"Is there a problem here?" Mike asked. 

His voice wasn't a roar; it was a "soft yell"—a low-frequency, vibrating intensity that made the air in the hallway feel thick with pressure. He walked right between Fallon and the doctor, his presence forcing the younger man to take an instinctive step back.

The doctor straightened his coat, trying to regain his footing. "Sir, this young girl is trying to force her way into the NICU. She claims to be the mother of one of the infants, which is obviously—"

"Which is obviously the truth," Mike interrupted. His voice dropped an octave, vibrating with a quiet, dangerous fury that made the doctor’s eyes widen. "She is the mother. She has every legal and moral right to be on this ward, in that room, and with her daughter. Do you understand me?"

The doctor blinked, the sheer force of Mike’s authority hitting him like a physical blow. "But sir, she’s a child. Surely the hospital protocol regarding—"

"I don't give a damn about your interpretation of protocol," Mike hissed, his face inches from the doctor’s. He wasn't shouting, but his words were like stones. "I see a mother who has more courage in her heart than you have in your entire body. I see a girl who has fought for twenty-four days to see her child get strong. And I see a doctor who is using his own petty prejudices to bully a thirteen-year-old girl on Christmas morning."

"I... I was only—"

"You were standing between a mother and her child," Mike said, his voice a low, thunderous growl. "She is a Key. She is the mother. And if you ever speak to her like that again, or if you ever make her feel like she doesn't belong at her daughter’s side, I will personally ensure that your career at this hospital is a very short one. Now, open the door."

The doctor reached out with a trembling hand and pulled the door open. He didn't say another word as Mike guided Fallon through the threshold.

Mike kept his arm around Fallon’s shoulders as they walked past the rows of incubators. "Don't let him into your head, Fal," he whispered, his voice finally softening back to the father she knew. "You’re the mother. That’s the only truth that matters in this building."

When they reached Room Four, the nurse was already waiting. She smiled warmly, sensing the emotion in the room. "Merry Christmas, Fallon. Ready for your gift?"

The nurse carefully disconnected the non-essential leads. She reached into the incubator and lifted the tiny, four-pound bundle. Lÿsa Mandy was wrapped in a soft white blanket, her dark hair a faint smudge against the fabric.

Fallon sat in the rocker, her arms shaking. 

When the nurse placed Lÿsa Mandy against her chest, the hallway and the doctor and the judgment all vanished. There was only the warmth of the baby’s skin through the thin cotton of Fallon’s blouse, the tiny, bird-like heartbeat against her own, and the weight she had been waiting a lifetime to carry.

Lÿsa Mandy let out a small, soft sigh, her tiny head settling into the hollow of Fallon’s shoulder. 

Fallon looked up at Mike. He was standing by the window, his back to the room, looking out at the Hamilton skyline. His shoulders were shaking. He was crying.

"Merry Christmas, Lÿsa," Fallon whispered, her tears finally spilling over as she tucked the blanket tighter around her daughter. "I’ve got you. I’ve finally got you."
