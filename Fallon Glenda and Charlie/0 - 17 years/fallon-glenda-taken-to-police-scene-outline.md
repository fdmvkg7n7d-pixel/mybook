# Fallon Glenda Is Taken to the Police — Scene Outline

> **Scope:** Later the same day — after the bedroom disclosure, after the doctor's appointment. Mike and Terri Anne take Fallon Glenda to the Delegacia da Criança e do Adolescente — the Brazilian child and adolescent protection police unit in Rio de Janeiro. Fallon is terrified. She goes anyway. That is the bravest thing she has ever done.
>
> **Time:** May 2, 2045 — the same day as the bedroom disclosure and the doctor's appointment. Afternoon. Fallon Glenda is twelve years old.
> **Place:** The Key family car. The streets of Rio de Janeiro. The Delegacia da Criança e do Adolescente — the child protection police unit.
> **Characters present:** Fallon Glenda, Mike Key, Terri Anne Williams-Key. The investigating officer/delegada. A child protection specialist or social worker present during Fallon's statement.
> **Mood:** Dread → the long corridor → the room where it is said again → something shifts when she is believed.

---

## 1. Before They Leave — At the House

- **The decision:** After the doctor's appointment, they come home. The other girls are still in the house — Kiley Rose managing the twins, the younger ones in their orbits. Mike takes Fallon and Terri Anne into a quiet room and tells Fallon, plainly and without softening it, what needs to happen next.

  *"We need to go to the police today, Fallon. Today — not next week, not when it feels easier. Today, while everything is clear. You don't have to do anything except tell them what you told us this morning. That's all."*

- **Fallon's reaction:** She goes very still. Not the numb stillness of the morning — this is a different kind. The stillness of someone who has heard a thing they already knew was coming and hoped would somehow not come.

  *"Do I have to?"*

  She asks it quietly. Not defiantly. Just the question of a child who is exhausted and frightened and has already said the hardest thing she has ever said twice today and does not know if she has a third time in her.

- **Mike's answer:** He does not say *yes, you have to.* He does not say *it will be fine.* He says:

  *"I will be there. Mum will be there. You will not say a single word alone. And what you say today is what stops him from doing this to another child."*

  A long pause. Fallon looks at her hands.

  *"Okay,"* she says. The word she has been using all day. Still too large. Still the only one she has.

- **Terri Anne:** She helps Fallon wash her face and change her shirt. She does not make it a production — just the quiet, practical care of a mother who knows that feeling slightly more like yourself before you walk into a hard room matters. She brushes Fallon's hair back and holds her face for a moment and says nothing. Fallon leans into her hands just slightly, like a child half her age would. Terri Anne lets her.

---

## 2. The Drive

- **The car:** For the third time today, the three of them in the car. Rio outside. The afternoon light different now from the morning — lower, warmer, the city shifting toward the long golden hour of a Brazilian afternoon. Fallon watches it go past.
- **The silence:** Nobody fills it. There is nothing to say that would make this easier and all three of them know it. Mike drives. Terri Anne sits in the back with Fallon. Fallon's hands are in her lap.
- **What Fallon is thinking:** She is thinking about what it will feel like to say it again. She said it in the bedroom — to her parents, who love her, who held her while she said it. She said it, in a different way, in the clinic, to a doctor who was careful and kind. Now she will say it to a police officer she has never met, in a language that is not her first language, in a room she has never been in, about a thing she has been carrying alone for weeks.

  She is terrified.

  She does not say so. But Terri Anne feels her hand trembling and covers it with her own.

- **Mike, at a red light:** He looks at Fallon in the mirror. He says:

  *"You are the bravest person in this car."*

  Fallon shakes her head slightly — the instinctive refusal of a child who does not feel brave.

  Mike:

  *"Brave doesn't mean not scared. It means scared and going anyway."*

  Fallon looks at him in the mirror for a moment. Then she looks back out the window. But something in her posture changes — just slightly. Just enough.

---

## 3. Arriving at the Delegacia

- **The building:** The Delegacia da Criança e do Adolescente — the child and adolescent protection unit of the Rio de Janeiro civil police. It is a specific unit, established for exactly this kind of case. It is not a general police station. The people here are trained for this.
- **Walking in:** Mike holds the door. Fallon walks in between her parents. The building is official and institutional — the smell of a government building, fluorescent light, the sound of phones and keyboards. Fallon's eyes move quickly around the room, cataloguing exits and faces the way a frightened person does.
- **At the front desk:** Mike speaks — his Portuguese fluent, his manner the particular authority of a man who is used to rooms turning toward him, deployed here entirely in his daughter's service. He gives Fallon's name. He explains why they are here. He is calm and direct. He does not perform grief and he does not perform anger. He states facts. The officer at the desk makes a call.
- **Waiting:** They sit in a row of chairs along the wall. Fallon in the middle. She is very still. She looks at the floor. Mike does not take out his phone. Terri Anne does not take out hers. They sit with her, in the same unoccupied way they have sat with her all day — not performing patience, just actually there.
- **A child protection officer comes to meet them:** The delegada or a specialist from the unit — a woman, experienced, with the particular manner of someone who has done this many times and has not become hard from it. She introduces herself to Fallon first. Not to Mike. Not to Terri Anne. To Fallon. She crouches slightly to be closer to her eye level and says:

  *"Fallon. I'm going to talk with you today. Your parents will be nearby the whole time. You set the pace. If you need to stop, we stop. Okay?"*

  Fallon looks at her. Assessing — the way she has always assessed new adults in new spaces. Then she nods.

---

## 4. The Room

- **The child interview room:** It is not an interrogation room. It has been designed for children — softer lighting, chairs that are not intimidating, a table that is not a barrier. There may be a small window. There is a box of tissues on the table. Someone has thought carefully about this room.
- **Who is present:** Fallon, the delegada or child protection specialist, a second officer for the official record, and Terri Anne — sitting slightly to the side, close enough to be felt, not so close that she is between Fallon and the officer. Mike waits outside the room. This is his choice, not a requirement — he decides that the room belongs to Fallon and Terri Anne, and that his presence might change the temperature of it. He sits in the hallway with his elbows on his knees and he waits.
- **Mike in the hallway:** He sits very still. Outside the room, he allows his face to do what it could not do in front of Fallon all day. His jaw is tight. His eyes are somewhere between the floor and the wall. He is thinking about his twelve-year-old daughter on the other side of that door, saying it for the third time today. His heart, which has been breaking since the bedroom this morning, breaks a little further. He does not move. He breathes. He waits.

---

## 5. Fallon's Statement

- **Beginning:** The officer does not start with the incident. She starts with Fallon — who she is, what she likes, where she goes to school. Small questions that are not small — they are the officer building a relationship, establishing that this is a conversation between two people, not an interrogation of a suspect. Fallon answers in the careful, slightly formal way of a child speaking to an authority figure in a second language. Her Portuguese is functional. Where it fails her, Terri Anne quietly supplies a word.
- **The officer's approach:** Patient. Unhurried. She uses open questions — *tell me about your swimming teacher, tell me what happened.* She does not lead. She does not put words in Fallon's mouth. She lets Fallon find the shape of it herself, at her own pace.
- **Fallon beginning to speak:** She is still frightened. Her voice is thin at first — almost as thin as it was at the bedroom door this morning. She starts at the edge of it, the way she has learned to start — approaching the worst of it obliquely, circling closer. The officer waits without pressure.

  When she gets to the hardest parts, she stops. Looks at her hands. Looks at Terri Anne. Terri Anne nods — just once, just the smallest nod that says: *I am here. Keep going.*

  She keeps going.

- **Being believed:** This is the moment that matters more than anything in the room. The officer does not look skeptical. She does not pause in a way that suggests doubt. She listens to Fallon the way the delegada has listened to many children — with the particular attention of someone who knows that what a child says in this room is the hardest thing they will ever say, and that how it is received will shape what they believe about themselves for years.

  At one point the officer says, simply:

  *"I believe you, Fallon."*

  Fallon looks up. She has been looking at her hands, at the table, at anywhere but the officer's face. Now she looks up.

  *"I believe everything you have told me."*

  Something happens in Fallon's face. It is not relief exactly — it is the specific expression of a person who has been carrying a thing they were told they might not be believed about, and who has just been told: *I see it. I see you. You are telling the truth and I know it.*

  She does not cry. She has cried everything out. But her shoulders come down about an inch — an involuntary, physical release of something she has been holding since before this morning.

- **The end of the statement:** The officer thanks Fallon. She tells her she has been very brave. She tells her what happens next — that the information Fallon has given will be used to investigate the swimming teacher, that her statement is now part of the official record, that someone from the unit will be in contact with her parents. She asks Fallon if she has any questions.

  Fallon thinks for a moment.

  *"Will you arrest him?"*

  The officer:

  *"That is our job now. Yes."*

  Fallon nods. She looks at the table. Then she looks back up.

  *"Good,"* she says. Quietly. With a twelve-year-old's complete and uncomplicated certainty.

---

## 6. Coming Out of the Room

- **Mike in the hallway:** He hears the door. He is on his feet before it opens. Fallon comes through first, Terri Anne behind her. Mike looks at his daughter's face — reading it the way he has always read his children's faces, faster and more completely than any other text he has ever studied.
- **What he sees:** Exhaustion. The particular hollowness of someone who has given everything they had three times in one day. But also — something else. Something fractionally different from the face that went into the room. Not peace. Not resolution. Something smaller and more real than either of those: the face of someone who has been believed.
- **Mike and Fallon:** He does not say anything immediately. He opens his arms and she walks into them — not the way she did in the bedroom, not the falling-apart crying, but something quieter. She puts her face against his chest and he puts both arms around her and they stand in the hallway of the Delegacia da Criança e do Adolescente while the institutional world continues around them, and he holds her for as long as she needs.
- **What he says, eventually:** Into the top of her head. Very quiet.

  *"You did it. You did it, Fallon."*

  She does not say anything. She holds on.

- **Terri Anne:** Her hand on Fallon's back. The three of them in the hallway. Complete.

---

## 7. Leaving

- **Walking out:** The same way they walked in — Fallon between her parents, Mike holding the door. The Rio afternoon outside. The light is lower now, the city beginning to shift toward evening.
- **Fallon on the pavement:** She stops again, the way she stopped outside the clinic. She looks at the street. The traffic, the colour, the ordinary world.

  Then she takes a breath — a real one, the kind that goes all the way down — and she starts walking toward the car.

- **The drive home:** Quieter than the drive there. A different kind of quiet — not the dread-quiet of the morning, not the numb-quiet of the clinic ride. Something that does not yet have a name but is not the same as what it was before.
- **Fallon, partway home:** She is looking out the window. Then, without turning from it:

  *"She said she believes me."*

  Not a question. Not seeking confirmation. Just saying it out loud. Putting it in the air of the car where Mike and Terri Anne can hear it.

  Terri Anne:

  *"She does. And so do we. We believed you the moment you said it."*

  Fallon nods slowly. She keeps looking out the window. The city goes past. She is very tired. She is twelve years old. She told the truth three times today and was believed every time.

- **Closing image:** The car moving through the Rio evening. Fallon's head resting against the window. Her eyes open but not quite focused — somewhere between here and sleep. Mike and Terri Anne in the front, quiet, steady. The swimming teacher's world is already ending — the police have the statement, Mike's calls will begin the moment they are home, the full force of everything the Key family can bring to bear is already in motion. Fallon does not know the details of that yet. She only knows she told the truth and she was believed. For tonight, that is everything.

## 8. Character Work

### Fallon Glenda (12)
- **Terrified and going anyway:** She asked *do I have to?* She said *okay.* She got in the car. She sat in the room. She said it a third time. That is the whole arc of her courage in this scene — not the absence of fear, but the decision to move through it.
- **Being believed:** The moment the officer says *I believe you* is the emotional centre of the scene. Fallon has been carrying the fear of not being believed alongside everything else — placed there by the swimming teacher's manipulation, the natural self-doubt of a child who has been told by an adult that she is complicit. Being believed by a stranger in an official room dismantles something the teacher built in her.
- **"Good."** Her response when the officer says *yes, we will arrest him.* One word, quiet, utterly certain. It is the most powerful thing Fallon says in this scene.

### Mike Key
- **The hallway:** He sits outside the room and lets his face do what it cannot do in front of Fallon. He is still. He is breaking. He waits. When she comes through the door he is on his feet and his arms are open and that is all.
- **"You did it."** Two words into the top of her head. The simplest truth he could offer. She did it. She went into the room. She said it. She did it.
- **What he does not do:** He does not go into the interview room. He decides that the room belongs to Fallon and Terri Anne. He sits in the hallway and he waits. That restraint — that choosing to be in the hallway rather than in the room — is one of the most important things Mike does in this scene.

### Terri Anne Williams-Key
- **The nod:** In the interview room, when Fallon looks at her in the hardest part — Terri Anne nods. Just once. Just the smallest nod. *I am here. Keep going.* That nod carries the whole weight of the morning — the bedroom, the clinic, everything — compressed into a single gesture.
- **"We believed you the moment you said it."** On the drive home. The most important sentence in the car. Not *the police believe you* — though that matters. *We believed you.* First. Before anyone else. The moment she said it.

### The Officer / Delegada
- **Function:** She is the institutional face of being believed. She receives Fallon's statement with the particular skill of someone trained to hear the hardest things children say without flinching, without doubting, without rushing. Her *I believe you* is not performed — it is the specific, deliberate language of a professional who knows exactly what those words do for a child in that room.
- **She crouches to Fallon's level.** Like Mike always does. This is not accidental — it is the right way to be with a frightened child.

## 9. Thematic Motifs

- **Saying it a third time:** Fallon has said it in the bedroom, in the clinic, and now here. Each time the room is different — her parents' bedroom, a doctor's office, a police unit. Each time the people receiving it are different. Each time she is believed. The repetition is not redundant — it is the architecture of how truth is documented and how a child slowly begins to believe that what happened to her is real, and that it was wrong, and that she is not to blame.
- **Brave doesn't mean not scared:** Mike's line in the car. The definition of courage that Fallon needs — not the absence of fear, but the decision to go anyway. She goes anyway. Three times today.
- **"Good."** Fallon's response to *we will arrest him.* One syllable. Twelve years old. Absolute.
- **The nod:** Terri Anne's nod in the interview room. Everything in one gesture.
- **Mike in the hallway:** He chose the hallway. He chose to give Fallon the room. Sitting in the hallway with his face doing what it could not do all day is the scene's image of what it costs a father to hold himself together for his child — and what it means to keep holding anyway.
- **She said she believes me:** Fallon says it on the drive home — not asking for confirmation, just putting it in the air. Trying it out. Letting it be real.

## 10. Continuity Notes

- This scene takes place on **May 2, 2045** — the same day as the bedroom disclosure and the doctor's appointment. It is the third major event of the day for Fallon.
- The **Delegacia da Criança e do Adolescente** is the Brazilian civil police unit specifically responsible for crimes against children and adolescents. In Rio de Janeiro this is the DCAV (Delegacia da Criança e do Adolescente Vítima). The interview process follows Brazilian child protection protocols.

- **Fallon says it three times today:** the bedroom, the clinic, the police. Each time she is believed. This is deliberate — the Key family's ethic of radical truth means there is no gap between disclosure and action.
- **Kiley Rose** is at home with the twins and the younger sisters. She does not know the full picture yet. The sisters will be told together, when Fallon is ready — established in the previous scene.
