# Lÿsa Mandy Receives Fallon Glenda and Charlie's Reply Letter at French Woods

## Placement

**When:** During Lÿsa Mandy's second summer / second season at French Woods, after Fallon Glenda and Charlie have received her first letter in Amsterdam and written back.

**Where:** French Woods, during the first half of her two-session / six-week stay.

---

The letter came after lunch.

The day had already been full enough to make time feel bent. Ballet in the morning, then swimming, then the noisy blur of the dining hall, where everything smelled like sun-warmed wood and wet towels and food being carried too quickly on plastic trays. Lÿsa Mandy had been trying very hard not to keep asking whether mail was coming yet.

Trying did not mean succeeding.

She had asked once at breakfast.
She had asked once after ballet.
Then she had decided not to ask again because asking again would make it take longer somehow.

By the time the mail basket appeared, she was sitting cross-legged on the edge of the bench outside the cabin, still damp at the nape of her neck from the pool, holding one sneaker by its lace and not putting it on.

Mara Feld came up the path with the stack of envelopes in one hand.

"Mail call," she said in that calm, plain way of hers that made everything sound manageable.

The girls around Lÿsa Mandy shifted instantly — leaning forward, calling out names, pretending they did not care and then caring very much. Mara sorted through the envelopes with practiced fingers.

"Tessa. Mina. Rachel."

Lÿsa Mandy kept still.

Then:

"Lÿsa Mandy."

She stood up so fast the loose sneaker dropped to the ground.

Mara smiled a little and handed over the envelope. "From home."

It was her mother's writing on the outside — or at least the version of home-writing that belonged to Fallon Glenda and Charlie together. Lÿsa Mandy could tell the difference, even if she could not have explained it. The front was neat and clear. Not crowded. Not rushed.

"You can read it here if you want," Mara said, "or somewhere quieter."

Lÿsa Mandy looked at the envelope, then at the cabin door, then back at Mara.

"Can I do both?" she asked.

Mara's mouth tipped at one corner. "Yes. That's a very Lÿsa answer."

So she took the letter inside first, because opening important things in front of everybody felt too wide, and then sat on her bunk with one knee folded under her and the envelope balanced carefully against her leg.

The sounds of the cabin kept moving around her — drawers opening, two girls arguing over a brush, somebody laughing too loudly and then apologizing for laughing too loudly. It did not matter. The letter made its own small room.

She opened it carefully so it would not tear.

Dear Lÿsa Mandy,

We loved your letter so much.

First, thank you for telling us real things and not just saying "camp is good." We could tell from your letter that you are really there and really doing your summer, and that made us very happy.

Lÿsa Mandy blinked once and sat a little straighter.

That was good. That was exactly right. She had wanted them to know real things.

She kept reading.

We smiled at the part about the pool looking smaller and then feeling the same once you got in. That sounded exactly like you.

We are very glad that a girl from last summer remembered you, and we are especially glad your counselor remembered that you like things explained regular and not too fast. We knew that part would matter to you.

Her mouth pressed into something that was almost a smile, but deeper than one. It was always strange, in a not-bad way, when adults understood the right detail. Not the giant, shiny version of a feeling. The actual part.

Not too fast.
That helped.

She read on.

We are also very happy that you are doing **ballet and gymnastics both**. We would never ask you to choose when you love them equally, and we are proud of you for saying that clearly.

A tiny burst of satisfaction went through her.

Good.
They understood that too.

She had not wanted anyone to act like loving both was confusion. It was not confusion. Ballet and gymnastics were different, and because they were different, she needed both. Ballet made certain parts of her feel long and exact, like lining things up properly. Gymnastics made other parts feel strong and bright and direct. Swimming was something else entirely. Swimming was not choosing anything. Swimming was just what happened when her whole body stopped arguing with itself.

She kept reading.

We hope swimming still feels good in your body, especially after a long day.

Amsterdam is beautiful. The water here is very pretty, and the canals shine in the evening. We thought you would like looking at them, even though they are different from swim water.

Our hotel room is nice. It has a window we like, and we talked about you when we read your letter sitting on the bed.

That part made her pause.

She read it again.

Our hotel room is nice. It has a window we like, and we talked about you when we read your letter sitting on the bed.

The picture of it came to her whole: her mother on a hotel bed, Charlie beside her, the letter in one of their hands, both of them reading every word and not skipping or only pretending to read. She could feel the rightness of that all the way down to her stomach.

Not because she was homesick exactly.
Not because she wanted to be there instead.
But because she wanted the two things to be true at once:

that she was at camp,
and that they still had her.

The letter gave her both.

You do not need to worry about us. Your job is to have your camp summer. Our job is to be proud of you, write back, and listen to every detail you want to tell us.

We love you very much.

Love,
Mommy and Charlie

When she finished, she stayed sitting for a minute with the page open in her lap.

Outside, someone shouted about swim caps. A screen door banged. The whole camp kept going at its regular speed, but inside the cabin the letter had made time hold still just long enough for her to fit herself back together around it.

Tessa leaned over from the next bunk.

"Did they write back?"

Lÿsa Mandy nodded.

"Is it good?"

She considered this with appropriate seriousness.

"Yes," she said. "It is regular-good. Not weird-good."

Tessa accepted that immediately, because Tessa had known her since last summer and understood that categories mattered.

"Okay," she said. "That means actually good."

Lÿsa Mandy looked back at the page.

A moment later, Mina appeared in the doorway and said, "We're going to the field in a minute if you want to come," and then noticed the letter. "Oh. Home mail."

"Yes," Lÿsa Mandy said.

Mina came closer but not too close. "Did they say Amsterdam is pretty?"

"Yes," said Lÿsa Mandy. "The water is pretty but not swim water."

Mina laughed. "That sounds fair."

Lÿsa Mandy folded the letter once, then unfolded it again because she wanted one more look at the line about ballet and gymnastics both. She liked that they had written it exactly. Not ballet and gymnastics too. Not some version that flattened it. Both.

Mara passed by the open cabin door and glanced in.

"Everything all right?"

Lÿsa Mandy lifted the page slightly.

"They wrote back."

"I can see that."

"They said I can have my camp summer."

Mara nodded as if this were not sentimental at all, only factual. "That sounds wise of them."

Lÿsa Mandy thought so too.

She folded the letter carefully this time along the proper lines and slid it into the pocket of her bag, not because she was finished with it, but because she wanted to carry it through the rest of the day. The page felt like proof of something she had not known how to explain before.

At home, they knew her.
At camp, she could still be herself.
Neither truth erased the other.

She stood up and put on the sneaker she had abandoned.

"Are you coming?" Mina asked from outside.

"Yes," Lÿsa Mandy said.

Then, because the letter had settled something good and strong inside her, she added, "I have to write them back later though."

"About what?"

Lÿsa Mandy stepped into the afternoon light.

"About how ballet and gymnastics are still equal," she said, as if this were obviously urgent. "And maybe about swimming too."

Tessa, already halfway down the path, called back, "You always say maybe swimming too and then it ends up being a lot about swimming too."

"That is because swimming is important," Lÿsa Mandy said, with dignity.

Mina laughed again, and the three of them started toward the field with the easy motion of children who already knew where they were going.

Halfway there, Lÿsa Mandy touched the outside of her bag just to make sure the letter was still inside.

It was.

That was enough.

The rest of the afternoon could happen normally now — the way camp afternoons were supposed to happen when you were eight and away for your second summer and loved ballet and gymnastics equally and still had a mother and a Charlie who wrote back exactly right.
