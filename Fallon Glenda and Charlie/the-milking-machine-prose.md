# THE MILKING MACHINE

The sound of the NICU was a symphony of mechanical breathing and soft, rhythmic beeps, but to thirteen-year-old Fallon Glenda, it sounded like a barrier. She sat by Incubator Four, her hands pressed against the cool plexiglass. Inside, Lÿsa Mandy was a tiny, fragile heartbeat, her skin almost translucent under the UV lights. 

A nurse was adjusting the feeding tube that snaked into the baby’s mouth.

"The hospital is giving her a few milliliters of donor milk through the tube every few hours," the nurse said gently, noticing the way Fallon was looking at the syringe. "Her stomach is just so small, Fallon. She’s not ready to coordinate nursing yet, so we have to go slow."

Fallon looked down at her own lap. She felt a heavy, aching fullness in her chest—a biological promise she was desperate to fulfill. "I want it to be my milk in that tube," she whispered. "I’m supposed to be the one feeding her."

"You will," the nurse promised. "But for now, the best thing you can do is provide. We need your milk for the tube feeds. It’s the best medicine she can get."

That afternoon, Mike and Terri Anne arrived. They didn't come empty-handed. While the hospital provided standard pumps, Mike had made a few calls. He wasn't interested in standard. 

"We brought something for your room at the mansion, Fal," Mike said. He was carrying a heavy, chrome-cased medical crate, and a second, identical one was already being wheeled into the private lactation suite he had secured for her at McMaster.

He opened the crate to reveal an industrial-grade milking machine—a hospital-standard, dual-motor pump used in the world’s leading neonatal centers. It was a serious, powerful piece of machinery, designed for maximum efficiency and comfort.

"The lactation consultant said the standard ones can be a struggle," Mike explained, his voice low and practical. "We got the industrial model. If you’re going to do the work, you’re going to have the best tools. There’s one for the house and one for here."

Fallon looked at the machine. It looked like something from a laboratory, all clean lines and digital readouts. To a thirteen-year-old, it was intimidating, but it was also a physical answer to her helplessness.

"How does it work?" she asked.

Terri Anne stepped forward, her touch steady. "It helps you do what Lÿsa isn't strong enough to do yet, Fallon. It does the work for her. Come on. Let’s get you settled."

They went into the private suite. Terri Anne helped her adjust the flanges and set the rhythm of the dual motors. When the machine hummed to life, it was a steady, powerful thrum that vibrated through Fallon’s chest. 

It was work. It was mechanical and demanding, requiring her to sit still every three hours, day and night. But as the first few drops of colostrum—thick and gold—began to pull into the sterilized collection bottles, Fallon felt the first stir of real maternal agency.

She wasn't just a thirteen-year-old girl in a hospital gown anymore. She was a provider.

An hour later, Fallon stood by the incubator again. She watched the nurse draw the small amount of milk into a syringe and carefully hook it to Lÿsa Mandy’s feeding tube. 

"There you go, little one," the nurse whispered. "Dinner from Mama."

Fallon watched the plunger of the syringe move down slowly. Lÿsa’s "little stomach" was receiving exactly what it needed. 

"I did that," Fallon said softly, her hand finding the glass of the incubator again. 

Mike stood behind her, his hand resting on her shoulder. He didn't need to say anything. He had seen the shift in her—the way her shoulders had squared, the way the "victim" look was being replaced by the "mother" look. The industrial machine was just a tool, but in Fallon’s hands, it was a weapon against the trauma of the Rio months. 

"You did, Fal," Mike said. "And you’ll keep doing it until she’s strong enough to do it with you."

The hum of the machine in the next room was the new heartbeat of the Key family’s Christmas season—the sound of a thirteen-year-old girl building a bridge to her daughter, one ounce at a time.
