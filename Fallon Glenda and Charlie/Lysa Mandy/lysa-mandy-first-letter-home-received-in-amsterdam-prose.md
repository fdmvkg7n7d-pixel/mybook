# Lÿsa Mandy's First Letter Home — Received in Amsterdam

## Placement

**When:** Early in Fallon Glenda and Charlie's Amsterdam honeymoon, after Lÿsa Mandy has settled into her second summer at French Woods.

**Where:** Their hotel room in Amsterdam.

---

The letter was waiting for them downstairs when they came back in from the canal.

It was late enough in the day that the room had gone honey-soft with evening light. Amsterdam did that to everything. Even the furniture looked gentler than it had in the morning. Fallon set her bag down on the chair by the window, and Charlie closed the door with his heel and held up the envelope between two fingers.

"From camp," he said.

Something in Fallon changed before she even took it from him.

Not panic. Not exactly. But the old instinct in her body — the one that believed love and vigilance were supposed to be the same thing — rose fast and silent all the same.

Charlie saw it.

He always did.

"Open it," he said quietly.

She sat on the edge of the bed to do it. The envelope had Lÿsa Mandy's name in an adult hand in the corner and Fallon felt, absurdly, a rush of gratitude toward whoever had written it there neatly enough to reassure her before she had even unfolded the page. Charlie sat down beside her, not touching her yet, only near.

Inside was a single sheet, folded twice.

Fallon opened it carefully.

Dear Mommy and Charlie,

I got here okay and I know where more things are now because I was here before. The pool looked smaller than I remembered but then when I got in it felt the same. It was cold first and then not cold.

I saw a girl I knew from last summer and she remembered me too. That made me happy. I am doing ballet and gymnastics both so don't think I picked because I didn't. I wanted both again.

My counselor is nice and she remembered I like things explained regular and not too fast. That helped.

You can still do your trip because I am okay.

Love,
Lÿsa Mandy

For a moment Fallon did not speak.

She read the line about the pool again. Then the one about ballet and gymnastics both. Then the counselor. Then the last sentence.

You can still do your trip because I am okay.

Charlie leaned his forearms on his knees and looked at the page from the side.

"Well," he said, after a moment. "That sounds like her."

It did.

That was the thing that broke the pressure.

If the letter had been too polished, Fallon would have distrusted it. If it had been too bright, she would have heard strain underneath it. If it had been vague, if it had been full of nothing but I miss you and come get me and camp is fine, she would already have been halfway back across the Atlantic in her head.

But this was Lÿsa Mandy exactly.

Concrete. Particular. The pool first. The cold first. The correction. Ballet and gymnastics both so don't think I picked because I didn't.

Fallon let out a breath so slowly it was almost a laugh.

"She put that in there because she thought I'd assume she chose one," she said.

"You would have asked," Charlie said.

"I would have needed to know."

"I know."

He still had not touched her. Fallon looked over at him then, and whatever he saw in her face must have told him the worst of the tension had passed, because he reached for her hand and laced their fingers together.

"Read the counselor part again," he said.

She did.

My counselor is nice and she remembered I like things explained regular and not too fast. That helped.

Fallon closed her eyes for a second.

That helped.

Not It is okay, Mommy, don't worry.
Not Everyone is being nice.
Not the broad, blurred language adults used when they wanted a child to seem settled.

That helped.

A real sentence. A sentence with edges on it. A sentence that meant Lÿsa Mandy had needed something particular and had found it.

When Fallon opened her eyes, the room had softened further around them. Outside the window, one of the canal boats moved through the water slowly enough to seem almost staged.

"She's really there," Fallon said.

Charlie turned his head. "What do you mean?"

Fallon looked back down at the letter. "I mean she's not just enduring it until she comes home. She's there. She's in it. She already has a counselor she's measuring things by. She saw somebody from last summer. She's correcting me in advance about ballet and gymnastics."

Charlie smiled.

"That does sound very her."

Fallon nodded once.

"If this had been her first summer, I don't think I could have done this." Her voice was low now, with no performance in it at all. "Not really. I could have physically gotten on the plane, but I don't think I could have been here."

Charlie didn't answer too quickly. That was one of the things about him. He never rushed to smooth out a truth just because it was tender.

"But it isn't her first summer," he said.

"No."

"And she's not somewhere strange. She's somewhere she already knows how to be."

Fallon pressed her thumb against the folded edge of the paper.

"Yes."

They sat quietly for a minute after that.

Charlie took the letter from her hand and read it himself, smiling a little more openly now.

"The pool looked smaller than I remembered but then when I got in it felt the same," he read aloud. "That might be the most Lÿsa sentence anyone has ever written."

Fallon laughed properly this time.

"I know."

"And she wants us to know very clearly that she picked ballet and gymnastics both."

"As she should."

He looked at the page a moment longer, then folded it carefully along the same lines Lÿsa Mandy had made.

"She's good," he said.

Fallon leaned into him then, finally letting the relief have her.

Not because she loved her daughter less from a distance, but because the distance had been given shape. French Woods was no longer an idea they had placed Lÿsa Mandy inside. It was a real place, with a real pool, a real counselor, a real remembered friend, a real schedule of ballet and gymnastics both. It existed now in the same solid way Amsterdam did outside the window.

Charlie kissed the side of her head.

"You want to keep it with us or put it in your bag?" he asked.

"With us," Fallon said immediately.

He handed it back.

She slid the letter into the drawer of the bedside table as though that were somehow safer than leaving it in the open, then looked back at him.

"Okay," she said.

"Okay what?"

She drew in a breath and let it go. "Okay, now I can be here."

Charlie's face changed at that — not surprise, exactly, but recognition of how large the sentence was.

He stood and held out a hand to her.

"Then come here," he said.

She took it.

Outside, the canal light kept shifting against the glass. Inside, with Lÿsa Mandy's real words folded six feet away in the bedside drawer, Amsterdam at last felt like the city they had actually come to share.
